# Bornholm Deposit

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a surviving disposal manifest and after-action report of the Baltic chemical-munitions programme and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **One disposal voyage of +2–+3 pT, reconstructed: a competent crew putting a defeated arsenal over the side into the Bornholm Basin, knowing exactly what the cold sea would and would not do to it.** The tonnage, the destination order, and the date are the manifest's; the men, their afternoon, and where the cargo actually went down are reconstruction.

---

They had been putting it over the side since first light, and by the forenoon the mate could read the whole shape of the job in the set of the deck: a coaster loaded low, standing off the island with her engine barely turning, and a working party at the rail in oilskins on a morning too fine for them. The cargo had come down to the quay under a word nobody said aloud — crates of shells and squat drums, banded at the shoulder, yellow for the most of it, blue for the sort that made a man sneeze and retch, and a few lots in a cleaner, newer paint that the officer checked himself against his folder and did not explain. The manifest called it by weight and by lot number and by the deep water it was bound for. It did not call it by name. You did not need the name to know the smell that would be under the tarpaulins if a seam had gone — faint, not unpleasant, cut garlic or a horseradish left too long — and every man aboard past a certain age knew it and said nothing.

The old man knew it best. He worked the forward crates slow and square, and he kept the boy on the windward side of him without ever seeming to arrange it. Thirty years back, in the other war, he had lain a night in a flooded shell hole that a yellow-banded round had found before him, and he had come out of it with the backs of his legs blistered from the water he had lain in, not from any cloud in the air. The stuff had gone down into the mud and the standing water and simply stayed there, oily and patient, and it had burned him cold, hours after the barrage had moved on. He had learned in one night what the folder in the officer's hands said in longer words. It did not wash away. Rain did not take it. The next week's rain had found it still in the hole.

That was the thing about the cargo that the mate turned over as he watched it go: the sea was meant to be the answer to it, and the men who knew it best did not believe the sea was any answer at all. The order was sound on its face. Take it out over deep water — the Gotland Deep, the folder said, a hundred fathoms of it — and let it down where no plough would ever turn it and no child would ever come on it, and let the whole cold weight of the Baltic stand between it and anyone. Dilution. The oldest cure there was. But the stuff in the drums was oily and heavier than water and did not care to dissolve; a spoonful stirred into a bucket would sit at the bottom of the bucket and wait there. In fresh water it would rot itself away in the time it took to smoke a cigarette. In the sea it took its time, the cold and the salt between them slowing it to a crawl — and in any quantity greater than the sea could take up at its very skin it did not rot at all. It formed a rind. A tough brown crust where the water touched it, and behind the crust the rest of it stayed exactly what it had been. The sea did not undo it. The sea wrapped it and set it down.

The men who wrote the folder knew this. It was not a secret and it was not new; it had been in the journals since the first war, since the first time anyone walked back into a shelled wood a fortnight on and was burned by ground that looked clean. The officer with the folder was not a fool and was not cruel. He had his order and his deep water and his weight to be rid of before the autumn, and he had the arithmetic every administration on that coast carried that year: a beaten country's whole poison, more of it than any furnace then built could burn in the years they had, and a sea right there that asked for nothing and sent no bill. So they chose the cheaper of the two things they knew how to do, and they knew, while they did it, which one it was. She was one hull of many that season. Before the ice came, tens of thousands of tonnes would go down into that one narrow stretch of sea, and the worse third of a defeated arsenal with it.

They did not reach the Gotland Deep. The mate had known by mid-afternoon they would not — the glass falling, a sea getting up out of the north-east, the skipper unwilling to work a low deck in a swell with what they had on it, and the light going the way it goes early that far north. Somewhere short of where the folder said, over a basin that ran ninety metres, a hundred at the most, the skipper brought her round and held her, and they finished the job there instead. Crate after crate to the rail and over, the splash and then the long silence as it went down. The officer wrote the deep water into the after-action report, because the deep water was the order and the order was what a report was for. Where the coaster actually lay when her deck came up light again went into no column, because no column asked for it. The record would be exact to the rail and would follow the cargo not one metre past it.

They did the job well, which is the part hardest to hold beside the rest of it. Not one crate went into the water slack; not one drum was let swing; the boy was kept upwind and gloved and taught, crate by crate, how to take the weight so a corner would not stove in against the rail. When the last of it was gone they scrubbed the deck down and flaked their lines, and the old man sat the boy aft and made him strip his oilskins over the side and go over his own hands and forearms with his eyes, slow, for any place that itched and should not — because the burn came late, and you had to look for it before it thought to look for you. Good seamanship on a bad errand. It was the only kind of care the job left room for, and they spent all of it.

The shell the boy put over last went down through ninety metres of water four degrees above freezing and settled into the soft grey mud of the basin, upright by chance, and lay there. The steel had years in it yet. The sea worked at the casing the slow way it works at all iron, and worked at the oily fill the only way it could — at the one seam where salt water reached the stuff through a weep in a joint — and there, and only there, the fill turned to a brown skin the thickness of a thumbnail and stopped. Behind the skin the rest stayed liquid. Behind the skin it stayed itself. The crust sealed the core the way the resin of that same coast seals a fly, holding the thing entire against everything that would take it apart, keeping it whole and waiting far longer than the men who set it down would be waiting anywhere. This was not learned afterward. It was in the books before the coaster ever loaded. The sea was never going to cleanse it. The sea was only ever going to keep it.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The surviving evidence is paper — a loading manifest and after-action report of the +2–+3 pT (1947–1948 CE) Baltic disposal programme, dated to the day and held in ordinary archival custody. The tonnage and the order to make the Gotland Deep are transcribed; the crew, the shortfall, and the release position are inferred from the gap between what such a record fixes and what it never carried.¹ ² The chemistry is real and pre-Contraction, and was published: the bulk-mustard crust that seals an unreacted liquid core for decades had been understood since the agent's first battlefield use in −28 pT (1917 CE). The Bornholm Basin took ~32,000 t of munitions (~11,000 t chemical fill, mostly mustard with arsenical and nerve-agent fractions), the largest documented single share of the ~1.6 million t on the world's seabed (a floor, +72 pT); Skagerrak is larger by tonnage but more dilute. A disarmament treaty of +48–+52 pT exempted pre-+40 pT dumping from any recovery obligation; no signatory has filed a notification.
