# Bornholm Deposit

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **This article is about the Combustion-Age chemical-munitions dumpsite in the Bornholm Basin of the Baltic Sea, the largest single deposit of its kind by tonnage.** For the comparable dumpsite in the Norwegian Trench, see **Skagerrak Deposit**. For the land-buried counterpart theatre in the northeastern continental interior, see **Haerbaling**. For the archival deposit at 78° N whose naming convention this article's title extends, see **Longyear Deposit**.

---

The **Bornholm Deposit** is the seabed accumulation of chemical-warfare munitions placed in the Bornholm Basin of the central Baltic Sea across +2 and +3 pT (1947–1948 CE), on the order of the military administration then governing the northern sector of occupied Germany, as one part of a wider multi-power disposal programme that also filled the Gotland Basin, the Little Belt, and the Norwegian Trench with the greater share of a defeated state's chemical arsenal. Bornholm alone received an estimated 32,000 tonnes of munitions, of which some 11,000 tonnes were the chemical fill itself — overwhelmingly sulfur mustard, with lesser quantities of arsenical vomiting agents and the nerve agent tabun. The site lies at 90–105 m depth, unmarked and unfenced, and is, since the turn of the sixty-seventh decade pT, among the most heavily surveyed stretches of seabed in Terran history.

The Combustion Age left two classes of sealed, dated cargo that a scholar may open the way a Bronze Age scholar opens a wreck. One class is the two intact merchant hulls catalogued as the Uluburun analogues (see **Combustion Age**, §4), sunk by accident, their inventories undisturbed because nobody meant them to sink. The other is a scattered family of deliberate consignments, of which Bornholm is the largest, sunk on purpose, by their own owners, expressly so that the cargo would cease to be an inventory at all. The name follows the convention fixed for the archival site at 78° N — both are places where an institution set something down and administratively stopped looking at it, one to preserve a memory and one to lose a liability — and the word now denotes any single-episode, sanctioned act of deposition, as against a midden's slow accretion. Nothing else about the two Deposits resembles the other. That is rather the point of giving them the same word.

What is unusual about Bornholm, set beside every other great deposit of the age, is that its paperwork survives. Occupying administrations audited demilitarisation the way they audited everything else, and the requisition orders, loading manifests, and after-action reports for the Baltic and Skagerrak operations sit today in three national archives, legible, dated to the day, none of it owed to any exotic taphonomy. What that paperwork records is authorisation. It does not reliably record placement: the vessels were not required to log where over the side they released their holds, several fell short of their designated grounds, and an undetermined supplementary tonnage was dumped by vessels of opportunity outside the programme entirely, attested only by what later survey found where no manifest predicted anything. A treaty concluded two generations afterward, when the hazard was already published science, declined to make its recovery anyone's legal obligation, and no signatory has since volunteered. This is not, in other words, a case of a lost record producing an uncertain past. It is a case of an excellent record certifying a decision, and a silence about its consequences that nobody since has been required to break.

## 1. Name and terminology

The waters are known, across the four coastal jurisdictions that border them, simply as "the Bornholm grounds," or by the basin's hydrographic name; nothing in ordinary usage distinguishes the dumpsite from the basin containing it, because for most of the interval between deposition and rediscovery there was no institutional reason to make the distinction. *Bornholm Deposit* is a Faculty coinage, extending to a marine hazard the usage fixed for the archival site at 78° N: a dated, bounded, administratively authorised act of placement, not an accumulation over time. It is routinely confused, in popular treatment, with the Gotland Basin dumpsite 300 km to the northeast, which received under a tenth of Bornholm's tonnage under the same authorisation and is barely distinguished from it in the period's own surviving correspondence — itself a small symptom of how little the operation's authors expected the distinction to matter to anyone, later.

A third and smaller consignment, found only by survey in the +60s pT and absent from every wartime manifest, lies in the Gdańsk Deep. It carries no name at all in the primary sources, because the primary sources do not know that it exists.

## 2. Sources and the attestation problem

The discipline's ordinary difficulty is a shortage of physical evidence beside a glut of institutional intention: coal, concrete, and plutonium outlast the paper that explained them. Bornholm inverts this. The intention is superbly attested. Two independent orders survive instructing that the Baltic consignment be taken to the Gotland Deep specifically, correspondence discussing tonnage and shipping schedule survives, and a chain of custody from arsenal to dockside survives complete enough to reconstruct the operation almost hour by hour up to the point of loading.

It is the last several hundred metres of that chain — the run from the rail of the vessel to the seabed — that the record does not carry. Logging a release position cost nothing and was not required. Crews under instruction to reach the Gotland Deep are known, from what was later found rather than from what was recorded, to have released cargo short of it on more than one occasion; a supplementary tonnage moved by vessels never itemised in the surviving manifests at all. A riparian survey programme spanning +66 to +69 pT, mapping the basin systematically by side-scan sonar and sediment assay, located a substantial, previously undocumented scatter in the Gdańsk Deep, fifty kilometres from anything any manifest describes. One assessment eight years later put the global seabed total, from all theatres, at a floor of 1.6 million tonnes of chemical munitions, and estimated that as much as half of it lies at locations no surviving document names. Okonkwo's study of the Bornholm manifests (2985) is the standard treatment of the asymmetry: the paper trail, she observes, is the best-attested single logistics operation of its administration's entire tenure, and among the least useful, because authorisation and placement were never, for this operation, the same fact. Halvorsen's shorter formulation, from her extension of the taphonomy of metal media to marine corrosion regimes, is the one now generally quoted: *the record is excellent up to the gunwale.*

## 4. The chemistry of persistence

Sulfur mustard hydrolyses quickly in fresh water — a half-life on the order of five to nine minutes at ordinary temperature — and considerably more slowly in the high-chloride water of an open sea, where the same reaction can take an hour or more to run its course. That difference alone would only have deferred the danger. What compounds it is bulk behaviour: mustard held in any quantity greater than what the surrounding water can immediately dissolve reacts only at its surface, forming a hydrolysed, amber-coloured crust that is far less permeable than the liquid beneath it and that can protect an unreacted core for decades. The sea, in other words, does not neutralise the agent it was assumed to dilute. It encases it. This was not a discovery of the survey era; the qualitative chemistry of mustard's hydrolysis had been studied since the weapon's first battlefield use, three decades before the disposal decision, and is not treated in the period's own literature as a surprise. The arsenical agents fare differently again: several do not hydrolyse in the ordinary sense at all, and their organoarsenic breakdown products, rather than the parent compound, are what the sediment record now carries and what a modern assay dates by.

## 5. The metal

The casings corrode at rates that differ by construction. Thin-walled aerial and practice munitions are assessed, in the most recent surveys, as likely to lose structural integrity within a decade of the assessment itself; heavier artillery-shell bodies are projected to corrode through by approximately +155 pT. Release in either case is not linear: an agent escapes slowly, as pinhole perforations open in the wall, until roughly half the housing's original metal mass is gone, at which point the remaining contents are released essentially at once. A separate consignment, from the later and unrelated western programme, was deliberately set in poured concrete before disposal specifically to arrest this process — an engineering response to the identical hazard, executed competently and as specified. It worked. Not every choice made about this material was a bad one; only most of them were made once, in haste, and never revisited.

## 6. Contact and injury

At least ninety-one fishing casualties east of Bornholm are individually documented since the disposal, nearly all from mustard brought up in a trawl net and handled before its origin was recognised. Seven fishermen were hospitalised in a single incident in +40 pT after a shell surfaced intact and later leaked in the hold. A commercial diver working the American coast in +71 pT sustained a second-degree blistering burn from a dredged shell of an earlier war entirely, and after three months in hospital and a skin graft, his hand still carries the mark. Four riparian governments jointly publish, and periodically revise, an illustrated pamphlet teaching trawler crews to recognise a leaking shell by smell and by the blister it raises on contact. It does not teach a crew how to avoid the hazard. There is no way to avoid it, only ways to notice it in time, and the pamphlet exists because recovering the material itself was never going to happen.

The amber-coloured hydrolysis crust described in §4 has its own minor but recurring casualty history, distinct from the fishing injuries: lumps washed ashore have been repeatedly gathered by people expecting ornamental Baltic amber, a genuinely abundant and prized regional material, and the resemblance is close enough that the error long survived correction in the period's own chemical literature. A coastal heritage collection assembled during the early Consolidation catalogued several such pieces as worked amber; a reassay for thiodiglycol residue, decades later, corrected the attribution, and the correction was published quietly enough that regional visitor literature along the same coast continues, in places, to repeat the older description.

## 7. The disposal question

Why the choice fell on dumping rather than destruction is contested, and the surviving record cannot adjudicate it, because the officials who made the decision left an order but no minute explaining the reasoning behind it. The **capacity school** holds that no technology existed, in +0 to +3 pT, capable of destroying the volume of chemical matériel on hand at any tolerable speed; incineration and large-scale neutralisation were, at best, pilot processes, and a continent still counting its own dead had neither the plant nor the years to spare. The **cost-deferral school** replies that nascent neutralisation and incineration methods were already documented in the period's own chemical-industrial literature by the mid-1940s, that they were merely slower and dearer than towing a shipload out to open water, and that administrations already committed to reconstruction on every other front chose, correctly by their own arithmetic, the option that sent an invoice to nobody. Both schools agree on the tonnage, the dates, and the chemistry. They disagree only about whether the absence of an alternative was a fact about the world or a fact about the budget, and nothing in the surviving paperwork settles which.

## 8. The treaty exemption

A global chemical-disarmament treaty, concluded in +48 pT and in force from +52, obliges its signatories to declare, secure, and eventually destroy chemical-weapons stockpiles under international verification. Its operative articles carry an explicit exception: material buried before +32 pT that remains buried, or dumped at sea before +40 pT, is excluded from the declaration and destruction regime at each signatory's own discretion. No signatory has, since the treaty entered into force, submitted a formal notification of a historical sea-dumping operation at all. The cutoff antedates the hazard's own scientific documentation by only a few years; it was written by negotiators who already had the chemistry, the tonnage estimates, and the fishing-injury record in front of them, and who chose the date that left the largest share of the problem outside anyone's obligation.

The contrast with the land-buried theatre in the northeastern continental interior (see **Haerbaling**) is instructive and uncomfortable. There, an abandoned wartime stockpile — several hundred thousand items at the single largest site, discovered and re-asserted as a live hazard well after the war's end — became the subject of an active, funded, multi-decade destruction programme, beginning operations at +69 pT and processing tens of thousands of items by +78. The difference is not the chemistry, which is the same family of agents in the same condition of decay. It is that a land-buried stockpile sits inside one state's sovereign territory and can be attributed, bilaterally, to one other named successor state, which is compelled to pay for its removal. A seabed dumped jointly by three or more occupying administrations, in international or contested waters, has no comparable address. Responsibility diffused across the ocean turned out to be no responsibility at all, and the treaty that might have reconstituted it was drafted, deliberately, not to reach that far back.

## 9. The Contraction and after

Through the Contraction (c. +133 to +195 pT), riparian monitoring capacity around the Baltic fluctuated with everything else administrative in the period; survey funding lapsed for some seasons and resumed for others, and no comprehensive assessment was published for stretches of two decades at a time. The Deposit needed no institution to persist. It is worth setting beside the Longyear Deposit, four thousand kilometres to the north, precisely because the symmetry is exact and unintended: at 78° N, an institution had arranged for information to survive without requiring anyone to keep tending it, and largely succeeded. At Bornholm, no institution arranged anything of the kind, and the hazard survived without tending regardless. Both facts are true. Neither was the lesson anybody meant to teach.

## 10. Assessment

| Evidence class | Survival | Governing mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Munition casings (steel) | Largely intact to date; full structural failure projected by c. +155 pT | Seawater corrosion rate against original wall thickness |
| Chemical agent, encapsulated bulk | Substantially intact where a hydrolysed crust formed | Self-limiting surface hydrolysis; low ambient temperature |
| Disposal authorisation record | Excellent; ordinary archival custody | Routine institutional audit, no exotic taphonomy required |
| Placement record (coordinates) | Poor; an estimated 40–50% of global tonnage unlocated | No logging requirement at release; undocumented supplementary dumping |
| Casualty record | Good from +10 pT forward | Clinical and hospital reporting, independent of military channels |
| Legal recovery obligation | Never established | Treaty cutoff fixed a few years after the hazard's own documentation began |

The Bornholm Deposit is, by every measure the discipline uses elsewhere, an exceptionally well-attested site: its authorisation dated to the day, its chemistry independently confirmed from three directions, its casualty record continuous for three-quarters of a century. What it demonstrates is that good attestation and a resolved hazard are different things, and that a record can be excellent precisely up to the point where a decision stopped being anyone's to make.

A single 105 mm shell, raised in +67 pT from a survey transect south of the basin at 97 m, had an original wall of 6 mm reduced by corrosion to 1.8; its fill, on assay, was unreacted. It had waited seventy-two years for someone to check. Halvorsen's formulation stands: *the record is excellent up to the gunwale.*
