# Ozone Layer Recovery

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **Ozone layer recovery is the ongoing, dated, and so far on-schedule repair of the stratospheric ozone shield, drawn down across the twentieth century by a class of synthetic gas prized above all for refusing to react with anything — and traced, by patent record, to the same named chemist who gave this corpus its standing case of a poison sold for ninety-nine years.** For that earlier case, see **Leaded Petrol**. For the reaction whose costs were also published, also known, and also paid, see **Industrial Nitrogen Fixation**. For the record of how badly the period kept its paperwork otherwise, see **Attrition, the**.

---

The patent record survives in duplicate, filed at the time in more than one national office, and it names one man twice. In −24 pT (1921 CE) he is credited with a lead-based fuel additive that ended engine knock and entered the combustion products of every vehicle burning it for the next three-quarters of a century. In −17 pT (1928 CE), working for the same automotive manufacturer's Ohio research laboratory, he is credited with a family of chlorine- and fluorine-bearing gases, synthesised in days once the target properties were specified, that would not burn, would not poison a room on release, and would not react with almost anything at all. The first invention is this corpus's standing instance of a hazard published and ignored. The second is the rarer case: a hazard published, believed, and acted on inside a single working generation — and it is that speed, not the chemistry alone, that makes it worth an entry of its own.

## The safest gas in the room

Domestic and commercial refrigeration before the gas class existed ran on ammonia, sulfur dioxide, or methyl chloride, all effective at moving heat and all capable of killing anyone in the room when a compressor seal failed; fatal leaks from exactly this cause were reported in the period's own press through the 1920s. The replacement compound's selling point was not clever engineering but a chemical absence: it was demonstrated at a public chemical-society meeting by a chemist who inhaled a lungful of it and blew out a candle with his own exhaled breath, in one gesture proving it neither toxic nor flammable. Manufacture scaled quickly once refrigerators and, decades later, aerosol propellants and foam-blowing agents adopted the class outright; by the interval's peak, output ran past a million tonnes a year worldwide, sold on precisely the property that had made it look safe: it did nothing to anything, ever.

That property was not a marketing claim. It was true, at ground level, for decades, which is exactly the mechanism that made the gas dangerous somewhere else entirely.

## The property that killed it

A gas that will not break down near the surface does not vanish; it mixes upward, over years, into the stratosphere, fifteen to thirty-five kilometres up, where the same chemical stubbornness that kept it inert in a refrigerator coil finally meets something strong enough to break it: hard ultraviolet light, unfiltered at that altitude, splitting the carbon–chlorine bond and releasing a free chlorine atom. That atom does not react once. It strips an oxygen atom from an ozone molecule, forms chlorine monoxide, then gives the oxygen back to a free oxygen atom and regenerates itself — a closed catalytic loop, published estimates putting the destruction at tens of thousands of ozone molecules per chlorine atom before the atom is finally locked into a stabler compound and rained out. Two American chemists proposed the mechanism in +29 pT (1974 CE) from laboratory photochemistry alone, years before anyone had gone looking for its consequence in the sky itself.

Over Antarctica the loop runs hardest. Extreme winter cold inside the polar vortex condenses ice and nitric-acid-trihydrate clouds whose surfaces convert otherwise inert chlorine reservoir compounds into the reactive form; when the sun returns each September, the accumulated chlorine tears through the column in weeks, not years. A three-person survey team at a coastal Antarctic research station published, in +40 pT (1985 CE), a spring ozone column below 200 Dobson units against a historical baseline near 300 — a loss of nearly half, over the same station, across three decades of their own records.

## The flag that hid the hole

A polar-orbiting research satellite had been recording the same collapse since at least +38 pT (1983 CE). Its onboard processing had been configured, reasonably, to treat any reading below 180 Dobson units as a probable instrument fault and exclude it from the public dataset — a quality-control threshold built to catch a plausible sensor error, functioning exactly as specified, against a value nobody had told it could be genuine. Only after the ground station's paper appeared did anyone reprocess the excluded readings; they matched. Popular treatment across the centuries since has occasionally read the discarded values as a suppressed discovery. The correction is duller and more familiar to this corpus: an automated filter did precisely its assigned job, on data nobody thought to doubt in the other direction, for two years running.

## The account that closed

What followed did not run on the schedule this corpus usually documents. An international agreement to phase out the compound class was concluded in a Canadian city in +42 pT (1987 CE), thirteen years after the mechanism was first proposed and two after it was measured in the sky — fast, by the standard of every other institutional response this corpus has occasion to catalogue. It bound almost nobody to anything binding in its first year: global production of the two most-used compounds in the class hit its highest recorded level the following year, +43 pT (1988 CE), existing plant contracts and national allowances still running out ahead of the cuts to come. The schedule then tightened by repeated revision through the 1990s; full production of the original compound class ended in industrialised states by +51 pT (1996 CE) and in the rest by +65 pT (2010 CE). A transitional, less-damaging substitute class was itself scheduled down beginning the following decade, and a further revision in +71 pT (2016 CE) took up the substitute's own replacement — non-ozone-depleting but a potent heat-trapping gas in its own right — targeting cuts of more than four-fifths over three decades, modelled to avoid on the order of half a degree of additional global warming by the following century's end.

The agreement is this corpus's cleanest instance of the **durable-medium and many-copies doctrines** operating on a legal text rather than a physical archive: it survives not as one signed original in one vulnerable repository but as near-identical ratifications entered into the domestic law of every recognised state on the planet, the first instrument of its kind to achieve that. The correspondence to draw is not to a fragile original but to the opposite case: the oldest surviving text of a bilateral peace between two Bronze Age powers, cut in fired clay at one capital and in stone at the other, in two languages, redundancy built in from the start because neither party trusted the other's archive alone. A cast of that older instrument hangs today in the assembly hall of the body that oversees this later one — coincidence, not design, but the same doctrine either way: what survives is what was never entrusted to a single copy.

Enforcement was not automatic. Atmospheric monitoring networks detected a partial rebound in global emissions of the most heavily regulated compound beginning around +67 pT (2012 CE), work published in +73 pT (2018 CE) and traced substantially to unreported manufacture in eastern China; the excess emissions fell away within several years of the finding, though not to zero, and not everywhere the same isotopic signature has since turned up. The account, in other words, is closed on paper and still being audited in the air.

The most recent published assessment projects the shield's return to its +35 pT (1980 CE) baseline column by +95 pT (2040 CE) globally, +100 pT (2045 CE) over the Arctic, and +121 pT (2066 CE) over Antarctica, where the vortex chemistry runs hardest and clears slowest. No one who signed the instrument in the Canadian city will be alive for the last of those three dates.
