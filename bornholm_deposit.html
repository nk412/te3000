Bornholm Deposit

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a surviving disposal manifest and after-action report of the Baltic chemical-munitions programme and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Bornholm Deposit is the largest documented single site of the Combustion Age’s sea-dumped chemical weapons: a defeated arsenal let down into the cold Baltic in the belief that deep water would break it down, and instead kept whole by it. The tonnage, the composition, and the destination order are the manifest’s; where the cargo actually came to rest is inferred from the point at which the record stops.

The Bornholm Deposit is a chemical-munitions dumpsite on the floor of the Bornholm Basin, in the southwestern Baltic Sea, where a defeated continental power’s surplus chemical arsenal was put over the side into deep water across +2–+3 pT. It holds on the order of 32,000 tonnes of munitions carrying some 11,000 tonnes of chemical fill — the majority sulfur mustard, with smaller arsenical and nerve-agent fractions — and is the largest documented single share of the roughly 1.6 million tonnes of chemical-warfare ordnance known to rest on the world’s seabed, a total published at +72 pT as a floor and not a ceiling, up to half of it at locations never recorded at all. The neighbouring Skagerrak Deposit, laid down in the same interval in the Norwegian Trench, is larger by gross tonnage — on the order of 130,000 to 170,000 tonnes — but far more dilute.

The disposal was carried out as a dilution, on reasoning that was standard and sound on its face: put the agent under a hundred metres of cold, saline water, where no plough would ever turn it up and where, in time, it would break down. Mustard does break down in water. In fresh water it hydrolyses within minutes. But the agent is oily, denser than water, and sparing of its solubility; in the cold and the salt of the Baltic the reaction runs far more slowly, and in any bulk greater than the water can take up at its surface it does not run to completion at all. The exposed skin of a dumped mass hydrolyses to a tough, gelatinous crust, and the crust then seals the unreacted liquid behind it. The sea does not neutralise the agent. It encases it.

None of this was learned after the fact. The self-sealing behaviour of bulk mustard had been in the literature since the compound’s first battlefield use at −28 pT, and the orders that sent the cargo out were written knowing what the cold water would and would not do. The consignment was authorised, weighed, catalogued to the day, and let down. A global chemical-disarmament treaty of +48–+52 pT later exempted all material dumped before +40 pT from any obligation to declare or recover it, and no signatory has ever filed a sea-dumping notification under it. Why the era chose to dump rather than to destroy is contested and unresolved: the capacity school reads it as a genuine constraint of wartime technology, the cost-deferral school as a cost moved quietly onto the future.

The disposal

The programme ran across the seasons of +2–+3 pT, in the aftermath of a continental war, as a defeated power’s chemical stockpile was disposed of — more of it than any incinerator then built could have burned in the years available. Loaded coasters stood off the island and worked their cargo over the rail into the basin: shells, aircraft bombs, and bulk containers, banded and painted by fill type, the mustard rounds the largest fraction. The manifests recorded each load by weight, by lot number, and by the deep water it was consigned to. They did not always record where it went down. The authorisation is exact and the placement is not: crews working a low deck in worsening weather released short of the ordered position more often than the paper shows, so the tonnage is firm and the map is soft. This asymmetry between an excellent authorisation record and a poor placement record is the standing subject of the deposit’s scholarship, whose watchword is that the record is excellent up to the gunwale and stops there. What can be fixed with confidence is the total that entered the basin and its composition; what cannot is the scatter of it across the mud. The Bornholm Basin — cold, saline, and for long stretches of the year poor in oxygen at depth — is a slow environment, and the munitions have moved little since.

The chemistry of persistence

Sulfur mustard, bis(2-chloroethyl) sulfide, is a persistent agent by chemistry as much as by design. It is an oily liquid, about a quarter again as dense as water and only sparingly soluble in it, so a mass released into the sea sinks, pools, and presents only a small surface to the water around it. Hydrolysis, the reaction that destroys it, proceeds only at that wetted surface and only as fast as the surrounding water — cold, saline, and slow-moving — will allow. In warm fresh water the reaction completes in minutes; in the near-freezing, high-salinity deep of the Baltic it is slowed by orders of magnitude. More consequential than the rate is what the reaction leaves behind. The products of surface hydrolysis are not soluble compounds that carry the agent away but a viscous, polymerising residue that stiffens into a crust — a rind, millimetres to centimetres thick, skinning the pooled mass. The crust is self-limiting: once formed, it holds water off the interior, and the reaction that made it cannot continue through it. Behind the rind the bulk agent stays liquid, stays concentrated, and stays hazardous for decades. The steel of a shell or a drum corrodes on its own, slower schedule — marine ferrous corrosion is the best-catalogued regime in the discipline’s taphonomy — and where a casing finally weeps, salt water reaches the fill at that one seam, forms its crust at that one seam, and seals it again. The mechanism intended to make the sea a solvent makes it, instead, a preservative.

The treaty exemption

The instrument that governs chemical weapons in the surviving record is a multilateral disarmament treaty concluded across +48–+52 pT, which bound its signatories to declare and destroy their stockpiles under verification. It does not reach the Bornholm Deposit. The treaty explicitly exempts material dumped at sea before +40 pT from any obligation to declare, monitor, or recover it — a category written to grandfather exactly this practice, carried out by many states in the years after the war and awkward for all of them. No signatory has ever filed a sea-dumping notification under it. The deposit therefore lies, by the express design of the one instrument that might have compelled its recovery, outside any duty to recover it. The paper follows the same contour: the authorisation to dump is excellent, the placement is poor, and the legal instrument that succeeded them both specifically declines to require that anyone go and look. Up to half of the world’s seabed chemical ordnance rests at locations that were never written down; the treaty asks after none of it.

Two readings

Why the era dumped its surplus agent rather than destroying it is the deposit’s one genuinely contested question, and it is not settled. The capacity school holds that the choice was a real constraint of the moment: a defeated arsenal larger than any furnace then built could process in the years available, released into a sea that asked nothing and charged nothing, because destruction at that scale and speed did not yet exist. The cost-deferral school holds that destruction did exist — merely slower, dearer, and chargeable to a budget — and that the sea was chosen precisely because it sent no invoice, the cost of the agent’s persistence externalised onto a future with no vote. The two readings divide on intent and agree on the chemistry, because the chemistry was never in dispute: both take as given the one fact the period’s own chemists had established and published before the first hull loaded, and that the centuries since have only confirmed. In the cold and the salt the agent does not disperse and does not decay to nothing. It forms its rind and it keeps. The crust seals the core. The sea does not cleanse it. The sea wraps it and sets it down.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction built on paper. The surviving evidence is a loading manifest and after-action report of the +2–+3 pT (1947–1948 CE) Baltic disposal programme, dated to the day and held in ordinary archival custody; the tonnage, the composition, and the order to consign the cargo to deep water are transcribed from it, while the release positions are inferred from the gap between what such a record fixes and what it never carried.¹ ² The chemistry is real and was published before the Contraction: sulfur mustard’s slow seawater hydrolysis and its self-limiting bulk crust had been understood since the agent’s first battlefield use at −28 pT (1917 CE). The Bornholm Basin holds ~32,000 t of munitions and ~11,000 t of chemical fill (mainly mustard, with arsenical and nerve-agent fractions), the largest documented single share of the ~1.6 million t on the world’s seabed, a floor published at +72 pT (2017 CE); the Skagerrak Deposit is larger by gross tonnage but more dilute. A chemical-disarmament treaty of +48–+52 pT (1993–1997 CE) exempts material dumped before +40 pT (1985 CE) from any recovery obligation, and no signatory has filed a sea-dumping notification.³ ⁴
