# The Last Sounding

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a drowned relay-station logger recovered from anoxic fjord mud, cross-read against matching firmware preserved in the Code Deposit, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The Last Sounding is the name — given by an early Consolidation-era listening community and never superseded — for the final years of automated transmission from a single mooring in a North Atlantic overturning-circulation monitoring array, received in its own time, and some ways since, as an intentional human signal.** The mooring's engineering record is bit-exact where it survives at all; what its last transmissions meant to the people who listened for them is reconstruction, and the two readings have not converged.

---

**The Last Sounding was the final several years of output from mooring MOOR/019, one of forty-two instruments in a transbasin array built in the 2040s CE to measure, in real numbers rather than modelled ones, whether the North Atlantic's overturning circulation was approaching the collapse its own century had long predicted for it.** The array's shore relay lost its crew, then its building, to the same rising water it existed to give warning of; its forty-one sister moorings died on schedule, one by one, as their sealed batteries ran out. MOOR/019 alone survived into the Consolidation, transmitting a routine fault code on a stretching, self-rationed interval to a shore station that had not existed for decades. A resettled hamlet on the same fjord picked the signal up on salvaged gear, mistook its dumb, faithful repetition for address, gave it a name, and cut that name into stone. The name is now the only name anything about the mooring has left. What it was actually saying — decoded a set of pT centuries later, against an independent copy of its own source code — was a battery voltage and a retry count. Whether anyone had, for some part of that interval, still been listening back is a narrower question the correction did not settle, and does not claim to.

## The Sill Watch

North Atlantic surface water carries a disproportionate share of the ocean's poleward heat transport as it runs north in the upper kilometre of the water column; losing that heat to the air over the Labrador, Irminger, and Nordic Seas is what makes it dense enough to sink, forming North Atlantic Deep Water, which returns south below roughly 1,500 metres and closes the loop. Measured in Sverdrups (1 Sv = 10⁶ cubic metres of water per second), the whole circulation moved a transport then estimated near 17 Sv. A modelling literature already decades old by the array's construction had shown the loop was not simply strong or weak but bistable: a minimal two-box treatment of the ocean's overturning admits two stable states for the same surface forcing — one vigorous, one collapsed — separated by a threshold set by the freshwater budget of the sinking regions. Add enough fresh water at the surface, from an ice sheet's runoff or heavier high-latitude rain, and the surface layer stops getting dense enough to sink at all, however hard the air above it still cools it; nothing about the atmosphere has to change for the ocean beneath it to flip. Both inputs were already measured and already rising through the Late Combustion decades, and the published odds of a full collapse within the following century were real, wide, and disputed. The array existed to replace a modelled probability with a counted one: forty-two moorings strung across the shallow ridge — sill crest under a kilometre deep — that the overflow of dense water must cross to leave the Nordic Seas at all, each profiling temperature, salinity, and pressure through the column on a fixed cadence and relaying the cast home.

## Losing the Shore

The array's onshore relay occupied the lower floor of a converted fish-processing building on a sill-fjord on the Norwegian Sea coast, staffed by a rotating crew who serviced the moorings by boat and forwarded their casts inland by leased satellite link. The regional grid and the shipping schedule the crew depended on did not fail on a date; they thinned, through the +130s pT (2070s CE), until the last fully crewed maintenance cruise — which replaced MOOR/019's primary battery pack with a full charge — sailed in +139 pT (2084 CE). By +141 pT (2086 CE) the station ran unattended, logging casts it no longer had staff to review. A winter storm surge, riding a sea level already metres above the Middle Combustion datum, took the ground floor in +158 pT (2103 CE); the relay racks went down with it into the fjord's own bottom water, which a shallow sill restricts from renewing and which had stood functionally anoxic for longer than the station had existed. The low oxygen that had done nothing for the fjord's fish stocks did a great deal for the racks: corrosion that would have reduced the casings to rust in open air instead stalled for the better part of three centuries, preserving the logger boards, and with them the array's own local record of itself, until a survey recovered them intact.

## Orphan Protocol

A sealed lithium primary pack of the class the moorings carried stores on the order of 650 watt-hours per kilogram; a forty-kilogram pack, as fitted, held some 24,000 Wh at manufacture. A standard cast-and-relay cycle — winch-driven profile to depth, return, and a satellite burst home — metered out close to 24 Wh, giving each mooring a design life near 1,000 cycles: at the array's standard ten-day cadence, a fraction over twenty-seven years. Deployed in +102 pT (2047 CE), the other forty-one moorings died within that budget through the +120s to +150s pT, on schedule, unremarked, each simply running out mid-cycle. MOOR/019, refreshed to a full pack in +139 pT, had roughly three hundred cycles left when the relay went under water in +158 pT and stopped answering. Its controller carried a contingency routine for exactly that case: after four hundred days without acknowledgment from shore, it began stretching its own interval between casts, trading frequency against the charge it had left rather than spending that charge at the old rate against silence. None of this manufactured energy. The routine could only trade tempo for duration against a store of chemical potential sealed into the pack the day it was built; it could not add a single additional watt-hour to it, and when the accumulated draw of some three hundred stretched-out cycles finally emptied the cells, in the neighbourhood of +201 pT (2146 CE) on the schedule's own arithmetic, the mooring did not fail so much as finish a sum it had been running the whole time.

## Third Landing

A fishing hamlet reformed on the same fjord in the early Consolidation, its people salvaging what radio gear the drowned station had not taken with it, and by +198 pT (2143 CE) its watch was logging a faint, regular burst on a cadence near seven weeks — MOOR/019, by then deep into its stretched interval, still faithfully checking for an answer that had not existed for forty years. Nothing in the burst was legible to a receiver without the array's own decode tables, which nobody at Third Landing had; what the watch heard was a short, evenly spaced tone, and what it wrote down was an interpretation:

> "Heard again at first light, same as every seventh week now. We — all of us, watch and off-watch both — sat with it the full cycle, no? Nothing we send back reaches it. We answer anyway." — watch log, Third Landing, entry undated.

The Faculty reads the bracketed gloss on "we" and the closing tag as an early written attestation of two features since fixed in Standard Terran: the inclusive–exclusive distinction the log-writer felt the need to clarify by hand, and the invariant question tag appended regardless of the clause it closes. Both are read elsewhere as inheritances of the age's most displaced populations; here they surface in a coastal watch-log two generations into the Consolidation, already fully formed. In +199 pT (2144 CE) the hamlet cut a name into a stone hauled from the old quay and set it at the point on the shingle nearest the mooring's charted position: THE LAST SOUNDING, with two dates. The final burst was logged in +201 pT (2146 CE); nothing followed it, and the watch log's own last entry records only that the cycle due had not come.

## The Fault String

The stone survived; the array's working name did not, and nothing else durable enough to carry it has been found. What ended the sentimental reading was not the stone but a separate recovery: an Institute for Deep Record Studies survey lifted the drowned relay's logger boards from the fjord mud in +445 pT (2390 CE), and a matching build of the mooring's own firmware source turned up, independently, in the Code Deposit — routine engineering software, bundled years earlier into a public repository by a contractor who had no reason to think it would outlast the hardware it ran. Marchetti, L., reading the two against each other in +473 pT (2418 CE), matched the burst's bit pattern exactly to a hard-coded diagnostic string the firmware emitted on low-voltage fault, cycling in a fixed loop with no field for a message and no routine capable of composing one:

> `MOOR/019 CHK-FAIL BATT<2.10V CH-B RETRY(14) NO-ACK`

Seven decades of listening, on both ends of the record, had been given to a battery reading, a channel identifier, and a retry count. It was, in her own idiom for this class of error, another trivial machine artefact mistaken for composed testimony — the discipline's oldest kind of misreading, run this time not on a line of code's comments but on the plain fact of a signal's persistence, which had been enough by itself to read as intention. The correction did not touch the stone's inscription, which the Faculty catalogues but does not amend.

One reading was left standing. Solheim-Achebe, P., granting the fault string entirely, has argued from the maintenance cruise log that the four-hundred-day silence threshold could, on the surviving firmware build, be reset by a manual command as easily as by an automatic acknowledgment — so that some of the transmissions Third Landing heard, in the years before the record shows the relay building going under, cannot be dated to the automated regime with certainty rather than to a period in which someone was still, briefly, at the console resetting the clock by hand. Marchetti's answer is that the same ambiguity is symmetrical: a manual reset and an automatic retry write an identical line. Which of the mooring's last years were unattended and which, if any, were not is exactly the kind of question the surviving record was not built to answer, and the two readings stand unadjudicated, on the same evidence, still.

## Provenance and ground

The entry rests on three independent fragments: the relay station's own logger boards, recovered intact from anoxic fjord-bottom mud after a taphonomic run Halvorsen, E. dates to the station's flooding in +158 pT; a matching firmware build recovered bit-exact from the Code Deposit, the single Sunday snapshot of the world's public software; and the inscribed stone at Third Landing, the only surviving name-bearing object associated with the array at all. Okonkwo's standing method of reading incidental telemetry as an unintended record supplies the mooring's operating chronology from the logger boards' own timestamps; the array's official designation, its consortium, and the identities of its crew are not attested in any of the three fragments and are not reconstructed here.
