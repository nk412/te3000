# Cardiac Pacemaker

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **A cardiac pacemaker is a device, worn externally or set inside the body, that delivers a timed electrical pulse to drive a heartbeat the body's own conduction system can no longer produce reliably on its own.** It is not, whatever an early and still-repeated misreading of the recovered object concluded, a votive offering; nor is it, in its later titanium form, the fragile thing an amulet is assumed to be. For the era's other engineered biological dependency that also required continuous outside maintenance to keep working, see **Cryogenic Biobank**. For the general mechanism by which the rest of the age's paper record failed to survive it at all, see **Attrition, the**.

---

The object outlasts the sentence written about it. A sealed titanium capsule the size of a matchbook, its interior a lithium cell and a few grams of switching circuitry, its exterior passivated by an oxide layer that forms itself and does not thereafter react with bone, soil, or seawater, will still be exactly what it was found to be a thousand years after the institution that implanted it, insured it, and recorded its serial number has left no trace of any of the three. The paper card that named its owner is gone within a generation, by the ordinary mechanism of **Attrition, the**. The device that named nothing but itself, in a fine engraved hand nobody thought to make legible to the far future because nobody thought about the far future at all, is still there to be read.

The first version of the device to reach a human chest did so on 8 October +13 pT (1958 CE), in Stockholm: a single-transistor circuit built by a Swedish medical-electronics engineer, implanted by a Swedish cardiac surgeon, in a patient whose heart was stopping twenty to thirty times a day and who agreed to the operation as a last resort rather than a considered one. The first unit worked for three hours before it failed; its replacement, fitted the following morning, lasted six weeks. The patient underwent more than twenty further replacements across the rest of his life and outlived both the engineer and the surgeon who built the first two, dying decades later of a cause the device had nothing to do with. Nothing about that sequence was a triumph delivered whole. It was a triumph delivered as a maintenance contract, one failing unit at a time, for over forty years — which is, this article will argue, the correct way to understand almost everything else about the object as well.

## A quarter century of hand-cranks and vacuum tubes

The idea preceded the working device by a generation. An American physiologist built a spring-wound, hand-cranked electromechanical stimulator in -13 pT (1932 CE) and gave the whole class of device the name it still carries, though his own instrument found no commercial interest and its maker's own account of what became of it is contested; the machine itself may or may not survive in an institutional collection, and the discipline that would settle the question is not this one. Two decades later, in +7 pT (1952 CE), an American cardiologist demonstrated a fully external unit — powered from house current, delivering pulses of roughly 150 volts through the closed chest wall — that kept a patient alive for fifty-two hours and, in its wake, functioning enough to survive a further six months on the same principle. A Canadian national research programme, working from the same premise a few years later, built an alternating-current, vacuum-tube version of the external device before the whole line of development moved, in 1958, inside the body itself.

Every early unit shared one problem the transistor did nothing to solve: the power source. The first cells were mercury-zinc, chosen because nothing smaller and more energetic existed, and they were unreliable on two fronts at once — an ordinary service life of about two years, forcing a fresh surgery on that schedule alone, and a tendency to vent hydrogen gas as they discharged, which a sealed implant cannot safely do. The fix, when it came, was chemical rather than mechanical, and it is the one part of this history genuinely worth calling elegant.

## A battery that is also its own container

From +27 pT (1972 CE), pacemakers built around a lithium-iodine cell began reaching patients, supplied almost entirely by a single American manufacturer that would go on to account for something like seven-tenths of the world's pacemaker batteries. The chemistry does something no earlier implantable cell did: as the lithium anode reacts with iodine vapour from a solid iodine–polymer cathode, the reaction product — lithium iodide — deposits directly between the two electrodes as a thin, ion-conducting solid film, and that film is simultaneously the battery's discharge product, its separator, and its own seal against internal short-circuiting. The battery manufactures its own container as it runs down. There is no free liquid to vent, no gas to build pressure, and a self-discharge rate low enough that shelf life and service life are close to the same number. By the time one manufacturer's own count had reached 150,000 supplied units, not one had failed or run down prematurely; more than fifteen million such cells would eventually be fielded. Service life stretched from two years to a decade or more on the strength of chemistry alone, with no change to what a patient had to do, which was nothing.

The housing kept pace. From roughly the same decade, the sealed epoxy or metal-and-epoxy shells of the first implants gave way to a hermetically welded titanium can, laser- or resistance-welded shut around the electronics and battery, with the only points of penetration a small number of glass- or ceramic-insulated feedthroughs carrying the lead wires out to the heart. Titanium's advantage is not strength but indifference: exposed to tissue or, later, to soil or seawater, it forms a thin oxide layer within seconds that is chemically inert, does not dissolve, and does not thereafter react with much of anything — a passivation Halvorsen's established taphonomy of ferrous ordnance and copper barriers does not apply to at all, because titanium simply declines to participate in the corrosion chemistry that governs everything else this discipline studies in metal. A pacemaker can recovered today is not a weathered artefact requiring conservation. It is, allowing for the leads, essentially as made.

## The reading that got it wrong

For the better part of a scholarly generation after the first substantial recoveries, a sealed metal disc found lying against or within the ribcage of an unburned inhumation — smooth-cased, faintly domed, with two or more fine wires running from it toward the chest cavity — was catalogued as a pectoral ornament or a protective amulet, on the reasonable-looking grounds that objects placed deliberately over the heart in a grave, in every other period this discipline studies, generally are one. The wires were read as suspension cord, corroded to metal filament; the smoothness of the case, as evidence of handling and value. It was, on this reading, a heart charm, and a moderately common one, since the objects turn up wherever unburned Late Combustion burials survive at all.

The correction came from opening one. A sectioned example shows no cavity for a stone, no chamber for anything a pectoral ornament of any prior period would hold — only a densely packed lithium-iodine cell, a small integrated circuit, and the same laser-welded seam every other recovered specimen carries. The wires are not suspension cord; run to their far ends, they terminate in electrode tips seated, or once seated, against cardiac tissue, in a placement no ornament in this discipline's entire prior catalogue has ever required. It is, functionally, a piece of load-bearing medical equipment, badly disguised by nothing but its own inoffensive shape and by an examiner's very reasonable prior experience of what a sealed metal object placed over a heart usually turns out to mean. The correction is now standard; the older catalogue entries, in institutions slow to revise them, are not, and a popular account of Late Combustion burial custom still in wide circulation describes the objects, uncorrected, as protective heart-talismans buried with the dying. Marchetti's general model of canon formation under sampling bias applies here without much adjustment: a recovered object, read confidently and wrongly by the first hands to hold it, then cited by the second hand and the third, becomes very difficult to unread precisely because the wrongness was reasonable the first time.

**The votive heart** is now the standing term for the misreading, not for the object; Halloran, coining it, is also responsible for this discipline's flattest verdict on the whole affair, worth quoting exactly as she wrote it: *"we buried them with their hearts twice — once when they died, and once when we found the second one and did not know what it was."*

## The fee nobody charged

One further fact about the object is worth stating plainly, because the age that made the device also published it, in its own professional literature, decades before the age ended. An unvented lithium-iodine cell, brought to cremation temperature, does not simply melt; the sealed can, unable to release the pressure building inside it, ruptures. Later experimental work — testing devices of this kind directly in working crematoria — recorded ignition-to-rupture intervals of a few minutes at ordinary cremation temperatures, on the order of four to six minutes across a temperature range of 800 to 1,100 °C, with reported sound levels loud enough to exceed the era's own workplace hearing-damage threshold and, in older facilities, damage to the brickwork of the cremator itself. Professional guidance calling for the device's surgical removal before cremation was published and current for decades. A national survey of one country's own crematoria nonetheless found that roughly half had detonated one regardless, and an earlier count from the same profession found the cremator damaged beyond repair in three cases out of every hundred, with at least one recorded injury to staff. The hazard was never secret. Compliance simply required someone, at the point of death, to know the device was there, say so, and pay to have it taken out — three separate and independent failure points, any one of which breaks the chain.

Two readings of that failure stand in this discipline without a verdict between them, and both extend frameworks already established elsewhere in this corpus. The **cost-deferral school**, in Solberg's later extension of her own institutional-economics programme, reads the compliance gap as an ordinary case of an internalisable cost — a mortuary's or a family's fee for surgical explantation — deferred onto a risk the fee-payer would very likely never personally experience. The counter-reading, from Ibarra, applies her established capacity-constraint model — built originally on wartime chemical demilitarisation and centennial waste-disposal programmes — to argue that the binding constraint was never the fee at all but a simple shortage of qualified hands: too few forensic examiners and too little mortuary throughput, in an era approaching its own contraction, to explant every device from every body on the schedule the guidance recommended. Neither side of the argument disputes the chemistry, the guidance, or the outcome. They disagree only about which failure to blame it on, and the surviving record — a set of professional surveys, not an accounting ledger — does not obviously decide between them.

## What the record could not keep

None of the identifying apparatus survives with the object. From +68 pT (2013 CE), regulation in one major jurisdiction required every implanted device to carry a scannable identifier encoding its manufacturer, model, and serial number — a genuine attempt, late in the interval, to give a durable object a durable name. It worked exactly as designed for as long as the databases behind the code existed, and not one day longer; no central registry linking that identifier back to a patient, a model history, or a recall notice is known to survive the Attrition in any form, and no consensus had even been reached, within the period's own professional literature, on which institution ought to be keeping such a registry in the first place. What each patient carried instead was a paper or laminated card, meant for a wallet, printed with the device's make, model, and implant date for use in an emergency neither this discipline's sources nor, evidently, most patients' own descendants ever needed to consult. Datta's general model of the migration debt applies to the registry with no modification at all: a record that depends on a subscription, an institution, or a continuously updated database is not a record but a promise, and this was one more promise among many that fell due, unpaid, in the same collapse that took everything else.

The asymmetry runs deeper than one lost card. Unlike the era's other deliberate biological dependency — the liquid-nitrogen dewar of a **Cryogenic Biobank**, resupplied on a season's cycle and failing, when it failed, within a single unwatched week — a pacemaker's maintenance interval was measured in a decade, not a season, and its failure mode on interruption was simply a slowing heartbeat rather than an instant loss; the device asked far less of any institution than the tank did, and outlasted every institution that ever serviced it regardless. It also asked nothing at all like what the era's other engineered reserves demanded of the ground that held them. **Onkalo** was built to hold its danger for a hundred thousand years and, on its own builders' reasoning, deliberately carries no warning marker to the far future at all; the **Svalbard Seed Vault** was built to hold its promise for centuries under an engineered permafrost floor. A pacemaker was never built to be found by anyone. It carries no warning and no explanation, only a name, in the one place — cut into structural titanium — a name could still be read after everything that once explained it was gone.

Recovery was never even. A device left in an unburned grave, in a jurisdiction with no strong cremation custom, survives; the identical device, in the same decade, from a patient cremated according to guidance correctly followed, does not, and no accounting from this period distinguishes the two outcomes as anything but private and unrecorded family choices. What this discipline holds, then, is not a sample of who received a pacemaker. It is a sample of who was, for reasons the record does not give, left in the ground with one.

By the interval's later decades, on the order of 1.4 million pacemakers were being implanted worldwide each year, with something like three million people carrying one at any given time, at rates differing by more than a factor of ten between the best- and worst-resourced health systems the period maintained side by side. A late variant, cleared for use from +71 pT (2016 CE), dispensed with the wire leads altogether: a self-contained unit the size of a large vitamin capsule, delivered by catheter and lodged directly against the wall of the heart's own chamber, its battery rated for twelve to seventeen years and its case the same inert titanium as every version before it. From roughly +65 pT (2010 CE), at least two independent charitable schemes — one run through an American university medical centre, one through a French collection service — began gathering explanted and unused devices from the wealthier of those health systems, testing each for at least four years of remaining battery life, resterilising the minority that passed, and returning them, under a regulatory compassionate-use exemption, to patients in health systems that could not otherwise afford one at all. One such scheme alone collected upward of twenty-five thousand devices over its first decade, found perhaps a fifth of them fit to reuse, and reimplanted something over a hundred, on the far side of the world, in a body that would, in due course, keep the same silent, decade-long time as the original owner's would have.

A specimen recovered from a subsided coastal cemetery, its grave goods otherwise unremarkable, its inhumation undisturbed by cremation or by anything else, carries on its titanium flank an engraving still perfectly legible under raking light: a model code, a lot number, and a serial string of eleven characters, precisely stamped, cross-checkable against nothing. It names the object exactly as its maker intended. It does not, and was never going to, name the person it kept beating inside.
