# Cardiac Pacemaker

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a recovered titanium implant of the votive-heart class and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The cardiac pacemaker is a sealed device, worn inside the chest, that supplies by a timed electrical pulse the heartbeat a failing conduction system can no longer produce; the first patient fitted with one outlived it by more than forty years and some twenty replacements, and died at last of a cause that had nothing to do with his heart.** The first implant's dates and failures, and the recovered titanium cans, are record; the owners the cans belonged to are inference.

---

The **cardiac pacemaker** is an implanted machine that reads or overrides the heart's own timing and delivers a small, measured pulse to a chamber wall, so that the muscle beats when its native signal does not arrive. The first one seated wholly inside a human body, in Stockholm in +13 pT, ran a single transistor off a ring of cells and delivered two-volt pulses a millisecond and a half wide, seventy to the minute. It failed after three hours. The second, fitted the next morning, lasted six weeks. The patient the pair had been fitted to lived another four decades, was reopened and re-fitted upward of twenty times, and died an old man of something unrelated to the rhythm the machines had been keeping.

The idea was older than the implant. A hand-cranked, spring-driven stimulator was built and named at −13 pT, of interest to no one at the time; an external unit run from mains current, throwing a hundred and fifty volts through a closed chest, held a stopped heart across fifty-two hours at +7 pT. What was new at +13 pT was smallness — a circuit and a battery small enough to stay inside a man. Within a generation the machine had become ordinary. By the interval's later decades some 1.4 million were fitted a year and about three million people carried one at any time, at rates differing by more than a factor of ten between the best- and worst-resourced health systems. It went on shrinking, until a catheter-delivered version no larger than a vitamin capsule, rated for a dozen years and more and carrying no wires at all, reached use at +71 pT.

The devices outlast everything around them. A hermetically welded titanium can, self-passivating, sits outside every corrosion regime the discipline otherwise catalogues in metal, and turns up intact in unburned graves — where, for a scholarly generation, it was read as a pectoral amulet worn over the heart for luck rather than as the machine that had been keeping the heart running. The mistake has a name of its own now: **the votive heart**. No registry survived to tie a recovered serial to a name. What can be said of the owners is inferred from the one life the record does hold, the first, whose shape is taken to stand for the rest.

## The device

The condition the first device was fitted to is intermittent complete block: the electrical signal that should run from the top of the heart to the bottom, setting the beat, fails to arrive, and the muscle stands idle until it catches again. The standard clinical account records twenty to thirty such lapses in a day, each dropping perfusion until the patient greys and falls, and no reliable interval between them. A pacemaker answers the fault directly. It watches for the beat that does not come and, in its place, discharges a pulse across two electrode tips seated against the chamber wall; the muscle, given a beat it cannot make for itself, takes it. The first implanted unit fixed its rate at seventy to the minute and did not vary it. Later units sensed the native rhythm and paced only into its gaps, which is why a recovered can, read out, reports not a metronome but a record of exactly how often its owner's own heart had failed him.

The early hardware was the weak part, never the patient. The +13 pT circuit was a single transistor; its housing was not yet titanium, and its cells were the trouble. Everything downstream of that first six weeks was the same machine, refined: the same two electrode tips, the same measured pulse, seated and reseated as each unit wore out.

## The self-forming cell

The first cells were mercury and zinc, good for about two years, and prone as they ran down to vent hydrogen — a gas with nowhere to go inside a sealed implant. The surgeries therefore came round on the battery's schedule rather than the patient's, whether he wished them or not. From +27 pT the chemistry changed under him. A cell built of lithium and iodine did a quiet, self-terminating thing: as it discharged, its own product laid itself down as a thin solid film between the electrodes and served at once as the cell's electrolyte, its separator, and its seal. It made its own container as it ran. There was nothing to vent and nothing to leak, and a working life of a decade or more where two years had been the rule. One maker's version came to sit in some seven of every ten pacemakers on the planet; more than fifteen million were fielded, and by that maker's own count not one of the first hundred and fifty thousand failed in service. The housing moved in the same decade to the welded titanium can. The patient would have registered none of this as chemistry. He would have registered it only as time — the operations no longer coming so often.

## The service life

The rescue did not arrive once and hold. It arrived as a standing arrangement, one machine at a time, renewed against the wear of each, and the arrangement held for more than forty years. The patient outlived the engineer who built the first unit and the surgeon who seated it. He was opened, closed, and opened again upward of twenty times, and each time walked out with a working heart he had not been born able to keep. As the cell chemistry improved, the interval between surgeries stretched from a couple of years toward a decade. The record does not describe those years as remarkable, and that was the whole of what the machine returned to him: ordinary years, the kind a man whose heart stops thirty times a day does not otherwise get.

The same arrangement, scaled, became a global commonplace and an unequal one. From +65 pT, charitable schemes worked the tenfold disparity directly — collecting devices explanted from patients in the well-resourced systems, testing each for years of remaining charge, resterilising the sound ones, and sending them to chests in systems that would otherwise have gone without. A titanium can rated for a decade routinely outlives its first owner with years still on the cell; the recovery economy is the arithmetic of not wasting them.

## Deprecation and recovery

The leaded can was superseded, not retired, by the leadless capsule from +71 pT, which dispenses with the wires that were always the frailest part of the system and is delivered up a vein. A scannable per-device identifier was mandated in one major jurisdiction from +68 pT, but no central registry linking the number back to a patient or a recall history is known to survive the Attrition, nor was one ever agreed on. The can names itself precisely, in a hand stamped to outlast any reader, and its owner not at all — an instance of **the migration debt**, a record that was a promise no institution stayed alive to keep.

Recovery is also literal, and it is where the votive-heart reading came from. A can survives to be recovered only because its owner was buried rather than burned: an unvented lithium-iodine cell ruptures explosively under cremation heat, with documented ignition-to-rupture intervals of four to six minutes at eight hundred to eleven hundred degrees. Pre-cremation removal guidance was published and current for decades, yet a national professional survey found roughly half of one country's crematoria had detonated one regardless. The gap between the guidance and the practice is read by some as a cost quietly deferred onto the mortuary and by others as a shortage of the hands that would have done the removing; the readings are set out and not adjudicated. What the survivors leave is the intact grave-borne can, and the reading that mistook it for a charm.

The first patient's last can was one of the titanium kind, keeping its steady seventy a minute. His own heart had quit on him in middle age; the one the machines kept ran him into deep old age, decades on, until he died of something else entirely. When he stopped, the can did not. The machine had been built to keep a heart from stopping, and it had; in the end the heart stopped because he did, and not the other way around.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The votive-heart can is real evidence — a hermetically sealed, self-passivated titanium implant, effectively immune to the corrosion this discipline otherwise tracks in metal, recovered intact from an unburned grave and read as a machine rather than an ornament only late (Halloran¹). The first implant's dates and failures are record: the external machine of +7 pT, the +13 pT operation in Stockholm, the three hours, the six weeks, the twenty-odd replacements, and the unrelated death decades on. The owners of the anonymous cans are inference. The mechanism is real throughout — a failed conduction signal driven by a timed pulse; a lithium-iodine cell that seals itself with its own discharge product; a titanium oxide that will not react; a sealed cell that bursts within minutes at cremation heat. No registry survived to give a serial a name (Datta²), and the removal gap that governs which cans survive at all is read as deferred cost (Solberg³) or as too few hands (Ibarra⁴) without a verdict.
