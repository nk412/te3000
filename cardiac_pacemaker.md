# Cardiac Pacemaker

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a recovered titanium implant of the votive-heart class and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The reconstructed life the first implanted cardiac pacemaker ran — read out of a recovered titanium can that a generation of scholars mistook for an amulet — a machine that failed twice in its owner's first six weeks and then, one replacement at a time, kept his heart beating for more than forty years, until he died old of something that had nothing to do with it.** The can and the first implant's dates are record; the man between them is inference.

---

For most of a scholarly generation the object was a charm.

It turns up wherever an unburned Late Combustion grave survives: a sealed metal disc, smooth and faintly domed, lying against the ribs, two fine wires trailing from it toward the heart. An object placed over the heart, in every earlier period this discipline knows, is an amulet — so it was catalogued, the wires read as a corroded suspension cord, the polish of the case as the wear of handling and worth. The reading held until someone cut one open. Inside the welded titanium shell there was no cavity for a stone: only a dense little cell, a chip of circuitry, and the wires — not cord — running out through glass-sealed ports to electrode tips that had once sat against the wall of a heart. It had not been worn over the heart for luck. It had been keeping the heart running. The term survives now for the mistake, not for the object: *the votive heart*.

What no such can will tell you is whose it was. The serial is stamped into the titanium in a hand meant to outlast any reader, cross-checkable against nothing — no registry survived that ever tied a number to a name, and the paper card each owner once carried in a wallet, printed with make and model and the date of the fitting, went the way of all the age's paper. The can names itself precisely and its owner not at all. But we know, from the record that did keep, how the first such life began, and the shape of that one stands for the rest.

His heart had begun to stop.

Not all at once — that would have killed him and ended the matter. It stopped for seconds at a time, twenty and thirty times a day: the electrical signal that should have run from the top of the heart to the bottom, setting the beat, simply failed to arrive, and the muscle stood idle until it caught again. Each time, he greyed, and his knees went, and the room swam and came back. A man cannot live like that for long. By the autumn of +13 pT, in Stockholm, he had agreed to have his chest opened — not as a considered choice but as the last one left.

The parts were barely a decade old. A physician across an ocean had shown, at +7 pT, that a stopped heart could be driven from outside the body altogether: a machine run off a wall socket, throwing current through the closed chest at a hundred and fifty volts, that had held one dying patient for fifty-two hours until his own rhythm returned. The idea was older still — a generation before, at −13 pT, a spring-wound, hand-cranked stimulator had been built, named, and set down, of no interest to anyone. What was new, in that northern city, was smallness. An engineer had fitted a single transistor and a ring of cells into a disc that would go inside a man and stay there, and a surgeon was willing to put it there.

They did. The circuit delivered two-volt pulses a millisecond and a half long, seventy to the minute, and his heart, given a beat it could not make for itself, took the beat and kept it. For three hours.

Then the first one failed.

They fitted the second the next morning. It lasted six weeks. And this, it turned out, was not the failure of the enterprise but the whole shape of it: the rescue did not arrive once and hold. It arrived as a standing arrangement, one machine at a time, renewed against the wear of each — and the arrangement held for more than forty years. He outlived the engineer who built the first one. He outlived the surgeon who seated it. He wore out unit after unit and was opened, and closed, and opened again, more than twenty times, and each time walked out of the hospital with a working heart he had not been born able to keep.

The early cells were the weak link, never the man. Mercury and zinc, good for about two years, and prone as they ran down to vent hydrogen — a gas with nowhere to go inside a sealed implant — so the surgeries came round on the battery's schedule whether he wanted them or not. Then, from +27 pT, the chemistry changed under him, and he would have felt the change only as time: the operations stopped coming so often. A cell built of lithium and iodine did a quiet, elegant thing. As it discharged, its own product — a thin solid film — laid itself down between the electrodes and served at once as the battery's output, its separator, and its seal. It made its own container as it ran. Nothing to vent, nothing to leak, and a working life of a decade or more where two years had been the rule. One maker's version of that cell came to sit in some seven of every ten pacemakers on the planet; of its first hundred and fifty thousand, by that maker's own count, not one failed early. The interval between his surgeries stretched from a couple of years toward a decade, and the disc that held it all was by now a hermetically welded can of titanium — a metal that meets air or flesh or seawater and grows, in seconds, an oxide skin that will not afterward react with anything at all. It was built to be indifferent. It outlasted everything that ever serviced it.

He grew old inside this arrangement. What had been a desperate operation on a dying man became, while he lived, an ordinary thing: by the later decades something like 1.4 million of the devices were fitted each year, some three million people walking about with one — though at rates that ran more than tenfold apart between the health systems that could afford them and those that could not, and schemes had grown up to carry the difference, collecting explanted cans from the rich systems, testing each for years of remaining charge, resterilising the sound ones, and sending them across the world to chests that would otherwise have gone without. The devices themselves kept shrinking; a later kind, cleared from +71 pT, no bigger than a large capsule, was slipped in through a vein with no wires at all, its charge rated for a dozen years and more. None of that was his. His was the first and clumsiest of the line, replaced and replaced. But the principle each of them ran on was the one that had taken his beat at +13 pT and simply not given it back.

The forty years were not remarkable, and that was the whole of the gift: they were ordinary years, the kind a man whose heart stops thirty times a day does not otherwise get. He did not die of his heart. It is worth saying plainly, because everything about the object invites the other ending. The heart he was born with had quit on him in middle age; the one the machines kept ran him into deep old age, decades on, until he died of something else entirely — an old man's ordinary death, with no more to do with his conduction system than with the weather. Somewhere in his chest, when he stopped, a titanium can was still keeping its steady seventy a minute, faithful to a beat that no longer had anyone to give it to. The machine had been built to keep a heart from stopping. It had. In the end the heart stopped because he did, and not the other way around.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The votive-heart can is real evidence: a hermetically sealed titanium implant, self-passivated and effectively immune to the corrosion this discipline otherwise tracks in metal, recovered intact from an unburned grave and read as a machine rather than an ornament only late (Halloran¹). The first implant's dates and failures are record — the external machine of +7 pT, the +13 pT operation in Stockholm, the three hours, the six weeks, the more than twenty replacements, the unrelated death decades on. The man between them is inference. The mechanism is real throughout: a failed conduction signal driven by a timed pulse; a lithium-iodine cell that seals itself with its own discharge product; a titanium oxide that will not react. A can like his keeps only because its owner was buried, not burned — a sealed cell at cremation heat bursts in four to six minutes, a hazard published for decades, its removal-gap read as deferred cost (Solberg²) or too few hands (Ibarra³) without a verdict. No registry survived to give the serial a name (Datta⁴).
