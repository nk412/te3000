Mass Coral Bleaching

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a single young coral head grown on a deployed tile and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

Mass coral bleaching is the thermally driven expulsion, by a reef-building coral, of the symbiotic algae that supply it most of its energy and all of its colour, leaving the bare white carbonate skeleton visible through clear, starving tissue. The four global events, the chemistry of the water, and the cryobiology are real and were published in the age’s own literature; the fragment is one coral head whose innermost band records where a larva settled on ground a person laid.

A reef-building coral polyp farms a garden of single-celled algae inside its own tissue. The garden photosynthesises and returns as much as nine parts in ten of the sugar the animal lives on, and gives it all its colour besides. Hold the water a degree or two above a reef’s ordinary summer ceiling for a few weeks and the algae’s own machinery begins to poison its host; the polyp expels the garden rather than be cooked by it, and what a diver sees then is the white lime of the skeleton through transparent flesh. Cooled within a few weeks, the animal takes up a fresh garden and colours again. Left hot, it runs down over a month or two on the tenth of its energy its own body can make, which is not enough. The condition is named for the colour, not the outcome: bleaching is not, at first, death.

Bleaching went from local to planetary across the interval, in four events each more extensive than the last: 21% of the world’s reef area in +53 pT (1998 CE), better than a third in +65 pT (2010 CE), better than two-thirds across +69–72 pT (2014–2017 CE), and 84% across +78–80 pT (2023–2025 CE) — a severity that forced three new tiers onto a satellite bleaching-alert scale its own builders had never expected to need. Under the heat, more slowly, the water itself changed. Roughly a third of the carbon dioxide the age released entered the sea rather than the air, and the surface ocean’s pH fell from about 8.2 to about 8.1, close to a quarter more hydrogen ions than the reef’s animals had ever built lime in. Making lime grew harder in step. A 400-year, 328-colony coral-core record found reefs thickening their skeletons 14.2% more slowly than a generation before, against nothing like it in the full depth of the record.

From +66 pT (2011 CE), against that arc, a conservation body froze the reproductive cells of a reef into liquid nitrogen and drew on them — crossing the heat-tolerant lines of a warm reef into the eggs of a cooler one the heat was climbing toward, an assisted evolution the reefs could not perform for themselves. The trial fertilisations took about nine times in ten. Almost none of that work’s own records survive; what reaches 3000 CE is one young coral head recovered on a fired-clay tile, its innermost growth band a settlement scar on substrate a person laid — a colony that lived through the summers that bleached its neighbours. The bleaching is read by some as the clearest single marine index of the age’s warming, and by others as its first widely reversed loss; it is the effort, and not the loss, that the surviving fragment records.

The expelled symbiont

The symbiont is a single-celled alga held in the coral’s inner tissue, and the exchange is the reef’s whole economy: the alga fixes carbon in the light and hands back as much as nine-tenths of the host’s energy, taking shelter and nitrogen in return. The trigger is not a temperature but an accumulation of it. A national satellite programme reduced the whole condition to a running sum of heat above a reef’s ordinary summer ceiling, the Degree Heating Week: at four Celsius-weeks significant bleaching is expected, at eight bleaching with mortality across most species, at twenty a reef is near-completely killed, above four-fifths of it gone. The expulsion is reversible where the accumulation stops in time — the animal recolonises from the surrounding water within weeks — and lethal where it does not. It has no single date. It is a season arriving in a wave that could be seen coming a week out and could not be turned.

The four events and the water

The four events climbed the Degree Heating Week scale as if reading from it, and the fourth ran so far past its top that the monitoring service bolted three new alert tiers onto a ladder its builders had thought would never need them. The change was never only heat. As the surface ocean absorbed carbon dioxide it turned fractionally more acid, and the balance that lets an animal precipitate calcium carbonate shifted against it: the aragonite saturation state, on which reef-building depends, fell from a condition in which about nineteen reefs in twenty could calcify freely to one in which about three in five could. The 400-year coral-core record — 328 massive colonies, one skeletal couplet a year read like the grain of felled timber — was published in +64 pT (2009 CE) and showed calcification down 14.2% from a +45 pT (1990 CE) baseline, a decline without precedent across the whole four centuries behind it. The reef was being asked to build more, and faster, in thinner mortar.

The growth-band clock

Massive corals lay down their skeleton in paired dense-and-open bands, one couplet a year, resolvable on an X-radiograph and described as a method in +27 pT (1972 CE). It is a marine dendrochronology: a core is cross-dated against others the way a Bronze Age wreck’s timbers are, and it is disputed the same way — some low-latitude colonies deposit two couplets in a year rather than one, a doubling problem the age’s own literature never resolved. The skeleton records the chemistry of the water it grew in, and it runs as a clock only for as long as the animal depositing it lives. Read against the interval’s other dateable markers — the lake-sediment nitrogen shift, the polar-ice lead curve — it agrees with them and adds a fourth (Devereux 2998, the calcified verdict): the coral is the only witness in this corpus that wrote its own testimony in the substance of its own death.

The bank

The bank held the reproductive cells of one reef system frozen in liquid nitrogen at −196 °C. Cold that deep does not freeze a correctly prepared cell so much as still it: loaded with the right protectant and taken below about −130 °C, the water in the cell passes into glass rather than ice and lays down no crystal to tear the membrane, so a sample drawn one season and a sample drawn a decade later are, to the egg, the same age. By the later counts the bank held cells in the trillions, from some three dozen species. Unlike the reserve seed vault cut into the northern rock, which took its deposits and was proud never to be asked for one back, this bank was drawn on every spawning. A dewar runs on no current; it runs on refilling, a topping of nitrogen against the boil-off every week or two — an archive resupplied on a season’s cycle rather than a generation’s, and the one that does not forgive a missed season (Furtado 2979, the vitreous debt).

What was drawn was put to a cross. Some colonies came through the hot summers barely marked, holding their garden at temperatures that stripped their neighbours bare, and the bank held the milt of such animals caught at a warm, low-latitude reef years before. Thawed into the fresh eggs of a cooler reef the heat was climbing toward, it carried a warm sea’s tolerance across a distance and a decade the animals could not have crossed themselves — no current runs between the two reefs, and they are never ripe in the same week. About nine fertilisations in ten took. The figure is a trial’s, not a reef’s: it measures a dish, and the standing caution against reading a successful trial as a claim about scale applies to it exactly (Kagiso 2978).

A coral larva of this kind is born empty of algae and must take its symbionts up from the water after it lands, so it can be offered, in the tank, the very heat-hardy strain its parentage was chosen to hold. It then swims and drifts and tastes the floor for the one signal it is built to obey: the pink crust of calcified algae that means, to a coral, safe ground. Fired tiles seeded with that crust were laid in the shallows where the reef had gone bare. The arithmetic behind the effort was never in dispute — a living reef breaks about 97% of a wave’s energy before it reaches shore, better than a hundred million people lived behind that service, and restoring a reef cost on the order of a fifteenth of pouring a breakwater to do the same work — and no funding trajectory ever matched it at the scale of the loss.

The fragment records the rest. The recovered coral head’s innermost band is a settlement scar: on the tile a person laid, one larva of the thousands stopped swimming, pressed its body flat, spread to a disc, drew in its first heat-hardy symbionts, and laid down beneath itself — against the acidifying water, in mortar thinner than its grandmothers had used — the first white ring of a skeleton that lasted. Everything outward of that ring is the reef it went on to make.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. No fertilisation record survives — the programme’s logs ran on satellite ground segments and data services that needed continuous upkeep and did not get it (see Attrition, the) — and no cryogenic sample from before the Attrition is known to reach the present. What reached it is a single young coral head recovered on a fired-clay tile, its innermost band the settlement scar of a colony that lived through the summers that bleached its neighbours. The skeleton is evidence, read by X-radiograph a couplet a year, though some low-latitude colonies bank two (Devereux 2998; the calcified verdict); the crossing and the deployed substrate are inference from it and from the published method. The bleaching mechanism, the Degree Heating Week thresholds, the pH and aragonite figures, the four global events, and the cryobiology — glass below roughly −130 °C, the ~90% thawed-sperm fertilisation, the crossing of heat-tolerant lines — are all real, and were published in the age’s own literature.
