Mass Coral Bleaching

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a single recovered coral head grown on a deployed tile and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

Through the four summers a warm ocean turned its reefs white, a conservationist worked a bank of coral milt frozen in liquid nitrogen — thawing a heat-hardy line into a cooler reef’s eggs, and breeding a cross that took. The bleaching, the chemistry, and the cryobiology are real and published; the woman, the night, and the cross are reconstructed from one coral that settled on a tile a person laid and lived through the heat that bleached its neighbours.

The reef spawned once a year, on a few nights after a full moon when the water held its warmth past dusk, and she had learned to read the days off it the way another season’s people read a planting calendar. The whole shelf let go at once — a hundred kinds of coral, each polyp opening and releasing a single pink bundle of egg and sperm that rose through the dark like a slow inverted snow, so thick some nights the surface went to slick and the smell of it carried to the boat. It was the most fertile few hours on the planet, and it happened while almost everyone slept. She did not sleep on those nights. She caught what rose.

What she caught, she meant to keep past the lifetime of the animal that made it. On the shore, in a low room that drew more attention than everything else on the site combined, stood the tanks: wide-mouthed steel vessels of liquid nitrogen at a hundred and ninety-six below zero, and inside them, racked in thin straws, milt from thirty-odd species drawn off one reef across a decade and more — by the later counts, cells in the trillions. Cold that deep does not freeze a properly prepared cell so much as still it. Below about a hundred and thirty below, water loaded with the right protectant slips past its own freezing into glass, laying down no crystal to tear the membrane, and a cell held in glass keeps no time at all. A straw drawn her first season and a straw drawn a dozen summers on were, to the egg, the same age. They were the same night.

She thought of the tanks as the opposite of the seed reserve cut into the far northern rock, the one everyone had heard of, that took its deposits and sat on them in the dark and was proud never to have been asked for a single one back. Hers was asked every year. A dewar runs on no current — it runs on refilling, a topping of nitrogen every week or two against the boil-off, a debt paid in seasons rather than in the lifetimes an archive is usually measured against; miss the payments for a summer and the glass warms and the cells tear and there is nothing to withdraw. She paid them. And every spawning she went to the tanks and drew something out and put it to use, which was the whole difference between them: the northern vault kept a world in case. She was trying to breed one.

She had her reasons, and they were written on the reef in white. Four times in her working life the shelf had gone pale across an ocean at once. The mechanism was not mysterious, and it was not, at first, death. A coral polyp farms a garden of single-celled algae in its own tissue, and the garden hands back as much as nine parts in ten of the sugar the animal lives on, and gives it all its colour besides. Hold the water a degree or two above the reef’s ordinary summer ceiling for a few weeks and the algae’s machinery begins to poison its own host, and the polyp spits the garden out rather than be cooked by it, and what a diver sees then is the bare white lime of the skeleton through clear and starving flesh. Cool the water within a few weeks and the animal can take up a fresh garden and colour again. Leave it hot, and it simply runs down, over a month or two, on the tenth of its energy its own body can make, which is not enough.

The monitoring service she trusted had reduced the whole thing to a running sum of heat — degree-weeks above the ceiling; four of them and the tender species bleach, eight and most of them bleach and some die, twenty and a reef is mostly gone. The four great events had climbed that scale as if reading from it: a fifth of the world’s reef in +53 pT, better than a third in +65 pT, better than two-thirds across +69 to +72 pT, and then, across +78 to +80 pT, eighty-four in a hundred — a summer so far past the top of the old scale that the service had to bolt three new tiers onto a warning ladder its own builders had never thought would need them. None of it had a single date. That was the thing she found herself explaining most often: it was never one white morning, it was a season, and then another season, coming in waves you could see arriving a week out and could not turn.

And under the heat, slower and quieter, the water itself had changed its chemistry. About a third of the carbon dioxide the age released had gone not into the air but into the sea, where it made a weak acid and shifted the water’s carbonate balance; the surface ocean had gone from about eight-point-two to about eight-point-one, which sounded like nothing and was, on that folded scale, close to a quarter more acid than the reef’s animals had ever built in. Making lime got harder in exact step. Where nineteen reefs in twenty had once sat in water saturated enough to calcify freely, three in five no longer did. A record read out of three hundred and twenty-eight old massive colonies — four unbroken centuries of it, one ring a year laid down in the skeleton like the grain in a felled tree — showed them thickening their walls fourteen percent more slowly than they had a generation before, against nothing like it in the whole four hundred years behind. The reef was not only bleaching. It was being asked to build in thinner mortar.

She knew what a reef was worth to the people who lived behind one — that a living one broke ninety-seven of every hundred parts of a wave’s energy before it reached a shore, that better than a hundred million people slept behind that service, and that growing one back cost, by the honest arithmetic, something like a fifteenth of pouring a wall of rock to do the same job. Nobody who did the sums disagreed with them. The sums were never the problem. The problem was only ever the scale, and the scale was not hers to fix in a low room by a beach. What was hers was the cross.

Here was the wonder of it, the part she never quite stopped feeling on the water at two in the morning with a pipette in the cold. Some colonies had come through the hot summers barely marked — individuals, whole lineages, that held their garden at temperatures that stripped their neighbours bare. Years before, on a reef far warmer and far to the equator side, she had caught the milt of exactly such animals at their own spawning and dropped it into glass. Now, on a cooler reef the heat was climbing toward, she thawed it — carried a warm sea’s tolerance across a distance and a decade the animals could never have crossed themselves, no current running between the two reefs, no season when both were ever ripe at once — and let it into a dish of fresh eggs lifted out of tonight’s slick. The bank folded the ocean’s geography and its calendar both. Nine tries in ten took. Under the scope the fertilised eggs began, within the hour, to cleave — two, four, a raspberry of cells — a warm reef and a cool one meeting for the first time in the history of either.

Over the next days the embryos rounded into planulae, small ciliated swimmers with no mouth and no garden yet, carrying only the yolk their mothers had packed. A coral larva of this kind is born empty of algae; it must take its symbionts up from the water after it lands, and so it can be offered, in the tank, the very heat-hardy strain its parentage was chosen to hold. Then it swims, and drifts, and tastes the floor for the one signal it is built to obey — the particular crust of pink calcified algae that means, to a coral, safe ground.

She put fresh substrate down for them where the reef had gone bare: plain fired tiles, seeded with that pink crust, laid in the shallows in the dark.

And on the third night one larva, of the thousands, stopped swimming. It settled to a tile, pressed its empty body flat, and turned in an afternoon from a thing that moved into a thing that stayed — spreading to a disc, drawing the first heat-hardy algae in out of the water, and laying down under itself, against the acidifying sea and in mortar thinner than its grandmothers had ever used, the first white ring of a skeleton that meant to last.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. No fertilisation log survives — those ran on satellite ground segments and data services that needed continuous upkeep and did not get it (see Attrition, the) — nor does the bank, no cryogenic sample from before the Attrition being known to reach the present. What reached it is a single young coral head recovered on a fired-clay tile, its innermost band a settlement scar on ground a person laid: a colony that lived through the hot years that bleached its neighbours. The skeleton is evidence, read by X-radiograph one growth couplet a year — though some low-latitude colonies bank two, unsettled in the age’s own literature (Devereux 2998; the calcified verdict); the woman, the night, and the cross are inference. The bleaching mechanism, the Degree Heating Week thresholds, the pH and aragonite figures, the four global events, and the cryobiology (glass below roughly −130 °C, the ~90% thawed-sperm fertilisation, the crossing of heat-tolerant lines) are all real, and were published in the age’s own literature.
