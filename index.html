23 articles on the Combustion Age (c. 1750–2140 CE).

Featured article

The Combustion Age (also Combustion Interval; stratigraphically the Gallus Horizon) is the period of Terran history defined by the systematic oxidation of geologically sequestered carbon as the primary energy basis of human society. It is conventionally dated from c. 1750 CE to c. 2140 CE, though regional chronologies differ by more than a century, and no single terminal date commands consensus.

Did you know…

  • … that the type fossil of the age is a broiler chicken, bred to grow too fast to stand, and the most numerous bird in Terran history?
  • … that a lunar rover’s 101 metres of wheel tracks will outlast every stone structure its makers ever built by a factor of about ten thousand?
  • … that the largest human gathering in history leaves no archaeological trace at all, because the river takes the sand?
  • … that one firm solved the hard problem of preserving its civilisation’s memory for a thousand years — and lost to the floor of a coal mine?

Combustion Age

The Terran historical period, c. 1750–2140 CE.

Attrition, the

The Attrition is the near-total loss of the Combustion Age's written and recorded output — not to any catastrophe, but because its dominant media held information only for as long as a funded institution kept copying it forward, on a cycle shorter than a human life.

Bhārat, Combustion Age

The South Asian peninsular polity from +55 pT (2000 CE) to the Contraction.

Bornholm Deposit

One disposal voyage of +2–+3 pT, reconstructed: a competent crew putting a defeated arsenal over the side into the Bornholm Basin, knowing exactly what the cold sea would and would not do to it.

Broiler Gallus

The three seasons in which a breeder set out to build a better meat bird and built, without meaning to, the ancestor of nearly every chicken then alive — reconstructed from the bone that her line became.

Cardiac Pacemaker

The reconstructed life the first implanted cardiac pacemaker ran — read out of a recovered titanium can that a generation of scholars mistook for an amulet — a machine that failed twice in its owner's first six weeks and then, one replacement at a time, kept his heart beating for more than forty years, until he died old of something that had nothing to do with it.

Catalytic Converter

A two-minute catalytic-converter theft late in the interval, reconstructed from the metals price it sold into and the claim tallies it left behind — the months when a parked car became a richer source of the rarest metals on Earth than either mine that made them.

Code Deposit

The Combustion Age left one bit-exact snapshot of its public software, and it says almost nothing about the people who wrote it — except in the timestamps, where the hour and the longitude of every change survive by accident, and can be read as a census of who was awake and at work.

Combustion-Age Languages

The spoken and written languages of the Combustion Age, and the reasons almost none of them can be heard.

Crash-Survivable Flight Recorder

The twenty-month hunt for a flight recorder lost with an airliner over open ocean, recovered intact and readable from three thousand nine hundred metres down long after its locator beacon had gone dark — reconstructed from the search authority's own operation logs.

Cryogenic Biobank

A cryogenic biobank kept living eggs, sperm, and embryos as an ice-free glass in liquid nitrogen at −196 °C — a state that held only for as long as someone kept paying the small, recurring cost of refilling it.

Industrial Nitrogen Fixation

One reaction pulls fertiliser out of open air; in a single season, and without a valve moved, it was turned to making explosives instead — the tale of the vessel that fed and killed by the same warmth, reconstructed from the isotope line the reaction wrote into the mud of still lakes worldwide.

Intermodal Shipping Container

Six days in +76 pT, when a single ship laid corner to corner across a one-lane canal held the better part of a planet's seaborne trade at anchor, seen from the bridge wing of one of the hundreds of hulls that waited.

Leaded Petrol

On the process floor of the works where a lethal fuel additive was first made at full scale, in the autumn its own makers began to die of it — a poison already filed as a poison, killing in open view of a press that printed the count.

Longyear Deposit

On a summer day at the silver-film archive inside a spent coal mountain at 78°N, a worker carries the last, late-arriving consignment up a ladder to the only shelf still free — the top one — not knowing that shelf height alone will decide, a thousand years on, what of the age survives.

Mass Coral Bleaching

Through the four summers a warm ocean turned its reefs white, a conservationist worked a bank of coral milt frozen in liquid nitrogen — thawing a heat-hardy line into a cooler reef's eggs, and breeding a cross that took.

Nuclear Weapon Accident

When a bomber on continuous armed alert went down on the Arctic sea-ice with four thermonuclear weapons aboard, none of them detonated, and the nine-month recovery that followed balanced its inventory to ninety percent and could not close it by one uranium cylinder.

Onkalo

The last shift of the crew that sealed a deep repository for spent nuclear fuel — a barrier built to outlast, by a factor of ten thousand, the institution that was meant to remember where it lay.

Solar-Powered Planetary Rover

The afternoon a mission scientist put human words to a dead rover's last signal — and the sentence that broke loose from her, until a whole planet remembered a machine saying what a person had only said about it.

Submarine Telecommunications Cable

On the night of −16 pT a magnitude-7.2 earthquake cut twelve seabed cables in sequence across five hundred kilometres of ocean floor, and the shore stations' timestamped fault logs — kept only so a repair ship would know where to sail — became the first direct proof that turbidity currents exist.

Svalbard Seed Vault

The one time the Arctic crop-seed reserve was ever emptied and refilled: a research institute, dispossessed of its home genebank by a siege, drew its own backups down out of the mountain, grew them out in exile, and carried the regenerated seed back up.

The Kharun Corridor

The last working weeks of an automated grain-allocation node on the Northern Plain, told from its own transaction ledger — a distribution system feeding fewer and fewer people with perfect fidelity, and never told to stop.

Voyager Records

The hour a recovery crew lifted one of the two outbound gold records out of the dark at 3,340 astronomical units, read the manual engraved on its own cover, and played it on the first attempt — the best-preserved artefact of the Combustion Age, sounding after nine centuries as though cut the week before.
