# Submarine Telecommunications Cable

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the shore-station fault logs of the −16 pT North Atlantic cable cascade and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **On the night of −16 pT a magnitude-7.2 earthquake cut twelve seabed cables in sequence across five hundred kilometres of ocean floor, and the shore stations' timestamped fault logs — kept only so a repair ship would know where to sail — became the first direct proof that turbidity currents exist.** The break-times, their order, and the thirteen-hour span are transcribed from the cable firms' own records; the watchman's night, and the reasoning that read a science out of it, are reconstruction.

---

The shock came up through the floor of the relay house before it came down the wires: a low roll in the boards and the cups a little after five in the afternoon, −16 pT, on a cold coast at the ocean's western rim, and then, a breath behind it, the cables beginning to fall.

He was the man on the evening watch, and his work was two things at once — hold the transatlantic circuits open, and write down, to the minute, every instant one of them closed. The instruments did the second part for him. Each cable fed a recorder that drew the incoming signal as a wavering line of ink; when a cable opened somewhere out under the sea, its line went flat and stayed flat, and the clock beside it said when. He had a dozen such lines to a wall. Six of them died together with the roll in the floor.

That much he could read at a glance. An earthquake out on the banks would whip the cables nearest it like struck strings and part the weak ones all at once; six gone in one minute was the shock's own signature, and he logged the six in a cluster, one time against all of them, and turned to the business of raising the far ends. The far ends did not answer. He was still working the dead six when the seventh went.

The seventh was wrong. It went alone, better than an hour after the others, long after the floor had gone still — and when he ran the resistance out to find where it had parted, so a ship could be sent, the break sat farther out than the six, off the edge of the shelf, down where the bottom falls away. He logged it and its time. Then, a good while later, an eighth, alone, farther still. Then a ninth. Something was out there in the dark, past the shelf and moving away from the land, cutting the cables in the order they lay, and it was in no hurry, and it did not stop.

A fault is one cable. A quake is every cable at once. This was neither — it was a procession, hours long, walking seaward down the slope into water three and four kilometres deep, where the cables run unarmoured across a plain because nothing down there is supposed to be able to reach them. He had no word for a thing that did that. He had a column for the time and a column for the place, and he kept filling them, one cable at a time, through the whole of the night. Out on the coast the sea had drawn far back off the flats and then come in over them, once, hard, and gone out again; he would hear about that later. On the wall, the lines kept going flat in order.

The last one flattened at dawn — thirteen hours and seventeen minutes after the roll in the floor, the farthest cable of all, hundreds of kilometres out on the deep plain. After it, nothing. He signed off the log with the times in it and did not know, could not have known, that he had just kept the only usable record of an event no instrument built to watch for it had been anywhere near.

The log went into a company file and sat there for twenty-three years.

At +7 pT two oceanographers asked the cable firms for the old fault records — not the messages, which had been gone the instant they were sent, but the two dull things the firms had always kept: the minute each break was noticed, and the distance out at which each break was found, because you cannot splice a cable you cannot locate. They laid the times against the distances and the whole night came open in front of them.

The first six told one story: many places, one time. That was the earthquake — or the great slump it shook loose — parting everything near at the same instant. The other six told a different one entirely: many times, in strict order, each break later than the last by just about the interval a moving thing would need to cross the gap between two cables. A shock cannot do that; a shock is everywhere at once. This had arrived at each cable in its turn, marching down the slope and out across the floor. Run the arithmetic backward — distance between two cuts, over the time between them — and it gave a speed: a front leaving the upper slope near a hundred kilometres an hour, still better than sixty over the fan below, slowing as it spread but running more than five hundred kilometres before the last cable stopped ticking.

Only one thing moves like that on the deep floor. The quake had shaken perhaps two hundred cubic kilometres of the continental slope loose from its resting angle; the loosened sediment, stirred into the water, made a slurry denser than the clear sea around it, and a denser fluid beneath a lighter one runs downhill under its own weight — a landslide that flows, a turbidity current, gathering speed on the steep and fanning out on the flat, thick enough with sand to scour and snap an armoured cable and keep going. Whether such currents existed at all had been argued for decades and settled by no one, because they run in the cold and the dark and the deep and are gone before anyone can reach them to look. This one had not needed a witness. It had crossed twelve cables, and every crossing was a clock, and the clocks had been read to the minute and filed for twenty-three years by an industry that read them for the sole purpose of knowing where to send a ship.

That is the turn the whole science hangs on, and it is worth saying flat. A submarine cable keeps nothing. These twelve were the old copper telegraph lines, the durable kind that had worked the ocean since −79 pT; the network the age went on to build from them — by its later decades some 559 systems and a million and a half kilometres of cable across every basin at once — kept even less, and did it on purpose, renewing the signal and letting it go at every relay, storing not one bit of what it moved anywhere along the way. Its ordinary wounds are dull: a dragged anchor, a bottom trawl, on the order of a hundred and fifty to two hundred a year, four in five of them from fishing and shipping, each located from the beach by the drop in a known resistance and mended in ten to twenty days by a fleet kept standing for nothing else. The armoured steel and the copper beneath it will lie in the mud as long as the seabed's chemistry allows, and the glass longer than either, and none of it will hold a single word to show for the traffic of a thousand years. The one thing such a network cannot help doing is to register, exactly, the instant it fails. Its own deaths are the only record it keeps — and on one night that habit, useless for the messages and indispensable for the repair, was aimed by pure accident at a thing on the deep floor that no seismograph of the day was placed to catch.

The floor taught it again, later, to people who by then knew to listen. At +61 pT a magnitude-7.0 quake off a Pacific island coast cut eight or nine cables in twenty-one places and took eleven ships forty-nine days to restore; and the break-times, plotted the same way, drew the same picture — a slope failing, a current running out and down, writing itself onto the wires as it went.

The discipline keeps a standing observation that the richest thing this network ever gave the record was never anything it carried, but the copies made beside it — the shore-side taps that skimmed a later age's traffic into its fullest surviving archive. This is the earliest instance of the pattern, and the purest. Long before any tap at a landing station kept a continent's words, a station on a cold coast kept, meaning nothing by it, the one thing its cables ever produced that outlasted the cables themselves. It was not a word anyone had sent. It was the order in which twelve of them died.

Reduced to what the record can actually bear, it is only this:

> `−16 pT · 18 November`
> `0:00   — cables 1–6, together, on and below the slope`
> `        — then 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, in order, each break deeper and farther than the one before —`
> `13:17  — cable 12, past five hundred kilometres out, on the abyssal plain`
> `        — and after cable 12, nothing`

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The hard evidence is two columns the cable firms kept for their own reasons — the clock-time at which each of twelve transatlantic cables opened on the night of −16 pT, and the seabed distance at which each break was later located for repair — obtained from those firms twenty-three years on and turned, at +7 pT, into the first direct proof that turbidity currents exist. The count, the order, the thirteen-hour-seventeen-minute span, and the roughly five-hundred-kilometre reach are transcribed; the watchman, the relay house, and its coast are inferred from the shape the times make. The physics was real then and is real now: a magnitude-7.2 shock, a slope failure of some 200 km³, a sediment-laden current denser than seawater running downslope at an estimated 60–100 km/h, parting cables in the order it reached them. The times themselves survive not as the firms' filing — the Attrition took that with almost everything on such media — but as the data table of the reconstruction that used them, carried past the loss because a founding record is a copied one (the many-copies doctrine). That a record beside the wire, and not the wire, is what endured and what taught is the thing this network is best known for (Brandvik).
