# Smallpox Eradication

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **Smallpox eradication is the deliberate extinction, in nature, of a human pathogen — the only such event this discipline can date to the calendar day — achieved not by a cure but by outrunning a virus with no other host to hide in.** It is not the story of the first vaccine, which predates it by a century and a half, nor of a disease that vanished on its own; both credit and cost are attributable, on the record, to named decisions on named dates. For the surveillance paperwork that did not survive the campaign that generated it, see **Attrition, the**. For an unrelated container built, unlike this one, expressly to be forgotten, see **Onkalo**.

---

A virus that can only infect one species, that gives no permanent shelter to a survivor of it, that announces itself with a rash before it becomes contagious rather than after, and that never varies enough from year to year to dodge a fixed vaccine, is a virus with nowhere left to run once enough of its hosts are closed to it. Variola, the pathogen behind smallpox, met every one of those conditions. Malaria, carried by a mosquito that does not need a human at all, does not. Measles, which sheds virus for days before its rash appears, does not. The biology made the campaign conceivable; it did not make it easy, and it did not make it safe. Two hundred years elapsed between the first deliberate immunisation against the disease and its last natural case, and the interval between them is a record of exactly how much institutional effort a favourable biology still requires.

The campaign's chronology is unusually well fixed, for a subject of this corpus, because it was fixed on purpose: an intergovernmental health-coordination body, chartered in the years after the second global war, resolved in +22 pT (1967 CE) to intensify a faltering programme against a disease still endemic in thirty-one countries, recording an estimated ten to fifteen million cases in that year alone. The last case acquired from nature is dated to +32 pT (1977 CE). The disease was declared, by the same body's main assembly, extinguished in the wild on 8 May +35 pT (1980 CE). No other infection of a free-living human population has a closing date this precise, because no other has ever had one at all.

It is not, quite, gone. What survives is not the disease — no human anywhere carries it, and no animal reservoir waits to reintroduce it — but the virus itself, deliberately kept, in two declared freezers, on two continents, since the year the last natural infection cooled. This article is built on that asymmetry: a pathogen erased from every warm body on the planet and preserved, by policy, in the cold.

## Three thousand years of scratching

The insight that survived exposure to a mild case of the disease conferred protection against a severe one is not a Combustion-Age discovery. Chinese sources describe deliberate inoculation — grinding dried scabs from a mild case to powder and blowing it into a healthy person's nostril — from around the tenth century CE; the practice, called variolation after the virus itself, travelled through South and Central Asia and was well established among Ottoman practitioners, chiefly older women working by lancet and scratch, by the early eighteenth century, when an English diplomatic household resident in Istanbul carried the method home. Its arithmetic was harsh and, for its era, favourable: a natural case of the severe strain killed perhaps three in ten of those it infected; a deliberately induced case, dosed low and monitored, killed on the order of one to two in a hundred. Thousands of families across three continents ran that trade before any of them had a germ theory to explain why it worked.

What ended variolation was not its logic but a better version of the same logic, arrived at in England at the close of the eighteenth century (−147 pT on this article's own dating convention, for a disease whose eradication would not be settled for another century and three-quarters): the observation, already folklore among dairy workers, that a mild pox caught from cattle conferred the same protection without the risk of the human strain, followed by the deliberate, documented test of that folklore on a healthy child. Vaccination — the word is built from *vacca*, cow — replaced variolation within decades across the countries that could afford it, and would not reach the countries that could not for another century and a half.

The disease had left its mark on human tissue long before either intervention. A Twentieth Dynasty pharaoh, dead in 1157 BCE, was mummified with a pustular rash still visible across his face, neck, and shoulders — a lesion pattern read, for the better part of a century, as smallpox's oldest attested case, and defended by some skin- and immunological sampling. The reading has never closed cleanly: light microscopy of the preserved tissue found none of the diagnostic changes the disease should leave, only three comparably marked mummies are known from the whole period, and a rival reading proposes a bubonic infection instead. Adeyinka-Osei's reassessment treats the case as exactly what a three-thousand-year-old skin sample allows — probable, structurally consistent, and permanently short of proof. The uncertainty is itself the more honest inheritance: a Bronze Age body bearing the marks of the exact disease a Combustion-Age campaign would later erase, and a diagnosis this discipline can neither confirm nor let go of.

## The needle and the flask

Two devices, neither of them clever in the way a laboratory instrument usually is, did more to make the +22 pT intensification survivable in the field than any single administrative decision. The standard liquid vaccine of the mid-century spoiled within days outside refrigeration, which made it nearly useless across the tropical and often electricity-poor regions where the disease remained endemic — a single-filament dependency on a cold chain, in the sense this corpus reaches for elsewhere, running from a laboratory refrigerator to a village clinic with no redundancy at any link. A London public-health laboratory's freeze-drying method, developed through the 1950s using a stabilising sugar-protein mixture, broke that filament: the dried vaccine held its potency for months at ordinary tropical ambient temperature and for a year or more under refrigeration, and could be reconstituted with sterile water at the point of use. It solved a problem the cure itself could not.

The second device was a flattened steel wire, forked at one end into two prongs, that held a fixed, tiny volume of vaccine by capillary action between the tines and delivered it by a few rapid punctures through the skin — a design that used roughly a quarter of the vaccine a syringe required, could be sterilised by flame and reused indefinitely, and could be taught to an unskilled worker in an afternoon. Between them, the freeze-dried vaccine and the bifurcated needle turned a fragile, refrigerator-bound, syringe-trained procedure into one that a health worker could carry on a bicycle into a village with no clinic at all.

The campaign's operational method changed under the same pressure that produced the needle. A vaccine shortage during an early outbreak in West Africa forced field teams to abandon mass vaccination of an entire population in favour of finding each new case, vaccinating only its immediate contacts, and building a ring of immunity around it rather than blanketing the region — a strategy that turned out to close outbreaks faster, with a fraction of the doses, than mass coverage ever had. Surveillance-containment, as the method came to be called, is the reason the campaign's final years read less like a vaccination programme and more like a manhunt: a network of local informants and case-report telegrams, chasing single infections to zero one village at a time.

## Zero

The case count that had stood at ten to fifteen million a year in +22 pT fell, region by region and on no coordinated single schedule, across the following decade, tracking the spread of ring vaccination more than the spread of the vaccine itself. The last case traceable to natural transmission was diagnosed in a hospital cook in the port town of Merca, in the country that had reported the disease latest of all; an intensive contact-tracing effort around him found no onward transmission. Two years of continued global surveillance, finding nothing, preceded the +35 pT (1980 CE) declaration.

The programme's intensified phase, +22 to +35 pT, is costed in the surviving public-health record at some three hundred million dollars of the period's currency, two-thirds of it paid by the endemic countries themselves rather than by outside donors, against a saved recurring cost — in vaccination and treatment obviated every year since — estimated at several times that sum annually. Set beside the other single-filament infrastructures this corpus catalogues, most of which the record shows collapsing, or costing more than anyone priced in, or dumped on the seabed rather than paid for outright, this is a rare instance in the corpus of an institution taking on a large, well-understood, and well-published cost and discharging it in full, on schedule, for a return the record can actually show. Some things worked.

## What escaped after the war was won

No human being has died of a natural smallpox infection since the Merca case. One human being died of smallpox after it. In +33 pT (1978 CE), a medical photographer employed one floor above a research laboratory studying the retained virus, at a university medical school in the English Midlands, developed the disease by a transmission route her own government's subsequent inquiry could not conclusively determine; she died some weeks later. The laboratory's supervising virologist, in the days before her death, took his own life.

The fact does not diminish the eradication; it sharpens it. A disease that had, at its peak, run through tens of millions of untreated bodies a year, and that a decade of deliberate global effort had closed off from every wild host on the planet, still found exactly one more victim — not through any failure of the field campaign, which had already succeeded, but through the continued existence, in a laboratory, of the thing the campaign had spent a decade erasing everywhere else. The war against the disease in nature was over. The custody of what remained of it in captivity was not, and has not been since.

## Two rooms

Since +38 pT (1983 CE), by formal international agreement following the Birmingham death, exactly two institutions have been permitted to hold live variola virus at all: a national disease-control agency's high-containment laboratory in the country that had led the eradication campaign's science, and a virology research institute in Siberia, to which the second stock was moved in +49 pT (1994 CE) from an earlier facility. Both hold their stocks under mechanical deep-freeze, requiring uninterrupted power, uninterrupted refrigerant supply, and an uninterrupted institution willing to keep paying for both — the identical dependency this corpus documents for every other perishable archive of the age, reduced here to its narrowest possible case: not many copies on many shelves, but two vials, in two freezers, on two continents, and nothing else standing between the species and never having to think about this virus again.

The coordinating body's assembly first voted to destroy both stocks in +41 pT (1986 CE), set a date, and then deferred it — to +48 pT, then to +54 pT, then, in +57 pT, to an indefinite continuation pending further research, a pattern of scheduled endings not kept that this corpus recognises from elsewhere. Two schools have argued the question ever since, in earnest, without resolution. The containment school holds that the retained stocks are the only source from which future diagnostic tests, antiviral compounds, and reference vaccines against a reintroduction — deliberate or synthetic — can be validated, and that destroying the last two authenticated samples would remove precisely the tool needed to confirm any future outbreak was the real thing. The closure school, extending an argument built elsewhere in this corpus for a different kind of deferred cost, holds that every year of retention is itself the risk it claims to guard against, that the technical case for destruction was already sound in +41 pT, and that the repeated deferral has never once been driven by a new finding, only by the same institutional reluctance to close a account that keeps every migration debt in this corpus open past its term.

No later resolution appears in the sources this discipline has recovered. The last securely dated custodial record confirms both freezers running, both under guard, both drawing power. What became of them afterward — whether the agreement held, whether the power did, whether anyone was still paying — is not stated anywhere this corpus has read, and the silence is not evidence of either answer.
