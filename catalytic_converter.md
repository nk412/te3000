# Catalytic Converter

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the intercepted metals-price telemetry and buy-side scrap classifieds of the Wasatch Corpus and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The catalytic converter was a passive exhaust device that destroyed three regulated pollutants at once on no power but engine waste heat, using a film of three of the rarest metals then mined — and in doing so made a parked car briefly a richer source of those metals than either ore body that produced them.** The metals prices and the theft tallies are transcribed from the record; the salvage economy read out of them is inference from the shape they make.

---

For a stretch of months late in the interval, a parked car held the rarest metals on Earth at a richer grade than either mine that produced them, and the units that held them were cut from parked vehicles in the tens of thousands.

The catalytic converter was a canister of ceramic honeycomb, washed in a film of platinum, palladium and rhodium thinner than a fingerprint and bolted into a vehicle's exhaust. Hot, it retired three regulated poisons at once — carbon monoxide burned to carbon dioxide, unburned fuel to carbon dioxide and water, nitrogen oxide reduced to plain nitrogen — on no power but the engine's own waste heat, with no moving part, at nine parts in ten or better, a figure its makers were held to and met. It cleaned the roads for two human generations. It also, at a fractional rate its certification never covered, produced a fourth poison of its own.

The metal that did the work came from two geologically freakish ore bodies and, per unit, amounted to only a few grams — a few grams at a concentration above either source's ore. When the price of the rarest of them climbed past every level in living memory, the cans began coming off parked vehicles across one large national market in counts its insurers logged in the tens of thousands, then fell away as fast when the price broke. The wave is read as a recovery economy the interval ran on itself — individuals absorbing, in real time, a cost the device's design had never eliminated (Solberg).

## The device

The catalyst reached series production in +30 pT (1975 CE) as an oxidation-only unit; a chemically complete three-way form, held to its stoichiometric air–fuel ratio by a zirconia oxygen sensor under closed-loop feedback, became standard from +36 pT (1981 CE). A working catalytic exhaust reactor had been patented and briefly sold for stationary and marine use as early as +10 pT (1955 CE), but could not reach the road: the antiknock lead additive then in pump fuel fouled the coating metal permanently, so road adoption waited on the additive's removal (**Leaded Petrol**).

It worked only hot. Below its light-off temperature — about 250–300 °C — the ceramic passed the exhaust through unconverted, and on some drive cycles seven or eight tenths of a trip's total tailpipe hydrocarbon left the pipe cold, in the first one to three minutes, before the unit lit. The nitrogen-oxide reaction, for its part, could stall a step short of nitrogen, at nitrous oxide — a gas on the order of three hundred times worse than carbon dioxide for the warming and stable in the air for centuries. The converter retired three regulated poisons and generated a fourth, unregulated one — **the swapped poison** (Marchetti) — and was certified on the three. The same gas is on record left unpriced by **Industrial Nitrogen Fixation**.

## The two ore bodies

Almost every troy ounce of newly mined platinum, and a larger share still of the rhodium, came out of a single layered igneous intrusion emplaced about two billion and sixty million years ago in the northern interior of a southern country, the platinum-group metals sorted into thin flat reefs by a settling of the cooling magma that has never been fully accounted for. Almost all the palladium came from an unrelated source — a nickel-and-copper sulphide body in Siberia, worked for base metal, that gave up platinum-group metal only as a by-product and in one recent year yielded some eighty-four tonnes of palladium, more than any other single source on Earth. Two ore bodies, one an ancient melt and one a mining accident, refined very nearly the whole interval's supply — a single-filament dependency extended from a manufactured product to a mined ore (Ibarra). A passenger-car unit carried three to seven grams of platinum, a comparable range of palladium and one to two grams of rhodium: at those figures the coating was a richer grade of platinum-group metal than either producing mine's own ore.

## The salvage economy

Rhodium — the rarest of the three, recovered mostly as a by-product of the other two — had traded for most of living memory in the low thousands of dollars the ounce. Late in the interval its price climbed without an explanation the trading desks agreed on: past ten thousand, past twenty, and on a single day in +76 pT (2021 CE) above US$29,000 the troy ounce, better than fifteen times the price of gold by weight.

The cans came off. In one large national market the insurers logged 50,503 catalytic-converter theft claims in +76 pT (2021 CE) and 64,433 the following year, +77 pT (2022 CE); the repair record put the true count that second year nearer 153,000 units gone, the gap a measure of how many owners never reported it. The buyer paid by the core, and whether the unit still worked made no difference to its price. A replacement, uninsured, ran to four figures and could stand a vehicle off the road for a month waiting on the part.

Solberg reads the wave not as crime incidental to the device but as the interval's own salvage economy: an unauthorised, real-time recovery of a resource the market had priced too high to leave bolted under a car, the owner and not the manufacturer left holding a cost the design had never removed. The price broke in +78 pT (2023 CE) and the reported thefts fell with it, near enough by half; the trade closed about as fast as it had opened. On its single highest day one troy ounce — thirty-one grams — of the grey metal that a cooling planet had spent two billion years sorting into a single seam of rock had printed above US$29,000, and a gram or two of it rode, uncounted, in the unit under every second vehicle on the road.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The record is the interval's own arithmetic — the daily metals-price telemetry and the buy-side scrap classifieds that survive inside the **Wasatch Corpus**, with the published insurance and repair tallies of the theft wave: 50,503 claims in +76 pT (2021 CE), 64,433 and some 153,000 units in +77 pT (2022 CE), the wave breaking with the metal price in +78 pT (2023 CE); rhodium's single-day high above US$29,000 the troy ounce falls in +76 pT. Those figures are transcribed; the reading of the wave as a salvage economy is inference. The chemistry and geology are real — the three-way reaction, the ~250–300 °C light-off, the cold-start hydrocarbon fraction, the fractional stall to nitrous oxide, a layered intrusion crystallised ~2.06 billion years ago and a Siberian nickel-copper body between them supplying almost the whole interval's platinum-group metal. The coating itself does not corrode on any timescale this discipline can measure, sitting outside the corrosion regimes the taphonomy of the age otherwise catalogues.
