Submarine Telecommunications Cable

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the shore-station fault logs of the −16 pT North Atlantic cable cascade and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The submarine telecommunications cable was the seabed conduit that carried the overwhelming share of the Combustion Age’s intercontinental traffic, and by its own design held not one bit of it at rest. For the shore-side archive skimmed beside it, see Wasatch Corpus. The counts, the order, and the roughly thirteen-hour span of the −16 pT cascade are transcribed from the cable firms’ own fault records; the causal account is inference.

A submarine telecommunications cable is a seabed conduit — armoured steel wire laid over a concentric copper power conductor over one or more optical-fibre pairs — built to carry data between continents and to keep none of it. The first working transoceanic link, at −87 pT (1858 CE), carried a few hundred messages and failed within weeks; a durable replacement followed at −79 pT (1866 CE), and the optical descendants of that early copper line came, by the interval’s later decades, to number some 559 systems, about 1.4 million kilometres of cable, and more than 1,600 landing points. The signal along them was relayed and never stored: inline optical amplifiers spaced every 50 to 100 kilometres lifted every wavelength at once without ever converting the light back to an electrical form that could be written down, so the network held no bit at rest, and everything it moved was gone the instant it arrived.

Its ordinary faults were dull and frequent — on the order of 150 to 200 a year, four in five of them from dragged anchors and bottom trawls — each located from shore by the drop in a known resistance and mended in ten to twenty days by a standing fleet of specialised ships kept for nothing else. Built for about twenty-five years of service, a spent cable was recovered for its metal only where the copper and steel repaid the cost of the ship, and otherwise left in the mud, intact and inert; the silica fibre at its core, which corrodes in cold seawater at a small fraction of a micron per millennium, will outlast both metal layers by a wide margin and hold, at the end of it, exactly nothing.

The network’s one indispensable habit was to register, precisely, the instant it failed. On the night of −16 pT (1929 CE) that habit was aimed by accident at the deep-ocean floor. A magnitude-7.2 earthquake off a North Atlantic shelf shook roughly 200 cubic kilometres of the continental slope loose from its resting angle; the loosened sediment, stirred into the water as a slurry denser than the clear sea around it, ran downhill under its own weight as a turbidity current estimated at 60 to 100 kilometres an hour and severed twelve cables in sequence across some 500 kilometres of seabed over about thirteen hours. Each station logged the minute its cable went dead and, afterward, the distance offshore at which the break was located — the two facts a repair ship needs, and the only two the firms troubled to keep.

Twenty-three years on, at +7 pT (1952 CE), two oceanographers obtained those old fault records and laid the break-times against the break-distances. The first cables had parted together, in one instant, in many places; the rest had parted one after another, each later than the last by just the interval a moving thing would need to cross the gap between two cables, in strict order down the slope and out across the plain. Distance over time gave a speed, and only one thing on the deep floor moves like that. The timestamped breaks were the first direct evidence that turbidity currents exist — a phenomenon argued over for decades and witnessed by no one, because it runs in the cold and the dark and is gone before anyone can reach it. The richest thing this network ever gave the record was not anything it carried, which it kept nowhere, but a copy made beside it: here a fault log, later a wholesale tap — the pattern the discipline files under the eavesdropped copy.

The cable

The cable is built in concentric layers around the light. At the centre lie one or more optical-fibre pairs, each glass strand carrying many wavelengths of laser light at once; around them runs a copper power conductor, and around that, in shallow water, steel armour wire and a polyethylene sheath against anchors and abrasion. Deep-water cable is thinner and laid unarmoured across the abyssal plain, because nothing down there is expected to be able to reach it. The copper does exactly one job: it powers the amplifiers. These are short segments of glass doped with a rare-earth element and pumped with laser light, spaced every 50 to 100 kilometres, each of which adds energy to every wavelength passing through it without demodulating any of them — the signal is never turned back into electricity, never addressed, never written. Shore stations at each end feed 3,000 to 15,000 volts of direct current at one to two amperes down the copper to keep the chain of amplifiers lit, the two ends sharing the load so that a single station can hold the line if the other drops.

The consequence is definitional. Because nothing along the path stores or reads what passes, the network held no bit at rest: a message existed on the fibre only while it was in transit — microseconds from coast to coast — and then not at all. A recovered cable, cut and lifted a thousand years on, carries no more of the traffic that crossed it than an empty pipe carries the water that once ran through it. The physical spine of the age’s integrated communications funnelled, at each coast, through a small number of landing stations, a corridor structure Ibarra reads as a case of single-filament infrastructure and as the same dependency the isthmus problem finds at the age’s two canals.

What survives of the object is the wrong part. Steel armour and copper conductor corrode on two different clocks in the same trench — the ferrous armour on the marine-corrosion regime Halvorsen catalogues elsewhere, the copper on another — while the silica fibre they were laid down to protect corrodes negligibly and outlasts them both. A cable abandoned at the close of its service life therefore decays from the outside in, toward a glass thread that keeps, physically, for millennia and that never held a single word of what it moved.

Faults and repair

For most of its working life the network was cut not by the sea but by the surface. Global faults ran at some 150 to 200 a year, and better than seven or eight in ten of them came from dragged anchors and the boards of bottom trawls — the cable and the fishery competing for the same continental shelf. A fault is located without ever leaving the beach: a cable of known length has a known electrical resistance, and the resistance measured to a break gives the distance out at which it lies, close enough to send a ship. A standing fleet of five to six dozen specialised vessels did nothing else, grappling the parted ends off the bottom, splicing in fresh cable, and lowering the repair back down over a typical ten to twenty days.

The system that could not read a word of what it carried could not help recording, exactly, when and where it stopped carrying. Every fault entered the station log as a line, a clock-time, and a computed distance offshore — the moment a circuit went dead and the place a ship should sail to. The traffic left no trace the instant it was sent; the network’s own failures were the only thing it wrote down. On an ordinary day this produced a hundred and fifty dull entries a year. On one night it produced the founding data of a science.

The −16 pT cascade

The magnitude-7.2 shock of −16 pT (1929 CE) parted the six cables nearest the epicentre at once — many places, one instant, the ordinary signature of a quake whipping taut lines like struck strings. The stations logged those six as a cluster and turned to raising the far ends. The far ends did not answer, and the seventh cable went alone, better than an hour later, well after the ground had stilled, with its break located farther out than the six, off the edge of the shelf where the bottom falls away. Then an eighth, alone, farther still; then a ninth; on down the slope and out across the deep plain, one cable at a time, in the order they lay, hours after the last tremor and slowing as it went. On the coast above, the sea drew far back off the flats and then came in over them, once and hard; that surge was the only part of the night anyone ashore witnessed directly, and it told them nothing of the twelve lines walking seaward beneath it in the dark. The logs of that night record each break under the station’s ordinary fault convention, one line and one clock-time to each. The convention had no value for a fault that moved, and none was added; the record does not show that anyone understood, that night, that the stations were logging one event and not twelve.

Read at +7 pT (1952 CE), the two columns opened the whole night. Run the arithmetic backward — the distance between two consecutive breaks over the time between them — and the sequence gave a front leaving the upper slope near 100 kilometres an hour, still better than 60 across the fan below, decelerating as it spread but running more than 500 kilometres before the last cable stopped ticking. A shock cannot do that; a shock is everywhere at once. This had arrived at each cable in its turn. Only a turbidity current behaves so: the quake had thrown some 200 cubic kilometres of slope sediment into suspension, the sediment-laden water was denser than the clear sea and so flowed downhill beneath it, gathering speed on the steep and fanning out on the flat, thick enough with sand to scour and snap an armoured cable and keep going. Whether such currents existed at all had been contested for decades and settled by no one, because they run where no instrument of the day was placed to catch them. This one had needed no witness. It had crossed twelve cables, and every crossing was a clock, and the clocks had been read to the minute and filed for twenty-three years by an industry that read them only to know where to send a ship.

Reduced to what the record can bear, the night is a single ordered series:

−16 pT · 18 November t = 0 cables 1–6, together, on and below the slope — the shock t + hours 7, 8, 9, 10, 11 — in order, each break deeper and farther than the last t + ~13 h cable 12 — past 500 km out, on the abyssal plain after cable 12, nothing

The floor taught it again, later, to people who by then knew to listen. At +61 pT (2006 CE) a magnitude-7.0 earthquake off a Pacific island coast cut eight or nine cables in twenty-one places and took eleven ships forty-nine days to restore; the break-times, plotted the same way, drew the same picture — a slope failing, a current running out and down, writing itself onto the wires as it went.

The eavesdropped copy

The discipline keeps a standing observation that the richest thing this network ever gave the record was never anything it carried, but the copies made beside it. The shore-side taps at landing stations skimmed a later age’s traffic wholesale into the fullest surviving archive of ordinary Combustion-Age life — the Wasatch Corpus, retained under a harvest now, decrypt later rule and read out once quantum period-finding broke asymmetric key exchange. The cable itself kept nothing; the tap beside it kept a continent’s words. Brandvik names the pattern the eavesdropped copy: an archive whose survival depends on a system it was never part of, and which owes that system nothing further once the copy is made.

The −16 pT fault logs are its earliest and purest instance. They survive not as the firms’ own filing — the Attrition took that, with almost everything committed to such media — but as the data table of the reconstruction that used them, carried past the loss because a founding record is a copied one (the many-copies doctrine). Okonkwo reads such incidental cable-exchange records, the routing logs and the fault ledgers alike, as an unintended census: evidence their keepers never meant to make, read for a purpose their keepers never held.

The network moved the traffic of a thousand years and kept none of it. What it kept, and did not mean to, was a column of clock-times and a column of distances, filed so that a ship would know where to sail; read together, twenty-three years on, they were a science. It was not anything the cable carried. It was the sequence in which twelve of its lines went dark.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The hard evidence is two columns the cable firms kept for their own reasons — the clock-time at which each of twelve transatlantic cables opened on the night of −16 pT, and the seabed distance at which each break was later located for repair — obtained from those firms twenty-three years on and turned, at +7 pT, into the first direct proof that turbidity currents exist. The count, the order, the roughly thirteen-hour span, and the reach of some 500 kilometres are transcribed; the causal account — a single slope failure feeding one density current down the slope and across the plain — is the reconstruction built on them. The physics was real then and is real now: a magnitude-7.2 shock, a slope failure of some 200 cubic kilometres, a sediment-laden current denser than seawater running downslope at an estimated 60–100 km/h, parting cables in the order it reached them. The times survive not as the firms’ filing, which the Attrition took, but as the data table of the reconstruction that used them, carried past the loss because a founding record is a copied one. That a record beside the wire, and not the wire, is what endured and what taught is the thing this network is best known for (Brandvik).
