Submarine Telecommunications Cable

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

A submarine telecommunications cable is a seabed conduit engineered to carry a signal across an ocean and to retain none of it. For the salvaged copy of what such a conduit carried, see Wasatch Corpus; for the two chokepoints its geography forced traffic through, see Intermodal Shipping Container; for a contrasting deposit built to keep what it held, see Longyear Deposit.

By the interval’s later decades a fleet of some 559 such systems, comprising nearly 1.4 million kilometres of cable and something over 1,600 landing points, carried the overwhelming share of the world’s intercontinental data traffic — voice, finance, and text alike — across every ocean basin at once. None of it was a repository. A submarine cable is built to move a signal, not to hold one: the light pulses that constitute a message exist for the microseconds it takes them to cross a given span of glass, are boosted, not stored, at each relay, and are gone, as completely as if they had never been sent, the instant the relay loses power. Of the millennium’s deliberate archives — a film vault in a coal working, a corpus of software on 186 reels, two identical phonograph records leaving the system entirely — the cable network is the inversion: the best-preserved physical object in this article and the worst possible source for what passed through it.

The mechanism is a repurposing of a much older one. A first working link, laid across a strait in −87 pT (1858 CE), failed within weeks under a voltage a hastily rebuilt equivalent avoided in −79 pT (1866 CE); the copper-and-gutta-percha telegraph line differed from its glass-and-repeater descendant in every particular except the one that survived the whole transition intact — a signal, launched from one coast, degrades with distance, and must be renewed at intervals rather than sent once at overwhelming strength. That single constraint, unbroken since, is the article’s spine.

The other constant is depth as a lottery. A cable laid across a shipping lane is armoured, buried under a metre or more of seabed, and still cut with some regularity by an anchor or a trawl; the same cable, once past the continental shelf, runs unarmoured and lightly buried across an abyssal plain where almost nothing physical threatens it and almost nothing physical ever finds it again either.

A relay that keeps nothing

A deep-ocean optical fibre attenuates light at a fixed, well-characterised rate — a few tenths of a decibel per kilometre for the silica in service across most of the interval — so that after several tens of kilometres a signal launched at full power has faded below the point a receiver can distinguish from noise. The fix, standard by the interval’s final quarter, is an inline optical amplifier spliced directly into the cable roughly every 50 to 100 kilometres: a short length of fibre doped with a rare-earth element, pumped by its own laser, that boosts every wavelength passing through it without ever converting the signal back to an electrical one for inspection or storage. A single fibre pair, multiplexed across dozens of separated wavelengths, might carry many terabits a second this way; a modern trunk cable does this on ten or twenty fibre pairs at once, laid side by side in one armoured jacket.

None of it works without power delivered from the shore, and the delivery mechanism is itself the whole cable’s second purpose. A copper conductor, concentric with the fibre core along the cable’s entire length, carries direct current at 3,000 to 15,000 volts and roughly one to two amperes from stations at each end, dropping perhaps 30 to 70 volts across each repeater it passes through, sized so the two shore stations meet in the middle with headroom to spare. Cut the power feed anywhere along the route and every amplifier downstream of the cut goes dark simultaneously, which is also, not incidentally, the fastest way to locate the cut: a technician reads the voltage drop against the known resistance per kilometre and states the fault’s position, from shore, before any ship has been dispatched to find it.

Nothing in this design stores a bit at rest. The amplifier repeats; it does not remember. A repeatered cable pulled from the seabed a thousand years after its last transmission — glass intact, copper intact, if the housing has held — will yield, on inspection, precisely as much of what it once carried as a length of ordinary pipe yields of what once flowed through it: nothing, because there was never anywhere for anything to be kept.

What breaks it, and how a break once taught a science

Global fault statistics, published candidly by the industry’s own information clearing-house for decades, run to roughly 150–200 cable faults a year, and a consistent 70 to 80 percent or more of them are not storms, not earthquakes, not predation, but a dragged ship’s anchor or a bottom trawl — ordinary fishing and shipping, doing to a shallow cable exactly what an uncharted, unarmoured wire on a busy seabed will eventually have done to it. A dedicated repair fleet, on the order of five to six dozen specialised ships worldwide, is kept on permanent standby to find each break, grapple the cable up from the seabed on either side of the fault, and splice a fresh length in — typically a matter of ten to twenty days, weather and depth permitting, for a network that a large fraction of global finance settles across in real time.

The single most informative break in the network’s history was also its most violent. A magnitude-7.2 earthquake at the edge of a North Atlantic continental shelf in −16 pT (1929 CE) triggered a submarine slope failure of some 200 cubic kilometres, which collapsed into a turbidity current — a dense, fast-moving slurry of mud and sand running downslope under gravity — that raced outward at an estimated 60 to 100 kilometres an hour and severed twelve telegraph cables in sequence across nearly 500 kilometres over the following thirteen hours. Each break stamped its own timestamp into the shore stations’ fault logs. Two mid-twentieth-century oceanographers, reconstructing the sequence against the surveyed distance between each cut more than two decades later, computed the current’s velocity and, in doing so, produced the first direct documentation that turbidity currents existed at all and moved as they do. The instrument that made the discovery possible was never intended as one: a communications network, laid to carry messages, incidentally recorded — in the one datum it could not help but keep, the instant each relay went dark — a geological event no contemporary seismometer was positioned to see.

A single regional earthquake off a Pacific island coast in +61 pT (2006 CE) cut eight or nine cables in twenty-one places, took eleven ships forty-nine days to fully restore, and in the meantime severed a dominant fraction of a hemisphere’s financial and retail traffic to its immediate neighbours — a corridor problem structurally identical to the one already established for the two canal chokepoints of the age’s shipping network (the isthmus problem, Ibarra), extended here from hulls to wavelengths.

The copy beside the wire

The network’s clearest evidentiary legacy to this discipline was never a property of the network at all. Bulk interception of transiting traffic, conducted by signals bureaux from taps installed at or near the shore-side landing stations where a cable’s fibres are patched into a terrestrial network for onward routing, is the documented origin of a substantial fraction of the Wasatch Corpus — retained “under harvest now, decrypt later,” in Ranganathan’s phrase, and read out complete only once period-finding broke the asymmetric ciphers protecting most of it. The cable itself, as this article has already established, held nothing; the tap, sitting beside the cable rather than inside it, held everything the tap’s own operators chose to keep. Extending her established model of infrastructure concentrated onto a small number of physical filaments, Ibarra reads the small number of landing stations through which most of a continent’s traffic necessarily passes as a second, silent chokepoint — one this network’s own designers never had reason to defend against, since nothing about a copy made beside a wire threatens the wire’s own function.

Brandvik names the resulting asymmetry the eavesdropped copy: an archive that is not the source it purports to document, made viable only because something sat beside an ephemeral system and kept what that system was never built to keep. It is the corpus’s inverse of the migration debt (Datta) — a recopied archive owes a payment on a schedule or it lapses; an eavesdropped copy, once made, owes nothing to the wire it was skimmed from, which may fail, or be decommissioned, or simply go dark, without the copy noticing at all. One recovered landing-station routing log, from an exchange decommissioned in the network’s final working decades, shows an automated table re-attempting a handshake toward addresses on the other side of an ocean for several weeks after every recorded reply from that direction had already stopped; the equipment had no way to know there was no longer anyone there to answer, and kept trying anyway, on schedule, until its own power was cut.

What the seabed keeps

The buried fraction of the network divides cleanly along Halvorsen’s established taphonomy. The steel wire armour wrapping a shore-approach cable corrodes in seawater by the same electrochemical regime she has already catalogued for sea-dumped ordnance and shipping containers, at a rate this discipline can read from the wire’s remaining cross-section; the copper power conductor beneath it is subject to the sulfide-driven attack she previously extended from silver-gelatin film to the copper canisters of a geological waste repository, running faster in anoxic mud than in open water and slower again once fully entombed. Extending her range a further step, Halvorsen reads the doubled corrosion regime of armour-over-conductor in the same trench as evidence that a single buried object can present two simultaneous, well-characterised decay clocks to a sufficiently patient reader.

The fibre itself outlasts both metals by a wide margin. Drawn silica glass is close to chemically inert in cold, near-neutral seawater; published corrosion-rate studies for glass under comparable repository conditions put dissolution at a small fraction of a micron per millennium, which would leave a fibre core a few thousandths of a millimetre across intact for a period this discipline has no established second marker to check it against. A cable’s copper is read by its corrosion; a cable’s steel is read by its corrosion; a cable’s glass, alone among the three, would still transmit light exactly as well as the day it was drawn, if there were still a laser at either end to send any.

No comparable durability attaches to the network’s economics. A cable’s design life is roughly twenty-five years, published without apparent alarm by every manufacturer in the same specification sheet that describes the system’s centrality to global settlement, trade, and defence; recovery of a retired cable — for its copper, its steel, and increasingly its fibre — has supported a small dedicated salvage industry since the early Contraction decades, one operator alone accounting for on the order of 100,000 kilometres of recovered cable, but recovery is undertaken only where the metal’s scrap value clears the cost of the ship; where it does not, the cable is left exactly where it lies, formally decommissioned and physically undisturbed, its repeaters permanently dark and its glass, by the arithmetic above, not going anywhere for a very long time.

Every letter, order, and confession this network carried across a thousand years of wires is retrievable, if it is retrievable at all, only from a copy nobody built to last, made beside a conduit that could have outlived the copy by ten thousand years and was never asked to keep a single word of what it carried to do so.

The strongest statement of the corridor reading; less interested in what the landing stations themselves did with the traffic passing through them.
  2. Halvorsen, E. (2994). “The Wire That Corrodes Twice: Copper Conductor and Steel Armour in the Same Trench.” J. Anthropogenic Stratigraphy 86: 140–178. — Extends her established taphonomy of silver-gelatin, marine ferrous ordnance, and sulfide-attacked copper to the doubled corrosion regime of a single buried power-fed cable.
  3. Okonkwo, D. (2991). “The Landing Station Ledger: Routing Logs as an Unintended Census.” — Extends her established interest in deposits and incidental metadata (previously the L-III commit clocks) to automated exchange logs; the source for the stranded handshake table cited above. Cautious on how representative any one recovered exchange’s logs can be taken to be.
  4. Brandvik, S. (2987). The Eavesdropped Copy: On Archives That Are Not Their Own Source. — Coins the term for an archive whose existence depends on a system it was never part of, using the Wasatch Corpus’s landing-station origin as the type case.
  5. Ranganathan, V. (2963). Reading the Wasatch Corpus: An Essay on Sampling. 3rd edn. — Source for the “harvest now, decrypt later” characterisation and the aphorism on what is known of what was said, versus what was said about it to machines.
  6. Datta, S. (2970). The Migration Debt: The Attrition as Maintenance Failure. — Cited by contrast: the migration debt names a cost that compounds until an institution defaults on it; an eavesdropped copy, once made, owes no further payment to the system it was taken from.
