Leaded Petrol

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the polar additive spike, a surviving press notice of the production-line deaths, and the minutes of a federal health conference, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

Leaded petrol was a motor-fuel additive: a lead compound blended into petrol at a few grams per gallon to suppress engine knock, and formulated with two further chemicals whose specified function was to carry the burnt lead out of the engine and into the open air. The ice is the record; the manufacturing and regulatory arc is inference from it and from the fragmentary written trace that survived the Attrition.

Leaded petrol was the retail form of tetraethyl lead, Pb(C₂H₅)₄, an antiknock agent sold as a minor additive to motor fuel across roughly seventy years of the Combustion Age. The compound had been weighed as a chemical-warfare agent in the last year of the war then recently ended, and set aside as too lethal to handle even for that — fatal to a man through unbroken skin at a dose of about five teaspoons — before its knock-suppressing property was discovered at all.

That property was found in −24 pT (1921 CE), drawn nearly at random from a long list of candidate compounds; the additive went on sale in −22 pT (1923 CE). In autumn −21 pT (1924 CE) the production line poisoned and killed roughly a dozen of the workers who blended it, across three plants, in open view of a press that printed the count. A federal health conference the following year (−20 pT) was convened to decide whether the product should reach the public at all; it recommended study rather than prohibition, and the product reached the market it had been built for.

It ran there for seven decades. At its peak, c. +28 pT (1973 CE), an ordinary gallon of fuel in the country that had invented it carried two to three grams of the metal, and that country alone put some 200,000 tonnes of lead into its own air each year from tailpipes. It was gone from that country’s road fuel by +51 pT (1996 CE) and off the planet’s by +76 pT (2021 CE); cumulative atmospheric release, all told, exceeded fifteen million tonnes. The written record of the century did not survive the Attrition. The ice did, and holds a lead concentration peaking at roughly two hundred times natural background.

The additive

Engine knock was a second, uncommanded detonation of the fuel–air charge that rang cylinder blocks and capped, for two decades, how far a given fuel could be compressed and so how much work could be drawn from it. A few grams of tetraethyl lead in a full tank suppressed it. An unpatentable antiknock — blending the fuel with alcohol — was known at the time and worked; the lead compound, unlike alcohol, could be sold under patent, and was. Burned alone, however, it left a grey lead-oxide residue that caked valves and fouled spark plugs within a few hundred miles of running, which made the pure additive unusable as a product.

The marketed formulation solved this. It was roughly three parts in five the heavy, sweet-smelling lead compound, the remainder two lighter halogen-bearing liquids — an ethylene bromide and an ethylene chloride — whose entire specified function was to meet the lead as it burned and convert its oxide to lead bromide and lead chloride, salts volatile enough to leave the combustion chamber with the exhaust rather than accumulate in it. The trade term for the two liquids was the scavengers. The formulation did not remove the lead; it relocated it, by design, from the one place a mechanic had reason to look for it to the atmosphere at large. The concentrate was dyed a deep red so that it could not be mistaken, in handling, for water or fuel. Its toxicity was not in question at any point in its commercial life: it was a known lethal poison before it was a product, and the dye was specified because of it.

The poisonings

The first demonstration of the hazard at scale was on the bodies of the workers who made the concentrate. In autumn −21 pT the blending crews fell ill in a fixed sequence — insomnia, then tremor, then hallucination, then a violent delirium that took several men to restrain — and died of it; the workers named the condition for the pale insects the afflicted reported settling on their skin in rooms where nothing flew. Roughly a dozen died that season across three plants running the same formulation, with dozens more permanently affected. The state examiner recorded the cause without euphemism as acute organic-lead poisoning. None of it was concealed: the daily press carried the count beside the name of the substance that produced it.

The following year a federal health service convened the manufacturers and the physicians in a single room to determine whether the additive should be sold to the public or restricted to the men who made it. The conference did not stop it. It asked for cleaner plants and for a programme of study into what the far smaller dose an ordinary motorist inhaled while filling a tank might do across years — a study whose eventual findings changed nothing about the product for the next half-century. Across that same period the blood-lead concentration at which a child was classified as poisoned was revised downward repeatedly, and never once upward.

The phase-out

Throughout its run the additive was manufactured by a handful of licensed plants worldwide — a single-filament supply chain (Ibarra) that dispersed one formulation, molecule by molecule, into essentially every petrol engine on the planet. Consumption peaked in the early 1970s (c. +28 pT) at two to three grams of lead per gallon, and at some 200,000 tonnes annually in its country of origin alone. Phasedown there began in +31 pT (1976 CE), driven in part by a mechanical incompatibility: the same lead salts the scavengers produced deactivated the Catalytic Converter then being fitted to exhaust systems for unrelated reasons. Road use in that country reached zero by +51 pT (1996 CE). The global phase-out completed in +76 pT (2021 CE), when the last national stockpile, in North Africa, was exhausted, after a multilateral campaign of roughly two decades. Cumulative atmospheric release from all sources exceeded fifteen million tonnes.

A national reanalysis conducted late in the period attributed to early-childhood exposure an average loss of 2.6 points of measured intelligence per person, rising to about 5.9 points for the cohort born at peak concentration, and summing to hundreds of millions of points in that one country. Its authors carried the figure with an explicit caution against running a dose-response fitted within one range out to a scale its data never touched (Kagiso); no comparable global count is known to have been completed.

The additive spike

Every engine running the fuel emitted the lead as an aerosol fine enough to cross a hemisphere before settling. A fraction of it fell with snow onto polar ice that no one mined, drank, or stood on, and stayed there in annual bands a later hand could count. The resulting curve is a chronological marker in its own right, concordant with the fly-ash and plutonium horizons and independent of the written record lost to the Attrition (Solanki; the additive spike). It climbs out of the −20s pT, past the years of the production-line deaths, and does not level there; it rises for four more decades to a maximum in ice dated +23 pT (1968 CE), and only then tracks the national phasedown schedules back down.

The same ice carries a second, far fainter lead horizon some two thousand years deeper — about four times natural background — tied by isotope fingerprint to the mining and wine-sweetening of classical antiquity, the same act of dispersing lead through a population conducted at roughly one-fiftieth the scale, and after a recorded warning against lead pipe and lead-boiled must that the age did not heed (Marchetti). Measured against natural background, the layer laid down while the workers were dying and for four decades after stands at approximately two hundred times.

Provenance and ground

The polar lead curve is direct physical evidence: a well-mixed aerosol settling onto accumulating firn, peaking c. 200× natural background in ice dated +23 pT over a fainter horizon ~4× background two millennia deeper (Solanki; Marchetti). The chemistry is unaltered — tetraethyl lead, Pb(C₂H₅)₄, discovered as an antiknock agent −24 pT, on sale from −22 pT, sold as a c. 60% mixture with two halide scavengers that volatilise combustion-chamber lead into salts leaving with the exhaust, and lethal (a set-aside chemical-defence candidate) before release. The autumn deaths and their count, the examiner’s diagnosis, the plain contemporary press coverage, and the following year’s federal conference recommending study over prohibition are the surviving written record; the manufacturing scale and its continuity are inference from that record and the ice. The peak figures, the phasedown and phase-out dates, the >15 Mt released, and the childhood-IQ reanalysis carried with its authors’ caution against extrapolation (Kagiso) are as published.
