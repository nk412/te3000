# Combustion Age

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty. See [Note on language](#note-on-language).*

> **This article is about the Terran historical period, c. 1750–2140 CE.**
> For the propulsion class, see **Internal Combustion**. For the stratigraphic unit, see **Gallus Horizon**. For the sediment marker, see **Spheroidal Carbonaceous Particle**.

---

The **Combustion Age** (also **Combustion Interval**; stratigraphically the **Gallus Horizon**) is the period of Terran history defined by the systematic oxidation of geologically sequestered carbon as the primary energy basis of human society. It is conventionally dated from c. 1750 CE to c. 2140 CE, though regional chronologies differ by more than a century, and no single terminal date commands consensus.

Like the Bronze Age, to which it is habitually and imperfectly compared, the Combustion Age is named for a material whose distribution was radically unequal and whose acquisition organised the political geography of the world. Unlike bronze, the material was not recovered when its objects were broken. It was destroyed in use and its residues released into the atmosphere, the ocean, and the sedimentary record, where they remain. This is the reason the period can be dated at all, and the reason it cannot be dated by the method its own scholars invented.

The period is simultaneously the most heavily documented and among the most poorly attested in Terran history. More text was composed during any single year of the Late Combustion Age than in the whole of the preceding five millennia. Proportionally less of it survives than of Old Assyrian commercial correspondence. A cuneiform tablet requires a fire to destroy it and a scholar to read it. A magnetic domain requires neither.

## 1. Terminology

### 1.1 Endonyms

The period's own inhabitants did not regard themselves as inhabiting a period. Their standard self-description translates as *the modern era* — a phrase that had been in continuous use, always self-referentially, for some four centuries by the time it ceased. Late in the interval a minority of their natural philosophers proposed the term **Anthropocene** for a formal stratigraphic unit beginning within their own lifetimes. Their own stratigraphic authority declined to ratify the proposal in 2024 CE. It was ratified, on essentially the evidence they had assembled, in 2412.

Other attested endonyms include *the industrial world*, *late capitalism*, and *the developed world* — the last a term of self-flattery whose reference class shifted continuously and which no two period sources define identically.

### 1.2 Exonyms and the naming convention

Three names competed in the post-period literature. **Plastic Age** was preferred by stratigraphers, since polymer is the most abundant diagnostic residue. **Silicon Age** was preferred by historians of technology. Both were displaced by **Combustion Age** under the Ravensbourne convention (2604), on the argument that polymer and silicon alike presuppose the energy, and that a period is best named for what it burned rather than for what it left.

The demonym **combustor**, common in popular writing of the twenty-seventh century, is deprecated in scholarship as tendentious. Standard usage is *the period populations*, or, for the stratigraphic sense, *the Crawford populations*, after the type-lake.

Dates are approximate. The tripartite division below is a stratigraphic convenience; no period source recognises it, and regional sequences diverge by up to 120 years. The second column gives the **post-Trinity (pT)** epoch, zeroed at the first fission detonation of 16 July 1945 CE, which is also the base of the global radionuclide marker.

| Phase | CE | pT | Diagnostic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Proto-Combustion | c. 1712 – 1830 | −233 to −115 | Atmospheric engines; localised coal extraction; no global signal |
| Early Combustion (EC) | 1830 – 1945 | −115 to 0 | Rail and steam navigation; fly-ash horizon appears in lake varves; first global market in a fossil commodity |
| Middle Combustion (MC) | 1945 – 2010 | 0 to +65 | Radionuclide spike; polymer horizon; nitrogen fixation at planetary scale; sevenfold rise in energy throughput |
| Late Combustion (LC) | 2010 – 2078 | +65 to +133 | Peak anthropogenic mass; peak record production; peak record loss |

| Consolidation | after c. 2140 | after +195 | — |

### 2.1 The dating problem

The Combustion Age cannot be radiocarbon dated.

Its detonations first flooded the atmosphere with ¹⁴C, roughly doubling the concentration between 1955 and 1964 CE. Its exhaust then diluted it: the carbon released by burning fossil deposits is old enough to contain no ¹⁴C at all, and by the middle of the twenty-first century the atmospheric ratio had fallen through the pre-industrial baseline and kept falling. An organic object manufactured around 2050 CE returns an apparent radiocarbon age of approximately one thousand years. Period researchers understood this and published on it decades in advance. They did not regard it as a problem worth solving.

Dating therefore relies on other markers: the spheroidal carbonaceous particles of high-temperature coal and oil combustion, which appear in lake and marine sediments from the mid-nineteenth century and terminate sharply; the ²³⁹Pu profile, still readily measurable at 3000 CE, having lost only some four per cent of its inventory; stable lead isotope ratios, which trace the rise and legislative abandonment of tetraethyl lead with a precision of a few years; and the morphology of domestic *Gallus gallus* bone, whose transformation between 1945 and 1975 CE is abrupt, global, and irreversible.

## 3. Sources and evidentiary problems

### 3.1 The Attrition

The **Attrition** is the standard term for the loss, between approximately 1990 and 2200 CE, of the overwhelming majority of the period's written and audiovisual production.

The causes are not mysterious and were not unforeseen. Period record-keeping migrated within two generations from durable, human-legible substrates to encodings that were dense, cheap, non-legible without machine mediation, and physically unstable on timescales of decades. Magnetic media demagnetise. Optical media delaminate. Flash cells leak charge. Every one of these substrates required active, funded, institutional recopying at intervals shorter than a human lifetime, and the recopying was performed only for what somebody was currently paying to keep. The period called this *bit rot*. Its own technicians warned about it in terms that read, at this distance, as unimprovable.

The result is a corpus with the shape of a sieve. What survives is not what mattered. What survives is what happened to sit on the far side of a boundary — of anoxia, of lithification, of nickel, of accident.

### 3.2 The lithic and durable record

Concrete is the period's most abundant construction material and among its least durable. Its steel reinforcement corrodes, expands, and spalls the matrix within a century or two of neglect; the unreinforced mass persists as a rubble that is trivially identifiable and nearly informationless. Glass, gold, platinum, stainless steels, and fired ceramic survive. Aluminium, anodised or not, survives well in dry contexts and constitutes the single commonest identifiable metal artefact class.

The richest textual source class is the **anaerobic refuse mound**. Period populations concentrated their discards in engineered pits, compacted them, and capped them with clay and membrane. The interiors are anoxic. Newsprint recovered from such contexts is legible; food residues are identifiable to species; the stratification is annual and undisturbed. Roughly nine-tenths of all directly-read Combustion-Age prose has been recovered from refuse. We know what they threw away. We know very much less about what they kept.

### 3.3 Deliberate archives

The period is the first in Terran history to have addressed the deep future on purpose. It did so at small scale, late, and with almost no institutional support.

- **The Svalbard reels.** Photosensitive film on polyester, stored in a decommissioned coal seam at 78° N — a fact the period's own commentators noted without apparent discomfort. Rated for a millennium; it has met the rating. Contents are overwhelmingly source code from a single deposit made in 2020 CE.
- **The Hallstatt tablets.** Fired ceramic, inscribed and glazed, interred in a salt mine. Individually trivial in content (the deposit accepted submissions from the general public). Collectively the finest demotic corpus of the period, and the only one not subject to institutional selection.
- **The nickel disks.** Micro-etched metal plates, human-legible under optical magnification with no machine required. Several were deposited on Terra; one consignment was carried to Luna aboard a lander that failed at touchdown in 2019 CE and is presumed to have dispersed across a few hundred metres of Mare Serenitatis. Partial recovery is ongoing. The associated cargo of desiccated tardigrades remains a subject of dispute in astrobiology and of some embarrassment in planetary protection.
- **The gold-plated records.** Two analogue audio disks on copper substrate, launched on interstellar trajectories in 1977 CE. The first was recovered in 2871 at 3,340 AU. It is the best-preserved Combustion-Age artefact in existence and the most misleading: a deliberate self-portrait, assembled by committee, intended to be read by strangers. It tells us less about the period than a single refuse pit.
- **The Long Clock.** A mechanical timekeeper in a limestone chamber, designed for a ten-thousand-year duty cycle. It is running. It is, at 3000 CE, approximately correct.

### 3.4 The Wasatch Corpus

The largest continuous body of Combustion-Age material is not an archive in the intentional sense.

During the Late Combustion Age, several state signals bureaux intercepted and retained networked traffic in bulk, storing the encrypted remainder against the possibility of later decryption. Their own technicians described the policy as *harvest now, decrypt later*. The policy worked. Asymmetric key exchange of the period rests on the difficulty of factoring and of discrete logarithms, and both fall to quantum period-finding; the traffic was read out, in bulk, within a century of its collection. The bureaux that gathered it had by then left almost no other trace.

The corpus is therefore our fullest continuous record of ordinary Combustion-Age life, and it is deformed in every direction at once. It over-represents one language and three continents. It contains almost nothing that was written on paper, spoken aloud, or thought. And it consists, by volume, overwhelmingly of automated advertising telemetry — machine-generated observations of what individuals looked at, for how long, and what was subsequently sold to them.

Ranganathan's formulation is standard: *we do not know what they said to each other; we know what was said about them to machines.*

### 3.5 The Sealed

A second class of encrypted material has never been read and never will be.

Symmetric ciphers of the period's later standard resist quantum attack. The best known algorithm reduces the search space quadratically, leaving a 256-bit key with an effective strength of 128 bits. No physical process available to us, or plausibly available to anyone, searches that space; the energy budget exceeds the mass–energy of the system in which the search would be conducted. Where the key was long, random, and lost, the plaintext is gone.

This is a category without precedent in the discipline. Linear A is *undeciphered*: it awaits a scholar, a bilingual, a lucky find. The Sealed corpora are *undecipherable*, and the period's own mathematicians proved it, in public, in advance. It is the first time a body of writing has been placed permanently beyond scholarship by design, by people who intended nothing of the kind. Estimates of the sealed fraction range from 20 to 60 per cent of all surviving Late Combustion bits.

### 3.6 Orbital and lunar evidence

Low orbit self-cleared within centuries. Geostationary orbit did not: several thousand period satellites remain in graveyard orbits above the belt, dry, cold, and — apart from micrometeoroid pitting — essentially as manufactured. They are the finest surviving assemblage of Combustion-Age precision engineering and, being unbudgeted, the least studied.

On Luna, six crewed landing sites and the associated hardware survive under vacuum. Three laser retroreflector arrays deployed between 1969 and 1971 CE remain functional and are ranged routinely. They are the only Combustion-Age instruments still returning data to their original specification, 1,030 years after emplacement, and the fact is noted in every introductory course and taken seriously by nobody.

### 3.7 Biological and genomic evidence

The period buried its dead in identifiable, densely packed, geochemically distinctive cemeteries, and interred them with dental work of stainless steel, gold, and ceramic. Genomic recovery from Combustion-Age remains is routine and effectively complete. We know the period's populations — their ancestries, their migrations, their pathogen exposures, their dietary isotopes, their mercury and lead burdens — with a resolution unavailable for any earlier period and, for reasons of custom, for any later one.

We know their bodies far better than we know their minds.

### 3.8 Sampling bias

The standard cautions may be summarised:

1. **The deliberate archives are unrepresentative by construction.** They were assembled by the small subpopulation that thought about deep time.
2. **The Svalbard bias.** Because the largest deliberate text deposit is a snapshot of one software repository, the period's intellectual life is systematically over-attributed to programming, and within programming to a single language whose ecosystem happened to consist of very large numbers of very small packages. Several twenty-seventh-century syntheses treat a widely-replicated eleven-line routine for padding strings as a canonical text. The error is now corrected in the literature and persists in the popular imagination.
3. **The Wasatch bias.** See §3.4.
4. **The refuse bias.** Discard is not a random sample of possession. It is a sample of *failure* — of the broken, the spoiled, the outgrown, the unwanted.
5. **The inversion.** For every prior period we have the deliberative record of elites and must infer the ordinary. For this one we have the ordinary in overwhelming, machine-collected detail, and must infer the deliberative.

## 4. Material culture

### 4.1 Energy

Period energy use rose from roughly 20 exajoules per annum in 1830 CE to a peak somewhere between 700 and 800 exajoules in the mid-twenty-first century — a rise of a factor of forty against a population rise of a factor of nine. The overwhelming majority of this was the oxidation of coal, petroleum, and methane. Approximately 1.7 teratonnes of carbon dioxide were released, of which a portion remains in the atmosphere and a further portion in the ocean, where it lowered surface pH by roughly 0.1 units within the Middle Combustion phase alone.

The second-order consequences were structural. The **fixation of atmospheric nitrogen** by high-pressure catalysis, from 1913 CE, made possible a nitrogen input to agriculture unbounded by biology. By the Late Combustion Age, roughly half of the nitrogen atoms in a typical human body had passed through an industrial reactor. The period fed itself on machinery. It is not clear that most of its inhabitants knew this.

### 4.2 Polymers

Cumulative production of synthetic polymer over the period is estimated at 30 to 50 gigatonnes. Under a tenth was ever reprocessed; a comparable fraction was oxidised; the remainder entered the sedimentary record, where it constitutes the single most reliable global marker of the interval. Polymer degrades mechanically long before it degrades chemically. The characteristic Combustion-Age deposit is a bed of subvisible fragments and fibres, continuous across every marine basin, thinning below and terminating above.

Where polymer occurs in bulk it lithifies with clastic sediment into an anthropogenic conglomerate for which the period coined the term *plastiglomerate*, and which survives as competent rock.

### 4.3 Concrete, steel, and anthropogenic mass

The period is the interval during which the total mass of human-manufactured objects overtook the total dry mass of living things on the planet. The crossover occurred around 2020 CE, at approximately 1.1 teratonnes each. Anthropogenic mass thereafter doubled roughly every twenty years until the Contraction.

Of this mass, the great majority was concrete and aggregate. Its stratigraphic expression is a global, near-instantaneous, unconformable layer of angular carbonate and silicate clasts with a diagnostic Portland-cement matrix and, where reinforced, a rust ghost.

### 4.4 The tin problem

The comparison with the Bronze Age is at its strongest, and most instructive, here.

Bronze required tin. Tin occurs in a handful of places. The Late Bronze Age palace economies depended on a filamentary trade in a scarce material across thousands of kilometres, and when the filament parted, the system that depended on it did not recover.

The Late Combustion Age had reproduced this arrangement in a purer form. Its characteristic artefact — the integrated circuit — could be manufactured at the leading process node in essentially **one place**: a coastal industrial district on a single seismically active island, dependent on imported energy and imported water. The lithography machines required to do it were built in essentially **one town**, by one firm, in the Low Countries. The optics for those machines came from one Bavarian works. Several elements essential to the process — high-purity gallium, certain rare earths, helium for the coolants, a class of neon used in the light sources — were each concentrated in one or two jurisdictions.

Period analysts described this arrangement as *efficient*. They were not wrong; the concentration is what made the artefacts cheap. The analogy to the Bronze Age is not that the arrangement was foolish. It is that the arrangement was rational, comprehensible, profitable, and understood in detail by everyone concerned, and was nevertheless a single filament.

Two wrecks — one in the Strait of Malacca, one at the northern mouth of the Suez canal, both of container vessels — have supplied the period's Uluburun: sealed, dated, itemised cargoes containing every artefact class of the age, from bulk polymer feedstock to packaged silicon, in the proportions the age actually moved them.

## 5. Economy and exchange

The period's exchange medium was a family of **fiat tokens**, issued by territorial polities without commodity backing, whose value rested on the issuing polity's capacity to demand them back in taxation. Approximately ninety-five per cent of the token supply existed only as ledger entries in institutions that lent it into being. The arrangement worked. It is, at this remove, extremely difficult to explain to students why it worked, and the period's own economists were divided on the question.

The dominant productive institution was the **corporation**: a non-kin, potentially immortal legal person, capable of owning property, contracting, and holding other corporations, whose obligations to its members were financial and whose obligations to everyone else were whatever the polity had most recently legislated. Nothing quite like it exists before, and it is worth stating plainly that the largest of these entities commanded revenues exceeding those of most contemporary polities while possessing no territory, no citizenry, and no obligation to persist.

Growth — the year-on-year expansion of the token-denominated value of transactions — functioned as the period's principal cosmology. It was the metric by which polities were ranked, governments retained, and futures underwritten. It was not primarily an economic doctrine; it was the frame within which economic doctrines argued. Its critics within the period are numerous, articulate, and, in the surviving record, uniformly marginal.

## 6. Polity and society

The characteristic Combustion-Age polity is the **nation-state**: a bounded, contiguous territory under a single legal sovereign, whose inhabitants were held to constitute a people. Membership was hereditary and conferred by birthplace or descent. Movement between polities was regulated by a small booklet, of which very large numbers survive; the differential in permitted movement between holders of different booklets was among the largest and least discussed sources of inequality in the period.

The period saw the completion of the **demographic transition** across nearly all of Terra. Mortality fell first, then fertility; population rose from roughly 0.8 to a peak near 10.3 × 10⁹ around the middle of the twenty-first century, and then, for the first time in the species' history absent catastrophe, began to decline for reasons that were substantially voluntary. Period commentators treated this decline as a crisis. The evidence that they were correct is thin.

Urban residence passed fifty per cent in 2007 CE and did not return.

## 7. Writing and records

The period wrote in alphabets, abjads, abugidas, and logographies, and encoded nearly all of them, by the Late Combustion Age, in a single unified glyph inventory whose descendants remain in use. The inventory's assignment of numerical code points to characters is the closest thing the period produced to a durable intellectual standard, and its early-twenty-first-century revisions — including its accession of several thousand small pictographic characters used for emotional and tonal marking in informal writing — are directly ancestral to the modern tonal diacritics.

Formats, not scripts, are the difficulty. Period text was rarely stored as characters. It was stored inside proprietary containers whose specifications were commercial property, undocumented, versioned, and abandoned. Most have been reconstructed by inference from the very large number of surviving instances, in a process methodologically identical to the decipherment of an unknown script with an abundant corpus. A minority remain unread — not because they are difficult, but because too few instances survive to constrain the inference.

## 8. Warfare

The Combustion Age developed, in 1945 CE, a weapon capable of destroying any city in a few seconds, and within twenty years possessed enough of them to end the species. Between 1945 and the close of the period, such weapons were used in war exactly twice, both times within a month of their invention, and never again.

This is the single most contested fact about the period, and it has generated a scholarly literature comparable in volume and in inconclusiveness to the literature on Bronze Age chariotry.

The **deterrence school** holds that the weapons enforced their own disuse, that the period's great powers understood this, and that the seven decades of great-power peace following 1945 are the direct consequence. The **contingency school** holds that the record contains at least a dozen occasions on which use was averted by the judgement of a single individual acting against procedure, that a policy which requires this is not a policy, and that the period survived on a run of luck whose prior probability we have no way to estimate from a single sample. The **materialist school** holds that both are beside the point: that the period's great powers were bound by an exchange network which made conquest less profitable than commerce, and that the weapons were a symptom of that arrangement rather than its cause.

The arsenals were still largely intact at the beginning of the Contraction. What became of them is treated in §10.4 and is not settled.

## 9. The biosphere

By the Late Combustion Age, the mammalian biomass of Terra was approximately 36 per cent human, 60 per cent human livestock, and 4 per cent everything else. Avian biomass was roughly 70 per cent domestic fowl. The period had, without deciding to, converted the terrestrial vertebrate biosphere into an appendix of its food system.

Terrestrial extinction rates ran two to three orders of magnitude above the background inferred from the fossil record. Simultaneously, the period redistributed species across ocean basins at a rate never previously achieved, such that local biotic diversity in many disturbed habitats *rose* while global diversity fell — a signature the stratigraphy records as a sudden worldwide homogenisation of assemblages.

### 9.1 The markers

Late in the period, several polities interred long-lived fission products in deep geological repositories intended to remain closed for on the order of 10⁵ years — a design horizon two orders of magnitude beyond any institution then existing. The problem of communicating the hazard across that interval was studied seriously and inconclusively. Proposals included monumental hostile architecture, engineered pictographic sequences, self-perpetuating priesthoods, and the deliberate breeding of a colour-changing animal that would serve as a living indicator, to be preserved in folklore after the science was lost. The last was not implemented, and its underlying argument — that ritual outlives language, which outlives explanation — has not been improved upon.

The repositories were located, catalogued, and left closed. Whether the markers were understood or merely obeyed is disputed.

## 10. The Contraction

### 10.1 Definition

**The Contraction** denotes the interval, conventionally c. 2078–2140 CE, across which the Late Combustion order ceased to function as an integrated system. The term is descriptive and deliberately weak. Stronger terms — *collapse*, *the Fall* — are not used in current scholarship, for reasons which are partly evidentiary and partly a reaction against the confidence of the twenty-sixth century.

### 10.2 The evidence

- **Coastal abandonment.** The transgression of the Late Combustion Age is unambiguous in the geomorphological record. Low-relief coasts were vacated over a period of decades, in an order that correlates with elevation and, far more strongly, with the wealth of the vacating population.
- **Isotopic and particulate discontinuity.** SCP flux falls by roughly two orders of magnitude between +120 and +160 pT. The kerosene signature characteristic of high-altitude aviation ceases almost entirely within a decade.
- **The archival discontinuity.** The Wasatch Corpus terminates. Its terminal stratum is not a crescendo of alarm; it is a thinning, then nothing. Recovered administrative record from the same interval shows an accelerating substitution of local for continental scale.
- **Skeletal evidence.** Stature and enamel hypoplasia in Contraction-phase burials show regional stress episodes of a severity comparable to the worst of the pre-industrial record, and — crucially — *not synchronised* across regions.

### 10.3 The schools

The **systems-fragility school** (Ibarra and successors) locates the cause in §4.4: a production network of extraordinary efficiency, minimal buffering, and single points of failure, coupled to a food system dependent on industrial nitrogen, industrial phosphate, and long-haul refrigerated logistics. On this account, no single shock was sufficient; any two, correlated, were.

The **climatic school** treats the network fragility as the mechanism and the climatic forcing as the cause, and marshals the drought, harvest, and migration proxies accordingly. Its opponents note that the forcing was gradual, that its trajectory was published in advance and broadly accepted, and that gradual, foreseen changes have rarely destroyed anything by themselves.

The **information school** — the youngest, and currently ascendant — argues that the period's decisive failure was neither material nor climatic but epistemic: that the Late Combustion Age constructed a communications environment in which the cost of producing persuasive falsehood fell to nearly zero, in which attention was the scarce good and was sold rather than allocated, and that its capacity for collective decision degraded faster than its physical capacity. The evidence is the Wasatch Corpus. The objection is that the Wasatch Corpus *is* the advertising apparatus, and that arguing from it that advertising destroyed the period is a circularity that ought to embarrass its proponents more than it does.

The **continuity school** (Zhou-Ferreira, 2899, and a persistent minority since) denies that a collapse occurred. It observes that global population at +200 pT exceeded that at −100 pT by a factor of five; that literacy, longevity, and caloric adequacy were higher at the end of the Contraction than at the beginning of the Late Combustion Age in the same regions; that the abandonment of the coasts was a relocation and not a die-off; and that what our sources record as the end of the world is in every case the end of a *particular archive*, maintained by a particular institution, that stopped being funded.

Zhou-Ferreira's warning is the first thing taught in the field and the last thing believed: **the end of the archive is not the end of the world.**

### 10.4 The arsenals

Not settled. The isotopic record of the Contraction interval contains no detonation signature above the Middle Combustion background, which places a firm upper bound on the scale of any nuclear exchange and rules out several popular accounts entirely. It does not rule out a small one. Inventory reconstruction from period documents is incomplete by design.

## 11. Legacy

Sea level at 3000 CE stands several metres above the Middle Combustion datum and continues to rise; the ice-sheet response to Combustion-Age forcing operates on millennial timescales and is not finished. The submarine archaeology of Combustion-Age coastal settlement is consequently the largest single subdiscipline of the field, as the underwater archaeology of the Bronze Age Aegean once was, and for the same reason.

The atmosphere retains an anomalous carbon isotope ratio. The sediments retain plutonium, fly ash, polymer, and a bird. The Moon retains six landing sites, three functioning retroreflectors, and a few hundred square metres of scattered nickel. Geostationary orbit retains a museum nobody visits.

The Combustion Age is the first period in Terran history to have deliberately addressed the future. It buried ceramic in salt and film in a coal seam. It etched nickel and threw it at the sky. It cut a clock into a mountain and set it running for ten thousand years. It marked its poisons with pictograms and worried, at length and in public, whether we would understand them.

Almost none of that is how the period reached us. What reached us was refuse, concrete, plutonium, polymer, and the bones of a bird bred to grow too fast to stand. As with the Bronze Age, we know the period best exactly where it was least self-conscious.

The difference is that this one was trying.

## Note on language

This article is rendered in **Reconstructed Late Combustion English**, the conventional scholarly register for period subjects, on the same principle by which Bronze Age scholarship retained *tell*, *tumulus*, and *stela*. It is not the language of its writer or of its readers.

Standard Terran has diverged from Late Combustion English by roughly the distance separating that language from the English of 1100 CE, though along different axes. Mass audiovisual recording, universal literacy, and a single dominant trade register slowed phonological drift sharply during the Middle and Late Combustion phases; the Attrition removed the recordings that had anchored it, and drift resumed at pre-industrial rates within two generations. The interdental fricatives are gone. The perfective is marked with a preverbal particle from a Late Combustion auxiliary. The first person plural has acquired an inclusive/exclusive distinction, almost certainly from Pacific contact varieties. Orthography was regularised, twice.

The opening of §3.1, in Standard Terran (transliterated):

> *Dey no kept dem rekord in ston. Dey don kept it in lite an in tin metal, an so de mos av it gon. Wat yumi hav, yumi hav bai aksident — no bai gift.*

Readers who find this passage transparent should be aware that they are reading it a millennium after it stopped being possible to read the sentence it translates.
