The Class-E1 Assemblage

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a skeletal assemblage and its interred metalwork, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Class-E1 Assemblage is a Contraction-era burial ground on a dry interior upland, poleward of the abandoned coasts, in which each of sixty-one recovered individuals was laid with a single high-accuracy mass standard — one certified reference weight to a body. The bones and the weights survive intact; the reason the two were buried together is reconstruction, and it is contested.

The practice’s own name is lost; the Faculty designates it by the class mark on the metal certificate-plates set beside the weights, the only legend the graves carry. The standards are single-piece austenitic stainless steel of the age’s highest accuracy class, near 8,000 kg/m³, glossy and non-porous. On recovery they were catalogued as votive coin, obols laid for the dead in the old manner; they are working mass standards.

The number that will not keep

A one-kilogram example of the class was certified true to within ±0.5 mg — five parts in ten million — but never in the metal alone: the air it displaces weighs some 150 mg, three hundred times the tolerance, so the figure held only against a known air density and an unbroken chain of comparison rising to the primary realisation of the kilogram, which after +74 pT (2019 CE) was a fixed value of the Planck constant realised in a handful of laboratories. Severed from that chain and left in soil, a polished standard gains mass by sorption and corrosion past its whole tolerance within a season, and its certified number becomes a claim no one can check.

The readings

Marchetti reads the weights as instruments and nothing further; Ibarra, invoking single-filament infrastructure, reads their burial as disposal — once the realisation was unreachable a certified standard was only a polished stone with an unverifiable number attached, and the grave is where such stones went; a third reading holds that those who kept them never parted the instrument from the grave-good at all. A verification-station log swept into the Wasatch Corpus preserves one exchange between a field operator and the central register:

— standard overdue, primary unreachable. certify? — CERTIFY. CHAIN ASSUMED INTACT.

The plate in grave 12 reads E1 · 1 kg · TRACEABLE · RETURN WITHIN 90 D; the weight it certifies was in the grave. Whether the assemblage records a standard demoted to a grave-good, or a grave-good that was always also a standard, the bones and the metal do not decide.

Provenance and ground

The assemblage was recovered intact from anoxic upland sediment and dated by its enclosing layer — radiocarbon being unavailable for the age — to the +150s pT (c. 2100 CE). The weights and their plates are the durable, inert fraction the period leaves; the soft record of why they were laid is not among the survivals. Ibarra (2891) reads the practice under single-filament infrastructure; Marchetti (2894) disputes the votive cataloguing.
