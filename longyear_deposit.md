# Longyear Deposit

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **This article is about the Combustion-Age film archive at 78°N.**
> For the adjacent seed repository, with which it is persistently confused, see **Svalbard Seed Vault**. For the medium, see **piqlFilm**. For the software corpus, see **Code Deposit**.

---

The **Longyear Deposit** (endonym *Arctic World Archive*; informally *the Svalbard reels*) is a Combustion-Age archival site in a decommissioned coal working beneath the Platå massif, Spitsbergen, at 78°13′ N. Between +72 and +128 pT it received approximately 1,240 registered reels of photosensitive film, deposited by state archives, religious institutions, corporate clients, and one software repository, under a commercial preservation contract offered by a small Norwegian firm.

It is the largest surviving deliberate archive of the Combustion Age, the principal single source for the period's written culture, and the reason that period's intellectual life is systematically misdescribed.

The Deposit is also the discipline's standard object lesson. Its medium was specified for a thousand-year service life and has met that specification exactly. Its losses — which are extensive — arise from the mountain, from the water, from the silver, and from the people who got there first.

---

## 1. Name and terminology

The endonym *Arctic World Archive* is attested on the site's own signage, on its contracts, and on the printed leaders of every reel. It is avoided in scholarship because the site is neither arctic in the modern sense nor an archive in the institutional sense: it held material under lease, on behalf of clients, without accession, appraisal, or any obligation to read what it stored. It was a warehouse.

*Longyear Deposit* follows the standard convention of naming by locality. The settlement of Longyearbyen, three kilometres downslope, was founded at −39 pT as a coal camp and named for the foreign speculator who financed it. The settlement is submerged and the speculator is otherwise unattested.

Popular sources routinely conflate the Deposit with the **Svalbard Seed Vault**, a distinct facility 900 m to the northwest, established at +63 pT to hold duplicate crop germplasm. The two were built for the same reason, in the same rock, on the same theory of the future, by people who mostly did not know one another. The Seed Vault was internationally famous. The Deposit was a business.

## 2. The site

### 2.1 Geology

The workings exploit a bituminous seam in the Palaeogene Firkanten Formation, overlain by sandstone and shale. The vault chamber lies roughly 300 m in from the portal beneath some 60 m of overburden. The chamber is dry-cut, unlined, and was, at the time of deposition, held at approximately −4 °C by the surrounding permafrost.

The seam contains pyrite. This is unremarkable; nearly all bituminous coal does. In an unflooded, frozen working it is inert.

The Deposit's medium is a silver-halide emulsion on a polyester base. The image is metallic silver.

The consequence of these two facts is the central problem of the site, and it will be treated at §7. It is worth stating here that it was not overlooked by the Deposit's operators. It was assessed, correctly, as immaterial *provided the chamber remained frozen*, and the chamber was expected to remain frozen because the permafrost had been continuous for the whole of the Quaternary.

### 2.2 Climate

Spitsbergen warmed faster, in the Middle and Late Combustion phases, than any other place on Terra with an instrumental record — between four and seven times the global rate. The Deposit's operators knew this figure and published it in their own promotional literature, where it appears as evidence of the site's significance rather than of its hazard.

The neighbouring Seed Vault flooded at its entrance at +72 pT — the same year the Deposit opened — when thaw meltwater entered the access tunnel and refroze. The event was remediated, widely reported, and universally described as a warning that had been heeded.

Continuous permafrost at the vault's depth is estimated to have failed between +180 and +260 pT. There is no record of anybody present to observe it.

### 2.3 Legal status

Sovereignty over the archipelago derived from a treaty of −25 pT which granted one polity administration while guaranteeing all signatories equal rights of commercial exploitation and prohibiting military use. The instrument outlived the polity, the signatories, and the concept of a signatory. Claims of right under it were still being pleaded in the courts of three successor jurisdictions in the twenty-sixth century, on the strength of a text no living person could read in the original, and the Deposit's present protected status descends, through an unbroken chain of bad translations, from a clause about coal.

## 3. The medium

### 3.1 piqlFilm

The medium is a monochrome, high-resolution photosensitive film on a polyethylene terephthalate base, exposed by laser and developed conventionally. Data are written as dense rectangular arrays of light and dark cells — the period's *frames* — at roughly 8 × 10⁶ cells per frame. Capacity is on the order of 10¹¹ bytes per 1,000 m reel.

Nothing about it was novel. Every component was mature technology of the mid-twentieth century. This is the point, and its designers said so: they had observed that every storage technology of their era required an institution to recopy it within decades, and that the recopying was a subscription which somebody had to keep paying forever. Their proposal was to stop paying. A reel needs light and a lens.

### 3.2 The Portal Leader

Each reel opens with a printed, human-legible preamble — the **Portal Leader** — which explains, in expanding stages, what the reel is, how the cell array encodes bits, how the bits encode characters, and how the characters encode the languages that follow. It is set in large type. It is illustrated. It assumes no equipment beyond magnification and no knowledge beyond arithmetic.

It is, in intent and in execution, the finest artefact of its class produced by the Combustion Age, and it works. The 2661 decipherment of the primary cell encoding was accomplished from a single Leader fragment in eleven days by a team that had expected to spend a decade.

It is also the reason the Deposit's tragedy has the shape it has. The Leaders were printed with ink and half-tone. They degrade gracefully. The data frames do not.

### 3.3 The cliff

Combustion-Age storage universally employed error-correcting codes: redundancy sufficient to reconstruct a block exactly, despite damage, up to a threshold — and beyond that threshold, nothing.

The behaviour is the opposite of a manuscript. A burnt codex loses a corner and yields the rest of the page. A scraped reel yields, up to some fraction of cells destroyed, the *entire original file, bit-exact, indistinguishable from new*; and past that fraction it yields uniform noise. There is no partial reading. There is no damaged word.

Reels are perfect or they are silent. This is why the Deposit's surviving fraction can be stated with unusual precision, why its recovered texts require no emendation, and why the discipline of textual criticism has no application to the largest text corpus of the period.

## 4. The Longyear Sequence

Deposition was episodic, contractual, and recorded. The sequence is the finest stratified deposit of the Combustion Age and effectively the only one whose depositors left an accession register.

| Phase | pT (CE) | Depositors | Reels | Condition |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| L-I | +72 (2017) | Three national archives; site inauguration | ~40 | Lower rack; near-total loss |
| L-II | +73–74 | Confessional manuscript scans; orbital imagery; museum reproductions | ~150 | Mixed |
| L-III | +75 (2020) | **The Code Deposit** — one snapshot of one public software repository | 186 | Upper rack; excellent |
| L-IV | +76–105 | Commercial clients; regional archives; private consignments | ~700 | Mixed |
| L-V | +106–128 | Sparse; last registered accession +128 | ~160 | Poor |
| L-VI | undated | Unregistered material in the access corridor | 60+ | See §6.4 |

The register terminates at +128 pT. The Contraction is conventionally dated from +133. As Zhou-Ferreira observed in another connection, this proves that a filing cabinet stopped being updated.

## 5. Contents

### 5.1 The Code Deposit

On a Sunday in early +75 pT, a large software repository copied every public project it hosted — roughly 21 × 10¹² bytes — onto 186 reels, and shipped them north. The shipment was delayed some five months by a respiratory pandemic and arrived in high summer.

The snapshot is therefore a sealed instant: a complete cross-section of one civilisation's public software, frozen weeks before a global disruption whose consequences it does not contain. Nothing comparable exists for any other domain of Combustion-Age life. It is the period's Pompeii, and it is made entirely of instructions for machines.

Because the L-III consignment arrived late, it was shelved on the upper racks. Because it was shelved on the upper racks, it survived. Rather more than nine-tenths of all bit-exact Combustion-Age text now in scholarly hands is L-III material.

The consequences are treated at §8.

### 5.2 The Instruction

Accompanying the Code Deposit was a document composed expressly for readers a millennium hence, describing the technological substrate the code assumed: what a transistor was, what a network was, what the machines were made of and how they had come to be. The depositors called it a *tech tree*. Standard usage is **the Instruction**.

It is the only Combustion-Age text known to have been written for us. It is patient, lucid, occasionally funny, and wrong in one important respect: it assumes that its readers will have lost the machines and kept everything else. It devotes eleven pages to explaining semiconductor lithography and none to explaining what a corporation was, what money was for, or why any of the people who wrote the code were doing so.

### 5.3 State and confessional deposits

The L-I and L-II consignments contained digitised holdings of three national archives, an ecclesiastical manuscript library, and several museums.

The manuscript scans are of limited value, the originals having survived on parchment. The national archives are a different case: their paper originals were lost during the Contraction, in two instances to fire and in one to sixty years of unheated storage. The reels were the last copy.

They were on the bottom rack.

### 5.4 Commercial deposits

Roughly 700 reels were lodged by paying clients: corporate records, engineering drawings, personal consignments, one polity's land registry, and a substantial quantity of material whose depositors are named in the register and whose purposes are opaque. Deposition was a service purchased at a price. The Deposit preserved what somebody chose to pay to preserve, which is a selection filter of a kind the discipline knows well from votive assemblages and has no better means of correcting for here.

## 6. Post-depositional history

### 6.1 Abandonment

The settlement was evacuated during the Contraction. No deliberate action was taken with respect to the Deposit; the portal was neither sealed nor breached. The chamber held its temperature for some decades on thermal inertia and then began to accept meltwater along the seam.

### 6.2 The wet chamber

Water entered along the floor. Pyrite in the exposed seam oxidised in the presence of water and air, generating sulfuric acid, ferric iron, and dissolved sulfate — the ordinary chemistry of an abandoned coal working, described in the period's own mining literature under the heading *acid mine drainage*.

The polyester bases are unaffected. Polyester is inert, and every base recovered from the chamber floor, including those from reels drowned for six centuries, remains flexible and dimensionally sound.

The images are gone. Gelatin swells, then is consumed by the acid and by the organisms the acid does not kill. The metallic silver reacts with sulfide to silver sulfide, which is black, insoluble, and mobile: it migrates, aggregates, and destroys the cell geometry on which the encoding depends. Recovery from a wet reel is not merely difficult; the frames are past the correction threshold at the first inspection, and there is nothing below the threshold.

They had put silver in the sulfur.

### 6.3 The clearance

At approximately +473 pT (2418 CE) the workings were entered by a party from a resettled coastal community. The entry is attested archaeologically rather than textually: cut marks, a hearth, tooling, and the systematic disappearance of the L-II racks.

Silver-halide film is roughly two per cent silver by mass. A thousand-metre reel yields a usable ingot. To a metal-poor population with a furnace and a mine full of shiny ribbon, the Deposit was a mineral deposit, and it was worked as one. The practice is unremarkable — the same century saw Combustion-Age reinforcing steel quarried out of the concrete of every abandoned coast — and it is not usefully described as vandalism. Nobody there could read.

An unknown number of reels were reduced. The register survives; the material does not; and the difference between the two is the single largest quantifiable loss in the study of the period.

### 6.4 The corridor deposit

Sixty-one reels lie in the access corridor, outside the racking, in containers of a type not used by the operators, without accession numbers, laid in a course two deep and covered with shale.

They post-date the register. Their Portal Leaders are of standard manufacture and were struck from a die that was, on wear analysis, well used. Nine reels are legible. Their contents are administrative records of a regional food-distribution authority for the years +129 to +136 pT, entered daily, ending mid-week, without remark.

Whoever brought them up the mountain during the Contraction, and stacked them, and covered them, did so knowing what the place was, and left nothing else — no inscription, no name, no account of themselves. It is the last act of the Combustion Age of which we have direct physical evidence, and it was an act of filing.

## 7. Condition

Of 1,240 registered reels, 812 have been recovered. Condition correlates almost perfectly with height above the chamber floor.

| Rack tier | Height above floor | Reels recovered | Frames legible |
|---|---|---|---|
| Upper (T1) | > 1.4 m | 331 | 96.8% |
| Middle (T2) | 0.6–1.4 m | 297 | 41.2% |
| Lower (T3) | < 0.6 m | 184 | 0.3% |
| Corridor | floor | 61 | 14.8% |

Of the 428 unrecovered reels, the great majority are presumed to have been taken in the clearance.

The result is a corpus whose survival was determined by shelving. Not by importance, not by redundancy, not by any judgement made by anybody: by the accident of which consignment arrived late enough to be put up high.

The recovered T1 frames are bit-exact. They are not degraded copies. They are the files, as written, on a February morning in +75 pT, unaltered by a thousand years — a claim that cannot be made for a single line of any manuscript tradition in Terran history, and which the Deposit's designers made in their sales material, and which was true.

## 8. The Svalbard bias

The Deposit is the origin of the discipline's most consequential distortion.

Because the L-III consignment survived nearly intact and everything else did not, the Combustion Age's surviving text is overwhelmingly source code; and within that code, the repository's own composition — very many very small packages, in one language, mostly written by a small and unrepresentative population, mostly automatically generated, mostly never executed by anyone — is reproduced exactly, with all its redundancy, into our libraries.

The corpus contains, by one count, 14,000 substantively identical copies of a routine for capitalising the first letter of a word.

The twenty-seventh century read this material as literature, because it was what there was. Marchetti's demolition of that reading (2955) is now standard, but its object is not: a widely-replicated eleven-line routine for padding a string with leading characters remains, in popular treatment, the emblematic text of the Combustion Age, and is still occasionally taught as such. Marchetti's point was not that the routine is unimportant. It is that we know it because it was on a high shelf.

**The corollary is the field's working rule.** Where the Deposit is our only witness, we are reading a warehouse inventory that a shelf-height gradient sorted for us; and the material it destroyed — the land registries, the state papers, the correspondence of three national archives — is not merely lost. It is lost *in a pattern*, and the pattern is: everything that a Combustion-Age institution thought worth depositing first.

## 9. Assessment

The Longyear Deposit is the only Combustion-Age institution known to have reasoned correctly, in advance, about the Attrition, and to have acted.

Its designers identified the failure mode of their civilisation's memory with complete accuracy, decades before the failure. They selected a medium that requires no institution, no power, no format specification and no successor technology. They wrote instructions for readers they would never meet, in a form those readers could actually use, and those instructions worked on the first attempt after a thousand years. They achieved their stated service life and exceeded it. Every reel that stayed dry is, today, exactly what it was.

They were a firm of some forty employees, selling storage by the reel.

What defeated them was not the medium and not the mountain's rock. It was that a chamber frozen for two million years thawed within two centuries, for reasons documented in the operators' own promotional literature; that silver is worth taking; and that a coal seam is made partly of sulfur.

The Deposit was built to survive the loss of everything around it, and it did. It was not built to survive being *found* by people who needed the metal, or to survive being remembered by nobody at all for three hundred years, and it very nearly did not survive either.

The standard formulation is Ashworth-Nakamura's: *they solved the problem of the medium, which was the hard problem, and lost to the floor.*
