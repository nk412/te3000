Broiler Gallus

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a Crawford population of the Gallus Horizon and a recovered growth trial, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The three seasons in which a breeder set out to build a better meat bird and built, without meaning to, the ancestor of nearly every chicken then alive — reconstructed from the bone that her line became. The contest, the two winning crosses, the growth curve, and the standing numbers are record; the breeder, her pens, and the seasons between the judgings are inference from the shape the record makes.

She could judge a breast by hand, in the dark, from a bird that had not yet been killed, and she was proud of that, and she was right to be. It was not a trick. It was thirty years of keels under the same two thumbs, and a card for every hen, and a habit of culling that hurt and that she did anyway. The cross she was building in those years ran a heavy-breasted fighting-stock cock over the fastest-feathering farm hens she had, and then the best of their daughters over the same kind of cock again, and she chose the daughters the way she always had: deepest keel, widest span across the shoulders, and quickest to put weight on in the first six weeks, because the first six weeks were the ones that paid. She kept the numbers because the numbers were the whole of it. A bird that dressed out well and ate its own bodyweight three times over to get there was a bird she did not breed from twice.

So when the notice came — a public competition, run in three rounds over what the reconstructed dating puts at +1 to +3 pT (1946 to 1948 CE), state heats, then regional judging, then a national final at a mid-Atlantic experiment station — she read the brief and laughed, because the brief asked, in as many words, for a bird with a breast like a turkey’s, and that was the bird she had been chasing since before the contest’s backers, a national grocery chain and a bench of government breeding scientists and volunteer judges drawn from more than forty jurisdictions, had thought to ask for it. You did not send your bird. That was the part she admired. You sent hatching eggs, and they hatched them together, under the same lamps, and raised them on one feed to one age and killed them on one morning, and judged the dressed carcasses — rate of gain, pounds of feed to the pound of meat, the width laid on the breast, the skin clean of tears and pinfeathers. It took the coop and the grain and the weather out of it. It left the bird. She thought it was the fairest test anyone had built, and she still thinks so, in the reconstruction, because there is no reason in the record to think otherwise.

Her cross placed second at the national final. A line off the far western coast took it outright; hers came in immediately behind, close enough that both were bought up and put into commercial hatching inside the decade. She would have gone home a little sore and entirely certain she had the better bird. What she could not have read, in the applause for the western line, was the arithmetic already running under both of them. Within a few years her operation held something near a quarter of the whole market for parent breeding stock; by the decade the records call +35 to +44 pT (1980–1989 CE) it held close to six in ten. She had come second in one contest and her bird had begun, quietly, to win every table on the planet.

What she did next was only more of what she had always done, and this is the part the corpus insists on getting right: nothing after the contest required a trick, a lie, or a thing kept quiet. It required selection, closed and measured and relentless, generation on generation — soon run by specialist pedigree houses rather than by any one farm, a bird’s whole lifetime of generations turned over every year, for half a century. Feed did not do it. A trial recovered intact from the durable text harvest settled that: three lines of the same lineage, one held unselected since +12 pT (1957 CE), one since +33 pT (1978 CE), and a working line current as of +60 pT (2005 CE), all raised side by side on identical modern feed. At forty-two days the old 1957 line reached about nine hundred grams. The current line, same feed, same forty-two days, reached better than four kilograms — over four times the live mass, off the same grain, with the genetics alone isolated and made to answer for it. A compounding gain near 3.3 percent added to the growth curve every year for nearly fifty years, and the share of that greater bird carried on the breast — the cut she had been reading with her thumbs — up by close to eighty percent on the same run, selected for, not merely carried along. The specification was met. It went on being met, faster than she could have wanted it, long after her hands were out of it.

Two lines came out of that final. Two firms grew from the two lines, and between them they came to hold on the order of seven to nine birds in every ten of the world’s commercial parent stock — a heritable trait narrowed onto two bloodlines, in one decade, decided at one contest, by people who wrote the narrowing down and published it. It is, the systems reading has it, the old stud-farm problem run at the scale of a planet: an empire’s whole cavalry tracing back to a handful of prize stallions at a few royal studs, a single bad season at any one of them a matter of state. The narrowing was never hidden. The trade press printed the market-share figures every year, plainly, the way a stud’s own steward posts a stallion’s covering book, and nobody with the standing to widen the pool ever found the risk worth what widening it would cost in yield. A bird now numbering in the tens of billions descended from a shortlist settled in three seasons. It cannot forage its own way to slaughter weight, and it was never asked to.

The body she had been breeding toward is the body the ground gives back. It is heavy and it is unfinished, both at once, in a pairing the discipline had not catalogued in any earlier bird: a leg bone from a six-week carcass can run close to double the breadth of the same bone in a fully grown wild junglefowl, the ancestor the whole lineage still shares nearly its entire genome with, while every marker of maturity in it reads as a juvenile’s — porous, open at the growth plate, grown for size before it was ever grown for strength. A twist at the base of the neck vertebrae, absent from the wild stock and from every domestic line before this one, runs through a small measurable fraction of the birds from the egg. For a scholarly generation the deposits of this bone — oversized, porous, often malformed, and dug overwhelmingly out of what had been landfill — were read as a recurring death of the young. The correction came from the chemistry, not the shape: a carbon signature of a diet built overwhelmingly on one grain, maize, unlike the mixed feed every earlier domestic chicken carried in its own tissue, showed these were not dead children at all but adults, slaughtered at market weight while their skeletons were, by every other measure, a juvenile’s still. They survive because the landfill that took them was anoxic, and anoxia does not rot collagen so much as arrest it — the routine discard of the age’s most successful engineered animal, kept without anyone meaning to keep it. It is the single most common vertebrate bone in the type-locality core at Crawford Lake, and it is the type fossil by which a geological interval is now dated.

She is, in the record, the pair of thumbs and the culling and the cards, inferred from a bone she never saw and could not have imagined the quantity of. She wanted a heavier bird by the next spring, and she got one, and so did everyone after her. By the interval’s last decades the number of them alive on the planet at any single instant ran to some twenty-five thousand million, and the living weight of all of them together, set against every wild bird of every wild species still in the world, came to roughly three times theirs.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. What physically survives is the bone — a Crawford population of the Gallus Horizon, kept by the same anoxic landfill that arrests collagen rather than lets it rot (Osei’s surplus that survives).¹ The contest, the two winning crosses, the two-firm hold on the world’s parent stock, and the three-generation growth trial are record, recovered from the Code Deposit’s technical-text harvest;² the breeder and her seasons are inference from the shape those records make, and offered as such. The physiology is transcribed, not invented: the isolated, feed-controlled genetic gain in mass at forty-two days, the ~3.3 percent compound annual growth-rate gain, the near-doubled leg-bone breadth at skeletal immaturity, the diagnostic cervical twist, and the maize-diet carbon signature that overturned the juvenile-die-off reading.³ The narrowing of one heritable trait onto two bloodlines is read after Ibarra as single-filament infrastructure extended from a supply line to a gene.⁴
