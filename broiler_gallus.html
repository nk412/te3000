Broiler Gallus

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

Broiler Gallus is the fast-growing, short-lived domestic fowl bred, from the mid-twentieth century, for meat yield and market age alone, and it is, by both standing population and combined mass, the most numerous bird this planet has carried at any point in its history. It is not a distinct species from the wild ancestor it shares nearly its whole genome with, and it is not, despite an early and long-uncorrected reading of its own bones, evidence of a mass death of the young. For the reaction that fed it at industrial scale, see Industrial Nitrogen Fixation. For the stratigraphic horizon it names and dates, see Combustion Age.

At any moment across the interval’s final decades, on the order of 25 × 10⁹ of these birds were alive on the planet at once, and their combined living mass alone is estimated to have run to roughly three times the combined mass of every wild bird species on the planet added together — a standing population that a single reconstructed generation earlier did not exist at all. A bird of this lineage reaches its slaughter weight, several kilograms of it, inside five to seven weeks; the same lineage’s own unselected mid-century stock, raised on an identical modern diet for comparison, reached barely a fifth of that mass in the same six weeks. Its skeleton, recovered from a landfill horizon or a sediment core alike, is oversized for its evident age, porous where a mature bone should have finished closing, and chemically distinct from every domestic chicken that came before it — a bird bred so heavily toward one trait that this discipline now dates a geological interval by its bones.

The lineage did not arrive by chemistry or accident, the way the fly ash and the plutonium did. It arrived by design, from a coordinated postwar breeding effort that set out, in plain and published terms, to solve a protein shortage with genetics, and that succeeded so completely and so fast that the industry it created has never since needed to ask the same question twice. This is one of the interval’s genuine triumphs, delivered exactly as specified. It is also, read from a thousand years on, the clearest surviving case in the corpus of a single-filament dependency built entirely on purpose, by people who filed the risk they were building and then filed it away.

A schedule bred into bone

A controlled comparison run late in the interval — a line held genetically unselected since 1957, a second held unselected since 1978, and a current commercial line as of 2005, all raised side by side on identical modern feed — found that the 2005 line reached roughly 4.2 kilograms of live weight at forty-two days old, against 1.8 kilograms for the 1978 line and 0.9 kilograms for the 1957 line raised under the same conditions. The comparison isolated genetics from diet, and it was genetics that did nearly all the work: over four hundred percent more live mass at six weeks old from the same feed, the same schedule, the same forty-two days, a compound rate of roughly 3.3 percent added to the growth curve every single year across nearly half a century — and the proportion of that greater mass sitting on the breast alone, the cut the whole programme was aimed at from its first contest, rose by close to eighty percent on the same run, selected for on top of the weight gain rather than merely carried along by it.

Line (unselected since) pT / CE Live weight at 42 days
1957 stock +12 pT (1957 CE) ≈905 g
1978 stock +33 pT (1978 CE) ≈1,808 g
2005 stock +60 pT (2005 CE) ≈4,202 g

No new feed additive produced this curve and no antibiotic did either; both existed already and neither changed across the comparison. What changed, generation on generation, was which bird was allowed to breed the next one — a closed, relentlessly measured selection on growth rate and breast yield, run by specialist pedigree operations rather than by any individual farm, repeated over roughly a bird’s-lifetime of generations every year for half a century. It worked. The specification called for more meat, faster, from the same grain, and by every measure this discipline can still check against a controlled modern trial, the specification was met in full. Nothing about the curve above required deception, concealment, or an error anyone later had to explain away; it is simply engineering, applied to a vertebrate, and it is one of the few places in this corpus where the record of the age’s own competence needs no correcting at all.

Two winning birds, one filament

The programme that set this curve running began as a public competition, staged in three rounds — state heats across 1946, regional judging in 1947, a national final in 1948 (+1 to +3 pT) at a mid-Atlantic agricultural experiment station — organised by a national grocery retailer working with government breeding scientists and volunteer judges recruited across more than forty jurisdictions, in open pursuit of a bird carrying, in the contest’s own published brief, “a breast like a turkey’s.” A cross developed on the American West Coast won the national final outright; a cross bred by a Connecticut smallholding family placed immediately behind it. Both lines were commercialised within the following decade. By the interval’s own count, descendants of the winning cross sired something on the order of six in ten broilers raised in the country that had staged the contest within eleven years of the final judging; the runner-up’s breeding operation held roughly a quarter of the entire market for parent breeding stock within a few years of the same contest and something like six in ten of it by the 1980s (+35–44 pT).

Two firms, tracing their pedigree stock to those two winning crosses, went on to consolidate the majority of the world’s commercial broiler genetics between them — published industry estimates put their combined share of the global market for parent breeding stock at anywhere from seven to nine birds in every ten by the interval’s later decades, a concentration one of the two firms deepened further, within the interval’s final working decade, by acquiring a smaller surviving competitor outright. It is the Bronze Age’s stud-farm problem, run at planetary scale: an army’s entire cavalry, in the deep past, could trace itself to a handful of prize stallions held at a small number of royal studs, and a single lost season at any one stud was a strategic event rather than a livestock one. Extending her established model of infrastructure narrowed to one physical or genetic filament, Ibarra reads the postwar broiler programme as the model’s cleanest case yet: not a supply chain concentrated by accident onto one port or one plant, but a heritable trait concentrated by design onto two bloodlines, in a single decade, at one contest, on purpose.

The concentration was never hidden. The industry’s own trade press published the market-share figures candidly, year on year, the way a stud farm’s own steward would have published a stallion’s covering book; nobody with the standing to diversify the pedigree pool ever found the risk worth the yield it would have cost. A species now numbering in the tens of billions, at any hour of any day, descends overwhelmingly from a shortlist decided at one regional contest three-quarters of a century into the interval — a bird bred, with total success, to convert grain into meat at a rate nothing before it had ever approached, and specified, in the same decisions, for nothing at all beyond that one trait. It cannot forage its own way to slaughter weight, and it was never asked to.

What the bone will not lie about

The skeleton this breeding produced is not merely large; it is large and unfinished, in a combination this discipline had not previously catalogued in any bird. A recovered tibiotarsus from a six-week-old bird of this lineage can show a distal breadth close to double that of a fully grown wild Red Junglefowl — the ancestral species this lineage still shares almost its whole genome with — while remaining, by every ossification marker this discipline reads in bone, structurally immature: porous, underdeveloped at the growth plate, built for size before it finished being built for strength. A distinct malformation of the neck vertebrae, absent from the ancestral stock and from every earlier domestic line, appears in a small but measurable fraction of the lineage from birth and turns up more often still among embryos that never hatched at all — a skeletal signature specific enough that a single recovered cervical vertebra, with no other context at all, can be assigned to this lineage and no other.

For roughly a scholarly generation after the first substantial recoveries, isolated deposits of this bone — oversized, porous, frequently malformed, and recovered overwhelmingly from what were once landfill horizons rather than from any burial context — were catalogued as evidence of a recurring mass death among juvenile birds of an otherwise unremarkable domestic species. The correction came from the bone chemistry rather than the bone shape: the isotopic signature of a diet built overwhelmingly on a single grain crop, distinct from the varied, mixed diet every domestic chicken before this lineage carried in its own tissue, showed the birds were not a die-off of the young at all, but a routine slaughter of a lineage engineered to reach adult market weight while its skeleton was still, in every other domestic bird this discipline knows, a juvenile’s.

The deposits survived for the same reason the misreading did: an anoxic landfill does not so much decay an interred bone as arrest it, preserving the collagen and, in places, the attached soft tissue along with the porous architecture that once suggested immaturity. Extending her established interest in this lineage’s typology, Osei coins the surplus that survives: an archive made, without any curatorial intention whatsoever, out of the routine discard of the age’s most successful engineered animal, in a burial environment built for waste and not for memory, which is nonetheless the reason this discipline can hold the bone at all. The horizon’s own boundary vote heard the bone twice — once, provisionally, among the period’s own geologists in 2024 CE (+79 pT), and once, conclusively, before the Terran Stratigraphic Commission in 2412 — as corroborating typology rather than as the primary dating tool, that role belonging throughout to the fly-ash and plutonium signal running alongside it. Corroboration was enough. The vote did not need the bone to carry it, only to agree with it, and by 2412 it did.

A single cervical vertebra, recovered from a sediment core at the horizon’s own type locality and catalogued, with several hundred more from the same stratum, into what this discipline terms a Crawford population, still carries the diagnostic twist at its base. It is not a rare find. It is the single most common vertebrate bone in the core.
