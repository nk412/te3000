Industrial Nitrogen Fixation

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the light-nitrogen line in still-water sediment and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

Industrial nitrogen fixation is the catalytic synthesis of ammonia from atmospheric nitrogen and hydrogen, the single reaction that came to supply a large fraction of the Combustion Age’s food and, in one season and without a valve moved, the explosives that extended its largest war. The pan-hemispheric sediment isotope curve and its dates are the transcribed record; the works, the closed sea, and the redirect are reconstruction read against where the curve bends.

Industrial nitrogen fixation is the fixing of atmospheric dinitrogen into ammonia at works scale — the tamest sentence in chemistry, and among the hardest ever made true. Two nitrogen atoms in the air hold to one another by a triple bond of about 941 kilojoules to the mole, one of the strongest in ordinary matter; before the reaction, the only agents on the planet that broke it in quantity were lightning and a narrow guild of root-dwelling bacteria. From −32 pT (1913 CE) a bed of iron did it on demand, by the tonne, at a chosen temperature and pressure.

The reaction displaced the single mined source that had fed the world’s fields — a nitrate desert supplying about four-fifths of world trade, the ground itself the object of a war a generation before. On the outbreak of a naval blockade in −31 pT (1914 CE) the same plant, unmodified, was turned from fertiliser to explosives feedstock, and a war otherwise headed for a nitrate-shortage collapse inside a year ran four years more. After the war it scaled to some 150–180 Mt of ammonia a year; by the interval’s second half, published estimates placed two-fifths to one-half of all people then alive as dependent on it for the nitrogen in their food.

The reaction feeds and kills by the same warmth, the difference between the two lying downstream and out of its sight — a matter of which pipe the ammonia left by. It is read, at a millennium’s remove, from neither ledger nor works — both gone with nearly all the age’s writing — but from the isotope line the reaction wrote into the mud of every still lake that would hold a layer.

The reaction

The synthesis combines one part atmospheric nitrogen with three parts hydrogen and closes to two parts ammonia — N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ — releasing about 92 kilojoules for every mole formed. The reaction is exothermic and reversible, so it is favoured by pressure and by cool; but at cool the rate is nil, and the working compromise is a hot, high-pressure vessel held at 400–500 °C and 150–300 atmospheres, the steel poured to hold the pressure without going brittle at the heat.

What breaks the 941 kJ/mol bond is a floor of iron. The catalyst is grown from crushed magnetite reduced in place, its fine crystallites propped against their own slumping by a wash of alumina and handed a small generosity of electrons by a trace of potash — an ugly chemistry that no one claimed was elegant, and that a full century of search did not improve upon: with the age long over, nothing better had been found to put in its place. The trouble had never been the wanting. The air is four parts in five nitrogen; a body breathes a whole life of fertiliser and starves in a field of it, because the bond does not open at any temperature a plant can reach. What the works did was teach the strongest bond in the ordinary world to come apart on a bed of rust, at a chosen convenience, and it did so the first year and every year after.

From a mined desert to a reactor

Before the works there had been one desert. For the better part of a century the reactive nitrogen a whole planet’s agriculture could buy came, mostly, through a single door: a rainless coastal basin whose caliche was scraped and shipped the long way round a distant cape, one country holding perhaps four parts in five of the world’s traded nitrate by about −50 pT (1895 CE). The same salt went to the field and to the shell-filler both, out of the same pits and in the same hulls — bread and powder drawn from one ground. The ground itself had already been fought over: a war of some four years, −66 to −62 pT (1879–1883 CE), a full generation before the reaction existed and longer before the shortage it would one day answer. It was the oldest shape in trade — one thing, essential, from one place, on one thin line of ships (single-filament supply, Ibarra).

The reaction removed the desert as a constraint. Run at works scale from −32 pT (1913 CE), it made nitrogen out of the open air, which no distance and no cape could ration. What had been a mined monopoly became a manufactured commodity, and the desert’s near-total share of world trade fell away inside a generation.

The redirect

The reaction had run at true works scale for barely a year and a season when a naval blockade in −31 pT (1914 CE) closed the lane to the desert. The nitrate had fed the fields; it had also made the nitric acid, and the nitric acid the propellant and the burst, and there was no other industrial source of either. On a sober reckoning the war that had just begun should have ended inside a year for want of the one salt — and ended for every party the same way and at the same time, there being not enough fixed nitrogen left in the country to kill with.

The works did not let the war end. Ammonia oxidises to nitric acid by a further, well-known step, and the plant simply took it; the turning cost nothing — not a valve moved, not a grain of the catalyst changed, not one word of the tame sentence rewritten. The nitrogen that would have gone under a field went into a shell instead, and the reaction, which knows nothing, ran on exactly as warm. No one recorded the substitution as strange, because by the only measures a plant is given — was it running, was it clean, was it warm — nothing about it had changed. The war ran four more years.

What did not stay on the field

When the sea opened the works turned back to what it was built for and scaled as no desert could, to some 150–180 Mt of ammonia a year by the interval’s later decades, about four-fifths of it bound for the field. The cost of the hydrogen was paid in gas drawn from the ground and a share of the age’s carbon put into the air by a reaction whose own product carried none — on the order of 1–2% of all the primary energy the age burned, and 3–5% of its natural gas, spent on the one salt.

Half the nitrogen laid on a field is taken up by the crop. The other half leaves — down the water table, up as gas, out to the river mouths — and gathers, at scale, into water with the oxygen gone out of it: a eutrophic dead zone off a well-fed delta, running from single-digit thousands of square kilometres at a single river’s mouth to about 70,000 km² in one enclosed northern sea, the same basin that holds the Bornholm Deposit by an unrelated toxin. The gas that leaves upward is nitrous oxide, stable and long-lived and a quiet agent against the high ozone; by the age’s own late accounting it was the largest ozone-depleting substance still rising when the record thins, and no one ever wrote the treaty that would have covered it. All of it was published, in the age’s own journals and in its own hand, and none of it moved a valve.

The number

What the reaction made of the people who ate off the field is a matter of arithmetic the works was never asked to price. Published estimates place two-fifths to one-half of the global population, at any date after the mid-twentieth century, as dependent on synthesised nitrogen for the nitrogen in their food; and the population so fed did the one thing no other animal’s numbers had done, rising with a chemical yield rather than against a natural supply, held up by the tonnage because the tonnage was, at the bottom of everything, the food.

The record that keeps this count is not a ledger and not a works. Both are gone, with nearly all the age’s writing. It is the mud. The nitrogen the reaction fixes is a shade lighter than the nitrogen the bacteria fix — the fifteen against the fourteen, a hair’s difference in the weight of the atom — and that lightness rained out of the shared sky into the bottom of every still lake that would hold a layer: a line that begins to shift within a decade of the desert losing its monopoly and steepens hard across the years the works multiplied, on every continent, no basin joined to any other except through the air they all breathed. The mud kept the count the paper did not. And the count is this: by the age’s second half, something close to half of the nitrogen atoms in a living human body had passed, at least once, through a reactor of the kind that had, a generation before, fed a war.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The transcribed record is the isotopic one: a lightening of the ¹⁵N:¹⁴N ratio in lake-bottom sediment across the Northern Hemisphere, onset c. −50 pT (1895 CE) and steepening through −35 to −15 pT (1910–1930 CE) — a durable clock that survives in the mud, independent of the written record the Attrition took (Petrosyan). The desert monopoly, the blockade, and the redirect are reconstruction, read against the interval’s one great war. The chemistry is real and unaltered: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, ΔH ≈ −92 kJ/mol, over a magnetite-derived iron catalyst promoted with K₂O and Al₂O₃ at 400–500 °C and 150–300 atm, breaking an N≡N bond of ≈941 kJ/mol. First run at industrial scale −32 pT (1913 CE); redirected without modification from fertiliser to explosives feedstock on a blockade in −31 pT (1914 CE), extending a war otherwise headed for nitrate-shortage collapse by some four years; postwar output ≈150–180 Mt ammonia/yr; two-fifths to one-half of the population dependent for its food’s nitrogen. The dead are not counted here, and no collapse is claimed.
