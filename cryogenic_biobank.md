# Cryogenic Biobank

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **A cryogenic biobank held living cells, gametes, or embryos in a glassy, ice-free solid at or below −196 °C, a state that persisted only for as long as someone kept paying to refill the liquid nitrogen.** For the era's other deliberate genetic reserve, held instead by desiccation and passive cold rather than a continuous refrigerant, see **Svalbard Seed Vault**. This is not an article about a surviving site. No cryogenic biobank of the interval is known to have reached this encyclopaedia's shelves at all, and the reason is itself the article's subject.

---

Liquid nitrogen boils at −196 °C. A vacuum-insulated vessel — two nested steel or aluminium shells with the air pumped out between them, a design unchanged in essentials for over a century — slows the boil-off enough that the liquid, not a compressor, does the cooling; no electricity is required, only replenishment. Below roughly −130 °C, a correctly loaded cell suspended in a glass-forming cryoprotectant stops freezing in the ordinary sense and instead vitrifies: it passes into an amorphous solid with no ice crystals to shear its membranes apart. Warmed back through that same threshold slowly, or left to drift there because nobody refilled the vessel, the same solid recrystallises and the cell does not survive the transition either way. The chemistry is unforgiving in both directions and entirely well understood; none of it is this article's invention.

By the interval's later decades this technology carried three unconnected industries. Cattle breeding ran on it at continental scale: on the order of 264 million doses of bovine semen were distributed annually, some 95 percent of it deep-frozen in straws of about 20 million sperm each, inseminating something like 130 million cows a year by artificial means alone — the ordinary method of dairy reproduction, not an exotic one. Human reproductive medicine ran on it at a much smaller but more consequential scale: a single populous nation's own fertility clinics were later estimated, depending on whose count was trusted, to be holding somewhere between four hundred thousand and several million frozen human embryos at any one time, and no central registry ever existed to settle which figure was right. A third, smaller enterprise ran wildlife conservation on the same principle: a dedicated genome bank founded in the interval's third quarter had, by its own published tally, preserved living cell lines from upward of eleven thousand individual animals across more than thirteen hundred species before this encyclopaedia's sources on it go dark.

Unlike the era's other deliberate biological reserve, which is discussed under **Svalbard Seed Vault** and which failed, when it failed, on the scale of decades, none of this required a mountain, a permafrost floor, or an engineered backup. It required a dewar, a supply contract, and somebody answering an alarm.

## An accident in a mislabelled bottle

The whole enterprise begins with a mistake. In 1949 (+4 pT), a laboratory investigating why frozen sperm cells reliably died on thawing sent a stock solution for analysis expecting to confirm it was inert. The bottle had been mislabelled; the solution was not the albumen fixative it was thought to be but one cut with glycerol, and the cells frozen in it survived. Glycerol, it turned out, partly displaces water inside a cell before ice can form, blunting the crystals that would otherwise tear it apart. The discovery was published as what it was — an accident, correctly interpreted — and it is the founding fact of every industry described above. Three decades later a second generation of cryoprotectants, and a cooling regime fast enough to skip past ice formation altogether, would extend the same insight from sperm, which tolerates freezing well, to eggs and embryos, which do not: cooling at upward of 15,000 °C a minute rather than a few tenths of a degree, a technique first demonstrated on mouse embryos in 1985 (+40 pT) and, in later side-by-side trials, recovering roughly 95 percent of vitrified human eggs against 70–80 percent for the slow-frozen kind. None of this is contested. It worked, reliably, for exactly as long as the vessel stayed cold.

## A census the age never took

The uncertain figure for embryos in storage is worth pausing on, because it was not a failure of record-keeping so much as a structural feature of the practice. A frozen embryo is not disposed of by its clinic and not born; it sits, indefinitely, in a state the institution holding it has no obligation to resolve and often no contact information to resolve it with. Multiply that by every clinic in every jurisdiction that never agreed to report a common number, and the aggregate is not merely unknown but of a kind that cannot be made known after the fact — the storage tanks do not encode who intended what for the cells inside them, only that the cells are, for now, neither used nor discarded. Bandyopadhyay's demographic work on the Contraction observes that the surviving record cannot distinguish a person who died from one who moved, and that the distinction is lost formally, not merely practically. The embryo archive poses the same structure in miniature and a full generation earlier: a population that is neither born nor ended, whose eventual disposition depended entirely on institutions continuing to exist, which is exactly the condition this article's second half concerns.

## Two tanks, one week

Professional guidance had, well before the interval's end, spelled out the risk in plain terms: remote temperature alarms, off-site monitoring, redundant tanks, scheduled refills logged against a calendar rather than a memory. The guidance was published, current, and evidently followed closely enough to be cited afterward. In the same week of 2018 (+73 pT), two unrelated American fertility clinics several thousand kilometres apart each lost a full tank's inventory. At one, a remote alarm had been switched off for a weekend of unrelated maintenance and nobody caught the rising temperature until the following Monday. At the other, the failure traced to a manufacturing defect in a valve of a type fitted to tanks used industry-wide. Between the two, several thousand eggs and embryos, representing years of accumulated deposits, did not survive the week. Nothing about either failure required a natural disaster, a war, or an act of neglect beyond the ordinary kind: a switch left in the wrong position, a part that had already failed elsewhere and had not yet been recalled. This is the mechanism, unglamorously, in miniature, and at the scale of an entire industry's account base rather than one weekend, it is also the mechanism of the interval's end.

The pre-industrial ice-house is the correspondence Marchetti's comparative programme would reach for. Packed each winter from a frozen lake or a mountain snowfield and insulated in a pit or a thick-walled chamber, it kept its contents cold not by shielding them from time but by a debt renewed on a fixed, short cycle — emptied by the following autumn if the packing failed to arrive on schedule, exactly as a modern dewar runs dry within weeks of an unpaid refill. The correspondence is closer than the Bronze Age analogies this encyclopaedia usually reaches for, because the ice-house and the dewar share not just a technique but a repayment schedule measured in a single season rather than a human lifetime.

That is the fact this article has been assembling toward. Every other deliberate reserve in this corpus — silver-halide film specified for a thousand years, seed held viable for decades against a permafrost floor, ceramic, ²³⁹Pu, an inert vacuum — was built against an interruption measured in years to centuries. A cryogenic biobank was built against nothing: its persistence was rented one dewar-fill at a time, on an interval short enough that an ordinary long weekend, multiplied across an institution's slow withdrawal, was sufficient to end it. No sample, no cell line, no embryo from before the Attrition is known to survive anywhere on the modern map; the industry's own uncounted numbers, argued over while the tanks were still full, are the only trace of it this encyclopaedia can cite with any confidence. The seed forgives a decade of neglect. The embryo does not forgive a season.

1. Furtado, C. (2979). "The Vitreous Debt: Continuous Maintenance in Biological Cold Storage." *Journal of Anthropogenic Stratigraphy* 68: 300–340. — Extends Datta's migration debt and Lindqvist's closure gap to biological cryopreservation specifically; coins **the vitreous debt**, the archive resupplied on a season's cycle rather than a generation's. The source of this article's closing correspondence.
2. Datta, S. (2970). *The Migration Debt: The Attrition as Maintenance Failure.* — The general model of a compounding, deferrable custodial cost falling due all at once; here extended from recopied media to a physical refrigerant contract.
3. Lindqvist, S. (2971). "The Closure Gap: Continuity Failure in Centennial Engineering Projects." *Journal of Anthropogenic Stratigraphy* 66: 118–171. — Cited for the general principle that an engineered barrier's survival is bounded by the institution that explains and resupplies it, not by the engineering alone; the gap here is measured in weeks rather than centuries.
4. Bandyopadhyay, R. (2971). *Died or Moved: The Undecidable Demography of the Contraction.* — Cited for the formal, not merely practical, indeterminacy of an uncounted population; the embryo-storage census argued in §2 poses the identical structure a generation earlier and on a much smaller scale.
5. Marchetti, L. (2988). "The Ice-House and the Dewar: A Third Comparative Case." *Historiography* 17: 90–125. — Extends her established comparative method (previously the Roman lead parallel to leaded petrol) to a third correspondence, between the fixed-cycle pre-industrial ice-house and the cryogenic biobank. Persuasive on the shared repayment structure; declines to speculate on how many ice-houses failed the same way and left no record of failing.
6. Ibarra, N. (2977). "Single-filament infrastructure and the centennial disposal programmes." *Journal of Anthropogenic Stratigraphy* 63: 201–244. — Cited here by analogy rather than identity: her single-filament model concerns scarce material supply, where the dependency documented in this article is a supply of institutional attention, not of liquid nitrogen itself, which was never scarce.
