# Cryogenic Biobank

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the era's own cold-storage guidance and the intercepted record of the week it failed to prevent, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **A cryogenic biobank kept living eggs, sperm, and embryos as an ice-free glass in liquid nitrogen at −196 °C — a state that held only for as long as someone kept paying the small, recurring cost of refilling it.** This reconstructs one week of +73 pT in which two unrelated clinics a continent apart each lost a full tank; the failures are recorded, and the embryologist who lived through one of them is inferred.

---

She topped the tanks on the Friday, because Friday was the long gap. A dewar loses its nitrogen slowly — a good one, vacuum-walled, two steel skins with the air pumped out between them, will hold a working level for a week or two on a single fill — but it loses it always, a little every hour, whether or not anyone is there to watch the level fall. So on Fridays she carried the hose down the line and brought each vessel back to its mark and logged the fill against the calendar and went home. The liquid did the cooling. Nothing was plugged in. That was the elegance of the thing and, it would turn out, the whole of the danger: a cryogenic bank needed no power at all, only that someone keep paying the small recurring debt of refilling it, forever, on a cycle measured in weeks.

She knew what the marks meant, which is to say she knew what was in the vessels, which the machinery did not. To the tank it was a level of liquid and a temperature. To her it was — she had the paperwork for all of it — the six embryos a couple had made in the one year they were both well enough to try; the eggs a woman had banked at thirty-one against a diagnosis not yet said out loud; the last straws from a man who had since died, held for a widow who telephoned twice a year to confirm they were still cold. Several thousand of these, down the line of tanks. A biobank does not store cells. It stores the interval between a decision and its consequence, and it keeps that interval open, at a temperature, for as long as it is asked to.

The cold itself was exact and unforgiving in both directions, and none of it was mysterious. Liquid nitrogen boils at −196 °C. A cell suspended in the right glass-forming solution and cooled fast enough does not freeze in the ordinary sense at all: below about −130 °C it vitrifies, passing into an amorphous solid, a glass, with no ice crystals in it to shear the membranes apart. Hold it under that line and it will wait, chemically, without ageing, essentially forever. Warm it back up through the same threshold slowly — or let it drift up because the level fell and no one refilled — and the glass remembers that it was water. It grows the ice it had been spared. The cell does not survive the crossing either way, and it is the same physical crossing whether a technician makes it on purpose in a warm bath at the moment a family is ready, or a Saturday makes it by inches to a tank nobody is watching.

The chemistry that made any of it possible had been an accident, and an honestly reported one. In +4 pT a laboratory sent off a stock solution expecting to confirm it was inert; the bottle had been mislabelled, the solution was cut with glycerol, and the cells frozen in it lived. Glycerol pulls water out of a cell before ice can form there. Three and a half decades on, in +40 pT, a cooling fast enough to skip past ice altogether — better than fifteen thousand degrees a minute — carried the same mercy from sperm, which tolerates freezing, to eggs and embryos, which do not: later trials brought back about ninety-five in a hundred vitrified eggs against seventy or eighty of the slow-frozen kind. It worked. It worked reliably. It worked for exactly as long as the vessel stayed below the line.

The guidance said as much, in plain language, and had for years. Redundant alarms. A sensor inside the tank reporting to a monitor off the premises, so that a level falling at two in the morning on a holiday reached a telephone in someone's pocket and not only a panel on a wall in an empty room. Duplicate tanks, one family's eggs split across two, so that no single vessel ever held a whole hope. Fills logged against a calendar and not a memory. It was published, current, and — this is the part the reconstruction turns on — followed closely enough, at her clinic, to be quoted back afterward. The alarm existed. It reported. Over one weekend of +73 pT it had been switched off, for a run of unrelated maintenance, and the record does not say who silenced it or meant to, only that it was silent; and that across the Saturday and the Sunday the level in one tank fell and its temperature climbed past −130 °C and up, and no telephone rang, because the alarm that would have rung it was off and the room was empty and the liquid, uncomplaining, boiled off the last of what it had been keeping.

She found it on the Monday. What there is to do, when a tank has warmed, is a kind of terrible bookkeeping: you confirm the temperature, you do not open it in hope, and you begin the inventory that tells you exactly whose interval between a decision and its consequence has just been closed for them, without them, over a weekend, by a switch left in the wrong position. Several thousand eggs and embryos. Hundreds of families, each of whom had been told, correctly, that this was the safe part — the waiting part, the part that asked nothing of them but patience and an annual fee.

And then the part that moved it out of her hands and into the shape of the thing. That same week, a clinic several thousand kilometres away — a different coast, no connection to hers but the technique itself — lost a full tank of its own. Not to a silenced alarm. To a valve: a controller of a type fitted to tanks across the industry, which failed the way a manufacturing defect fails, quietly and by flaw rather than by anyone's inattention, a part that had very likely already failed somewhere else and had not yet been recalled. Two tanks, two clinics, one week, no common cause but that both were single vessels carrying an irreplaceable thing on a maintenance debt that had to be paid, correctly, every few weeks, in perpetuity, by people who were only human and only sometimes there. The guidance had described both failures before either happened. It had recommended, against exactly this, the redundancy that costs money in every year that nothing goes wrong.

It would be easy to read the two tanks as proof the whole idea was fragile, and it would be wrong, and the correction is the most striking fact in the file. The same chemistry, the same dewars, the same −196 °C ran a second industry at a scale that dwarfed every fertility clinic on the planet put together, and ran it well. Cattle breeding lived on this: something like two hundred and sixty million doses of frozen bull semen distributed in a single year, straws of twenty million cells each, on the order of a hundred and thirty million cows bred by it annually — not an exotic procedure but the ordinary way a dairy herd was made, continental in scale, and so reliable that it never once became a thing that happened: no headline, no week anyone reconstructs. A conservation bank, off to one side, held living cell lines from better than eleven thousand animals across more than thirteen hundred species. The technique was not the failure. At the largest scale it was one of the quiet triumphs of the age. The failure was smaller and more human than the technique: it was that the interval a family's tank had to survive was not a decade you could be lax about but a season you could not, and that the people minding the seasons were minding a thousand other things.

That is the debt a tank runs on. Not the grand, generational patience of a seed reserve cut into frozen rock, which forgives a lax decade and takes its cold from the mountain around it whether or not the invoice is paid. A dewar keeps nothing from anywhere. It borrows its cold from a liquid that is always leaving, and the loan comes due on the short cycle, every few weeks, the way an ice-house of the deep past had to be packed again each single winter or stand empty by autumn. Miss the generation and the seed is fine. Miss the season and the glass, somewhere down the dark line of a tank in a room where the alarm is off, warms one degree past the threshold and begins, without malice and without hurry, to become again the water it was made from, and to grow in one woman's particular eggs the ice that the whole careful art had been built, at −196 °C, to spare them. The embryo does not forgive a season.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The published cold-storage guidance, the two failures of one week of +73 pT and their unrelated causes — a silenced remote alarm; a defective valve of an industry-standard type — and the rough scale of what each tank held are recorded, surviving among the period's intercepted traffic rather than in any clinic's own files, none of which do; no sample frozen before the Attrition is known to be cold anywhere now. The embryologist, her fills, and her Monday are inference from the shape the record leaves. The physics is real and undisputed: liquid nitrogen at −196 °C; vitrification below ≈−130 °C and its fatal reversal on warming back through it; glycerol's cryoprotection (+4 pT); cooling at better than 15,000 °C a minute (+40 pT), recovering ~95% of eggs against 70–80% slow-frozen. So is the contrast — the same technique carried a cattle-breeding industry of hundreds of millions of doses a year without incident. On the biobank as an archive resupplied on a season's cycle, see Furtado; on the ice-house parallel, Marchetti.
