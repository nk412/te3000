# Cryogenic Biobank

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the era's own published cold-storage guidance and the intercepted incident record of the week it failed to prevent, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **A cryogenic biobank was a facility that held living cells, gametes, and embryos indefinitely as an ice-free glass suspended in liquid nitrogen at −196 °C — a state kept by no current and no machine, only by the recurring act of refilling the vessel.** The mechanism and the two documented tank losses of one week are recorded; the count of what each vessel held is inferred.

---

A cryogenic biobank stored not cells but an interval: the span between a reproductive decision and its consequence, held open at temperature for as long as someone kept paying to hold it open. The physics required no power. A vacuum-walled vessel — two steel skins with the air pumped out between them — lost its liquid nitrogen slowly, a working fill lasting a week or two, but lost it always, whether or not anyone watched the level fall. Nothing was plugged in; the boiling liquid did the cooling. That was the whole of the elegance and, it emerged, the whole of the exposure: the bank needed no electricity at all, only that the small recurring cost of refilling be met, on a cycle of weeks, in perpetuity.

The scholarship had a name for the arrangement. Datta's migration debt reads the Attrition as a recopying cost deferred until the institution carrying it lapses; Furtado extended the model to biological cold storage and named the result **the vitreous debt** — an archive resupplied not on a generation's cycle but on a season's. Three unconnected industries carried that debt by the interval's later decades. The record shows it discharged with near-perfect fidelity at continental scale, and lost, across a single week of +73 pT, at two unrelated clinics a continent apart, each of which had followed the published guidance closely enough to quote it back afterward.

Some read the two lost tanks as evidence that the technique was inherently fragile. Against that reading stands the scale at which, in the same years, on the same chemistry and the same vessels, it did not fail. Neither reading is disputed on the facts; they differ only on which figure is taken to be the representative one. No cryogenic biobank sample from before the Attrition is known to be cold anywhere now.

## The vessel

The cold was exact and unforgiving in both directions, and none of it was mysterious. Liquid nitrogen boils at −196 °C. A cell suspended in a glass-forming solution and cooled fast enough does not freeze in the ordinary sense at all: below roughly −130 °C, its glass-transition threshold, it passes into an amorphous solid — a glass, with no ice crystals in it to shear the membranes — and there it waits, chemically, without ageing. Held under that line it will keep, in principle, indefinitely. Warmed back up through the same threshold, whether deliberately in a bath at the moment a family is ready or by inches because the level fell and no one refilled, the glass grows the ice it had been spared, and the cell does not survive the crossing. It is the same physical crossing either way.

The chemistry was found by accident and honestly reported. In +4 pT (1949 CE) a laboratory froze cells in a stock solution it believed inert; the bottle had been mislabelled and cut with glycerol, which draws water out of a cell before ice can form there, and the cells lived — the first demonstrated cryoprotectant. Three and a half decades on, in +40 pT (1985 CE), a cooling rate fast enough to skip ice formation entirely — better than 15,000 °C a minute — carried the same protection from sperm, which tolerates freezing, to eggs and embryos, which do not. Later trials recovered about 95 in 100 vitrified oocytes against 70 to 80 of the slow-frozen kind. The technique worked, and worked reliably, for exactly as long as the vessel stayed below the line.

## Maintenance

The maintenance was procedural and had been specified in plain language for years. Because a dewar borrows its cold from a liquid that is always leaving, the fill fell due on a short cycle: a level topped on one week reached its mark and began, that same hour, to fall toward the next fill. The guidance current across the field recommended redundancy against the interval. Redundant alarms. A sensor inside the tank reporting to a monitor off the premises, so that a level falling at two in the morning on a holiday reached a telephone and not only a panel on a wall in an empty room. Duplicate tanks, one family's material split across two, so that no single vessel held a whole hope. Fills logged against a calendar rather than a memory.

What each vessel held was not legible to the vessel. To the tank it was a level of liquid and a temperature; the record it was kept against described eggs banked against a diagnosis, the last straws of a man since dead, the embryos a couple had made in the year both were well enough to try. Several thousand such units could sit down a single line of tanks. In one populous nation, with no reconciling registry to confirm the figure, the clinics were estimated to hold somewhere between 400,000 and several million frozen embryos at any one time. The maintenance that stood between that inventory and its loss was a refill, correctly performed, every few weeks, by people who were only sometimes present and minding many other things.

## The two failures

Two unrelated clinics a continent apart each lost a full tank across the same week of +73 pT (2018 CE). At one, the off-site alarm had been switched off for a run of unrelated maintenance; the record does not say who silenced it, only that it was silent, and that across a weekend the level in one tank fell, its temperature climbed past −130 °C and up, and no telephone rang, because the alarm that would have rung it was off and the room was empty. At the other, the cause was a controller of a type fitted to tanks across the industry, which failed by manufacturing defect rather than by inattention — a part that had very likely already failed elsewhere and had not yet been recalled. Two tanks, two clinics, one week, no common cause but that both were single vessels carrying an irreplaceable inventory on a debt due every few weeks in perpetuity. Each held several thousand eggs and embryos; each stood for hundreds of families, each of whom had been told, correctly, that this was the safe part — the waiting part, the part that asked nothing of them but patience and an annual fee. The guidance had described both failure modes, by name, before either occurred, and had recommended against exactly them the redundancy that costs money in every year nothing goes wrong.

## Scale

The same chemistry, the same dewars, and the same −196 °C ran a second industry at a scale that dwarfed every fertility clinic on the planet combined, and ran it without incident. Cattle breeding lived on it: on the order of 264 million doses of frozen bovine semen distributed in a single year, some 95% of it deep-frozen, roughly 130 million cattle bred by it annually — not an exotic procedure but the ordinary way a dairy herd was made, continental in scale and reliable enough that it never once became an event anyone reconstructs. A conservation genome bank, founded in the interval's third quarter, held living cell lines from more than 11,000 individuals across more than 1,300 species. The vessel was not the point of failure. At the largest scale it was one of the quiet competences of the age.

The failure was smaller than the technique and lay in the cycle. Unlike the desiccated, passively cold **Svalbard Seed Vault**, which takes its cold from the permafrost around it and forgives a lax decade, a dewar keeps nothing on its own; it borrows its cold from a liquid that is always leaving, and the loan comes due on the short cycle. Marchetti set the arrangement beside the fixed-cycle pre-industrial ice-house, which had to be packed again each single winter or stand empty by autumn: not a barrier against time but a debt renewed each season. That is the debt a tank ran on. Miss the generation and the seed keeps. Miss the season and the glass, somewhere down a dark line of tanks in a room where the alarm is off, warms one degree past the threshold and becomes again the water it was made from. The seed forgives a decade; the embryo does not forgive a season.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The published cold-storage guidance, the two tank losses of one week of +73 pT and their unrelated causes — a silenced off-site alarm; a defective valve of an industry-standard type — and the rough scale of what each vessel held survive among the period's intercepted traffic rather than in any clinic's own files, none of which do. The physics is real and undisputed: liquid nitrogen at −196 °C; vitrification below the ≈−130 °C glass transition and its fatal reversal on warming back through it; glycerol's cryoprotection (+4 pT); cooling at better than 15,000 °C a minute (+40 pT), recovering ~95% of oocytes against 70–80% slow-frozen. So is the counterpoint — the same technique carried a cattle-breeding industry of hundreds of millions of doses a year, and a wildlife bank of thousands of cell lines, without recorded loss. No cryogenic biobank sample predating the Attrition is known to survive. On the biobank as an archive resupplied on a season's cycle, see Furtado; on the ice-house parallel, Marchetti.
