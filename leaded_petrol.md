# Leaded Petrol

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a surviving press notice of the autumn deaths at the tidal-flat works and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **On the process floor of the works where a lethal fuel additive was first made at full scale, in the autumn its own makers began to die of it — a poison already filed as a poison, killing in open view of a press that printed the count.** The toll, the diagnosis, and the conference the following spring are record; the man on the line is reconstruction, and the number the ice kept is real.

---

The fluid came off the line the colour of a wound, and that was on purpose.

They dyed it red so that no one could take it for water or for fuel or for anything a man might pour without thinking, and the men who made it moved all shift through a faint sweet smell, oily and not unpleasant, that settled into the hair and the backs of the hands and did not wash out with one washing. On the flat the wind came off the harbour and the works ran day and night into its second month at full rate, and the man on the blending floor had stopped, some nights, being able to sleep, and had not yet made anything of that.

He knew the recipe better than the reason for it. A thimble of the bright liquid in a whole tank of motor fuel stopped the knock — the second, unasked-for detonation that had rung engine blocks like struck bells and had capped, for twenty years, how hard a cylinder could be squeezed and so how far a fuel could be made to go. But the lead compound alone, burned, left a grey oxide that caked the valves and fouled the plugs inside a few hundred miles; so the fluid was cut, three parts in five the heavy sweet liquid and the rest two lighter ones whose entire task was to meet the lead as it burned and carry it, on their bromine and their chlorine, out of the engine as a salt light enough to leave with the exhaust. The men called the two lighter liquids the scavengers and did not think past the word. What the recipe did, in plain terms, was see to it that the lead did not stay in the one place anyone had reason to look for it. It was built to leave. He did not have that phrase. He had the mixing valves, and the red fluid going heavy into drums, and the knowledge that it went out on rail to tanks he would never see and out of pipes on roads he would never drive.

The first sign was always the sleep, and after the sleep the hands.

A man on the packing floor had begun that week to brush at his sleeves. At the air. At things that landed on his forearms and were not there — pale things, he said, settling and lifting and settling, and would they not get them off him. The others had a name for the building before the physicians had a name for what was in it. They called it the House of Butterflies, because that was what a man saw near the end: insects, soft and light-coloured, alighting on his skin in a room where nothing flew. It was not a cruel name. It was the kindest available account of what they watched happen, which was that a man they had eaten beside stopped sleeping, and then shook, and then saw the butterflies, and then had to be held.

Five died on the flat inside a month. They went the same way, in order — the insomnia, the tremor, the seeing, and then a violence that took several men to contain, so that the last of it was straps and a locked room and a body working against them until it stopped. Men were dying that same autumn at two other plants inland, making the same fluid to the same recipe, so that the season's dead across the three came to about a dozen, with dozens more who lived and did not come right again. The state's examiner wrote the cause without softening it: acute organic-lead poisoning, a madness with a metal at the bottom of it. None of it was hidden. It was in the daily papers, plainly, with the number of the dead set beside the name of the thing that killed them.

The thing that killed them had been written down as a killer three years before anyone thought to put it in a fuel tank. Not by the men on the flat — by the ones who, in the last year of the war just then ended, had weighed the same compound as something to load into shells, and had set it aside as too lethal to handle even for that: a few spoonfuls taken through unbroken skin, enough. It was found to stop knock only afterward, drawn nearly at random off a long list. And it had gone on sale a year and a half before the works on the flat ever ran a full shift — which is to say the red fluid was already in the public's tanks, already leaving public tailpipes into public air, before the first man on this floor stopped sleeping. Nobody discovered its danger in the autumn of −21 pT. The danger was on file, in two hands, in two languages of caution. The autumn only demonstrated it, on the bodies of the men standing nearest the vat.

What worked, that autumn, was the reading and the printing. The men watched one another for the sleep and the shake and walked a stumbling man out before he could be strapped, having no other instrument than each other and using it. The examiner read the bodies correctly and named the metal. The papers set the count in type and did not bury it. The city across the water stopped local sale inside a few weeks. All of that was competence, and it was real, and it was spent early and fast.

The following spring — −20 pT — a federal health service brought the makers and the physicians into a single room to decide whether the fluid should reach the public at all, or only the men who made it. The room did not stop it. It asked for cleaner plants, and for a programme of study into what the far smaller dose an ordinary driver breathed while filling a tank might do to him across years — a study whose findings, whatever they came to, changed nothing about the product for the next half-century. The fluid reached the market it had been built for. The line on the flat went back to full rate.

The man on the blending floor does not appear in the record again; the press notice that holds him is a single brittle column, the kind of thing the age kept almost none of. What can be said past him is the shape of the run he began. By its peak, +28 pT, an ordinary gallon of fuel in the country that invented the fluid carried two to three grams of the metal, and that country alone was putting some two hundred thousand tonnes of it into its own air every year from tailpipes; the whole seventy-year run was made at a handful of licensed plants, one recipe dispersed molecule by molecule into every engine on the planet. The blood-lead level at which a child was called poisoned was revised downward across that century again and again, never once up. A late reanalysis in that one country put the cost at an average near two and a half points of childhood intelligence per person exposed, close to six for the children born at the peak — a figure its own authors warned should not be run past the range that had fitted it. It was gone from that country's road fuel by +51 pT, off the planet's by +76 pT, when a last stockpile emptied in the north of one continent. Fifteen million tonnes and more, all told, into the open air.

The paper did not keep the count. The ice did.

Every tailpipe running the fluid put the metal up as an aerosol fine enough to cross a hemisphere before it fell, and some of it fell with the snow onto sheets of ice no one was mining or drinking or standing on, and stayed there, in bands a later hand could count like years. The curve climbs out of the −20s pT, past the autumn on the flat, past the men who saw the butterflies, and does not level where they died. It rises for four more decades, to a layer dated +23 pT — a year none of them lived to see — in which the same ice that had carried a steady, faint trace for the three thousand years before it carries, abruptly, far more. No one built the ice to hold the number. No one meant it to. It held it anyway.

Two hundred times.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The autumn deaths and their count, the examiner's diagnosis, the plain contemporary press coverage, and the federal conference the following spring recommending study rather than prohibition are record; the blending-floor worker and the men watching one another are inference from the shape those facts make. The chemistry is unaltered: tetraethyl lead, Pb(C₂H₅)₄, discovered as an antiknock agent −24 pT, on sale from −22 pT, retailed as a roughly three-fifths mixture with two halide scavengers whose job was to volatilise combustion-chamber lead into salts that leave with the exhaust — the deliberate dispersal mechanism, and known lethal (a set-aside wartime chemical-defence candidate) before release. The polar curve — a well-mixed aerosol settling onto accumulating firn, peaking c. 200× background in ice dated +23 pT, over a fainter horizon ~4× background two millennia deeper tied by isotope to antiquity — is real (Solanki; Marchetti), as are the peak figures, the phase-outs, the >15 Mt released, and the childhood-IQ reanalysis carried with its caution against extrapolation (Kagiso).
