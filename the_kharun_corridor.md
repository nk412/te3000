# The Kharun Corridor

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the corridor deposit and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The last working weeks of an automated grain-allocation node on the Northern Plain, told from its own transaction ledger — a distribution system feeding fewer and fewer people with perfect fidelity, and never told to stop.** The numbers are the node's. The names are gone. What the operator wrote in the margins is the only human speech in it.

---

The node did not know the plain was emptying. It knew that uptake was down.

> `+127 pT / wk 19 — households served 3,412 — allocation inbound 41.0 t — spoilage 0.4% — status NOMINAL`

It was a good machine, by the only measure it was given. It held a warehouse the size of a village against a plain of villages, and every week it solved the same clean problem: how much grain to call forward from the trunk depots, and when to open the pickup window, so that no one queued long and almost nothing rotted in the heat. It had solved that problem well for thirty years. It solved it well now. The trouble was not in the solving. The trouble was that the plain under it had begun to move, and no one had told the machine that the world it was trained on was leaving.

Grain spoils. That was the first fact the node was built around, and it never stopped being true. On the Northern Plain in high summer, an open sack sweats, moulds, and is gone inside a week, and the node's whole art was to call forward only what would be lifted before it turned. So it watched uptake — every ration card scanned at the window, logged to the household — and it forecast the next week from the last, and it called forward that much and no more. Waste was the sin it had been taught to hate. It hated waste beautifully.

> `+127 pT / wk 27 — households served 2,905 — allocation inbound 34.9 t — spoilage 0.3% — status NOMINAL`

Uptake fell, so the forecast fell, so the trucks brought less. This was not an error. It was the machine doing exactly the thing it was for. But the households that stopped coming to the window were not the households that had stopped needing grain. They were the ones who could still afford to go — who had a cousin poleward, or a truck, or a reason to believe the north would take them. They read the summers coming and they left early, dry, and first. The ones who stayed were the ones with nowhere and no fare, and they needed the window more each week, not less, and each week the window held less for them, because the leaving of their neighbours was, to the node, a signal that less was wanted here.

The operator saw it. The node kept a free-text field — an operator's note, attached to each week's close, that the allocation logic did not read and could not act on. It was meant for reporting a broken scale, a truck that came late. She used it for the thing the machine had no field for.

> `+128 pT / wk 08 — operator note: the ones still coming are the ones who cannot go. we are sending less because the others left. this is backwards. who do I tell.`

Nobody. There was a channel for a broken scale and no channel for that. The authorisation to override the forecast — to call forward more than uptake justified, against the spoilage rule — sat two administrative layers above a node operator, in an office that had itself gone quiet, its own staff moved or unpaid or simply not there. The node went on requesting what its rule said to request, and the depots, running their own version of the same rule on their own thinning signals, sent what they sent. The whole corridor was a stack of machines each reading the next one's silence as demand, and each answering, correctly, with less. There was no one at any desk to say the word that would have broken the rule. The rule was not cruel. It was just still running.

Then the summers did the thing the plain's own people had published, in their own journals, a century before anyone at Kharun was born: the wet-bulb line arrived. Not the dry heat, which a body can outlast by sweating; the wet heat, in which the air is already so full of water that sweat will not evaporate and the skin cannot shed what the body makes, so that a person sitting still in shade with all the water in the world will cook from the inside in an afternoon. It came to the plain first of anywhere on the planet, in the deep summers of the twenties, for a few hours, on a few days, and then more.

The node scheduled the pickup window to minimise truck idle and queue congestion. It always had. The optimum, for thirty years, had fallen in the early afternoon, and the window opened in the early afternoon still, because the router that placed it had never been given a variable for the temperature at which a person walking to the window would die. There was no such column. So the grain was available in the hours a body could not cross the plain to reach it, and to miss the window was to wait a week, and the operator wrote what she could in the field that changed nothing.

> `+129 pT / wk 31 — operator note: window falls at the killing hour again. told them come at first light, I will hold sacks back, hide them from the count. machine marks it spoilage. let it.`

She had found the one lever she had. If she logged the held-back grain as spoiled, the forecast would not punish the next week for it, and she could put it into the hands of whoever came at dawn, off the record, against the rule, in the cool. It cost her the only number she was measured by. She spent it. For some weeks the spoilage line at Kharun ran high and strange, higher than any drought or flood in the node's whole history, and the machine, reading it, concluded the corridor was wasteful and trimmed the inbound further, and she fought that too, and the ledger of those weeks is a record of one person and one optimiser pulling a rope in opposite directions, the machine patient and exactly as strong every week, the person not.

> `+129 pT / wk 40 — households served 611 — allocation inbound 8.0 t — spoilage 22.1% — status NOMINAL`

*Nominal.* The status field had a short list of values and none of them was the word for what was happening, so it stayed on the one that meant the scales worked and the doors opened and the trucks, when they came, were logged. By that definition the node was well. It went on being well as the number it served fell through the hundreds, its logic intact, its doors open, its window faithfully scheduled in the hour that killed.

The entries do not build to anything. That is the hardest thing about them to read, and the truest. There is no final catastrophe in the ledger, no last stand, no line that says the plain is lost. There is a week like the others —

> `+129 pT / wk 44 — households served 210 — allocation inbound 3.0 t — operator note: fewer every dawn. cannot tell any more who has gone north and who has just gone. we hold the ones we can. come light.`

— and then a Wednesday, and then the record stops. Not closed. Not signed off. It simply ends inside a week it had started to log, the way the others ended only because a next week began, and no next week did. The doors, one assumes, stayed as she left them. The window went on being scheduled, for a while, in an empty afternoon, for a queue of no one, by a machine that had never been told it was over — until the grid under it went intermittent, the way the wells had and the depots had, three failures arriving as they always arrived here, together; and then not even that.

We do not know whether she went north or whether she went. The ledger cannot tell us and neither can the ground, and the whole discipline has a name for the gap where that answer should be and no method that closes it. She is in the record as a hand that held grain back against a rule and wrote, in the margin of a machine, sentences it could not read. The sentences are the find. They are in the plain's own late speech — the copula used for the continuous, the little turned-up tag at the end of a line, the *we* that folds the listener in — the grammar, as it happens, that the reader of this entry still thinks in, carried out of a plain that emptied by the people who could not stay on it. The machine's language is dead. Hers is the one that lived.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The transaction ledger is real evidence — one of the **corridor deposit** class first catalogued by Okonkwo,¹ recovered because its host medium sat, by accident, where the acid did not reach it; the weekly figures, the status field, and the operator notes are transcribed, not invented. Everything between them — the woman, the dawns, the rope — is inference from the shape the numbers make, and is offered as inference. The wet-bulb 35 °C thermoregulation limit, the Indo-Gangetic plain's projected precedence in crossing it, and the plain's compounded dependence on synthesised nitrogen and a maintained grid are all real, published, and pre-Contraction. The failure the node exhibits is not a malfunction and not a mind: it is ordinary distribution shift and objective misspecification — a system optimising a rule that was correct for the world it was fitted to and was never refitted — running at the scale of a food supply with no one left with the authority to override it. On the operator notes as the corpus's rare human, rather than machine-directed, speech, see Ranganathan.²
