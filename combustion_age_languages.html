Combustion-Age Languages

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

This article is about the spoken and written languages of the Combustion Age, and the reasons almost none of them can be heard. For the scholarly register in which this encyclopaedia is written, see Reconstructed Late Combustion English. For the living descendant of the period’s dominant vernacular, see Standard Terran. For the period’s artificial languages, best attested of all, see Code Deposit.

The Combustion Age entered its Late phase carrying, on the best reconstruction, on the order of seven thousand distinct languages. It was the most voluble interval in the history of the species. More words were composed, transmitted, and stored in any single year of the period than in the whole of recorded speech before it, by a margin that grows the closer to the Contraction one looks. It is also, of any literate period, the one whose languages we can least often hear.

The reason is a property of the subject and not an accident of recovery. A language is not an object. It is a behaviour, realised in moving air and, for a minority of its uses, in marks on a surface. Nothing of the air survives. What survives of the marks survives on the same terms as any other Combustion-Age inscription: by the durability of the substrate and by whether anyone had reason to cut the marks into something that lasts. For the overwhelming majority of what was said and written, the substrate was a magnetic or semiconductor domain with a maintained service life shorter than the speaker’s own, and the Attrition took all of it. The languages of the period are therefore known the way the Bronze Age is known — from the fraction that was, for incidental reasons, put into metal, stone, fired clay, or vacuum.

This produces the discipline’s central inversion. The comparative method, developed in the period’s own final centuries, can reconstruct an unattested parent language from its surviving daughters with startling confidence; the period reconstructed a language spoken five thousand years before any writing existed and got much of its sound system right. That method still works, and Combustion-Age linguistics leans on it heavily, because so little of the period speaks for itself. What has changed is only the scale of the silence. We can reconstruct the ancestor of the living tongues. We cannot recover the voices of a period that recorded itself more completely than any before it, and knew it was doing so, and chose the recording medium anyway.

1. Name and terminology

The period had no single word for its own linguistic condition and would not have needed one. Its speakers lived inside the ordinary multilingualism of every human age and did not think it remarkable. What requires a term is our register, and here two conventions must be kept apart.

Reconstructed Late Combustion English is the scholarly language of this encyclopaedia: a standardised, partly artificial reconstruction of the high written English of the period’s last literate centuries, used for period subjects on exactly the principle by which the period’s own archaeology retained tell, tumulus, and stela for the Bronze Age. It is nobody’s mother tongue. It is a professional instrument, chosen because it is the best-attested single variety of the age and because a stable reference register spares each article the labour of its own philology.

Standard Terran is the living language in which the reader of the year 3000 in fact thinks. Its descent from a specific Combustion-Age vernacular is not in dispute and is treated at §5. The two are related as a reconstructed Latin is related to a living Romance speech: one is the studied ancestor, the other the unstudied descendant, and the gap between them is itself a datum about how the period’s language changed.

The distinction matters because most readers meet the period’s words already dressed in one register or the other, and both are conventions imposed by us. The period’s own name for the thing it spoke was, in the surviving inscriptions, almost never given. People do not label their own language on a coin. They label the issuing authority, and assume the tongue.

2. Sources and the attestation problem

Everything that can be said about a Combustion-Age language is a function of which of five channels preserved it. The channels do not sample the same languages, the same registers, or the same speakers, and their union is not a language community. It is a cross-section of substrates.

2.1 The immateriality of speech

Speech leaves no direct trace and never has. The period was the first able to record sound at all, and it recorded prodigiously — but onto magnetic tape, magnetic and optical disc, and semiconductor memory, every one of which required an institution to recopy it within decades. The recopying was a subscription, and when the institutions lapsed the subscription lapsed with them. Of the period’s recorded speech — its songs, its broadcasts, its billions of hours of private capture — effectively nothing survives in its native medium. We possess the voices of the Combustion Age on the same order as we possess the voice of the Bronze Age: not at all, save where a voice was deliberately etched into something that does not need power.

2.2 Durable name-bearing media

A small, biased sample of language reaches us in the media that carry proper names across a millennium without maintenance: struck coinage and its legends; monumental dedication and gravestone; and, uniquely to this period, the commit message and identifier of surviving source code. These are the epigraphic corpus of the age. They are formulaic, short, over-representing officialdom, the dead, and the machine-facing, and they carry names precisely where names survive nowhere else — which is why period articles give a proper name only from such a medium and otherwise recover a function. A gravestone preserves a genitive and a date. A coin preserves a polity’s self-description in eleven characters. Neither preserves a sentence anyone spoke to another person.

2.3 The deliberate multilingual deposits

Against the loss, the period mounted several deliberate salvage attempts, and these are the reason any breadth of languages survives at all. The finest is a class of micro-etched nickel disks: one, roughly 76 millimetres across, carries some thirteen thousand pages documenting over fifteen hundred languages — for each, a note on the speech community, a map, an account of the writing system, and a short parallel text — the whole readable with an optical microscope and rated by its makers for two thousand years. One consignment of nickel-plate archives was scattered across Mare Serenitatis at +74 pT (2019 CE) and lies there yet. A gilded plate launched at +32 pT (1977 CE) carried spoken greetings in fifty-five languages out of the system; it was recovered at 3,340 AU in 2871, and is treated with the other Voyager records. To these add the parallel-text corpora: the canonical scripture of a widely dispersed monotheism, rendered whole into some seven hundred languages and in part into some three thousand five hundred, and a mid-century covenant of asserted universal entitlements (proclaimed +3 pT; past five hundred languages by +71 pT), the most-translated single document of the age.

The deliberate deposits share one property that governs everything downstream. Somebody chose the languages. The choice was made under the constraints of the period — which languages had a described grammar, a settled orthography, an available translation, a digital text to draw from — and every one of those constraints correlated with the number of speakers a language already had. The salvage was real, competent, and met its specification. It preserved, disproportionately, the languages that least needed preserving.

2.4 The Wasatch Corpus and the machine-directed register

The single largest body of period language to reach us is the Wasatch Corpus, the bulk-intercepted network traffic read out after quantum period-finding broke the era’s key exchange. By raw volume it dwarfs every deliberate archive combined. By content it is, in the main, not people talking to people. It is people addressing machines and machines addressing people about people — queries, telemetry, and, above all, advertising. Its standard caution is Ranganathan’s, and it is the governing sentence of the whole subfield: we do not know what they said to each other; we know what was said about them to machines. The corpus over-samples the connected, the urban, the purchasing, and the handful of languages the period’s machines could process; the thousands it could not process are absent from it entirely, though their speakers were alive and talking throughout.

2.5 The best-attested languages were the ones nobody spoke

The Code Deposit — one snapshot of the world’s public software, shelved high enough to survive — preserves, in continuous and legible bulk, the period’s artificial languages: the formal notations in which its programs were written. These are, by a wide margin, the best-attested languages of the Combustion Age. They survive complete, with their full grammar, their reserved vocabulary, and millions of instances of running text, at a fidelity no natural language of the period approaches. Read literally, the plurality of complete, well-formed imperative sentences surviving in the period’s own hand are addressed not to persons but to processors. The commit metadata that accompanies them yields, by Okonkwo and Halvorsen’s method, a labour census from timezone offsets; the identifiers and messages yield a thin, clipped, characteristically abbreviated English. It is the register in which the age speaks to us most clearly, and it is the register in which it was speaking to nobody.

4. The shape of the loss

The losses are not random, and their pattern is the article’s single most important fact. Survival of a language into 3000 CE is a steep function of two period quantities: how many people spoke it, and how large a footprint it had left in durable and machine-readable media. The first drove the second — populous languages got described grammars, standard orthographies, translations, and digital text; small ones did not — so the two compound. The result is what may be called the attestation gradient: a small number of languages attested to saturation, a long tail attested by a single line in someone else’s list, and, below that, languages attested only by the negative space where a described neighbour borrowed a word from them.

The period measured the gradient itself, precisely, and published the measurement. Roughly two in five of its languages were assessed as likely to lose their last first-language speaker within the century; fewer than one language in twenty had any substantial presence in the machine-readable record at all. Both figures were established science, restated in the period’s own reference works and in the promotional literature of its salvage projects, where the count of endangered languages appears as a reason to fund the salvage rather than as a description of what the salvage would fail to reach. Every party understood the arithmetic. The languages most certain to vanish were the languages least likely to be written down, and the machinery built to save languages ran fastest on the languages in least danger.

The consequence is stark and quantifiable. The finest deliberate archive documents fifteen hundred languages out of roughly seven thousand. The five and a half thousand it omits are disproportionately the ones with no other durable attestation whatever. For several thousand Combustion-Age languages, the sum of what reaches the year 3000 is the fact of a name — a name recorded, in most cases, in one of the very few languages that survives to carry it.

The revisionist reply, in the manner of Zhou-Ferreira, is that this misdescribes the event. A language whose speakers shifted to a larger one did not die; its speakers lived, and carried much of it — a substrate, a set of sounds, a syntax — into the language they shifted to. On this reading the gradient records not extinction but confluence, and the proof is audible in the descendant tongue, which is a composite of exactly the varieties the maximalist count writes off as lost. The two readings cannot both be tested against the record, because the record is the thing at issue. This is treated at §7.

5. The descent to Standard Terran

Where the period does speak for itself in an unbroken line, it is through its dominant vernacular, whose living descendant is Standard Terran. The descent is reconstructed by the comparative method from the modern language and from the frozen sample of period English in the deliberate deposits, and its main features are secure.

The English of the Late Combustion Age was already, by +55 pT, spoken by more people outside its homeland than within it, and its centre of gravity had moved to the South Asian subcontinent, whose variety was by +100 pT the plurality form on Terra. Standard Terran inherits from that variety a retroflex consonant series, an invariant interrogative tag, and an aspectual use of the copula that the period’s prescriptivists corrected out of their formal prose while their own children spoke it, as documented in Bhārat, Combustion Age. It has lost the interdental fricatives — sounds present in fewer than one language in twenty worldwide and already, in the period’s vernaculars, giving way to stops and labiodentals by a well-documented drift. It marks the perfective with a preverbal particle descended from a Late Combustion completive auxiliary, and it distinguishes an inclusive from an exclusive first-person plural on the model of the period’s Pacific contact vernaculars.

Each of these is a change already visible, in progress, in the surviving period record — which is what lets the comparative method run at all. The opening of §2.1 above, in Standard Terran (transliterated), reads:

A langgwij no de a ting. It de a ting yu du. De doing gon wid de air, an de air no kip. So wat yumi hav av dem, yumi hav bikaz sumbadi kut it in metal — no bikaz dey talk.

The reconstruction is recorded, form by form, in Adeyemi’s grammar. Two forms in the sample carry most of the history. The perfective particle don, from the completive done of several period vernaculars, marks completed action; and the inclusive plural yumi, transparently you-and-me, from a Melanesian contact vernacular in which it was already the inclusive pronoun — so that the language of the year 3000 distinguishes a we that includes the hearer from a we that excludes them by means of a word two people built out of you and me on a Pacific waterfront in the Middle Combustion Age.

6. Errors of decipherment

The period is misread in the specific ways any heavily attested, poorly contextualised corpus is misread, and two errors are standard enough to name.

The first is a placeholder text. A short, corrupt fragment of an ancient Latin philosophical treatise, scrambled past sense and used throughout the period as meaningless filler to occupy space in draft layouts, survives in enormous quantity across the design and printing record. Its very ubiquity, its fixed and repeated opening, and its archaic language marked it, to early decipherment, as a canonical or liturgical text of extraordinary reach — the single most widely reproduced passage of the age. Marchetti demolished the reading, showing the text to be typographic filler chosen precisely because it meant nothing and would not be read; the demonstration is the standard case study in canon formation under sampling bias. The misreading persists in popular treatment, where the fragment is still occasionally printed as though it said something.

The second is a face. A small pictographic glyph depicting a weeping face, admitted to the character standard at +65 pT, recurs in the intercepted corpus at a frequency exceeding most words. Read as an image, it was taken for a mourning or funerary icon and its frequency for evidence of a pervasive death-cult. It denoted, in period use, the opposite: it marked laughter, the involuntary tears of extreme amusement, and functioned as paralinguistic punctuation on informal text. The reversal — a glyph of weeping that meant delight — is the discipline’s stock caution against reading the period’s pictographs as pictures.

7. Contested questions

Two questions are genuinely unsettled, and neither can be settled, because the record that would decide them is the record in dispute.

The first is what the Wasatch Corpus attests. The maximalist school reads its enormous volume as a broadly representative sample of period language and infers ordinary speech from it directly. The corpus-critical school replies that the corpus is a machine-directed register — advertising, telemetry, and query, addressed to or generated by processors — and that inferring conversation from it is Pylos-and-the-Aegean: reconstructing a society from its palace inventory. The dispute cannot be adjudicated, because the conversational speech that would confirm or refute either reading is exactly the material that did not survive.

The second is whether the period lost its languages at all. The extinction account takes the published projections at face value: thousands of languages, tracked toward a last speaker, on a documented schedule. The continuity account, following Zhou-Ferreira, holds that language shift is not language death — that the speakers lived, and that their languages persist, transformed and composited, in the living descendant, whose retroflex series and inclusive pronoun and stopped interdentals are the audible sediment of the very varieties the extinction count writes off. The record cannot decide between them, because a substrate absorbed into a surviving language and a substrate lost with a vanished one look identical once the original is gone. What is agreed is only the negative fact: nobody living through the shift agreed that anything was ending, and the count that said otherwise was compiled by people who went on speaking.

8. Assessment

The languages of the Combustion Age reach us through the following channels, in descending order of fidelity and ascending order of how much they distort.

Evidence class What survives What it distorts
Artificial (source) languages Complete grammar, vocabulary, running text Not spoken by anyone; addressed to machines
Deliberate multilingual deposits ~1,500 languages, described and sampled Curated toward the already-populous
Parallel-translation corpora One text in hundreds of languages One register, one genre, one voice
Durable name-bearing media Names, dates, formulae No connected speech; officialdom and the dead
The Wasatch Corpus Vast machine-directed text Not conversation; a handful of processed languages
Recorded speech in its own medium Nothing —

The period built, against its own foreseen loss, an instrument that worked. It is a disk of electroformed nickel, seventy-six millimetres across, its glyphs standing about a hundred nanometres proud of the surface, rated by its makers for two thousand years and now not yet half that age; under a microscope the pages are sharp, and the parallel texts are legible in fifteen hundred tongues. For several hundred of those languages it is the only thing, anywhere, that records them: a name, a map, a paragraph on the sounds, and one short passage rendered into a speech that by 3000 CE has no other trace and no living speaker. After the passage the disk is silent, and so is everything else.

The standard formulation is Adeyemi’s: a language did not survive because it was spoken; it survived because it was copied, and it was copied because it was already loud.
