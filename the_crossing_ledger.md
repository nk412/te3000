# The Crossing Ledger

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a single bound fee book recovered dry from a coastal salt store and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The Crossing Ledger is a Contraction-era fee book from a landing on a 260-kilometre strait, recording the unnamed carrier who was, for eleven years, the only connection between the data networks of its two shores.** The book is record; the carrier, the cargo, and what the crossings held together are inference from it.

---

**The Crossing Ledger** is a bound fee book of 1,131 lines, two to a crossing, kept at a landing on a 260-kilometre strait from +141 to +152 pT (2086–2097 CE), recording weekly payments to one carrier who sailed the gap with solid-state storage after the undersea cable failed and no vessel remained that could lift it.

## One vertex

Radio could not close the water: with standard refraction a mast sees about 4.12√h kilometres, so the path wanted near a thousand metres of steel on either shore; the surviving high-frequency sets carried a few hundred bits per second, and one terabyte a week is a sustained thirteen million. For eleven years the four hundred thousand addresses of the two shores formed one connected graph through this one person — an articulation point, a minimum cut of one — and neither side's routing tables showed anything but a slow link. Settled runs are closed in the keeper's hand with the vernacular perfective *done cross*, now the ordinary past of Standard Terran. The carrier's name appears nowhere and is unrecoverable; the fee column holds only the word for the role. The last line enters the outbound fee. The particle was never added.

## Provenance and ground

The book survived by desiccation, in a document chest in the landing's salt store: the salt kept the chest dry, and the dryness kept the paper. The horizon figure assumes standard refraction, an effective Earth radius of four-thirds; the graph terms are the period's own, their application to the carrier the Faculty's.

## Notes

Whether the last crossing failed at sea or the keeping simply ended, the book cannot say, and the two readings have never been separated.
