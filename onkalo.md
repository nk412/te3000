# Onkalo

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **This article is about the Combustion-Age deep geological repository for spent nuclear fuel beneath Olkiluoto Island, on Finland's west coast.** For the surface film archive built on the same theory of the future, see **Longyear Deposit**. For the interval that interrupted its closure, see **Contraction, the**.

---

**Onkalo** (endonym; Finnish, "the hollow" or "the cavity," attested on the facility's own operating records and correspondence) is a Combustion-Age underground disposal facility for spent nuclear fuel, excavated in crystalline bedrock beneath Olkiluoto Island in the municipality of Eurajoki. Construction of its access tunnel began at +59 pT (2004 CE); routine emplacement of sealed waste canisters began at +81 pT (2026 CE), making Onkalo the first facility of its kind anywhere on Terra to move from characterisation to operation. Its deposition tunnels lie between 400 and 450 m below the surface, in gneiss dated to approximately 1.9 billion years before the present — rock that had already been rock for roughly four hundred million years when the first multicellular life is presently understood to have appeared.

The standard comparison is to a Bronze Age tomb built by engineers who had also read the excavation reports of every tomb robbed before it. Onkalo's operators studied the failure of monumental warning at other deep-time sites and reasoned, in published working papers, that a marker is itself an advertisement: a structure that says *do not dig here* is read, across a long enough interval, as a structure that says *something is buried here worth digging for*. They chose the opposite instrument — depth, engineering redundancy, and near-total surface silence — over inscription. It is one of the few instances in which a Combustion-Age institution constructed its own version of the very comparison this discipline now constructs about it.

The facility's twist is not that its designers were wrong about the sealed vault. On the physical evidence recovered to date, they were nearly right: the copper, the clay, and the gneiss have done approximately what they were specified to do. The twist is that the obligation to explain the vault — a legal requirement of the period's own statute, publicly acknowledged, never disputed — was discharged in the period's ordinary paper and digital record, a medium known even then to last a small fraction of the vault's designed service life. They built the container to outlast the civilisation that filled it, and entrusted its label to the civilisation.

## 1. Name and terminology

*Onkalo* is a common Finnish noun before it is a proper name — "hollow," "cavity," "hiding place" — and its adoption as the facility's working name, rather than any acronym of its operating consortium, is itself a small datum: the joint venture of the state's two nuclear utilities that built and ran it evidently found the plain descriptive word more durable than its own corporate style, and the plain word is what survived into scholarship. *Olkiluoto Repository* is used interchangeably in the older secondary literature and is preferred by this article's convention only where the reference is to the surface installations and the encapsulation plant rather than the underground workings themselves.

The island lends its name to an adjacent nuclear power station, whose reactors supplied the majority of the fuel eventually emplaced. The two sites are distinct: one generated the material, the other received it, and the record of each degraded by an entirely different mechanism, treated at §7.

## 2. Sources and the attestation problem

Onkalo is, in one respect, the inverse of every other deposit treated in this encyclopaedia. At Longyear, the artefact is a fragile silver-and-gelatin medium and the enclosing rock is incidental. At Onkalo, the artefact — several thousand tonnes of ceramic fuel pellet sealed in metal — is close to the most durable class of object the period produced, and the fragile thing is everything *around* it: the written record of what was put where, in what quantity, on what date, and why.

The period's own nuclear-energy statute required that the facility's existence, contents, and hazard be made available to whatever institutions existed in the future. This obligation was met, in the overwhelming majority of surviving documentation, in the same media as any other Combustion-Age administrative record: acid paper, magnetic and optical storage, and institutional websites, in the national language, with English-language technical summaries lodged with an international regulatory body. No purpose-built long-service medium — no equivalent of a Portal Leader, no photostable reel — was ever produced for the explanatory record, though the technology to do so existed throughout the facility's operating life and was in commercial use at a site less than 1,500 km away.

The consequence is stated plainly by the discipline's standard formulation, after Lindqvist: *the container was engineered for a hundred thousand years and the label for perhaps eighty.* Most of what the operators knew about Onkalo is, at 3000 CE, either gone or held in scattered fragments recovered from unrelated deposits — a filing-cabinet fate, not a vault fate. What survives instead is the vault, largely mute, and a physical record that must now be read the way any unlabelled deposit is read: by excavation, not by consultation.

The planned closure date and the Contraction's conventional span overlap almost exactly — a coincidence the record did not have to produce and did. A century-scale engineering programme that required uninterrupted institutional capacity to its final decade was scheduled to finish inside the very interval that this discipline defines by the failure of century-scale institutional capacity elsewhere. Nobody who set the 2120 target date could have read the Contraction into it; nobody needed to.

## 4. The engineered barrier

### 4.1 The canister

Each disposal canister consists of a nodular cast-iron insert, holding up to twelve spent fuel assemblies in a rigid grid, enclosed within an outer copper shell 50 mm thick. The copper is cast as a single blank of some seventeen tonnes and machined down to a finished cylinder of roughly twelve; the lid is joined by friction-stir welding, a solid-state process that never brings the copper to its melting point and so avoids the porosity a fusion weld would introduce. A filled canister weighs on the order of twenty-five tonnes.

The design division of labour is exact and was published as such: the cast iron carries the mechanical load and shields against the fuel's own radiation field; the copper carries nothing but the corrosion problem. Copper was chosen because, in the absence of dissolved oxygen, it is close to thermodynamically inert in water — a property the operators' own literature called *immunity*, a word this discipline has learned to read carefully wherever it appears in a Combustion-Age safety case.

### 4.2 The buffer and the rock

Each canister is lowered into a vertical hole bored roughly eight metres into a deposition tunnel floor and packed in compacted bentonite clay, which swells on contact with groundwater to a near-impermeable seal, buffers the canister mechanically against rock movement, and — critically — slows the diffusion of any dissolved species toward the copper to a rate measured in millimetres per millennium. The tunnels themselves, once a row of holes is filled, were backfilled with a mixture of crushed rock and clay and, where the record allows it to be checked, sealed with a concrete plug at the panel entrance.

The host rock is a migmatitic and veined gneiss of the Fennoscandian Shield, formed in the Svecofennian orogeny approximately 1.9 billion years ago and essentially undisturbed by any tectonic event since. Groundwater at repository depth grades from fresh near the surface to brackish and then saline with increasing depth, a chemistry the site characterisation programme measured for decades before construction and built directly into the corrosion allowance calculated for the copper.

### 4.3 Capacity

The facility was designed to receive some 6,500 tonnes of uranium as spent fuel, packaged into approximately 3,250 canisters, deposited across on the order of 50 km of tunnel at completion. At the rate attested by the surviving pre-Contraction record, this implies a planned operating life of just under a century from first emplacement to final closure — the span given in §3.

## 5. The corrosion question

The safety case rested on the copper shell remaining intact for a regulatory horizon of at least 100,000 years, with supporting analysis extended to a million years to bound the dose from the longest-lived fission products the fuel contains. Iodine-129, present in every assembly, has a half-life of 15.7 million years; over the roughly one thousand years separating emplacement from the present encyclopaedia, its inventory has decayed by an amount too small to measure by any means available to this discipline. Whatever hazard a breached canister posed at closure, it poses today, essentially unchanged. The corrosion question is not academic to a recovery excavation; it is the excavation's central instrument risk.

Two schools of the operators' own period addressed it, and neither has been more than provisionally settled by anything recovered so far.

The **immunity school**, representing the operators' licensing consensus, held copper to be thermodynamically stable in the anoxic, low-flow conditions the buffer and the rock were engineered to guarantee, and identified the one credible attack pathway as trace sulfide — a species measured, in low but non-zero concentration, in the site's own groundwater, generated by microbial sulfate reduction in the surrounding rock. The corrosion allowance built into the 50 mm wall thickness was calculated directly against this sulfide flux and the diffusion resistance of the bentonite, and the calculation, with its assumptions, was published.

The **anoxic-corrosion school**, associated with a materials-science research group at a Swedish technical university, published from the mid-2070s pT a series of experimental results arguing that pure, oxygen-free water itself corrodes copper, by a reaction that evolves hydrogen rather than requiring a complexing agent like sulfide at all — a claim, if correct, that would remove the thermodynamic floor the whole barrier concept assumed. The claim was disputed by the operators' own consultants and by the bulk of the corrosion-science literature of the period, on grounds this discipline is not equipped to adjudicate; the dispute was still open, in the regulatory and scientific record both, at the last securely dated filing recovered.

**This is the fact the period published about itself and did not resolve before it stopped being able to.** A live, peer-reviewed, publicly filed disagreement about the central engineering assumption of a facility already receiving waste was never closed by experiment or by consensus. It was closed, if it has been closed at all, by an excavation a thousand years later, cutting into canisters that cannot be cross-examined about which model was correct — only measured. See §7.3.

## 6. The marker question

The operators' considered position — stated in public consultation records, contested within their own advisory bodies, and never formally reversed — was to build no monument. The reasoning, quoted above at §0, treated conspicuous warning as a net hazard rather than a net protection, on the explicit precedent of grave-goods assemblages and burial monuments of the deep past, which this discipline still excavates for the same reason a Combustion-Age tomb robber once dug them open. It is a rare case of a period reasoning correctly, by its own later evidence, about the discipline that would eventually study it.

The **obscurity school** was not the only position taken on the general problem in the period. A comparable, geologically distinct transuranic-waste facility, operated in the other hemisphere in a bedded salt formation rather than crystalline rock, took the opposite course: an advisory panel, convened by the responsible government agency in the early 1980s pT-equivalent dating and including a semiotician among its membership, designed an elaborate, deliberately redundant marker system intended to communicate hazard across ten thousand years without depending on any surviving language. Its proposals — a field of large stone spikes angled outward from a central marker, evoking bodily harm without narrative; earthworks legible from the air; multilingual and pictographic inscription panels; and, more speculatively, a hereditary body of custodians charged with transmitting the site's meaning through myth and ritual across generations, modelled explicitly on a religious institution's attested continuity of some two thousand years — were extensively documented and never fully built as specified. A companion proposal for a genetically distinctive sentinel organism, paired with a folklore meant to keep its significance legible after any written instruction had been forgotten, was judged, even within the panel that floated it, unworkable without the very narrative context the whole exercise was trying to avoid depending on.

The two schools never converged, because they were answering different questions with the same word. The **marker school** assumed information would need to cross the interval actively, by inscription. The **obscurity school** assumed information would fail to cross the interval regardless of what was inscribed, and chose to spend its effort on the container instead. Onkalo's excavators, finding no marker of either kind at the surface and no readable warning below it, cannot report which theory would have performed better; they can only report that the second theory's author built the vault they are now standing in, and did not test the first theory's central premise, because nobody in this hemisphere ever raised the marker at all.

An early season of the recovery excavation misread a cluster of precisely stacked bentonite blocks around one intact canister — cut, by the buffer's engineering specification, to a uniform grain size and montmorillonite content, and packed in a ring around a central object — as a deliberate votive arrangement, on direct visual analogy with a Bronze Age cist grave: a central body, a packed clay surround. Geochemical analysis of the blocks corrected the reading within a season; the specification, once matched to the surviving procurement record, proved unmistakably industrial. The error is noted here because it is instructive rather than embarrassing — the same analogy that misled the excavators for a season is the one the operators used correctly, in the other direction, to justify building no monument at all.

## 7. The Contraction and the closure gap

### 7.1 Interruption

The surviving pre-Contraction record documents panel-by-panel sealing on a broadly steady schedule from +81 pT to approximately +156 pT — some seventy-five years of the roughly ninety-four planned. On the assumption of a constant emplacement rate, this implies that on the order of four-fifths of the repository's planned deposition tunnels were excavated, filled, and sealed according to specification before the documentary record becomes patchy. What happened after +156 pT is attested only physically: partially excavated tunnel sections; deposition holes bored but not filled; at least one gallery in which canisters were lowered into position but the surrounding bentonite was never packed to specification, the loose fill visibly incomplete on the tunnel floor around it.

Ibarra's general model of single-filament infrastructure applies here without modification, though her own work addressed agricultural systems: a facility requiring one continuously funded, continuously staffed encapsulation plant, one continuously operating transport chain from reactor sites, and one continuously solvent operating consortium for ninety-four consecutive years is a system with a single point of failure extended across a century, and the Contraction is, definitionally, the interval in which such systems failed first and were noticed last.

### 7.2 The clearance that did not come

Unlike Longyear, Onkalo was never stripped for its metal. The 2418 CE entry recorded in the access tunnels — cut marks, tool fragments, a hearth near the portal — proceeded no more than a few hundred metres before stopping; nodular cast iron and 50 mm copper, a thousand metres underground, in the dark, past an unfinished and structurally uncertain excavation, offered a worse return on labour than an above-ground ruin of reinforcing steel, of which the resettled coastal populations of that century had an effectively unlimited supply. The vault was not saved by discretion. It was saved by being a bad mine.

### 7.3 What has been opened

Of the surveyed fifth of the repository, six canisters have been retrieved and opened under controlled conditions to date. Five show the pitting pattern the operators' own corrosion allowance anticipated: shallow, localised, consistent with sulfide-driven attack at the flux their site characterisation measured, well within the design margin. The sixth, recovered from a deposition hole in the incompletely packed gallery noted at §7.1 — where the bentonite buffer had not achieved its specified density before backfilling activity ceased — shows deeper and more irregular pitting, plausibly consistent with either accelerated sulfide ingress through underpacked clay or a distinct corrosion mode the immunity school's model did not anticipate. The sample is six canisters against a design population of 3,250, and this discipline's working rule against generalising from small, non-random recoveries applies to its own excavation as forcefully as to any Combustion-Age corpus it studies elsewhere. The anoxic-corrosion school is not vindicated by one irregular canister in one underpacked hole; nor is it retired.

## 8. Assessment

Onkalo's operators solved the engineering problem they set themselves with an accuracy this discipline can now check directly, hole by hole, canister by canister — a privilege it has for almost nothing else from the period. The copper is, on the evidence recovered, doing close to what the corrosion allowance predicted. The cast iron, the bentonite, and the 1.9-billion-year-old rock have not moved. Where the operators finished the job, the job has held.

What defeated the plan was not the barrier and not the geology. It was the same single fact that defeats most Combustion-Age century-scale projects examined in this encyclopaedia: a programme scheduled to run for ninety-four consecutive years, on one continuously funded institution, met an interval in which continuous institutions were not the reliable resource its planners had to assume they were. And the explanatory record — required by statute, produced in good faith, filed on time, in the country's own language, exactly as the law demanded — was written onto media with no more claim on the twenty-second century than any other filing cabinet of its age, by people who had, a short distance away and in living memory of the decision, watched a small commercial archive solve that exact problem with a different medium, and did not apply the lesson to their own.

The standard formulation is Lindqvist's, and it has become the discipline's general term for the pattern: *the closure gap is the distance between what an engineered barrier is built to survive and what the institution that explains it is built to survive, and the gap is never zero, and it is only sometimes published.* At Onkalo it was published, in full, in a regulatory filing, decades before the interval it did not survive.

The sixth canister, designated in the recovery team's own field record by its position rather than any inscription the operators left — Panel 4, Hole 19 — was drawn on a morning at +1055 pT from a gallery that was, on the physical evidence, mid-sequence when the sequence stopped: its copper wall, cast at 50.0 mm, now measures a minimum of 38.4 mm across its deepest recorded pit. It sat, unfinished and unmarked, exactly as an unfinished thing sits, for very close to a thousand years, in the dark, in rock older than the animals that would one day dig it up to look at it.
