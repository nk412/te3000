# Onkalo

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the sealed access tunnel at Olkiluoto and the surviving fragments of the closure record, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The last shift of the crew that sealed a deep repository for spent nuclear fuel — a barrier built to outlast, by a factor of ten thousand, the institution that was meant to remember where it lay.** The rock, the copper, and the closure sequence are read from the excavated repository; the crew, and the day, are reconstructed from the shape the seal was left in.

---

Four hundred and thirty metres down, the rock had been rock for one thousand nine hundred million years, and it had not moved in any of them.

He liked to put it to himself that way at the start of a shift, riding the transporter down the ramp with the last canister of the last panel behind him on its cradle: that the gneiss around the tunnel had cooled and folded and set before there was a single animal anywhere on the planet to see it, that it had been standing here, older than complex life, older than shells, waiting for nobody, and would go on standing after the coast above had done whatever it was quietly getting on with doing. The thought did not make him feel small. It made his hands careful. You did not do slack work inside a thing like that.

The canister came off the cradle at the tunnel face and the machine took its weight — near enough twenty-five tonnes, cast iron and copper and the fuel itself, lowered on a frame into the vertical hole bored eight metres straight down into the tunnel floor. He watched the descent on the readout and with his own eyes both, because that was the rule and because he had never learned to trust the readout alone. The insert inside was nodular cast iron, twelve assemblies gridded rigid in it to carry the load and hold the radiation. Around that, the thing that mattered: a copper shell fifty millimetres thick, cast from a blank of seventeen tonnes and turned down to twelve, its lid closed not by melting but by driving a rotating tool through the join until the metal went plastic and knitted, solid state, never once brought to its melting point, so that no fusion weld's porosity could ever open a path where none had been. In water without oxygen the copper was, by its own makers' word, *immune* — not slow to corrode, not resistant. Immune. He had read the safety case in his first year and the word had stayed with him, because it was not a word the trade used lightly and here they had earned it.

The hole took the canister. Above and around it went the buffer — bentonite, blocks and rings of it, packed to a density he could have recited in his sleep, clay that would take up the first groundwater to reach it and swell against the rock until it sealed itself and every gap it could find, and then go on doing the one slow thing it was for: holding the diffusion of anything dissolved down to millimetres in a thousand years. Whatever the water might one day carry toward the copper, the clay would meet it and not let it by. He set the last ring himself, by hand, checking the seat of it, and it was good.

There should have been eight of them on the panel. There were four.

Nobody had decided this; it had simply arrived, the way the thinning always arrived here, without a memorandum. The fuel had stopped coming first — the reactors up the coast going quiet one after another over a handful of years, so that the pool held less each season and the schedule that had them finishing long ago had gone loose and then meaningless. Then the deliveries of everything else began to miss: the bentonite, the concrete, the spares. The office two administrative layers above him, the one that answered questions and authorised departures from the plan, had gone quiet in the same way, not closed, not handed over, only slower to answer and then not answering, its people moved or unpaid or simply somewhere else. One of his own crew had taken his family inland the winter before and had not come back, and the man could not have said, and did not try to say, whether he had gone somewhere or only gone. You did not ask. The ones who could go, went; the work stayed with the ones who stayed.

And the work was sound. That was the thing he came back to, packing the plug behind the last ring, and it steadied him the way the age of the rock did. The barrier did not know the crew was four. The copper did not care that the depot was slow. Every canister in every filled hole behind him, panel on panel, near enough fifty kilometres of tunnel driven and loaded and closed over a working lifetime, would do precisely what it had been specified to do whether or not there was anyone left at a desk to be told it had. The law of the period had asked the barrier to hold for a hundred thousand years. The engineers, not trusting the law, had run their sums out to a million. Neither number meant anything a person could feel. Both were true.

What would not hold was the paper.

He knew this, in the flat way a man knows a thing he cannot fix. The record of what was down here — which hole held which fuel, from which reactor, at what burn-up, on what date — lived in the same registers and the same drives as any office's accounts, on media that had never once, in the whole history of the trade, outlived the institution that recopied them. They had built the container to outlast the country and written its label in the country's own hand, in the country's own failing ink. The distance between the two was not an oversight. It had been named, and measured, and published, decades before this shift, in a filing anyone could read: the gap between what a barrier is built to survive and what the memory of it is built to survive, which is never nothing, and is only sometimes admitted to. Here it had been admitted to, in full, and here he was closing the last hole regardless, because closing it was the only part of the promise still in his hands.

They had chosen, too, to leave the surface blank.

He had come in on the argument years too late to have made it, but he understood the reasoning, and standing at the plug he thought it was probably right. In the other hemisphere another crew, in a different rock — bedded salt, not this granite — had gone the opposite way. Their advisers had spent years designing a thing to be found and understood ten thousand years on without any surviving language: a field of stone spikes thrown up angled and hostile, earthworks legible from the air, panels of pictures, and, past all of that, a proposed hereditary order of keepers charged with carrying the meaning forward by rite, modelled on a priesthood that had in fact kept faith for two thousand years. A marker that shouted *nothing here, keep away, this place is a message and the message is danger.* His own people had read the same deep past the other crew had read — every rich grave the deep past had built and every one a later age had opened precisely because it was marked as worth the digging — and drawn the reverse lesson. A sign that says *do not dig here* is read, given enough centuries, as a sign that says *something here was worth burying.* So they raised no sign. They spent the effort on the container and gave the surface back to the field. The safest thing you could tell the future about the hollow was nothing at all; the last people who would ever know where it lay were the four of them, walking out.

The plug went in. The panel was closed. He signed nothing, because there was no longer anywhere the signature went.

They backfilled the access ramp behind themselves on the way up, the way it had always been meant to be done, from the deepest reach outward, clay and crushed rock tamped in against the walls so that the road that had carried every canister down would not be a road any more, only more of the Shield, filled and sealed and continuous with the rock it had been cut from. It was slow going with four. It was done correctly. Near the top, where the ramp met the daylight, he stopped once and looked back down the throat of it, at the dark closing behind them, and then he went out under an ordinary field on an ordinary coast with nothing on it to say what was beneath, which was the whole of the design.

The rock had been rock for one thousand nine hundred million years and would not feel the hundred thousand the law had asked of the copper, nor the million the engineers had checked against in case the law was wrong. Down in the sealed dark, undisturbed, the fuel kept the one appointment it had always kept and would keep after every institution that had ever heard of it was gone: iodine one hundred and twenty-nine, decaying at the only rate it has ever decayed, losing across the thousand years to come a fraction of itself too small for any instrument this discipline owns to find. The barrier is still keeping faith. Its half-life is fifteen million, seven hundred thousand years.

## Provenance and ground

A reconstruction. The evidence is the repository itself — backfilled panels, plugs, the sealed access ramp, and the recovered canisters; the ~1.9-billion-year gneiss, the barrier specification, the emplacement dates, and the ~6,500 tonnes of fuel across ~3,250 canisters in some 50 km of tunnel are transcribed. The four workers and the day are inference from the shape the seal was left in; whether the final closure was completed on schedule, or left a panel short, the record cannot decide. The physics is real and pre-Contraction: copper's immunity in anoxic water, the friction-stir weld, the self-sealing bentonite, the Svecofennian gneiss, a 100,000-year regulatory horizon tested to 1,000,000, and iodine-129's 15.7-million-year half-life, undecayed on any timescale this discipline can measure.
