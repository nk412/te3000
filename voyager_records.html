Voyager Records

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Voyager records are a pair of identical gold-plated audiovisual disks launched out of the Solar System in +32 pT (1977 CE), one of which — retrieved at 3,340 astronomical units in +926 pT (2871 CE) — is the best-preserved artefact of the Combustion Age and the most misleading. For the archive the period built for its own successors rather than for strangers, see Longyear Deposit. For the corpus whose sampling bias runs in the opposite direction, see Wasatch Corpus. For the fifty-five languages of the greetings, see Combustion-Age Languages.

The Voyager records are two copies of a single phonographic disk, 30 cm across, of copper plated in nickel and then gold, each fixed to the exterior of an instrument platform flung out of the Solar System by gravitational assist in +32 pT. Each carries, cut as a spiral analogue groove, about ninety minutes of music, spoken greetings in fifty-five languages, a twelve-minute essay of natural and mechanical sound, and 115 images encoded as audio-frequency scan lines; a stylus and cartridge travel with the disk. The platforms themselves fell silent within half a century, when the decay heat of their radioisotope generators dropped below the transmitters’ floor. The records were designed to outlive them by a factor of about twenty million: the compilers’ own published estimate gave the groove a legible life on the order of a billion years in vacuum, and nothing recovered contradicts them.

One disk was retrieved in +926 pT, at 3,340 astronomical units outbound; the circumstances of the retrieval belong to the history of our own era and not to this article. It was played, from the engraved instructions alone, on the first attempt. The uranium patch on its cover dated it correctly. The stylus was where the cover said it would be. Of the tens of billions of sound recordings the Combustion Age made, these two disks were very nearly the only ones it engineered to survive on the timescale this encyclopaedia works at, and the engineering was, on every point that can now be checked, correct.

That is precisely the difficulty. The record is the single most intact document the period ever addressed to a distant reader, and it is not a document of the period. It is a portrait the period sat for. The discipline’s working label — the best-preserved artefact of the age and the most misleading — is not a paradox but a description of method: the disk must be read the way a royal inscription is read, as evidence of what its makers wished to have been true, calibrated against everything they left out.

1. The instrument

The cover is aluminium, engraved with its own decoding manual: a diagram of the disk and stylus in plan; the correct rotation time, 3.6 seconds, written in binary multiples of 0.70 nanoseconds, the period of the ground-state transition of the hydrogen atom, drawn beside it as a key; a worked example of the image raster, so that a reader who got sound could get pictures; and a map locating the disk’s origin against fourteen pulsars, each labelled with its period in the same unit. Electroplated onto the cover is a spot of isotopically pure uranium-238, half-life 4.468 billion years: a clock requiring no calendar, no language, and no survival of anything else whatsoever, readable by mass assay of the daughter products. The Combustion Age is, notoriously, the interval that cannot be radiocarbon dated by its own physics; this one object was sent out carrying its own corrected clock.

A reader of this encyclopaedia will recognise the whole apparatus at once. It is a Portal Leader — a medium that explains its own encoding to a stranger, in pictures, from first principles — built four decades before the reels at 78° N, by a different institution, for a much stranger addressee. The Longyear convention did not descend from it; the two solutions were arrived at independently, which is in some ways the more damning fact. The period twice worked out, correctly, how to make information cross deep time: engrave it, in analogue, on something noble, with the instructions on the outside. It applied the method to one film archive and to two phonograph records, and to essentially nothing else it produced. The technique that could have carried the age’s memory was demonstrated, published, and celebrated in +32 pT. It was used twice.

The groove itself was cut directly into the copper, not pressed, and in the run-out area a technician added, freehand, the only unengineered mark on the object: a line of cursive reading To the makers of music — all worlds, all times. It is, by a wide margin, the oldest surviving handwriting recovered from beyond the orbit of Neptune, a distinction it is expected to retain.

2. The portrait

What the disk contains is best understood through what it omits, because the omissions were decisions, and the decisions were published. The compiling committee — a small academic group convened by the launching state’s space agency, working in under a year — issued a full account of its reasoning the following year, +33 pT. There is no war on the record. There is no disease, no famine, no poverty, no weapon, no ideology, and no law. The music leans on the launching culture’s devotional and orchestral repertory but reaches every inhabited continent; the images show anatomy and conception, street traffic, a supermarket, an X-ray of a human hand; the sounds include surf, a heartbeat, a kiss, a train, and one hour of a compiler’s own neural electrical activity, compressed into minutes. The agency vetoed exactly one image the committee had chosen — a plain photograph of an unclothed man and a pregnant woman, included so that the addressee might know what the senders looked like — and silhouette outlines were substituted. The one census of the human body was the one item censored.

The record also carries a printed message over the signature of the launching state’s chief executive, which reads in part: this is a present from a small distant world, a token of our sounds, our science, our images, our music, our thoughts, and our feelings. As a statement of intent it is exact. A token is what it is.

Set beside the rest of the evidentiary base, the shape of the thing becomes clear. This encyclopaedia dates the entire period from a fission detonation, because no other single event of the age so cleanly marks its stratigraphy. The age’s sole deliberate interstellar self-portrait does not mention fission, or the detonation, or the arsenals, at all — and the compilers said so, in print, at the time, as a point of policy. The nearest structural parallel is an inlaid Sumerian box, some forty-five centuries old, recovered from a royal grave, bearing a war procession on one face and a banquet on the other. The record is that object with the war face left blank, by committee resolution, on the record.

Three of the fifty-five greeting languages — Sumerian, Akkadian, Hittite — were languages of the actual Bronze Age, dead for two to four millennia when recorded. The committee reached for them deliberately, as proof of range: may all be well rendered in the oldest voices the period’s scholarship could reconstruct. The period, in other words, knew exactly how it read its own deep past — through a handful of durable, self-important, unrepresentative survivals — and chose, knowingly and in the same gesture, to be read the same way.

3. The reading

For roughly a generation after the retrieval, the disk was read straight. Early Consolidation-era treatments — and the popular literature of our own century still — presented the recovered record as the age describing itself: its music the music, its images the daily life, its ninety minutes a fair sample. Marchetti’s general demolition of canon formation under extreme sampling bias was written about a different artefact, but her test applies here without modification: a corpus assembled by a handful of scholars in under a year, under state veto, is not a sample, whatever its preservation grade. Nsiah-Larsen’s term for the resulting class of error — the peace-panel problem, a curated survival read as a sampled one — has entered the discipline’s general vocabulary. The correction is now orthodox within it and largely ignored outside it. The disk is simply too intact, and too beautiful, not to be believed.

The instructive comparison is the one Ranganathan built the sampling literature on. Of the Wasatch Corpus she wrote: “we do not know what they said to each other; we know what was said about them to machines.” The recovered record is the complement, not the corrective: it preserves what a committee wished had been said. Between the two great survivals — the involuntary one, all telemetry and purchase, and the voluntary one, all courtesy and orchestras — the ordinary speech of the Combustion Age falls almost entirely through, exactly as it fell through between palace archive and royal tomb four thousand years earlier.

Almost. The greetings were gathered in haste, from whoever could be found at one university with a language to offer, and the committee did not script them. Most speakers produced a formula of state — peace, friendship, goodwill, on behalf of all mankind. One, greeting in Amoy, a southern coastal language of the world’s most populous state, said instead: Friends of space, how are you all? Have you eaten yet? Come visit us if you have time.

It is the only sentence on the disk that asks the listener a question a household would ask, and the nearest thing to the age’s ordinary voice that has ever been recovered from anywhere, at any distance. The second record is still outbound, its position known by integration to within a few light-hours. It carries the same sentence.
