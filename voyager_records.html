Voyager Records

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the conservation report of the +926 pT retrieval and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Voyager Records are the two identical gold-plated phonograph disks the Combustion Age launched out of the Solar System at +32 pT — the best-preserved artefact of the age, and the most misleading. One was recovered at +926 pT, at 3,340 astronomical units, and played from the instructions engraved on its own cover on the first attempt; the other is still outbound. The condition, the playback, and the uranium-clock dating are the recovery report’s; the contents and the exclusions are the compilers’ own published account.

A Voyager Record is one of two identical phonograph disks — thirty centimetres of copper under a skin of nickel under a skin of gold — launched out of the Solar System aboard a pair of exploring machines at +32 pT (1977 CE) and inscribed to be found, decoded, and played by a reader who shares no word, numeral, or calendar with its makers. The groove was cut, not pressed. Each disk carries roughly ninety minutes of music, a twelve-minute essay of terrestrial sound, spoken greetings in fifty-five languages, and a hundred and fifteen images encoded into the audio band; a stylus and cartridge travel with it, and its aluminium cover is engraved edge to edge with the instructions for its own use. The makers’ published estimate of its legibility in vacuum was on the order of 10⁹ years.

The disks were compiled as a deliberate archive addressed to no reader the makers could name and to no particular time. One was recovered at +926 pT (2871 CE), at 3,340 astronomical units from the world that launched it, in effectively the condition it left in — the vacuum that had carried it having also preserved it — and was played, from the manual on its own cover, on the first attempt. The other remains outbound, its position known by integration to within a few light-hours. On every point the recovery report could check, the object did exactly what its makers said it would do, on a timescale nothing else of theirs survives to rival.

What went onto the disk was chosen over less than a year, by a committee whose reasoning was published (+33 pT / 1978 CE). There is no war on the record, no hunger, no disease, no poverty, and no weapon in any of the hundred and fifteen images or the ninety minutes of sound. The single photograph meant to show a stranger the body of an unclothed maker was struck from the set before launch and replaced with two blank outlines. The disk is accordingly held by some to be the age’s considered message to the future and by others its most misleading survival: a portrait the age sat for, at continual risk of being read as a photograph the age was caught in. It is the best-preserved artefact of the Combustion Age, and the most misleading.

The disc

Each disk is a copper substrate three hundred millimetres across, electroplated first with nickel and then with gold, carrying a single spiral groove cut by a stylus rather than stamped from a master. It plays at a rotation of one turn every 3.6 seconds. Recovered, its groove still held its edges to the eye: no pitting, no bloom, no corrosion, because interstellar space offered nothing to corrode it and held it within a few degrees of absolute zero for the whole of its flight. The gold-over-nickel-over-copper laminate sits outside every marine, sulfidic, and atmospheric corrosion regime this discipline otherwise catalogues; in vacuum the metal simply waited. The makers’ 10⁹-year legibility figure is, on the recovery evidence, not obviously optimistic — the disk that came back had aged, in nine centuries of flight, by no measurable amount. Whatever else the Combustion Age failed to keep, it cut this groove to outlast the species that cut it, and, on the sample recovered, it did.

The self-decoding cover

The cover is the record’s most remarkable feature, and the reason it could be read at all. It is aluminium, engraved edge to edge with a diagram of its own use: the disk in plan, the stylus laid in its starting position, and the correct rotation period — given not in any unit a finder could be assumed to keep, but in binary multiples of a single interval defined on the plate as a key. The key is the period of the ground-state hyperfine transition of the hydrogen atom, about 0.70 nanoseconds, the one tick available for free to anyone, anywhere, with hydrogen and the wit to look. Read the binary, multiply, and the rotation comes out at 3.6 seconds per turn — a value no convention fixes and any physics arrives at. Beside the diagram is a map: fourteen spikes from a point, each tagged with a pulsar’s period in the same hydrogen tick, the fourteen timings triangulating the world the disk was flung from. And there is a small electroplated patch of isotopically pure ²³⁸U, flagged on the diagram, whose decay against a half-life of 4.468 gigayears gives a finder the flight time from the ratio of daughter to parent — needing no shared calendar and no surviving clock anywhere else in the cosmos. The manual assumes nothing but physics, and hands its reader everything else.

The contents

The ninety minutes of music span the makers’ own instruments and eras — string, reed, struck skin, and voice, drawn from cultures across the launching world. The twelve-minute sound essay is terrestrial: surf, wind, thunder, rain, a heartbeat, a kiss, the long grinding voice of a train — and, diagrammed on the cover like everything else, roughly one hour of the electrical activity of a single human nervous system, compressed to about a minute: one person thinking, recorded and sent to be overheard by anyone at all. The hundred and fifteen images are pitched as scan lines into the audio band and reassemble, on a screen the makers never specified, into a street of traffic, a hall stacked with food, and the interior of a hand shown as bone. Over the mark of the launching state’s chief executive runs a printed line calling the disk a token of our sounds, our science, our images, our music, our thoughts, and our feelings — and as a description of what the object is, the word token is exact. The compilers set out to exclude war, disease, poverty, and weapons, and did; the single census of themselves they would not send was the one photograph of an unclothed body, which the launching agency vetoed for two silhouettes.

Recovery

The disk recovered at +926 pT was read from its own cover. The stylus was mounted where the cover said the stylus went, the plate was spun to 3.6 seconds a turn, and it played on the first attempt — a groove cut by a machine silent since before any institution now living was founded, sounding as though cut the week before. The uranium patch was assayed and its daughter-to-parent ratio read against the 4.468-gigayear half-life; the resulting flight time agreed, to within a few years, with the pulsar map and with the condition of the groove. It was a clock that had kept perfect time in the dark the whole way out, needing no calendar, no shared language, and no survival of anything else — which is more than can be said for any clock the age kept at home. The report’s authors, dry throughout, permitted themselves a single adjective for the first-attempt playback, and the adjective was vindicating: the engineering had done precisely what its makers promised, for a stranger, from first principles, on a timescale nothing else of theirs could touch.

The peace-panel problem

The greetings came in fifty-five languages, most of them formulas of state — peace, friendship, goodwill, on behalf of all the people of our planet — and several spoken in tongues two, three, and four thousand years dead when they were recorded, among them Sumerian, Akkadian, and Hittite, the makers reaching that far back to display their range. The standard critical edition of the contents² names the difficulty the whole disk raises and does not resolve it: a survival that was curated must not be read as a survival that was sampled; a portrait sat for is not a photograph caught. The omissions are the makers’ published choices, not an accident of preservation — which is precisely what makes the disk, whole and unscarred, so easy to mistake for a representative slice of the age rather than the face the age elected to show. The reading is contested and unsettled, and the object does not settle it.

One greeting on the disk broke the formula. In a southern coastal vernacular of the world’s most populous state, a single voice dropped the language of goodwill and asked the only question on the whole record a household would think to ask a stranger: Friends of space, how are you all? Have you eaten yet? Come visit us if you have time. The second disk is still outbound, carrying the same ninety minutes, the same hundred and fifteen images, the same unscarred face — and that sentence, somewhere ahead of everything, still asking.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The surviving fragment is the conservation report of the +926 pT (2871 CE) retrieval;¹ the object’s condition, the first-attempt playback, the uranium-patch dating, and the recovered contents are transcribed from it and from the compilers’ own published account of their selection (+33 pT / 1978 CE). The physics is real and pre-launch: the hydrogen ground-state hyperfine transition (period ≈ 0.70 ns) used as a universal time key; a 3.6-second rotation; a fourteen-pulsar origin map; an isotopically pure ²³⁸U dating patch (half-life 4.468 Gyr) readable by daughter-product assay; and a nickel- and gold-plated copper disk with an analogue-cut groove, estimated legible on the order of 10⁹ years in a vacuum that — absent air, water, or sulfide — no corrosion regime this discipline catalogues can reach. The ninety minutes of music, the twelve-minute sound essay, the fifty-five greetings, the hundred and fifteen images, the excluded war, disease, poverty, and weapons, and the veto of the one nude photograph for silhouettes are the compilers’ published decisions. The two readings — considered message against misleading survival — are set out but not adjudicated here.
