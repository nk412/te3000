# Voyager Records

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the conservation report of the +926 pT retrieval and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The hour a recovery crew lifted one of the two outbound gold records out of the dark at 3,340 astronomical units, read the manual engraved on its own cover, and played it on the first attempt — the best-preserved artefact of the Combustion Age, sounding after nine centuries as though cut the week before.** The playback, the dating, and the condition of the groove are the report's; the bay, the hands, and the hour of listening are reconstruction.

---

It came aboard cold, and it came aboard perfect.

That was the first thing, the thing none of them had let themselves say aloud on the way out: that after +32 pT and nine hundred years and three thousand three hundred and forty astronomical units of nothing, the disk was *whole*. Thirty centimetres of copper under a skin of nickel under a skin of gold, and the groove cut into it — cut, not pressed — still held its edges to the eye. No pit. No bloom. No corrosion, because there had been nothing out there to corrode it; the vacuum that had carried it had also kept it, and the metal had simply waited, at a few degrees above absolute, for someone to come and turn it. The conservator held it in the cradle under the lamp and understood, with a physical shock she had not budgeted for, that she was looking at a surface a hand had touched before the touch was a thousand years old, and that the surface had not aged a day.

They had expected to spend weeks learning to read it. The cover taught them in an afternoon.

It was aluminium, and it was engraved edge to edge with its own instructions, drawn for someone who shared no word, no numeral, no calendar with the makers — for someone, that is, exactly like them. A diagram of the disk in plan, and the stylus laid in its start. Beside it a clock: the rotation time given not in any unit a stranger could be assumed to keep, but in binary multiples of a single interval drawn out as a key — 0.70 of a nanosecond, the period of the ground-state flip of the hydrogen atom, the one tick the universe hands out free to anyone with hydrogen and the wit to look. Read the binary. Multiply. Three and six-tenths of a second per turn. The number was not a convention. It was a fact any physics anywhere would arrive at, left where a physicist would find it.

"They didn't write it *to* anyone," the one reading the cover aloud said, and stopped, because that was the wonder of it. They had written it to no one they could name — to all worlds, all times — and here was one of the worlds and one of the times, leaning over the plate, getting it.

There was a map. Fourteen spikes radiating from a point, each tagged with a period in the same hydrogen tick: fourteen pulsars, and the point their timings triangulated was the world the disk was flung from. And there was a patch, a small electroplated spot the diagram flagged and the assay confirmed: uranium, isotopically clean, the parent almost entirely undecayed. They ran the daughter products against the half-life — four and a half billion years, a number that does not care what century reads it — and the patch told them, to inside a few years, how long the thing had been flying. It agreed with the pulsars. It agreed with the groove. It was a clock that needed no calendar and no shared language and no survival of anything else in the cosmos, and it had kept perfect time in the dark the whole way out, which is more than can be said for any clock the age kept at home.

The stylus was where the cover said the stylus would be. They mounted it, spun the plate to three-point-six seconds a turn, and lowered it, and it played on the first attempt.

Ninety minutes of music came off it, off a groove cut by a machine that had been silent since before any institution now living was founded, and the crew stood in the bay and listened to a species tune its instruments across two millennia of its own composition, string and reed and struck skin and voice, a world's worth of it. Then the essay of sound: surf on a shore, a heartbeat, rain, a kiss, the long grinding voice of a train, and — the cover had a diagram for this too — one hour of the electrical weather inside a single compiler's living skull, compressed to minutes, the private storm of one person thinking, sent out to be overheard by anyone at all. And the images, a hundred and fifteen of them, decoded off scan lines pitched into the audio band and reassembled on a screen the makers never imagined: a street thick with traffic, a hall stacked with food for the taking, the inside of a hand held up to radiation and shown as bone.

It was, on every point that could be checked, correct. The engineering had done exactly what its makers had promised it would do, on a timescale nothing else of theirs could touch, and it had done it for strangers, from first principles, without a single word of instruction the strangers could not derive. This much is not reconstruction. This much is in the report, whose authors, dry as the vacuum they worked in, permitted themselves one adjective for the first-attempt playback, and the adjective was *vindicating*.

What the report does not say, and what the crew could not yet have had a name for, is the shape of what they were hearing.

There was no war on the record. There was no hunger in the hall stacked with food, no disease in the hand of bone, no weapon anywhere in the hundred and fifteen images or the ninety minutes or the twelve. There was a printed message over the mark of the launching state's chief executive — *a present from a small distant world, a token of our sounds, our science, our images, our music, our thoughts, and our feelings* — and it was, as a statement of what the thing was, exact: a token. The one photograph the makers had chosen to show a stranger what a maker's body looked like, unclothed, had been struck from the set before launch and replaced with two blank outlines; the single census of themselves was the single thing they would not send. They had sat for this portrait. They had decided, in a room, over less than a year, and they had published the deciding, that this was the face to send out — and the face had no scar on it.

The disk is the most intact thing the age ever addressed to a far reader, and it is not a record of the age. It is a record of what the age wished, on its best day, to be taken for. A later reader would give the difficulty a name and warn that a portrait sat for must never be read as a photograph caught; that warning is not the crew's, and it is not this tale's to settle. What the crew had was the thing itself, sounding in the bay, too beautiful and too whole not to believe.

The greetings came last, fifty-five of them, and most were formulas of state — peace, friendship, goodwill, *on behalf of all the people of our planet* — spoken stiff and careful into a stranger's ear, some of them in tongues two and three and four thousand years dead when they were recorded, the makers reaching that far back to prove their range. And then, without warning, in a southern coastal speech of the world's most crowded state, a single voice dropped the formula entirely and leaned in close, the way you lean toward someone at your own table, and asked the dark the only question on the whole disk that a household would think to ask:

*Friends of space, how are you all? Have you eaten yet? Come visit us if you have time.*

It hung in the recovery bay, and no one moved to lift the stylus. The second disk is still outbound, its place known by integration to within a few light-hours, carrying the same ninety minutes, the same hundred and fifteen images, the same clean unscarred face. It carries that sentence too. Somewhere ahead of everything, still asking.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The surviving fragment is the conservation report of the +926 pT (2871 CE) retrieval;¹ the object's condition, the first-attempt playback, the uranium-patch dating, and the contents are transcribed from it and from the compilers' own published account of their choices (+33 pT / 1978 CE). The bay, the crew, and the hour of listening are inferred. The physics is real and pre-launch: the hydrogen ground-state hyperfine transition (period ≈ 0.70 ns) as a universal time key; a 3.6 s rotation; a fourteen-pulsar origin map; an isotopically pure ²³⁸U patch (half-life 4.468 Gyr) datable by daughter-product assay; a nickel- and gold-plated copper disk with an analogue-cut groove, held legible on the order of 10⁹ years in the vacuum that — absent air or water — no corrosion regime this discipline catalogues can reach. The excluded war, disease, poverty, and weapons, and the veto of the one nude photograph for silhouettes, are the compilers' published decisions.
