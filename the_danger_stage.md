# The Danger Stage

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a corrupted river-telemetry intercept in the Wasatch Corpus, cross-read against a single recovered gauge-datum plate, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The Danger Stage is the cast-plate designation for the critical gauge height of an interior ford on a Contraction-era displacement route — the water level above which an automated advisory sign, and the single sensor feeding it, went on reporting the crossing safe after the flow had already passed the point at which a wading adult could not survive it.** The plate's threshold figure and the intercepted sensor record are physical evidence; how many people trusted the sign, and on which of the ford's last nights, is reconstruction, and the discipline has not agreed on it.

---

Two objects anchor the reconstruction, recovered forty-one years apart and never in the same institution until the Faculty's own comparison set them side by side. The first is a plate: cast aluminium-bronze, 340 by 210 millimetres, prised from a concrete gauge house by a Consolidation-era salvage crew and kept, unidentified, in a regional foundry-metal store for two centuries before anyone read what was stamped into its face. The casting has pitted along one edge where the house wall it was bolted to eventually failed, but the raised lettering across the centre is intact: a station code, a datum elevation, and, beneath both, in the smaller capitals reserved on these plates for a single operational instruction, DANGER STAGE — 4.85 M — DO NOT ENTER WATER ABOVE THIS LINE. The second object is not a thing but a signal: 1,204 telemetry frames, radioed hourly from the same gauge to a basin flood-forecasting service and swept up, incidentally, by a bulk interception program that was listening to a nearby microwave trunk for other traffic entirely. Of the frames, 683 decode; the rest are torn by fading and multipath on a link never engineered to be intercepted, and the gaps fall, as they generally do in the Wasatch Corpus, without regard for which hour mattered.

The gauge stood at a shallow rock-barred ford, one of perhaps a few dozen such crossings on an inland displacement corridor that, in the +130s and +140s pT (2075–2085 CE), carried a share of an unrecorded number of people away from a lowland the record does not name. What the plate and the telemetry recover, between them, is not the road or the people on it. It is one hydraulic threshold, one instrument reading it, and one nine-hour stretch in which the instrument and the threshold appear to have disagreed with each other.

## The stage and the danger

A river gauge does not measure discharge directly; it measures *stage* — water surface elevation above a fixed datum — and infers discharge from a rating curve built by repeated direct gauging across the years the station operated. At this ford the rating relation, reconstructed from fragments of the same telemetry stream, is consistent with an open channel obeying the Late Combustion period's own standard resistance formula, the Manning equation, mean velocity proportional to the two-thirds power of hydraulic radius and the one-half power of channel slope, divided by a roughness coefficient fixed by the bed material. For a boulder-and-gravel bar of the kind the ford crossed, that roughness coefficient sits high enough that even moderate discharges produce a channel that is fully turbulent by any reasonable measure — Reynolds numbers in the low millions are typical for a metre-scale flow at a metre per second, three orders of magnitude past the transition to turbulence in a pipe. Nothing about this river, at any stage anyone measured, was ever laminar. The question the Danger Stage plate answers is not whether the flow was turbulent but whether a person could still stand in it.

The period's hydraulic-safety literature, developed piecemeal across the Combustion Age from flume trials and post-flood surveys, converged on a rough criterion for human wading stability: the product of flow depth and mean velocity, not either alone. A shallow fast flow and a deep slow one can carry the same product and topple a standing adult by the same margin, because it is that product — depth times velocity — that sets the drag and lift forces a body must resist with nothing but the friction of two feet on a loose bed. Reported thresholds varied with the standing person's mass, footing, and the bed itself, but clustered near one square metre per second as the point past which an adult of ordinary build could no longer hold position against the current. At the ford, the rating curve puts the depth over the shallowest crossing bar at the plate's 4.85-metre stage near 1.6 metres, and the mean velocity near 2.3 metres per second: a product above three and a half square metres per second, several times the wading limit, arrived at well before the stage plate's engravers chose 4.85 as the line to cast in metal. The margin built into the plate was not a margin of theory. It was the margin the gauge house's builders wanted between the number a person could act on in time and the number at which acting was already too late.

## The advisory

By +124 pT (2069 CE) the ford carried an automated advisory sign, wired to the same stage sensor and to nothing else: a single amber-and-white indicator on the near bank reading FORD — SAFE or FORD — DANGER, switched at the plate's own 4.85-metre threshold with no local observer and no second sensor to check it against. The arrangement is an instance of what the discipline, following Ibarra's diagnosis of the age's infrastructure generally, calls a single-filament dependency: one instrument, one threshold, one binary output, and every decision downstream of it resting on that single filament holding true. The sign did not judge, deceive, or intend anything; it read a voltage against a setpoint and lit a lamp, precisely as built, for as long as it had power. Its failure, when it came, was not a failure of the sign or of anyone who wired it. It was a failure of the assumption, never stated because it did not need to be at the time the assumption was made, that the sensor beneath the sign was measuring the same water that would be waist-deep on a person crossing it.

## The night the reading fell

The surviving telemetry brackets one incident window, undated in the fragment itself but bounded by frame sequence numbers to a single night the Faculty places, from independent corroboration in a nearby settlement's burial record, at or near 14 August +149 pT (2094 CE). The stage trace rises steadily across the evening frames, as a monsoon-fed spate would be expected to rise, crossing the plate's 4.85-metre line at approximately the seventieth surviving frame. Nine frames later — under an hour, at the transmission's hourly cadence, though the exact interval is one of the gaps — the reading falls, sharply and without a matching fall in any of the upstream rainfall telemetry the same intercept happens to carry, from 4.97 metres to 4.32 metres. It holds near that lower figure for six further frames before rising again, just as sharply, to 5.41 metres. A real recession of that size, on a channel this size, in under an hour, with rainfall still reported upstream, has no ordinary hydrological explanation. The advisory sign, built to read voltage and not judgement, would have shown DANGER, then SAFE, then DANGER again, inside a single night, on a river that in fact never stopped rising.

## What the turbulence did to the reading

The gauge measured stage with a bubbler: a slow bleed of gas forced down a small-bore tube to an orifice fixed at the streambed, the back-pressure needed to force gas out against the water column above it standing in for the weight, and so the depth, of that column. The method is accurate exactly as long as the fluid above the orifice is fluid — a column of water at its ordinary density. A rock sill of the kind that made this a fordable bar in the first place is also, hydraulically, a channel control: at moderate discharge it forces the flow through critical depth, Froude number at or near one, the point at which surface gravity waves can no longer travel upstream against the current. Past a threshold discharge, rising tailwater downstream drowns that control and forces a hydraulic jump to stand over it — a churning, stationary transition from fast shallow flow to slow deep flow in which a large fraction of the flow's kinetic energy is destroyed as turbulence, and in which air is folded bodily into the water rather than merely dissolved at its surface. Void fractions of ten to thirty per cent, by volume, are unremarkable inside an aerated hydraulic jump's roller; this one, forming and drowning and re-forming as the tailwater itself fluctuated, would have swept aerated water directly over the bubbler's orifice.

A bubbler cannot distinguish a shallower column of ordinary water from a deeper column of water carrying entrained air, because it measures weight, not height, and an air-water mixture at even modest void fraction weighs measurably less per metre of depth than the water alone. A column that is genuinely 1.9 metres deep but averaging twenty per cent air by volume presses on the orifice as roughly 1.5 metres of plain water would. The instrument does not lie about what it senses; it senses correctly and reports a physically real quantity — reduced hydrostatic pressure — under a calibration that assumed a fluid this event did not supply. Read this way, the nine-frame dip is not a fall in the river. It is the arrival of the jump's aerated roller directly over the sensor, and the true stage behind that dip was never 4.32 metres; the telemetry's own upstream rainfall record, which the dip does not match, is the tell that says so.

Turbulence intensity compounds the problem past what any single reading, correct or not, could resolve. Natural channel flow does not hold its mean velocity; it fluctuates around it, typically by five to twenty per cent of the mean in ordinary reaches and considerably more inside and downstream of a jump. A hazard threshold built on mean depth times mean velocity is, by construction, a threshold the instantaneous flow crosses and recrosses many times a minute near the boundary, in both directions, whatever the gauge reports at the top of the hour. The Danger Stage plate names a single number because a sign can only show two states. The river it was bolted beside never held to one.

## The road that reached it

The displacement wave that reached this ford in +149 pT moved, like the rest of the corridor's traffic that decade, under no central register and left almost none of its own; what the Faculty has is the ford's instrument record and, downstream of it, the demography of who is buried, and who is not, in the settlements on the ford's far side. The pattern in what little dating survives is consistent with the same gradient recorded elsewhere on the corridor: households with private transport, or with standing arrangements at a paid barge crossing eleven kilometres downstream, are disproportionately absent from the far-bank burial ground for this exact window, while its graves cluster in the weeks the ford's telemetry shows the advisory cycling between its two states. The barge, unlike the ford, took bookings, and bookings could be, and were, made in advance by those who could pay for them and had a address for the invoice to reach. The ford was free, unscheduled, and, for one night, wrong about itself.

## Two readings

Voss-Okafor, working from the aeration analysis above, reads the incident as the discipline generally reads single-filament failures after Ibarra: an honestly built, honestly maintained instrument, correct within its design assumptions, delivering a false negative at the exact hour a false negative was fatal, to people who had no way to know the sign was reading anything but the river. On this reading the nine-frame dip is the entire event — the one interval in which trust in the sign was rational and wrong at once.

Zhou-Ferreira's reservation, characteristically, does not dispute the hydraulics; it disputes what the hydraulics are asked to explain. The corroborating burial dates cluster across several nights, not one, and nothing in the telemetry establishes that anyone crossed *during* the dip rather than in the hours before or after it, when the sign is not disputed to have read DANGER correctly. A false-negative instrument explains why a crossing in that hour would have looked safe; it does not by itself establish that the crossing everyone remembers, if there was one remembered event at all rather than an accumulation the record has since compressed into one, took place inside that hour and not around it. The telemetry fixes a fact about the instrument. It does not fix a fact about the crowd. Both readings are argued from the same 683 frames, and the frames that would settle it are among the 521 that did not survive.

## Provenance and ground

The plate survived by being scrap: unread and unvalued, it sat in a foundry-metal store's reject bin rather than a furnace charge, for reasons the store's own inventory, if it kept one, has not survived to explain. The telemetry survived because the microwave trunk it briefly rode was already being logged for unrelated traffic under a bulk-interception program of the kind that produced the wider Wasatch Corpus, and because that program's era-standard encryption later fell to the same quantum decipherment that opened the rest of the Corpus. The frame losses are ordinary link fades on a path never engineered for interception; nothing about which frames survived tracks which hour mattered.
