The Nine of Fifteen

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a burial ground of seventy-one individuals and their recovered genome, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Nine of Fifteen is the Historical Faculty’s name for a Late Combustion custodial community, reconstructed from its burial ground, whose members each held one metal-engraved share of a split cryptographic key, any nine of which would have opened one of the Sealed — and which was never again brought to nine. The bones, the genome, and seven of the fifteen share-plates are physical record; that these people were the custody of that particular sealed archive, and why they scattered, is reconstruction.

The assemblage is seventy-one individuals interred over roughly five generations in a single ground on a dry interior upland, poleward and inland of the abandoned coasts, and beside forty-one of them lies a small rectangular plate of a corrosion-resistant nickel alloy, hand-numbered and struck across its face with a long string of hexadecimal characters. The plates are the visible half of the entry; the genome recovered from the petrous bone of the temporal skull is the other. Read together they describe a community organised around the custody of a single object it could not itself read: one of the Sealed, an archive enciphered under a threshold scheme that split its key into fifteen parts and required any nine of them, brought physically together, to reconstitute it. The community held the fifteen parts across fifteen lineages and married within itself to keep them there, so that for a century the shares stayed in nine reachable hands. Then heat, drought, and the movement that followed pulled the lineages apart along the retreat, the marriages that had held the shares in one valley opened outward, and the ninth share was never again in the room with the other eight. The archive is a member of the category of sources that are not undeciphered but undecipherable, provably, by the period’s own mathematics; the seven plates that survive contain, by that same mathematics, no information whatever about the key. What the community called itself is not recoverable. The Faculty names it for the arithmetic that governed it.

The plates and the wall they are

Each plate carries one share: a coordinate pair in a finite field, an integer index paired with a long field element, struck in hexadecimal so that a plate could be transcribed by hand without ambiguity and would outlast any disk or card. The scheme is the standard threshold construction of the period, and its mathematics is exact rather than approximate. A secret — here the key that deciphers the archive — is set as the constant term of a polynomial of degree eight over a large finite field, the field of integers modulo a prime near two to the two-hundred-and-fifty-sixth. The polynomial’s other eight coefficients are drawn at random and then discarded. Fifteen shares are the polynomial’s value at fifteen distinct nonzero points. Because a polynomial of degree eight is fixed by any nine of its points and over-determined by more, any nine shares reconstruct the polynomial by interpolation and read the key off its constant term; and because eight points leave the constant term entirely free — for every candidate key there exists exactly one degree-eight polynomial passing through those eight points and taking that key at zero — any eight shares are consistent with every possible key, in equal measure.

This is the load-bearing fact of the whole entry, and it is worth stating in the strong form the period stated it in. Fewer than nine shares do not make the key hard to find. They make it undetermined. A holder of eight plates is in precisely the position of a holder of none: the key is, conditioned on the eight, uniformly distributed over the entire field, and no computation performed on the eight can change that, because the information is not hidden in them to be extracted — it is not in them at all. This is what the period’s cryptographers meant by information-theoretic, as against computational, security. A computationally secure cipher is one an adversary could break given enough operations, and merely lacks the operations; it falls the moment the operations become cheap, which is how the bulk-intercepted network traffic of the age — the harvested cable traffic held against a later key — was eventually read out, once machines arrived that made the era’s public-key operations cheap. The threshold scheme does not fall that way. There is no later machine for it. Eight shares withhold the key not by a margin of difficulty but as a matter of measure, and the margin does not narrow with the centuries.

The archive could in principle be attacked below the shares, at the cipher itself, by trying keys directly. The period had closed that door too, and closed it in physics rather than mathematics. The archive’s body is enciphered under a symmetric block cipher with a key of two hundred and fifty-six bits. To try keys is to advance a register through its states, and advancing a register is erasing and rewriting bits, and the erasure of a bit has a floor on its energy cost set by thermodynamics — about two point nine times ten to the minus twenty-one joules per bit near room temperature, the quantity the period wrote as the Boltzmann constant times temperature times the natural logarithm of two. Merely to count a two-hundred-and-fifty-six-bit register through all its states, at that floor, with no cipher evaluated and nothing else done, costs of order ten to the fifty-sixth joules — some twelve orders of magnitude beyond the entire radiant output of an ordinary star across the whole of its life. The number of keys is not large in the way a national budget is large. It is large in the way that forbids the search on any energy a civilisation could gather. The community, in other words, sat inside two independent walls: the mathematics that made eight plates say nothing, and the physics that made going around the plates cost a galaxy of stars. It needed only to keep the ninth plate coming to the room.

The sitting

The custody is legible as a working process because the ground preserves its shape and because the community, like the deliberate key-holding bodies the practice descended from, appears to have convened on a schedule. The Faculty reconstructs a sitting: a periodic assembly, several times a year, at which the holders of the shares came in person to one shielded room and performed together an operation that required the quorum and left the archive itself sealed. The physical grammar of such assemblies is well attested for the period — tamper-evident carriers for the share-tokens, a written script read aloud and countersigned, an air-gapped machine that never touched a network, witnesses whose presence was itself part of the record — and the grave goods carry its residue: beside the plates lie the remains of small sealed carriers, and a subset of the interred, distributed across the lineages and the generations, were laid with a stylus and a hand-bound ledger whose pages have not survived but whose clasp and rule have.

What the sittings were for is the entry’s quietest turn. A threshold custody does not need to open its archive to have work; its work is to remain openable, and to decide, again and again, that the time to open has not come. The reconstruction that fits the ground is of a body convened chiefly to confirm that the quorum still existed — that nine shares were still held, still legible, still in reachable hands — and to defer, at each sitting, the reading itself to a later and safer time. The sealing of such an archive is cheap and quick; it is the reading that is dear, in judgement and in risk, and the reading was in every case assigned forward, to a generation that would inherit both the shares and the standing to say when. The descendants inherited the shares. Across the ledger of sittings the Faculty can reconstruct, the standing business was the maintenance of a capability held in trust for a future that was expected to be calmer than the present and was not, and each sitting discharged that trust by handing it on unspent. The cost of opening was never paid because it was always the next quorum’s to pay.

The relaxing

The genome carries the community’s rule and the breaking of it in a single measurable quantity. A population that marries within a small closed set of lineages accumulates, in each new member, long stretches of chromosome that are identical on the copy inherited from the mother and the copy inherited from the father, because both copies descend from the same recent ancestor inside the set — runs of homozygosity, in the period’s term, quantified as the fraction of the genome they cover. The lengths of these runs are a clock. Recombination severs a chromosome about once per hundred centimorgans of genetic length per generation, so a stretch shared through a common ancestor some g generations back is eroded on both lineages and survives at an expected length near a hundred divided by twice g centimorgans — long runs meaning a recent shared ancestor, short runs an old one, across an autosomal map of roughly thirty-five hundred centimorgans in all. The earliest individuals in the ground, of the custody’s settled century from about +90 pT (2035 CE), carry the long runs and the high covered fraction of a strictly endogamous community: the marriages are close, the shares stay in the set, the lineages fold back on one another every few generations.

The relaxing is written in the later individuals as the long runs thinning and the covered fraction falling, and its direction is the tell. The out-marriage does not bring in the neighbours. The ancestry that enters the later genomes is not that of the adjacent valleys but of populations along the axis of the retreat — poleward, inland, upland — arriving in the generations after the +120s pT (2060s CE), in which the wells of the region are known, from the sediment, to have gone intermittent and the summers to have crossed into the range a body at rest cannot shed. The endogamy did not relax because the community decided its rule had served its purpose. It relaxed because the community was moving, and a community strung out along a road cannot keep its marriages inside fifteen lineages in one valley. The pattern tracks displacement and not doctrine, and the genome, which records where the lineages ended and not the journeys between, cannot say whether the opening of the marriages was the community loosening or the community coming apart — the same reading the discipline cannot force anywhere the record is only where people stopped.

What did not reconvene

The scattering is reconstructed and not witnessed. The ground’s latest interments are sparser, the covered fraction in them lower, the grave goods thinner; the sittings, on the ledger’s recoverable clasp-count, fall off and then are not attested. No plate is struck with the fact of a last sitting, because a custody does not know its last sitting is the last one; a quorum that fails to convene has not decided anything, and leaves no record of the deciding. At some sitting after which there was no other, fewer than nine of the fifteen shares were within reach of one room, and the archive passed, without any act and without anyone’s choosing it, from openable to sealed. The mathematics that had protected it from every adversary now held equally against every heir. The two walls that kept the contents from an enemy kept them, from that point, from the community itself and from everyone after.

Seven of the fifteen plates have been recovered — forty-one graves held a plate, but the great majority carry the same handful of indices, the shares of the lineages that stayed and were buried together, and only seven distinct indices are present across the whole assemblage and the manifest of the sealed deposit. The other eight indices went with the lineages that moved and are not in the ground, and there is no reason in the physics or the genome to expect them ever to be brought together with these; they dispersed along a displacement that kept no archive of itself. The ciphertext survives, durable and intact, in a deposit of the Sealed, its plaintext header still legible and still declaring the scheme — any nine of fifteen — and its body still a wall of uniform noise. The archive is known to exist, known to contain something, and known, by the period’s own proof, to be permanently unreadable. It is not undeciphered. Nothing was lost that a cleverer scholar could recover. What was lost was a quorum.

The reading

The scholarship divides on the one question the ground cannot close, which is whether the sealing was ever meant to be undone. One reading takes the custody at the face of its own grammar: the archive was sealed against a hazard the community judged real, entrusted to a quorum precisely so that no single holder and no small faction could open it, and meant to be read when the hazard had passed — so that its permanent sealing is an accident of the retreat, a capability lost the way the food ledgers and the registrations were lost, by the institutions that maintained it lapsing faster than they could hand it on. The other reading takes the endless deferral as the point: that a body which convenes chiefly to decide not to open, generation after generation, is a body whose real function is the not-opening, and that the archive was sealed by people who wanted it held shut and reachable but not read, with the reading deferred forward not as a delay but as a permanent condition dressed as a delay. The first reading makes the community the victim of its custody; the second makes the custody the community’s true purpose. They rest on the same secure facts — the scheme, the sittings, the relaxing genome, the seven plates — and part only on the intent behind a sealing, which is exactly what a cipher is built to keep, and which the ground does not hold. The Faculty adjudicates neither, and notes that the record cannot, the intent having been sealed under the same key as the rest.

Provenance and ground

The entry rests on the burial ground: seventy-one individuals on a dry interior upland, the assemblage preserved by the aridity and the alkaline upland soil that spared the bone, with the genome read from the dense petrous portion of the temporal skull after the taphonomic protocol of Halvorsen, who also accounts for the survival of the nickel-alloy plates in the same ground that consumed all softer grave goods. The reading of the genome as an unintended register of the community’s marriage rule and its relaxing is Okonkwo’s, in the standing method of taking a body’s incidental traces — here the runs of homozygosity and their thinning — as a record no one set down on purpose. The assemblage is not radiocarbon dated: the period’s carbon record is crossed by its own bomb and fossil-carbon signals and returns dates that are centuries wrong, so the ground is placed instead by the erosion clock of the homozygosity runs and by the context of the sealed deposit whose manifest names the same seven share-indices. The cryptographic facts — the degree-eight polynomial over a field near two to the two-hundred-fifty-sixth, the information-theoretic security of the threshold below nine, the thermodynamic floor on exhaustive search — are the period’s own established mathematics and physics, not this entry’s. What the archive contains, and whether it was meant to be read, are not in the record and are not reconstructed here.

Notes

The plaintext header of the sealed archive gives the threshold as nine and the count as fifteen and gives nothing else — no title, no date, no custodian. The convention of a legible header on an unreadable body was standard for the deliberate archives of the period, which expected a reader and provided for one; the provision was honoured, and the reader, when one came, was two plates short.
