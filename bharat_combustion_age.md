# Bhārat, Combustion Age

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **This article covers the South Asian peninsular polity from +55 pT (2000 CE) to the Contraction.**
> For earlier phases, see **Bhārat, Early and Middle Combustion**. For the physical region, see **Peninsular South Asia**. For the aquifer, see **Indo-Gangetic Basin**.

---

**Bhārat** (also *India*; both names were official and the choice between them was politically freighted throughout the period) was, from +78 pT, the most populous polity in the history of the species, and remains the largest single body of human beings about whom almost nothing is directly known.

Between +55 and +133 pT it grew from roughly 1.05 to 1.65 × 10⁹ people, tripled its energy consumption, quadrupled its concrete production, exhausted the fossil groundwater of its northern plain, contributed a disproportionate share of the world's software labour, launched machines to the Moon, and left behind — in the durable record — fly ash, cement, a subsiding delta, a shifted isotope ratio, an aquifer that has not refilled, and approximately one hundred metres of wheel tracks near the lunar south pole.

Its people were burned. Its money became a signal in a wire. Its arguments were conducted on media that no longer exist. The standard formulation is Ranganathan's: *Bhārat is the largest hole in the Combustion Age; everything about it is known except what anyone thought.*

## 1. Sources and the attestation problem

South Asia is the discipline's canonical **thin-attestation region**: a population maximum coinciding with an evidentiary minimum. Four mechanisms are responsible, and they are unrelated to one another, which is why the effect is so severe.

### 1.1 The cremation gap

The majority religious practice of the period disposed of the dead by open-air or electric cremation, generally within a day, with the calcined remains committed to running water. The proportion of period Bhāratīya dead who entered the burial record is variously estimated at 12 to 18 per cent, and those who did belonged overwhelmingly to minority confessional communities.

The consequence is that the skeletal series — the discipline's primary instrument for stature, nutrition, disease load, childhood stress, occupational trauma and violent death — is, for the largest population on Terra, drawn from an unrepresentative fifth of it. Every published estimate of Bhāratīya nutritional stress during the Contraction rests on cemeteries belonging to communities that were, on the surviving textual evidence, distinct in wealth, region, and diet from the majority.

The parallel loss is genomic. Cremation destroys ancient DNA. What is known of period population structure is inferred backwards from the genomes of living descendants — a method that recovers deep structure well and recent events poorly, and that cannot distinguish a person who died from a person who moved.

### 1.2 The numismatic termination

Coinage is the archaeologist's most cooperative artefact: durable, dated, portable, name-bearing, and produced by an authority with an interest in being identified. For Bhārat it fails twice inside a decade.

In late +71 pT the state voided, at a few hours' notice, some 86 per cent of its currency by value, requiring physical exchange of the demonetised notes at banks within a fixed window. The event is legible in the note record as a near-total discontinuity of series, and it is the sharpest single dated horizon anywhere in the Late Combustion material culture of the subcontinent. It was, at the time, described by its architects as a measure against untaxed wealth. Assessments of whether it achieved this were still being argued when the archives ceased.

The second failure is quieter and worse. Within a decade of the demonetisation, a state-sponsored instant-settlement network had absorbed the majority of retail transactions by count. The mints continued to strike, at declining volume; but a coin ceases to be a representative sample of exchange long before it ceases to be struck, and by +85 pT the physical currency of Bhārat records the behaviour of almost nobody. The transaction ledgers that replaced it were dense, complete, exhaustively itemised, and stored on rotating magnetic media. See **Attrition**.

### 1.3 The Code Deposit as a census

Bhārat's single richest textual witness is a film reel in a Norwegian coal mine.

The Code Deposit (see **Longyear Deposit** §5.1) preserves, bit-exact, one snapshot of the world's public software. Its value for South Asian history is not the code. It is the metadata: every recorded change to every project carries an author string, an electronic address, and — critically — a timestamp with a *timezone offset*.

The subcontinent kept a half-hour offset shared with no major neighbour. It is therefore trivially separable. From the L-III reels one may extract, for a substantial fraction of the world's software labour in early +75 pT: how many people were working, in what hours, on which days, in which of two dozen orthographies they wrote their comments, how their names distributed across the region's naming systems, when they stopped for festivals, and when, in the third week of March +75, an entire timezone's commit activity moved abruptly and permanently from daylight to night.

It is the only large-sample time-use dataset for any Combustion-Age population, and it describes perhaps four million people out of one and a half billion, all of them literate, urban, employed, male in a ratio of roughly four to one, and working for foreign clients.

Ranganathan's rule applies with full force. We possess a census of the people who wrote the software of the world, and no census at all of the people who grew its rice.

### 1.4 Lunar evidence

Bhārat's best-preserved artefacts are not on Bhārat.

Between +63 and +80 pT the polity placed at least four objects on or around Luna: an orbiter, still in orbit, silent, radar-located from Terra a decade after contact was lost; a lander destroyed on impact at moderate southern latitude in +74, whose debris field is mapped; and a lander and a small six-wheeled rover set down near the southern pole in +78, both intact.

The rover ran for one lunar day, travelled a little over one hundred metres, and stopped when its battery froze. It is upright. Its tracks are undisturbed. In the absence of atmosphere, water, and biology, they will remain legible for something on the order of 10⁷ years, degrading only under micrometeoritic gardening.

The site lies within the modern southern polar exclusion, and its perimeter fence is a routine navigational reference.

### 1.5 The stratigraphic record

Where the human record fails, the sediment does not. Bhārat's Combustion-Age signature is among the strongest on the planet, for a reason of pure geology: the subcontinent's coal is unusually ashy — commonly a third to nearly half of the mass burned was mineral matter — and the resulting spheroidal carbonaceous particle flux, per unit of energy obtained, exceeded that of any other major economy.

The country that burned it obtained less power and left more rock.

## 3. Water

The structural history of Late Combustion Bhārat is a history of water, and it is legible without a single written word.

The northern plain was farmed on a system of three inputs: industrially fixed nitrogen; electricity supplied to agricultural users at nominal or zero price; and groundwater, lifted by electric tubewell. The first two made the third free. Satellite gravimetry from +57 pT measured the result directly: the aquifer beneath the north-western states lost mass continuously, at rates that would have exhausted the accessible storage within a century, and the loss did not correlate with rainfall.

Every party to this arrangement understood it. The agronomy was published. The measurements were published, in the polity's own journals, by its own scientists. The subsidy on electricity was a political instrument of long standing and no government of the period attempted its withdrawal at scale.

The Deposit's designers thought it obvious that a subscription nobody funds will lapse. Bhārat's aquifer is the inverse case: a subscription that everyone funded, at a price of zero, until the account was empty.

**Consequences in the durable record:**

- Land subsidence across the western plain, measurable in the geomorphology, with a maximum of several metres.
- Nitrate and arsenic profiles in the shallow aquifer, dated by the fly-ash horizon, and irreversible on the timescale of interest.
- Abandonment of tubewell irrigation in the north-west during the +120s and +130s, and the reversion of a wheat-rice double crop to a single monsoon crop across an area larger than the Nile basin.
- The **Bengal transgression.** The eastern delta was subsiding under its own sediment load and starved of replacement by upstream barrages; sea level rose; the two effects are additively catastrophic and were known to be. The delta is now open water for some 120 km inland of its Combustion-Age shoreline, and its cities are the largest submarine archaeological complex on Terra.

The western metropolis was assembled during the Early Combustion Age by filling the channels between seven islands. Its fill is again seabed. The islands are again islands.

## 4. Heat

Human thermoregulation fails, irrespective of shade, water, or acclimatisation, at a wet-bulb temperature of about 35 °C. The Indo-Gangetic plain was identified in the +70s pT as the region of Terra most likely to reach that limit first, in papers whose projections have proved, at this distance, conservative in timing and accurate in geography.

The first documented excursions occurred in the +120s. They were brief, local, and killed people in numbers that the period's mortality registration could not resolve. The response was not evacuation but the enormous, and largely successful, deployment of mechanical cooling — which was powered, in the +120s, substantially by coal.

The population did not leave the plain because it was hot. It left, two generations later, because the wells were dry and the grid was intermittent, and by then leaving was called something else.

## 5. Energy, materials, emissions

Bhārat entered the period with per-capita emissions around a tenth of the wealthiest polities' and left it having emitted, cumulatively, a small single-digit percentage of the total anthropogenic carbon. It was, throughout, among the largest annual emitters, because it was very large.

This distinction — between the flow and the stock, between what a polity emits and what a polity has emitted — organised the entire international diplomacy of the Late Combustion Age and was never resolved. Bhārat's position, held consistently by governments of every complexion across the whole period, was that the stock was the relevant quantity. Its interlocutors' position was that the flow was. Both were correct about the physics, which is why nothing happened.

Materially, the period is defined by cement and by ash. Bhārat was the second producer of cement on Terra and the first, by a wide margin, in the production of coal fly ash per unit of electricity. Vast impoundments of the latter — settling ponds, embanked, unlined, on floodplains — are among the most conspicuous anthropogenic landforms surviving anywhere. Several failed catastrophically during the Contraction.

Against this: solar generation. Photovoltaic capacity in Bhārat rose from under 3 gigawatts at +69 pT to over a hundred within a decade and a half, at a delivered price that fell below coal's before +75. By +80 the polity's non-fossil generating capacity exceeded half of the total, five years ahead of its own commitment. The buildout continued through the Contraction, which is one of several facts that make the continuity school's case in South Asia stronger than anywhere else on Terra.

## 6. Polity

The historiography here is bad and will not improve.

What is secure: Bhārat conducted, throughout the period, the largest recurring exercise of the franchise in human history, with an electorate approaching 10⁹ persons, administered by a permanent commission, executed on some 10⁶ electronic machines, and validated by the application of a silver-nitrate stain to the voter's finger. The stain is the only physical residue of the process that survives. The machines are gone; the rolls are gone; the results are known, for most cycles, only through the numismatic and monumental record and through the Wasatch Corpus.

Also secure, from the currency itself: for the whole of the period, every denomination of every note bore the same portrait — an elderly, spectacled, ascetic male figure, unclothed above the waist. Nineteenth-century decipherment of the accompanying legend established that he was not a deity but a political leader assassinated in −3 pT, and that his image was retained on the currency by every subsequent administration, including those whose ideological ancestors had opposed him. The persistence of the portrait through the demonetisation, and its abrupt disappearance from the last struck coinage of the +130s, are two of the four securely dated political facts of Late Combustion Bhārat.

What is not secure: everything else. The period saw a transition around +69 pT from coalition governance to sustained single-party parliamentary dominance; a constitutional reorganisation of a disputed northern territory in +74; legislation on citizenship registration in the same year, followed by public disorder of a scale that the surviving record cannot measure and that partisans of every subsequent century have measured confidently; and, across the whole interval, an unresolved argument about the relationship between the polity's majority religious tradition and its constitutional secularism.

The Wasatch Corpus is our only continuous witness, and it is an advertising archive: it records Anglophone, urban, connected, purchasing individuals, in a polity where the great majority were none of these. The **maximalist school** reads the Corpus's contents as representative and describes a period of severe majoritarian consolidation. The **corpus-critical school** replies that the Corpus over-samples precisely the constituency most exercised by that question, in the language in which it was most exercised, and that inferring 1.4 × 10⁹ people's politics from it is methodologically identical to inferring the Bronze Age Aegean from the palace at Pylos.

Both schools agree on the demographic and hydrological facts, which is why those facts are what this article is mostly about.

Two institutional innovations of the period deserve notice regardless. The first is a single internal indirect tax, completed at +72, replacing a lattice of provincial imposts — a customs union of a sophistication achieved in Europe only after a century of effort, and enacted in one night. The second is a universal biometric register: a twelve-digit number, keyed to fingerprints and iris scans, issued to substantially the entire population, and used to authenticate welfare payments, subsidies, and eventually most financial transactions. It was the largest identity database ever assembled by any state, and it constitutes, at 3000 CE, precisely zero bytes of evidence.

## 7. Society

### 7.1 Endogamy in the genome

The subcontinent's population structure is exceptional and is fully legible in modern genomes. Descent groups practised strict marriage endogamy for something on the order of eighty to a hundred generations before the Combustion Age, producing thousands of distinct populations with founder effects comparable to those of small island communities, embedded within a subcontinent.

This structure is dateable from the length distribution of shared genomic segments. It relaxes, measurably, beginning in the +60s pT, first in the largest cities, then along transport corridors, then — sharply, during and after the Contraction — nearly everywhere, in a pattern that tracks displacement rather than doctrine.

The genomes of the living therefore record, with better resolution than any surviving text, both the persistence of the period's social order and the circumstances under which it stopped persisting. Whether that constitutes emancipation or catastrophe is a question the segment lengths cannot answer and about which the literature is not calm.

### 7.2 Language

Bhārat entered the period with two link languages and some twenty scheduled ones. The English it used was already, by +55 pT, spoken by more people within the subcontinent than in the language's homeland, and by +100 pT the plurality of all English speakers on Terra were South Asian.

Standard Terran's descent from that variety is not in dispute and is audible: the retroflex series, the invariant interrogative tag, the aspectual use of a copula that Late Combustion prescriptivists regarded as an error and corrected out of their newspapers while their own children spoke it. The prescriptivists are extinct. The children are the ancestors of everyone reading this.

### 7.3 The invisible gathering

At intervals of a few years, on a sandbank at the confluence of two rivers, Bhārat assembled the largest deliberate gathering of human beings in the history of the species — at its maximum, on the order of 10⁸ people across some weeks, in a temporary city of tents, roads, bridges, hospitals, and power distribution, erected on a floodplain and dismantled before the monsoon.

It leaves no trace whatsoever. The river takes the sand. The largest thing humans have ever done together is, archaeologically, indistinguishable from the river having been there.

The lesson is repeated in every introductory course, and it is not about Bhārat.

## 8. Biota

The period's characteristic Bhāratīya extinction event is instructive because it was an accident, it took under a decade, and its cause was a painkiller.

A cheap anti-inflammatory drug was administered to working cattle. It is lethal to *Gyps* vultures at trace dose. The birds, which had numbered in the tens of millions and constituted the subcontinent's entire large-scale carrion-disposal system, declined by more than 97 per cent between the +45s and the +60s pT. Nobody intended this; nobody noticed for years; the drug was withdrawn for veterinary use at +61.

The carrion did not stop being produced. It was consumed instead by feral dogs, whose population rose accordingly, and rabies with it. The period's own economists estimated the excess human mortality attributable to the vulture collapse, over five years alone, in the hundreds of thousands.

The same polity, in the same decades, doubled its tiger population under an intensive protection regime, and in +77 pT transported large cats from southern Africa to a central Bhāratīya reserve to reconstitute a species it had extirpated seventy years earlier. Both facts are true. Both are typical. The Combustion Age destroyed a genus by prescribing an analgesic and rebuilt another by flying it across an ocean, and did not experience these as related activities.

## 9. The Contraction in South Asia

South Asia is the continuity school's strongest ground and the systems-fragility school's clearest case, and both are arguing about the same evidence.

The subcontinent did not depopulate. Its population at +195 pT exceeded that at +55. Life expectancy, having risen from 62 to 70 years across the first three decades of the period, fell during the Contraction by an amount that the burial series — see §1.1 — cannot resolve to better than a decade, and recovered.

What ended was an arrangement: fossil groundwater, subsidised electricity, fixed nitrogen, a coastal export economy, a coal grid, and a settlement pattern that placed a fifth of the population within three metres of sea level. Each of these was independently understood, in the period, to be unsustainable on a timescale of decades. All of them ended within one lifetime, mostly not at once, and mostly not dramatically.

The northern plain emptied toward the south and the highlands. The delta emptied entirely. The Deccan, dry, high, and largely ignored by the Late Combustion economy, holds today the great majority of the region's population and every one of its major institutions.

There is no year in which anything fell.

## 10. Legacy

At 3000 CE the subcontinent's Combustion Age is attested by:

| Class | Survival | Bias |
|---|---|---|
| Fly ash, cement, plastiglomerate | Total | None; the physical record is superb |
| Aquifer isotopes, subsidence | Total | None |
| Coinage | +55 to c. +85, then unrepresentative | Severe after +85 |
| Skeletal series | c. 15 per cent of decedents | Confessional; unrepairable |
| Genomes (living) | Excellent for structure | Cannot distinguish death from migration |
| Longyear L-III metadata | Bit-exact | 4 × 10⁶ of 1.5 × 10⁹ people |
| Wasatch Corpus | Voluminous | Anglophone, urban, purchasing |
| Lunar artefacts | Perfect | Four objects |

One and a half billion people. Perhaps four million of them are individually named anywhere, and the names come from software commits.

The wheel tracks are 101 metres long. They run north from a lander, curve twice around a boulder, and stop. They will outlast the delta, the plain, the aquifer, the ash ponds, the reels, the Moon-orbiting silence, and every stone thing the subcontinent ever built, by a factor of about ten thousand.

The rover was named for a word in a dead language. It means *wisdom*.
