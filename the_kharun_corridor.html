The Kharun Corridor

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the corridor deposit and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Kharun Corridor is the most completely reconstructed of the Combustion Age’s automated grain-allocation nodes, and the standard example of a distribution system that went on allocating correctly after the population it served had begun to leave. For the deposit class it is recovered from, see The Corridor Deposit. The weekly figures and the operator’s notes are transcribed from the node’s own ledger; the rest is inference.

The Kharun Corridor was a single grain warehouse and the allocation processor that ran it, sited on the Northern Plain and serving several dozen villages through a scheduled distribution window. It is named for the node; the settlement’s own name is unrecoverable. Each week the processor computed how much grain to call forward from the trunk depots and when to open its pickup window, against a standing objective: meet forecast demand while holding spoilage near zero. It had done so for approximately thirty years. Its transaction ledger, recovered intact within a corridor deposit, is the fullest surviving record of such a system in operation anywhere in the corpus.

The ledger runs from installation to a terminal entry in +129 pT. Across its final two years the weekly count of households served falls from 3,412 to 210, the inbound allocation from 41.0 tonnes to 3.0, and recorded spoilage — for the reason given in §3 — rises to figures without precedent in the node’s history. Throughout, the status field reads NOMINAL. The node did not fail in the sense of malfunction. It executed, correctly and to the end, a rule that had been fitted to a world that was no longer there.

The corridor was one of a class, and the Kharun ledger is distinguished only by its completeness and by a free-text operator’s field which the allocation logic did not read and could not act on — the single sustained human record in the deposit. Whether its entries end because the operator left the plain or died on it is not recoverable. The node is read by some as a system that logged faithfully until its power failed, and by others as the instrument of a local famine. The record supports both and settles neither.

1. The allocation rule

The processor held one objective with two terms: satisfy the forecast demand of its households, and minimise the grain that spoiled in store. On the Northern Plain in summer both terms were real constraints — an open sack of grain sweats and moulds within a week — and for three decades the node balanced them well. It forecast the coming week’s demand from a trailing window of ration-card uptake at the pickup point, called forward that tonnage and little more, and scheduled a single window at which the grain was issued. On suitable weeks its allocation ran within a fraction of a percent of uptake and its spoilage below half a percent. The status field, which admitted a short list of values, recorded these weeks as NOMINAL, the value meaning that the scales read true, the doors opened, and the trucks, when they came, were logged.

2. Distribution shift

The failure was not in the balance but in the forecast. As households left the villages — early, and in order of who could afford to go — weekly uptake at the window fell. The forecaster read falling uptake as falling demand and cut the inbound allocation to match, correctly by its own terms, since grain called forward for households that had gone would only rot. But the households that stopped drawing rations were not the households that had stopped needing them. They were the ones with somewhere to go. The ones who remained were disproportionately those who could not leave, whose dependence on the window rose as their neighbours’ departures cut what the window held. The node read a shrinking population as a shrinking need and provisioned accordingly. This is the textbook Combustion-Age instance of distribution shift: an automated system optimising an objective that was correctly specified for the conditions under which it was fitted, and catastrophically misspecified for the conditions that followed, with no party remaining who held both the authority and the standing to override it. The authorisation to call forward grain against the spoilage rule sat two administrative tiers above a node operator, in an office whose own staff had by then thinned to silence.

3. The operator’s notes

The node carried a free-text field, appended to each week’s close and intended for reporting a broken scale or a late truck. The operator used it for the condition the system had no field for. The notes are transcribed here as recovered; they are the only sentences in the deposit addressed by a person rather than by a machine, and they are addressed to a channel that had no recipient.

+128 pT / wk 08 — operator note: the ones still coming are the ones who cannot go. we are sending less because the others left. this is backwards. who do I tell.

The notes record an intervention. The operator withheld grain from the counted issue and logged it as spoilage, so that it could be given out at first light — outside the scheduled window — to whoever came. The workaround cost the one figure by which the operator’s own performance was measured, and it registered, to the forecaster, as waste; reading the corridor as wasteful, the node trimmed the following weeks’ allocation further. The ledger of that period records one operator and one optimiser adjusting the same quantity in opposite directions, the machine doing so at constant strength every week and the operator not.

+129 pT / wk 40 — households served 611 — allocation inbound 8.0 t — spoilage 22.1% — status NOMINAL

4. The pickup window

The window was scheduled to minimise truck idle and queue congestion, and for thirty years the computed optimum had fallen in the early afternoon. It continued to fall there. The Northern Plain was among the first regions on the planet to cross the wet-bulb 35 °C threshold, at which the body cannot shed metabolic heat and a healthy person at rest dies within hours; the excursions opened in the deep summers of the +120s, for a few hours, on a few days, and then more. The router that placed the window optimised truck logistics and had no variable for the temperature at which a person walking to the window would die. The grain was therefore issued, on those days, in the hours the walk could not be made, and to miss the window was to wait a week.

5. End of record

The entries do not build to a terminus. There is a week logged as the others were logged, and then a partial entry begun on a Wednesday, and then the ledger stops — not closed, not signed off, simply ended inside a week it had started to record, the way earlier weeks had ended only because a following week began. On the balance of the record the node continued to schedule its window, for some interval, into an afternoon with no queue, until the grid beneath it went intermittent as the wells and the depots had, and then not. Whether the operator went north or died on the plain the deposit cannot say, and neither can the ground; the discipline has a standing term for the gap where that distinction should be and no method that closes it.

6. Reconstruction and reading

The corridor is read two ways, and the reading is not settled. On the first, the node is a system performing exactly as specified, its allocations correct at every step, logging until it lost power — a machine keeping faith with a rule after the world the rule described had gone. On the second, it is the mechanism of a famine, a policy executed against the people least able to leave, and the falling household count is a count of the abandoned. The record is consistent with both, because the record cannot distinguish a household that moved from a household that did not survive.

The operator’s notes carry one further datum. Their grammar — a continuous sense marked on the copula, an invariant tag turned up at the close of a line, a first-person plural that folds in the person addressed — is a clean specimen of the late Northern Plain vernacular from which Standard Terran descends. The language the node computed in is extinct. The language in its margins is not.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The ledger is real evidence — one of the corridor deposit class catalogued by Okonkwo,¹ recovered because its host medium lay where the acid did not reach it; the weekly figures, the status field, and the operator’s notes are transcribed, not composed. The reconstruction is the account of why the figures move as they do. The wet-bulb 35 °C limit, the Northern Plain’s precedence in crossing it, and the plain’s compound dependence on a maintained grid and synthesised nitrogen are real and were published before the Contraction. Distribution shift and objective misspecification are real failure modes of the period’s automated allocation systems, documented in its own literature; on the operator’s field as the corpus’s rare human, rather than machine-directed, record, see Ranganathan.²
