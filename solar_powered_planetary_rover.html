Solar-Powered Planetary Rover

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a much-copied epitaph and the interview account beneath it, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The afternoon a mission scientist put human words to a dead rover’s last signal — and the sentence that broke loose from her, until a whole planet remembered a machine saying what a person had only said about it. The sentence, the interview, the last stored-charge reading, and the dust are record; the woman, the room, and what she understood as she spoke are reconstruction.

She had read the machine’s moods off a column of numbers for the better part of fourteen years, and when the man with the recorder asked her what it had said at the end, she understood, in the half-second before she answered, that it had never said anything at all.

What it sent were numbers. Panel current, in amps. Battery temperature. State of charge, in watt-hours — a figure the whole team had watched climb every Martian morning and sag every Martian night for two decades, until they could feel the machine’s day in their own chests. There was no field in the telemetry for words. There had never been. It had a voice in exactly the sense a thermometer has a voice, and everyone in that operations room had spent fourteen years pretending otherwise, and had called it she, and tired, and cold, and game, and had meant every word of it.

It had been built to last ninety Martian days. A child had named it — had won a competition for the right, and named the pair of them, this one and its stuck twin half a planet away, both of them landing +59 pT, for two feelings rather than for anything either machine would ever do. Ninety days was the number on the warranty. This one ran five thousand one hundred and eleven. It crossed better than forty-five kilometres of a dead world at the pace of a slow walk stretched thin across years; it drove down into craters and hauled itself back out; it wintered by tilting its back toward a low sun to catch what little of it there was, and it reported, every single working day of all of it, a handful of numbers that meant it was still alive. It carried a few pellets of plutonium, each shedding about a watt of warmth for as long as physics would allow, but they only ever kept one small box above its own freezing point; everything else it did, it did on sunlight — gathered by day, banked in a battery, spent through the cold. It was, by every measure its makers had thought to write down, the most successful thing they ever built.

The twin had gone first, and quietly. It had bogged in soft ground after some two thousand two hundred sols, wheels turning in place, and could no longer point itself at the winter sun it needed, and so a season that this far one would have shrugged off simply closed over it. That was the shape of the ending on this world, when it came: not a fault, not a hazard, not a wrong turn. The withdrawal of the light.

They used to wake it with a song. Each Martian morning through the long missions, someone chose one, and the room played it while the first packet came down, a private joke that had hardened over the years into something closer to a devotion. The song was for them. The machine heard nothing, wanted nothing, grieved nothing; it counted watt-hours and sent them home. But a machine you have woken with a song ten thousand mornings running is not, by the end, a thing you can speak of in the third person, and none of them tried.

The storm came up out of the planet’s own southern spring, the way those storms do, and it did not stop at a region. It went global. The air filled with lifted dust until the sky over the machine was not dark the way a night is dark but thick — opaque, the sun a rumour somewhere behind it, an optical depth near eleven, something like ten times the murk through which the panels could still pull enough current to hold the battery. So the battery could not be held. And the heaters that kept the electronics above the cold at which solder cracks came off that same battery. On the tenth of June, +73 pT, it sent a partial image of a sky with almost nothing in it, and a number, on the order of twenty, and then it was quiet. The team went on sending into that quiet for eight months — several hundred commands, wake-up songs uplinked to the hour a Martian morning should have reached it — before the programme, on the thirteenth of February the following year, +74 pT, let it go.

The man with the recorder came weeks after that. He did not want the number. He wanted what the number meant, in words a person who was not an engineer could carry home. And because she loved the machine, and because translating its arithmetic into the first person had been as natural as breathing in that room for fourteen years, she gave him what he asked for. She said the charge was low. She said the light was going. Somewhere in the saying it slid, the way it always had, out of the third person and into the first, and she let it, because it was true, and because it was tender, and because the machine could not possibly mind.

My charge is low, and the light is going.

She had meant it as a gloss. A translation, handed to a layman, of a reading near twenty watt-hours under a sky at optical depth eleven. She had not meant a quotation, because there was nothing to quote; the machine had sent a number, and numbers do not speak in the first person, or in any person at all, and she of everyone alive knew it best.

It broke loose from her inside a single news cycle. The paraphrase shed her name and grew its own quotation marks; she said its charge was low became it said, and then the sentence stood up on its own, in the machine’s own mouth, an epitaph, and the epitaph went round the planet in a day and a night the way only the loud things ever did. People who had never once heard of the mission wept over it. Every word of it was true — the charge had been low; it had been getting dark — and that was precisely why no one thought to ask who had said it. A true sentence, kindly meant, seems to need no author. It was reproduced past all counting, and a thing reproduced past counting is a thing that survives; it outlived the machine, and it outlived the record of the room it was made in, so that later readers, finding the sentence everywhere and its source nowhere, took it for exactly what everyone already knew it to be — the last words a rover sent home across the dark.

The numbers did not survive. The morning packets, the charge curves, the eight months of commands into the quiet — the whole patient institutional record of why it stopped rested on upkeep that outlasted neither the machine nor the appetite to pay for it, and it is largely gone. The machine itself is not gone. It stands where it stopped, in the dust, intact, colder now than the day it fell silent, its tracks pressed into a ground that will hold them for something like ten million years, and it has never once been read again. Of everything it ever did, the one part that reached the far future in force was the single sentence it never sent.

She lived long enough to watch that happen and could not call it back, and could not, in the end, say what there was to correct. She had said a true thing, about a machine she loved, to a stranger who asked; the world had only moved the words an arm’s length to the left — from the person who spoke them to the machine they were about. The machine got the credit. The machine got the grief. The machine, which had asked for nothing and reported a voltage, got a deathbed line in a language it did not possess, addressed to the mourning of a whole planet it had never in fourteen years been built to address. She had lent it her voice for one afternoon and never got it back.

What the machine sent, on the tenth of June, +73 pT, was this: a picture of almost nothing, and a number near twenty, under a sky ten times too thick for its panels to drink. What the machine said was nothing, because saying was not among the things it could do. What everyone remembers, and will go on remembering, is a sentence in the first person about a low charge and a coming dark — composed by a woman in a chair, for a man with a recorder, about a machine that only ever counted watt-hours and never once, on that day or on any of the several hundred after it on which a dish on Terra was still turned faithfully toward its patch of empty sky, managed a single word. The record kept the sentence. It lost the chair. It does not lie about what was said; it only lies, sometimes, about who said it.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The sentence and its wording, the interview that produced it, the last stored-charge reading near twenty watt-hours, the storm’s optical depth near eleven, and the mission’s real durations and dates are record — the sentence most of all, which reached the future in more copies than anything else the mission left, because it was loud. The woman and the chair are inferred from the shape those copies make. The physics is real: a photovoltaic rover carries no sealed heat source of its own, so when lifted dust cuts the sunlight its panels drink, the battery cannot recharge and the heaters fail; a radioisotope generator would have freed it from sun and dust alike, at a cost in a single scarce isotope its programme had by then not made at home for a quarter-century (Ibarra 2994). The hardware is a mute survivor (Fujimori), intact and never read again; the misattribution runs backward through the record, the mirror of the authorship void (Sarmiento) on which Iyer built the term.
