Solar-Powered Planetary Rover

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

A wheeled robotic explorer that converts sunlight directly to working current rather than carrying a sealed source of its own heat, and is bound, on every world and in every decade it was built, by one absolute term: not distance, not terrain, not wear, but the withdrawal of its own star. It is not the short-lived, battery-only vehicle three crews drove and left standing on Luna within a single decade, and it is not the isotope-fuelled lineage that shares its wheels, its cameras, and its instruments but answers to no such term at all. For the southern-polar instance of the pattern, see Bhārat, Combustion Age. For the far more durable artefact its makers built to leave the system entirely, see Voyager Records.

Every machine in this entry was built to survive, for a while, the one force actually likely to end it, and in most cases the survival ran far past the date its own builders had printed as the probable end — years, in more than one case the better part of a decade, against a design life measured in weeks. The machines that did not outrun that date are the more informative case, because in nearly all of them the ending arrived, when it came, on schedule, in the same shape as every other ending in the set: not a collision, not a hazard, not an adversary, but the ordinary, foreseeable withdrawal of the sunlight each vehicle ran on. The interval this discipline can measure is therefore not how long a rover lasted — several lasted absurdly, almost embarrassingly, longer than anyone building them expected — but how long a given machine’s builders were willing to let it depend on the sun alone before they paid, in mass and in a single scarce material, to stop asking.

Two branches of the type run through the interval, unconnected in institution, separated in some cases by half a century, and converging on the same wall from four independent directions. One branch carried nothing but panels and a battery, sometimes cushioned by a few grams of a radioactive isotope doing nothing but keep an electronics box above its own freezing point; these machines had a designed life measured in weeks and, more than once, ran to years before something — cold, dust, or their own stuck wheels — finally caught them. The other branch carried a sealed generator heavy enough to warm and power the vehicle indefinitely, at a cost in mass, budget, and a single traceable material that the first branch never paid and, this entry argues, could not always have paid even had it wanted to.

The taphonomy is almost beside the point, and stated once: nothing on either world corrodes these machines. What ends a rover’s working life and what erases its physical remains are, for the first time anywhere in this corpus, almost entirely unrelated processes. The interesting question is not what survives — nearly everything does — but why the mission stopped, and what this discipline was still, three centuries after the fact, willing to believe a dead machine had said about it.

The two answers to the same night

A world with a thin or absent atmosphere loses heat to space fast once the sun is off it. Luna’s rotation gives a night some fourteen Terran days long, total and absolute, following a day of the same length; a Mars night is shorter, under thirteen hours, but the planet’s own atmosphere, thin as it is, can occasionally choose to make an artificial one lasting weeks, by filling itself with dust until almost no light reaches the ground at all. Either way the unheated interior of a machine left without power will fall toward the ambient cold — on Luna, below −150 °C is unremarkable — and at that temperature battery electrolyte loses most of its usable capacity or freezes outright, lubricants gel, and joints between dissimilar metals, having contracted at different rates through thousands of such cycles, eventually crack. None of this is exotic engineering. It is the reason a domestic thermometer is calibrated the way it is, run to a planetary extreme.

There were, from the first machine of the type, exactly two answers. Build cheap, light, and disposable, and let the first night or the first dust storm end the mission on its own terms — the design a caravan makes, provisioned only for the crossing it is actually attempting, timed to daylight and to season, carrying nothing against a winter it does not plan to meet. Or carry a sealed heat source that pays no attention to the sun at all — the design a permanent waystation makes, built and stocked, at a cost the caravan never accepts, to sit out exactly the season the caravan is built to avoid. The cheapest version of the second answer was a radioisotope heater unit: a pellet of an artificial isotope of plutonium, a few grams, doing nothing but decay and produce roughly a watt of heat apiece, with no moving parts to fail and an output set by physics rather than by anyone’s maintenance schedule. The most expensive version scaled the same principle into a full thermoelectric generator, converting that same steady decay heat into continuous electrical power as well, freeing a vehicle from solar geometry, from dust, and from night altogether. Not the ²³⁹Pu this discipline already reads as a dating horizon, incidentally, but that isotope’s shorter-lived, far hotter sister, ²³⁸Pu — a distinct material, made for a distinct purpose, on a production line distinct from any weapon.

Four decades, one wall

Every machine of the type met the same physics; not every machine met it the same way.

Read down the third column and the pattern is not survival bias; it is a design choice, made independently at least four times, on two worlds, by programmes with no institutional relationship to one another. Read down the fourth and the two branches separate cleanly. Every heater-carrying machine outran its own planned life, several of them by a factor of ten or more — cheap insurance, once paid for, tends to be underused rather than exactly used. The one machine in the table built with no heater at all ran to precisely the length its makers designed it to, no more and no less, and stopped on the single night its makers had already told the public to expect it would.

The isotope that stopped being made

Every heater in the table but the last row, and every generator, drew on the same material: an isotope made nowhere in nature in usable quantity, manufactured by irradiating a specific neptunium target in a small number of reactors and chemically separating the plutonium that results — a process producing negligible fissile byproduct, which is exactly why the material could be handled, unlike its weapons-grade sister, as an ordinary if closely tracked civilian commodity.

One of the two national programmes responsible for the twinned Mars pair and both full-generator machines stopped domestic production of that isotope entirely in 1988 CE, +43 pT, as the wider armaments infrastructure that had originally sized the production line wound down at the end of a confrontation between blocs. The decision was administrative, published, and attached to no controversy whatsoever at the time. Its consequence took a generation to become news: a stockpile inherited from the old production run, most of it already earmarked to missions already committed, and an independent published projection — years ahead of the date it named, in the period’s own specialist and general press alike — that the remaining usable material would not outlast the missions already in the pipeline much past 2018 CE. Only after that public accounting did the same programme resume domestic production, in test quantities from +68 pT (2013 CE), producing a first new batch of roughly one hundred grams in +70 pT (2015 CE). Twenty-seven years separate the last old-line gram from the first new one — the length, this discipline’s demographic tables suggest, of most of a working career, spent making nothing of the one material every long-duration exploring machine on either world ultimately needed.

Extending her established reading of infrastructure narrowed to a single filament — a canal, a landing station, a breeding contest — Ibarra (2994) reads the isotope line as the model’s most literal case yet: not a route or a trait but a single manufactured material, made at a handful of reactors, on which every heater-carrying and every generator-carrying machine in the record ultimately depended, and which one of its two major producers simply stopped making for the length of a working lifetime, on purpose, without concealment, and without much appetite to reverse the decision until the deadline it had itself already published was nearly on top of it.

Whether the southern-polar programme’s decision to fly no heater at all, decades into the record above, reflects that same scarcity, or reflects instead a preference the record cannot distinguish from one, is not settled. No programme that built a heater-less machine is known to have costed and rejected the alternative in any surviving document; the absence of a paper trail is, on Solberg’s established reading, itself the signature of a choice rather than a constraint — a short, complete, inexpensive success accepted on its own terms rather than merely endured for want of anything better. The record does not adjudicate between scarcity and preference. It was never asked to keep the evidence that would.

What the wall does not touch

Nothing above touches the machines themselves. A rover killed by cold, by dust, or by its own stuck wheels stops working; it does not decay. Vacuum and near-vacuum host no oxidation this discipline can measure on any useful timescale, no water, and — so far as the record allows anyone to say — no biology; a Martian surface, thin-atmosphered and laced with reactive perchlorate dust, is measurably less inert than Luna’s, but neither approaches, on a scale of centuries, anything an unshielded structure experiences on Terra. The wheel tracks of the southern-polar pair are legible today for the same reason the tracks of every other machine in the table are legible: nothing has come to disturb them but the slow, random battering of micrometeorite infall, gardening the regolith at a rate this discipline estimates will need on the order of ten million years to erase a track a few centimetres deep. Exposed polymer and paint embrittle under unfiltered radiation over centuries rather than millennia; the aluminium, titanium, and untreated steel beneath do not meaningfully move at all. Any radioisotope heater aboard a machine in this table has, by 3000 CE, passed roughly eleven half-lives of its ²³⁸Pu charge — its original watt of warmth fallen to a small fraction of one percent, cold now itself, in a machine that was cold already.

The record they left behind of why they stopped is a different matter, and survives far worse. The dust-storm forecasts, the fault logs, the ground-station handover schedules that let a signal cross from one hemisphere’s antenna to the next as Terra turned under it — all of it depended on the same continuous institutional upkeep this discipline elsewhere reads as a debt that falls due unannounced. Long stretches of the day-by-day mission record for every machine in the table are not recoverable at all, for the same reason the satellite forecasts that once tracked a warming reef are not: not because nobody was watching, but because the institution watching did not outlast what it watched. What reached 3000 CE without needing a login is the hardware itself, motionless, legible without a subscription, and further from erasure than almost anything its makers ever built to remain on their own planet — a stronger case of the mute survivor, Fujimori’s term for a crash-protected but silent memory, than the flight recorders she coined it for: those, at least, went dark within decades of their last reading. These have simply never been read again.

A sentence with no antenna behind it

The dust storm that ended the longer-lived of the twinned Mars pair is, by the volume of its own published record, the best-documented death in the table. Atmospheric opacity at the rover’s own position climbed to roughly ten times the level at which its panels could still recharge a battery through an ordinary daytime; a last coherent transmission arrived on 10 June 2018, +73 pT — a partial image and a numeric reading of stored charge, on the order of twenty watt-hours. Recovery commands continued, by the operating programme’s own count, into the hundreds, across the following eight months, before the mission was formally closed on 13 February 2019, +74 pT.

Within weeks of that closure, a single first-person sentence about a low battery and a darkening sky began circulating, attributed directly to the rover as its own final words. The attribution does not survive contact with the format it claims to quote: the machine’s telemetry carried no natural-language field of any kind, only numeric channel readings, and the sentence itself first appears not in any downlinked packet but in a published account of an interview, in which two of the mission’s own scientists explained, in plain language, for a public readership, what the last numeric packet meant. The paraphrase outran its source within the same news cycle, hardened from a scientist’s gloss into a direct quotation, then into an epitaph reproduced across the planet, then, this discipline must now record with some discomfort, into a fact simply repeated by two further popular generations of scholarship without a return to the interview it came from.

Extending Sarmiento’s established account of Late Combustion text that resists any confident assignment to a human or a machine author, Iyer (2995) reads the case as that problem’s mirror image — not a text nobody could attribute, but a text everybody attributed, confidently, to exactly the wrong author — and coins the borrowed voice: an utterance popularly credited to a machine’s own signal that the underlying technical record shows was, in fact, composed by a person, explaining that signal, for an audience the machine was never built to address. The record does not lie about what was said, she writes; it only lies, sometimes, about who said it. Marchetti’s model of an early, reasonable reading propagating uncorrected, developed for a padding routine misread as scripture, applies here too, and applies to a case that reached more readers, faster, than anything else in her own catalogue.

No signal, in the strict sense the mission’s own equipment was built to send, ever said anything about batteries or darkness. What the equipment sent, on 10 June 2018, was a number near twenty. What it meant, everyone competent to read it agreed at the time and agrees now. What it said, in words, it never once managed, on that day or on any of the many after it on which a dish on Terra was still, out of a habit nobody had yet found a reason to break, pointed at the right patch of an empty sky.
