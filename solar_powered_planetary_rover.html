Solar-Powered Planetary Rover

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a much-copied first-person sentence and the fragmentary mission record beneath it, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Solar-Powered Planetary Rover is a wheeled robotic explorer that drew its power from sunlight rather than from a sealed radioisotope source, and the standing example of a machine remembered across a thousand years for a first-person sentence that a person, not the machine, composed to explain it. For the sealed-isotope kind that owed nothing to the sun, and for the manufactured isotope both kinds depended on, see below. The sentence is record, and survives in more copies than anything else these machines left; the voltages beneath it, and the ground-segment record of why any single machine fell silent, largely do not.

The Solar-Powered Planetary Rover is a wheeled robotic explorer that took its motive and instrument power from photovoltaic cells rather than from a sealed radioisotope generator. Machines of this class were built and flown by at least four unconnected national programmes across two worlds between +25 and +78 pT, and they share one absolute constraint: an unheated interior falls toward the ambient cold once sunlight is withdrawn — a lunar night runs about fourteen Terran days end to end, and a Martian dust storm can black out a landing site for weeks — and cold cracks solder joints, gels lubricant, and freezes or permanently degrades battery electrolyte. To that constraint the age gave two engineering answers, and a machine was defined by which one it carried.

The record’s most decorated instance carried the cheaper answer. It was one of a twinned pair landed +59 pT, warranted for ninety Martian days, named — by a public competition, for two dispositions rather than for anything either machine would do — and left to draw its power from the sun. One of the pair bogged in soft ground after some 2,208 sols, could no longer tilt its panels toward the low winter sun, and was closed over by a season it would otherwise have shrugged off. The other ran roughly 5,111 sols and better than forty-five kilometres, at the pace of a slow walk stretched across years, until a dust storm out of the planet’s southern spring went global and lifted the atmospheric optical depth over it to about 10.8 — on the order of ten times the murk through which its panels could still hold charge. Its last coherent data came down on 10 June 2018 (+73 pT); the programme sent commands into the quiet for eight months, several hundred of them, and closed the mission on 13 February 2019 (+74 pT).

What reached the far future in force was none of this. It was a single sentence, in the first person, about a low charge and a coming dark — a sentence the machine never sent, because sending words was not among the things it could do. It reported voltages. A person, glossing those voltages for an audience the machine had never been built to address, put them into the first person; the paraphrase shed its author inside a news cycle and stood up in the machine’s own mouth. This is the type case of the borrowed voice (Iyer 2995), read as the mirror of the authorship void: not text of unrecoverable authorship, but text whose true author is recoverable and was simply, tenderly, mislaid. The record does not lie about what the sentence says. It lies only about who said it.

The two answers to the night

The constraint is thermal, not electrical. Sunlight can be banked in a battery and spent after dark; warmth cannot, and once the interior crosses the cold thresholds of its own materials the machine does not restart. The first answer was to accept the wall: build light, build cheap, and fly a mission short enough to end before the dark did. This was not merely a cost pushed onto the future but, on one reading, an accepted design limit chosen in the open — the standing counter to the capacity school (Solberg 2991). A southern-polar lunar pair flown with no heat source at all (+78 pT) ran exactly its one designed lunar day and stopped, as intended; see Bhārat, Combustion Age for that instance.

The second answer was to pay, in mass and in a single scarce manufactured isotope, for warmth that owed nothing to the sun. The isotope was ²³⁸Pu — distinct from the ²³⁹Pu that dates the whole Combustion Age in the strata — worked either into a radioisotope heater unit, a passive pellet shedding on the order of one watt with no moving parts, or into a full thermoelectric generator, roughly 4.8 kg of plutonium dioxide yielding some 110–120 electrical watts and immune to solar geometry and to dust alike. A machine built on the generator is bounded by neither night nor storm; the full-generator Mars pairs (+67 and +76 pT) would have driven through the dust that killed the solar rover without registering it. The twinned solar pair split the difference: each carried a few ²³⁸Pu pellets, about a watt apiece, but they only ever kept one small electronics box above its own freezing point. Everything else the machine did, it did on sunlight.

The machines and their runs

The lineage begins on the Moon. The first lunar pair (+25–26 pT) carried twelve polonium-210 heater units, was planned for roughly three lunar days, and ran eleven — about 322 days and 10.5 kilometres; the second (+28 pT) ran some four months and 39 kilometres. The first Mars rover (+52 pT) was planned for 7 to 30 sols and ran 83, ended not by cold but by the failure of its relay lander’s battery.

The twinned Mars pair (+59 pT) is the heart of the record, and its two endings are the two shapes the dark takes on that world. The stuck machine died of geometry: unable to point its panels at the winter sun, it fell below the temperature at which its electronics survive, and a routine season became a terminal one. The far one died of the sky. When the +73 pT storm filled the air with lifted dust, the panels could no longer recharge the battery, and the electrical survival heaters that drew on that same battery failed with it. The last packet was a partial image of a sky with almost nothing in it and a number, on the order of twenty watt-hours of stored charge, under an optical depth near 10.8. The morning wake-up commands went on being uplinked, to the hour a Martian morning should have reached it, for the eight months before the programme let it go.

The isotope that stopped being made

Every heater- or generator-carrying machine in the record depended on ²³⁸Pu, and ²³⁸Pu came from a small number of reactors — single-filament infrastructure extended from a route or a trait to a manufactured isotope (Ibarra 2994). The programme that built both full-generator Mars machines stopped domestic production of the isotope entirely in 1988 CE (+43 pT). An independently published projection warned that the inherited stockpile would not outlast committed missions past about 2018 CE; production resumed only in test quantities from +68 pT, the first new batch of roughly 100 grams in +70 pT — a twenty-seven-year gap in the one material every long-duration exploring machine on two worlds ran on.

The gap frames the decorated instance sharply. The rover the future remembers for a poignant death by dust was the solar kind — the kind whose vulnerability to dust was, by then, an accepted economy rather than a necessity — and the answer that would have carried it straight through the storm was an isotope its own world had, for a quarter of a century, chosen not to make. The record keeps the choice as a production figure and a projection date. It does not editorialise, and neither did the people who made it; the economics were published.

The borrowed voice

Weeks after the mission closed, a person explaining the last signal to a lay public translated its final reading into plain speech — the charge was low, the light was going — and the translation, being true and kindly meant, needed no author to travel. Within a single news cycle the paraphrase grew its own quotation marks; she said its charge was low became it said, and the sentence stood up on its own as an epitaph in the machine’s voice:

My charge is low, and the light is going.

Every word of it was accurate to the telemetry, which is precisely why no one thought to ask who had said it: a true sentence, tenderly meant, seems to need no speaker. It was reproduced past counting, and a thing reproduced past counting is a thing that survives. It outlived the machine. It also outlived the patient institutional record of why the machine stopped — the morning packets, the charge curves, the several hundred commands sent into the quiet — which rested on continuous funded upkeep and was largely taken by Attrition, the. Later readers, finding the sentence everywhere and its source nowhere, took it for exactly what everyone already knew it to be: the last words a rover sent home across the dark.

The hardware is a mute survivor (Fujimori 2983), the coinage extended from crash-protected flight memory to an intact, silent exploring machine never read again. It stands where it stopped, in the dust, colder now than the day it fell quiet; it does not corrode on any timescale this discipline can measure; the tracks it pressed into the crust are estimated legible for something like ten million years, there being no rain to wash them and nothing living to disturb them. The ²³⁸Pu pellets that once kept its one warm box alive have by 3000 CE passed roughly eleven half-lives, their warmth fallen to a small fraction of one percent, and the machine has never once been read again. What it sent, on the last day, was a picture of almost nothing and a stored-charge reading under a sky at optical depth near 10.8. What everyone remembers is a sentence in the first person. The record does not lie about what was said; it only lies, sometimes, about who said it.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The sentence and its wording, the fact of its lay-audience origin, the last stored-charge reading on the order of twenty watt-hours, the storm’s optical depth near 10.8, and the mission’s real durations and closure dates are record — the sentence most of all, which reached the future in more copies than anything else the mission left, because it was loud. The particular person who composed it, and the room it was composed in, are inferred from the shape those copies make; the misattribution runs backward through the record, the mirror of the authorship void on which Iyer built the term. The physics is real: a photovoltaic rover carries no sealed heat source, so when lifted dust cuts the sunlight its panels drink, the battery cannot recharge and the electrical survival heaters fail; a radioisotope generator would have freed it from sun and dust alike, at a cost in a single scarce isotope its programme had by then not made at home for twenty-seven years (Ibarra 2994). The hardware is a mute survivor (Fujimori), intact and never read again.
