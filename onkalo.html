Onkalo

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the excavated repository beneath the island and the surviving fragments of the closure record, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

Onkalo is the most completely excavated of the Combustion Age’s deep geological repositories for spent nuclear fuel, and the discipline’s standard case of a barrier built to outlast, by orders of magnitude, the institution that was meant to remember what it held. The host rock, the canisters, and the closure sequence are transcribed from the excavated repository; that no warning marker was ever raised at the surface is read from the empty ground above it.

Onkalo was a deep geological repository for spent nuclear fuel, cut roughly four hundred metres into the Fennoscandian Shield beneath an island on a northern coast. The host rock is a gneiss of the Shield that had been rock for some 1.9 billion years — older than shelled animals, older than complex life — and had not moved in any of them. Into it were driven, over a working lifetime, on the order of fifty kilometres of tunnel and a grid of vertical deposition holes, each hole taking a single canister of fuel packed in clay. At capacity the repository held about 6,500 tonnes of uranium (tU) in roughly 3,250 canisters. Its access tunnel was begun at +59 pT (2004 CE); routine emplacement began at +81 pT (2026 CE); closure at capacity was planned for about +175 pT (2120 CE).

The barrier was specified to isolate its fuel for far longer than any institution existed that could explain it. The regulatory safety horizon was one hundred thousand years; the engineers, not trusting the horizon, ran their calculations out to one million. The record of what lay in which hole — the fuel, its origin, its burn-up, its date — was kept on the same media, in the same registers, as any office’s accounts, and no such medium had ever, in the whole history of the trade, outlived the institution that recopied it. The distance between what the barrier was built to survive and what its explanation was built to survive is the closure gap (Lindqvist 2971), and at Onkalo the gap was named, measured, and published decades before the last hole was filled.

Onkalo’s designers closed that gap in one direction only. They built the container to outlast the country and made no attempt to keep the label legible: having read the deep past’s rich graves — every one marked, and every one opened because it was marked — they raised no warning marker at the surface at all. Closure fell inside the Contraction (+133–195 pT), and whether the final panels were sealed on schedule or left a panel short the record cannot decide. Onkalo is read against a repository in the other hemisphere that chose the opposite course, to announce its hazard as loudly as ten thousand years of erosion would allow; the two are the discipline’s paired case in whether a buried danger is better hidden or declared, and the case is not settled.

The barrier

The engineered barrier is three nested materials, each real and each specified against a failure the others do not cover. Innermost is the fuel, gridded into a cast insert of nodular cast iron that holds up to twelve assemblies rigid and carries the mechanical load. Around the insert is a shell of copper fifty millimetres thick. The shell’s lid is closed not by fusion welding, which leaves a weld’s porosity, but by friction-stir welding: a rotating tool is driven through the join until the copper goes plastic and knits in the solid state, never once brought to its melting point, so that no path is opened where none was before. In water without dissolved oxygen, copper is, by the makers’ own specification, not slow to corrode but immune. The finished canister masses on the order of twenty-five tonnes and is lowered upright into a hole bored some eight metres into the tunnel floor.

Around the canister goes the buffer: compacted bentonite, in blocks and rings, packed to a specified density. On contact with the first groundwater to reach it the clay swells against the rock and against the copper until it has sealed every gap it can find, and thereafter does the single slow thing it is for — holding the diffusion of anything dissolved to millimetres in a thousand years. The one recognised route by which the copper can still be attacked is dissolved sulphide, delivered by groundwater or generated by microbes at the clay’s margin (Halvorsen 2974); the buffer’s density is set, in part, to hold sulphide transport below the rate at which it would matter across the safety horizon. The rock supplies the last barrier and the oldest: 1.9 billion years of stability, chosen because it had already done, unwitnessed, far more than was now being asked of it.

The safety case

The safety case is a set of numbers no human institution has ever matched. The regulator required isolation across one hundred thousand years; the operator’s own calculations were carried, as a sensitivity test, to one million. Neither figure corresponds to anything a person or a state could feel. The oldest continuous human institutions of the period had persisted for thousands of years, not hundreds of thousands, and the media on which the repository’s own contents were recorded had a working life measured in years to a few decades before they required funded recopying — the general failure the discipline calls the Attrition.

The far term of the case is governed by a handful of long-lived fission products, of which the limiting one is iodine-129: soluble, mobile in groundwater, and effectively undiminished on any timescale the repository could be asked about. Its behaviour is why the buffer and the copper carry the burden they do, and why the case is stated in the units it is stated in. The record that would explain any of this to a later finder is precisely the part of the system with no such durability. The dependence is total and single-threaded — one continuing institution to fund the recopying, maintain the registers, and hold the meaning of the site — a single-filament dependency of exactly the kind that ran through the period’s other centennial disposal programmes (Ibarra 2977). Lindqvist put the general case flatly: “the closure gap is the distance between what an engineered barrier is built to survive and what the institution that explains it is built to survive, and the gap is never zero, and it is only sometimes published.” At Onkalo it was published in full, and the holes were filled regardless.

The marker question

Whether to mark such a site, and how, was a genuine and unresolved question of the period, and the surviving record preserves two opposed schools. The marker school is documented at a bedded-salt transuranic-waste facility in the other hemisphere, whose advisory panels, across the 1980s and 1990s of the Common Era, designed a warning meant to be understood ten thousand years on without any surviving language: a field of stone spikes thrown up angled and hostile, earthworks legible from the air, buried panels of pictures and text, and — past all of it — a proposed hereditary order of custodians charged with carrying the meaning forward by rite, an “atomic priesthood” modelled on a religious tradition that had in fact relayed a single message across some two thousand years. The whole apparatus was built to say one thing: that nothing of value is here, that the place is not honoured, that the danger is real and is to the body.

Onkalo’s operators read the same deep past and drew the reverse lesson. A sign that says do not dig here is read, given enough centuries, as a sign that something worth burying was hidden here; a marker advertises rather than deters. This is the obscurity school: spend the effort on the container, return the surface to ordinary ground, and tell the future nothing. Marchetti (2985) set the two approaches as a matched pair — Onkalo against the Longyear Deposit, the archive that explained itself with a printed leader anyone could read, and Onkalo the repository that deliberately did not — and declined to say which was the sounder bet, on the ground that the experiment cannot be graded inside the lifetime of anyone equipped to grade it.

Closure

The closure record shows the schedule that had the repository finishing decades earlier going loose and then meaningless across its final years. Deliveries — of fuel, of bentonite, of concrete and spares — are recorded as arriving less often; the administrative office two tiers above the site answers more slowly in the record and then not at all. The barrier’s specification is indifferent to this: a filled and plugged hole performs as specified whether or not anyone remains at a desk to be told so. The access ramp was backfilled behind the work from the deepest reach outward, clay and crushed rock tamped against the walls until the road that had carried every canister down was continuous with the rock it had been cut from, and the surface above was left as ordinary ground with nothing on it to say what lay beneath — which was the whole of the design.

The rock had been rock for 1.9 billion years and would not register the hundred thousand the regulator asked of the copper, nor the million the engineers checked against. Sealed in the dark below, undisturbed, the fuel keeps the one appointment every institution that ever heard of it will fail to keep: iodine-129, decaying at the only rate it has ever decayed, losing across the whole thousand-year span of the Attrition a fraction of itself too small for any instrument this discipline owns to detect. Its half-life is 15.7 million years.

Provenance and ground

A reconstruction. The evidence is the repository itself — the backfilled panels, the plugs, the sealed access ramp, and the recovered canisters — from which the ~1.9-billion-year gneiss, the three-barrier specification, the emplacement and closure dates, and the ~6,500 tU across ~3,250 canisters in some fifty kilometres of tunnel are transcribed. That the surface was left unmarked is inference from the empty ground above; whether closure was completed on schedule or left a panel short, the record cannot decide. The physics is real and pre-Contraction: copper’s immunity in anoxic water and its one sulphide-mediated corrosion route, the solid-state friction-stir weld, the self-sealing bentonite buffer, the ~1.9-Gyr Fennoscandian gneiss, a 100,000-year regulatory horizon sensitivity-tested to 1,000,000, and iodine-129’s 15.7-million-year half-life, undecayed on any timescale this discipline can measure. The paired marker case — the obscurity school here against the marker school’s spike field and hereditary custodians — is drawn from the period’s own published debate on how to warn a future that shares no language.
