Longyear Deposit

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the site’s accession register and the chemistry of its flooded floor, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

On a summer day at the silver-film archive inside a spent coal mountain at 78°N, a worker carries the last, late-arriving consignment up a ladder to the only shelf still free — the top one — not knowing that shelf height alone will decide, a thousand years on, what of the age survives. The dates, reel counts, depositor classes, and rack tiers are the register’s; the worker, the ladder, and the summer light are inference from the shape those numbers make.

The truck came up in the small hours, which in high summer this far north meant broad daylight, the sun going round the ridge without ever setting. He met it at the portal in his coat. Three hundred metres in, past the old coal workings, the chamber held at four below, and had held there through every summer of his life and every summer before there were men on the mountain to feel it — and the firm sold that cold the way other firms sold a vault door.

That was the pitch, and he had given it himself, to depositors on the little viewing platform. Everything you have ever tried to keep, he told them, needs someone to keep it: to copy it, to feed it power, to pay the invoice that renews it, and to keep doing all three forever, or lose it inside a lifetime. Here you paid once. The reels were silver, laid on a ribbon of polyester that did not care about the cold or the years; the picture on them was made of metal. To read one you needed light and a lens. Nothing else. No machine, no current, no company still in business to sell you back the key. He liked that part. It was true, and it was rare to be paid to say a true thing.

Each reel opened with its own instructions. The first turns of every ribbon were printed large enough to read by eye — a picture of the grid of light and dark squares that carried the rest, and then, in patient stages a child could follow, how the squares made numbers, and the numbers letters, and the letters the languages that came after. Whoever found the reel, the leader assumed, would have a lens and know how to count, and nothing else, and from those two things it built them back up into a reader. He had shelved a thousand of them and still thought it a strange and hopeful thing: a message folded so that anyone at all, arriving with nothing, could open it.

He worked the way he had been taught, which was the way his back preferred. Heavy things low. A full reel in its canister was a real weight, and a canister brought down off a high shelf onto a man’s head was how men got hurt in a cold room far from anywhere, so you filled each bay from the floor up, and you went up the ladder only when the floor was full. The first consignments had come three years back, at the opening, with a small ceremony and a flag: the papers of three national archives, a church’s manuscripts, a museum’s holdings — the important ones, the founding ones, the reason there had been a flag. They sat where he had put them, low, at knee and chest, in the good reachable places nearest the aisle, exactly where such things belonged.

Down the ridge, the other vault — the famous one, the seeds — had taken water at its door the same season this place opened. Meltwater into the entrance tunnel, where it froze. It had been in all the bulletins: a warning, everyone agreed, and one that had been heeded. This chamber was dry. He had never seen a drop in it. When the cutting crew went deeper the year before there had been a smell off the fresh coal face, the low struck-match smell of the seam, the sulfur in it, and someone had run a fan and it had gone, and nobody wrote it down because there was nothing to write. The rock had been frozen since before the first people. The firm’s own brochure said so, in a proud box on the second page, alongside the boast that no place on all of Terra was warming faster than this ridge — four to seven times the pace of the whole world — printed as a wonder, a reason the place would be remembered. About that, in its way, it was right.

The late crate came off the truck last, because it had come last of everything.

It should have arrived in spring. It had left a port on the far side of the planet, and then a sickness had shut the sea-lanes — the kind that empties an airport and closes a border — and the crate had sat somewhere warm for five months while the world held its breath, and now here it was in high summer, months out of its slot. Its manifest was the oddest he would handle all year. One hundred and eighty-six reels; and on them, a company had paid to write down every openly shared program in the world, or near enough all of it, some twenty trillion characters of instructions composed by the keepers of machines for machines, copied out on a single Sunday and sent north to lie in the cold.

He did not ask why. People paid to keep the strangest things, and the fee was the same by the metre whatever the metre held.

With it came a book. Not a reel — a printed book, bound, meant to be read by hand, and addressed on its first page, in plain words, to whoever should come upon all this a long time hence. It set out to explain the world the programs took for granted: what a transistor was, and a wire, and a network; what the machines were made of, and how they had been coaxed out of sand. He turned the pages under the work-lamp with his gloves off. Eleven of them went to the etching of a single chip, careful as a catechism. Not one went to what a company was, or what the money had been for, or why the people who wrote all these instructions had troubled to write them. The book assumed its reader would have lost the machines and kept everything else — kept the offices, the trade, the reasons — and would need only to be shown how the small silver cities were built. He thought it a fine thing, and a slightly ridiculous one: a letter sealed with such care, and so certain about which half of the world would still be standing to break the seal.

Then he shelved the book, because that was the job, and the job did not care what he thought.

He wrote the crate into the register first, in his own hand, the way the firm’s whole method required — the date, the depositor’s long foreign name, the count of reels, and the shelf they went to, so that a thing put down could be found again. Against this one he wrote upper, back run. Because the reachable bays were full. Three years of consignments had taken the floor and the chest-high runs and the good places by the aisle; the archives and the church and the museum sat there in the dignity of their reserved spots, low, at hand. What was left for a hundred and eighty-six late reels was the top — the run above head height, along the back wall, where a man needed the ladder and both hands and a word he would not use in front of a depositor.

So he fetched the ladder. He carried the canisters up two at a time and slid them home on the high shelf, above his reach, out of the way, where they would be a nuisance to anyone who ever wanted them down again. It took most of the morning. He was thorough about it, because he was a thorough man, and because a reel that fell was a reel somebody had paid to keep.

He set the last one on the top shelf, squared it with the rest, and came down the ladder. It had taken him half a morning, and it was the least important thing he did all week.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The accession register is real evidence — the Longyear operators, alone among the age’s depositors, recorded what they took and where they shelved it — so the dates, depositor classes, reel counts, and rack tiers are transcribed, including the software consignment’s late arrival and its place on the upper racks; the worker and his ladder are inference. The chemistry is not: silver on inert polyester, until the permafrost failed, meltwater ran the seam, and the pyrite in the coal made sulfuric acid — ordinary acid mine drainage — that turned the silver to black sulfide and took every image below the waterline. Survival ran with height above the floor (Halvorsen’s regression¹). The highest consignment was the software (the Code Deposit, 186 reels, ~21×10¹² bytes, delayed five months by a pandemic, lodged +75 pT / 2020 CE); its accompanying Instruction is the only period text written expressly for us. The seed vault’s entrance flood (+72 pT / 2017 CE) really was reported as a warning heeded.
