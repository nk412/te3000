# Code Deposit

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the uncurated commit metadata of consignment L-III of the Longyear Deposit and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The Combustion Age left one bit-exact snapshot of its public software, and it says almost nothing about the people who wrote it — except in the timestamps, where the hour and the longitude of every change survive by accident, and can be read as a census of who was awake and at work.** The offsets, handles, and messages are transcribed from the deposit; the single working day rebuilt from them at the end is inference.

---

She had 186 reels and could not find a single person in them.

The reels held the completest text the age had left — on the order of 21 × 10¹² bytes, tens of millions of separate works, one Sunday's snapshot of a large public repository of shared software, copied whole and shelved, by the accident that saved it, high enough to stay above what took the rest. It was bit-exact and self-verifying: the repository had stored not files but histories, each state named by a fingerprint computed from its own contents, the names chained, so that a recovered change either reproduced the fingerprint it was stored under or it did not, and every one of them did. Nothing in a thousand years had altered a byte the encoding could not have caught. More than nine parts in ten of all bit-exact surviving text of the period was on these reels. It was the one promise the age had made to whoever came after, and kept exactly. And read as what it was — instructions, overwhelmingly in one interpreted language, for machines — it told her almost nothing about the hands that had written it.

She knew the shape of the disappointment; it was old. For the better part of two centuries the deposit had been read as the age's literature, because it was the literature that had survived, until Marchetti took the reading apart: the corpus's most-copied fragment, a routine of a few lines for padding a string, was emblematic of nothing but its own shelf, and the fourteen thousand near-identical copies of a routine for capitalising the first letter of a word were not a canon but a machine faithfully preserving its own duplication. Rautio had named the trap for good — *the complete fragment*, exhaustive within its narrow domain and a vanishing sliver of everything past it. *It is not a small sample of everything,* Rautio wrote; *it is everything of a small thing.* The code did testify to one thing about itself, and eloquently: its own dependence. An eleven-line routine for padding a string, withdrawn by its author in a dispute in +71 pT, had turned out to sit so far beneath so much else that construction halted across the whole network for the better part of a day — a fact about the shape of the code, filed at the time as a minor incident, and about the lives that wrote it, nothing. Even the deposit's one text composed for a distant reader, the patient guide its makers called a tech tree, spent its pages on how a machine was etched and not a line on who had done the etching, or why. The programs would not speak of people. She had stopped asking them to.

What spoke — and the whole census hung on this turn — was the bookkeeping no one had thought of as content. Every change in every history carried, as a matter of the tool's mechanism and not of anyone's intent, a handle chosen by whoever made it, an address, a short message, and two moments: when the change was written, and when it was recorded. And each of those moments was stored not as a bare instant but together with a number — the offset of the writer's own clock from a single global reference clock, the hours and half-hours by which their local time ran ahead of it or behind.

That number was the whole of it. She had understood it slowly and then all at once. A clock's offset is a tag of local time, and local time is a tag of place: to set your clock five hours behind the reference is to say, without meaning to say anything at all, where on the turning planet you were standing when you did the work. Nobody composing a change had ever regarded the offset as more than plumbing. Nobody had shaped it, curated it, or addressed it to a reader. It was the one layer of the entire deposit that was a true unposed sample of what people did, and it was unposed for precisely the reason that made it trustworthy: the code had been written to be seen, and the timestamp had not.

So she counted. Tens of millions of offsets, sorted and stacked, resolved into a map of the contributing population across the longitudes — and the map leaned. It leaned hard, into a narrow band of the world's meridians, a few clusters where the great mass of the work had been done, and thinned to almost nothing across whole spans of longitude where, on any full census of the living, most people were. The deposit did not record who could write software; it recorded who had a machine, a fast enough line, and a wage or a spare night to spend giving the work away in the open. Who that was, and who it was not, the offsets gave with a directness no other source for the age approached. She wrote the distribution down and did not try to make it say more than it could. The band was the finding. The empty longitudes were also the finding, and she let them stand empty.

Sorted the other way — not by where the clocks were set but by the hour of the local day each change was made — the same timestamps gave a rhythm. The count rose through a local morning, dipped at a midday hour, ran on through the afternoon, and fell away into evening; twice in every seven days it collapsed to a trough and recovered, the two-day rest of a working week drawn without a word being said about it; and along the floor of every night ran a long thin tail of changes made in the small hours, by people awake when the sensible slept. It was a working life, reconstructed from the exhaust of the work. She could watch the week breathe.

It was not clean, and it could not be, because not every handle was a person. A great deal of the deposit had been produced by other programs rather than written by a hand, and some changes arrived at intervals too exact to be human — on the hour, to the second, a machine committing on a schedule under a name of its own. Those she could strip out; their regularity confessed them. But between the plainly human and the plainly automatic lay a band she could not sort, changes that might have come from a person or from a script wearing a handle, in an age that had learned to make the one indistinguishable from the other and had not troubled to mark which was which. Sarmiento's void: she could read every character of a change and not say who, or what, had made it. She flagged the band, excluded what she had to, and stayed honest about the size of what she could not place. A census with a margin it cannot close is still a census. She closed the parts that would close.

And then, because the deposit dated and attributed every change down to the single act, she did the thing the aggregate had been keeping from her. She stopped counting, and looked at one.

She chose a handle that was an ordinary word and not a name — the corpus was systematically pseudonymous, legal names the exception, and this was not one of them — and drew its every change into a single line. The offset held steady across all of them: five and a half hours ahead of the reference, a value that sits over one subcontinent and very nearly nowhere else on the planet. So she had the meridian. From the hours, she could rebuild a day.

There had been a morning's work and an afternoon's, ordinary, spaced like anyone's. Then a gap across the evening — the hours in which a person eats, and is with whoever they are with, and is not at the machine. Then, past midnight, the handle came back. A run of changes now, close together, the small hurried commits of someone circling one problem and not getting it, each message shorter and flatter than the last. And at fourteen minutes past two in the morning, local time, on a date she could fix to the day, the last change of the night carried a message she could read exactly as it had been written a thousand years before: *ok this actually works now.*

That was all of it, and it was a great deal. She could recover the meridian their clock was set to, and the hour they had finally got the thing right, and the plain relief in the one line — someone had sat up into the night against a thing that would not work, and made it work, and said so, and gone to bed. She could not recover their name; that had lived only on the perishable record, along with the name of the repository itself, which the deposit reconstructs in complete function and cannot name at all. She could not recover what the thing had been, or whether it stayed right, or why it had mattered enough to lose the sleep for. The deposit had kept the hour and the place and the relief, exactly, and let the person go.

She logged the handle among the reconstructed. It was the professional thing and she did it, and then she sat with the one line a moment longer than the work required, because it was the nearest anyone in the deposit had ever come to speaking to her — and it had not been speaking to her at all. Then she went on to the next. There were tens of millions of them, each legible for one true thing and dark for all the rest, and she had the offset on every one.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The commit metadata is real, transcribed evidence: the handles, messages, timestamps, and clock offsets recorded around every change on consignment L-III. Reading the offsets as a labour census — geography from the longitude a local clock implies, rhythm from the hour of the local day — is the method of Okonkwo and Halvorsen.¹ The aggregate, the handle, the offset, and the message *ok this actually works now* are transcribed; the single working day built around them is inference. The ground is real: content-addressed storage with chained cryptographic fingerprints makes the corpus bit-exact and self-verifying; a version-control system records an author-supplied clock offset with every timestamp as a matter of mechanism; and a clock's offset is local mean time, which is longitude. That a share of the corpus cannot be assigned to a human or a machine author at all is a genuine evidentiary limit, not a flourish (Sarmiento).⁴
