Catalytic Converter

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the intercepted metals-price telemetry and buy-side classifieds of the Wasatch Corpus and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

A two-minute catalytic-converter theft late in the interval, reconstructed from the metals price it sold into and the claim tallies it left behind — the months when a parked car became a richer source of the rarest metals on Earth than either mine that made them. The prices and the counts are transcribed from the record; the man under the car is inference from the shape they make.

He liked the tall ones. A high-riding truck or a work van sat far enough off the ground that a man could lie flat beneath it with room to bring the saw up — no jack, no ramps, nothing to fold away after. And he liked the near-silent battery-assisted cars best of all, for a reason that had nothing to do with the silence, though the silence helped.

He knew what was bolted up there. A canister the size of a large flask, packed with a corrugated ceramic honeycomb, several hundred channels to the inch, washed in a film of platinum, palladium and rhodium thinner than a fingerprint: three to seven grams of platinum, as much again of palladium, a gram or two of rhodium, and no more. He could not have named the chemistry and did not need to. He knew the weight, he knew the day’s price, and he knew that the dull grey can he was about to drop into a feed sack held, gram for gram, richer metal than any ore either mine on the planet dug it out of.

The thing worked, which was the comedy of it, not that he was after one. Hot and bolted where he found it, it took three poisons out of the exhaust at once and gave back air — carbon monoxide burned to carbon dioxide, raw fuel to carbon dioxide and water, nitrogen oxide broken down to plain nitrogen — three reactions together, on no power but the engine’s own waste heat, no moving part in it, nine parts in ten of all three, a number the makers were held to and met. It had done that on the roads for two human generations, though it had taken twenty years even to arrive: a lead additive in the fuel had once poisoned the coating stone dead, and the additive and the can could not share a tank, so the additive went first.

And it worked only hot. Below about three hundred degrees the can did nothing and passed the exhaust straight through; on some trips seven or eight tenths of a whole run’s unburned fuel went out cold in the first minute or two, before it lit off. Which was the secret of the quiet cars: running half their errands on the battery, they ran the can cold, so the makers packed them with extra metal to make the numbers back up. The cleaner the car, the fatter the score.

The saw was battery, reciprocating, fitted with a demolition blade. Two cuts — pipe fore, pipe aft — and gravity did the rest. He had it down to a rhythm: under, brace, first cut, the shriek that turned every head in the car park and moved not one of them, second cut, catch it, out, and away before anyone had settled whether the noise was their business. Daylight beat dark. In the dark, a man under a car was a man doing something. In daylight he was maintenance.

The buyer paid by the core, paid cash, and watched a screen. The number on the screen came off a metals desk half a world away and shivered all day, and for a stretch of months it climbed in a way nobody at that desk could explain upward to anyone’s satisfaction. Rhodium — rarest of the three, a grey afterthought, a by-product of a by-product — had traded for most of living memory in the low thousands the ounce, dear but sane. Now it went past ten, past twenty, and on one day it printed above twenty-nine thousand dollars the troy ounce, better than fifteen times the price of gold by weight — and there were one or two grams of it in the can under every second vehicle on the street.

So the cans came off. In one large national market the insurers logged fifty thousand five hundred and three theft claims the first year and sixty-four thousand four hundred and thirty-three the next; the fitting shops counted higher, near a hundred and fifty-three thousand cores actually gone that second year, the gap between the tallies a fair measure of how many owners never troubled to report it. A replacement, uninsured, ran to four figures and could stand a car on blocks for a month waiting on the part. None of that reached him. His business was the next tall car.

Where the metal came from he never once thought about, which was its own small joke, since where it came from was the whole reason a man would crawl under a stranger’s truck for it. Almost every gram of platinum and rhodium ever mined came out of one sheet of rock in the northern interior of a southern country, crystallised from a slowly cooling body of magma about two billion and sixty million years ago, the metals sorted into thin flat reefs by a settling no one has fully accounted for. Almost all the palladium came from a second place that was not a platinum mine at all — a nickel-and-copper body in Siberia that threw the stuff off as a leftover of digging for baser metal, and in one recent year gave up eighty-four tonnes of it, more than any single source on Earth. Two holes in the ground, one an ancient melt and one an accident, and between them very nearly every converter ever fitted to a car.

It had not even been quite clean, which no one had held against it. The step that turned nitrogen oxide back to nitrogen now and then stopped a stage short, at nitrous oxide — a gas some three hundred times worse than carbon dioxide for the warming, and good in the air for centuries — and that one had never been written into the test the can was built to pass. The device retired three poisons and quietly made a fourth, and was certified on the three. He would not have cared. The people who wrote the test had not obliged themselves to, either.

It could not last, and it did not. The price broke the following year and the thefts fell off with it, near enough by half, the trade folding about as fast as it had opened. The coating in his feed sack, though, would not: it would not corrode, would not tarnish, would not weather to anything on any human span; leave the can in a wet yard and the steel shell would rot off in a decade and leave the metal sitting in the muck, unchanged, waiting on nobody. The buyer paid him a couple of hundred for it, cash, no questions, that being the arrangement, and the screen kept the rest. On its best day the screen had read twenty-nine thousand dollars and change for one troy ounce — thirty-one grams — of the grey metal that two billion years of a cooling planet had gathered into a single seam of rock, a gram or two of which he had just carried out of a car park under his arm, in under two minutes. In daylight. As maintenance.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The record is the interval’s own arithmetic — the daily metals-price telemetry and the buy-side classifieds for scrap cores that survive inside the Wasatch Corpus, with the published insurance and repair tallies of the theft wave: 50,503 claims in one national market in +76 pT (2021 CE), 64,433 and some 153,000 cores gone the next year in +77 pT (2022 CE), the wave breaking with the metal price in +78 pT (2023 CE); rhodium’s single-day high above US$29,000 the troy ounce falls in +76 pT. All of that is transcribed. The man, the saw, and the two minutes are inference from the shape the numbers make; no word of his own survives, only the price he sold into and the advertisement that solicited him. The chemistry and geology are real — the three-way reaction, the ~250–300 °C light-off, the cold-start hydrocarbon fraction, the fractional stall to nitrous oxide (the swapped poison), a layered intrusion crystallised ~2.06 billion years ago and a Siberian nickel-copper body between them supplying almost the whole interval’s platinum-group metal.
