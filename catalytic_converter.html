Catalytic Converter

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The catalytic converter is a ceramic-honeycomb exhaust reactor, washcoated in platinum-group metal, that cut regulated tailpipe pollutants by an order of magnitude across the second half of the interval using a family of metals mined at commercial scale from only two ore bodies on the planet. For the fuel additive whose removal this device’s own chemistry required, see Leaded Petrol. For an earlier case of a captured nitrogen problem escaping sideways as an uncounted gas, see Industrial Nitrogen Fixation. For another whole technological base standing, unnoticed by its users, on two named production lines, see Broiler Gallus.

Bolt a corrugated ceramic cylinder into a car’s exhaust pipe, wash its million-odd internal channels in a thin coat of platinum, palladium, and rhodium, and three separate reactions run themselves in the fifteen or so litres of hot gas that pass through it every second: carbon monoxide oxidises to carbon dioxide, unburned hydrocarbon oxidises to carbon dioxide and water, and nitrogen oxide reduces to elemental nitrogen. No moving part, no added fuel, no external power beyond the engine’s own waste heat. At operating temperature, holding the engine within a narrow band around the ratio at which the fuel exactly consumes the available oxygen, the device converts on the order of nine parts in ten of all three pollutant classes at once — a genuine, specified, repeatedly met engineering claim, not a marketing one.

It reached series production only in 1975 (+30 pT), and only in a partial, oxidation-only form; a chemically complete version, closing all three reactions and holding the engine’s fuel mixture to the precision the third reaction demanded, did not become standard equipment until 1981 (+36 pT). The six-year gap was not metallurgical. A working catalytic exhaust reactor had been patented, and briefly commercialised for stationary and marine engines, as early as the mid-1950s (c. +10 pT); it sat unused on road vehicles for two decades because the one fuel additive available to raise every engine’s compression ratio without redesign — see Leaded Petrol — permanently fouled the very metal the reactor depended on. The catalyst and the additive could not occupy the same fuel tank. One of them had to leave first.

What replaced both problems was a third one, not yet solved when this encyclopaedia was compiled: the metals that make the reaction work are, among all ninety-odd naturally occurring elements, among the rarest in the planet’s crust, concentrated to workable grade in essentially two places, and by the interval’s later decades valuable enough, in a unit the size of a large tin can, to be worth removing from a parked vehicle by hand in broad daylight.

1. Three reactions and an unregulated fourth

The three intended reactions share one design constraint: the oxidation pair wants slightly excess oxygen and the reduction reaction wants slightly less, so a working three-way converter can only do all three at once if the engine is held, continuously, within a fraction of a percent of the stoichiometric air–fuel ratio — about 14.7 parts air to one part petrol by mass. That precision arrived only with a zirconia sensor reading residual oxygen in the exhaust stream and feeding it back to the fuel injection in real time, mass-produced from the second half of the 1970s; without it, the catalyst still worked, but only ever for two of the three gases at a given moment; with it, the device could hold all three inside its narrow window continuously.

The ceramic itself — a magnesium-aluminium-silicate honeycomb, several hundred parallel channels to the square inch, coated in a porous, high-surface-area alumina washcoat that raises the metal’s effective contact area from the geometric few hundred square centimetres of the block to tens of thousands of square metres — does not become catalytically active below roughly 250–300 °C. In the first one to three minutes of a cold start, before that light-off temperature is reached, the ceramic passes exhaust straight through unconverted; on some drive cycles, seventy to eighty percent of a trip’s entire tailpipe hydrocarbon output leaves in that first unheated minute or two, a fact published in the device’s own certification literature and unresolved, at scale, for the rest of the interval.

The reduction reaction has a second, quieter failure mode. Nitrogen oxide does not always reduce cleanly to N₂; a fraction of it stalls at the intermediate stage, nitrous oxide, N₂O — a gas the device’s own regulating authorities never required a manufacturer to measure, let alone limit, because it is not one of the three pollutants the converter was built and certified against. N₂O carries a global-warming potential on the order of three hundred times carbon dioxide’s on a hundred-year view and an atmospheric lifetime measured in centuries; a mechanism already on record in this corpus for an unrelated industrial process (see Industrial Nitrogen Fixation) reappears here as an unwanted output of the very machine built to close it off elsewhere in the exhaust stream. The regulated three gases fell. The fourth, never regulated, was never required to.

2. One melt, two producers

The metals are not merely rare; they are geologically eccentric, occurring at workable concentration almost nowhere the ordinary processes of crustal recycling would predict, because the two deposits that supply nearly the whole run trace to two entirely different kinds of accident, separated by most of the planet’s history.

The larger of the two, a layered igneous intrusion in the country’s northern interior, crystallised from a slowly cooling magma chamber roughly 2.06 billion years ago, sorting platinum-group elements into thin, laterally continuous reefs by a settling process this corpus’s own geochemists still argue over the details of. It supplies, by most published tallies, on the order of seven or eight in every ten troy ounces of the world’s newly mined platinum, an even larger share of newly mined rhodium, and more than four-fifths of its mined iridium — the last at a rate of only some six to seven tonnes a year, planet-wide, for a technology fitted by the tens of millions of units annually. The second source is not a platinum mine at all: a Siberian nickel-and-copper sulphide body, mined primarily for base metal, yields platinum-group metal only as a co-product of that unrelated operation — and yielded, in one recent published year, some eighty-four tonnes of palladium, more than any other single source on the planet, overtaking the first deposit’s own output of that metal outright.

Two ore bodies, one an ancient melt mined for the elements everyone wanted, the other a base-metal operation that happened to also carry them: between them, for the whole of the technology’s commercial life, effectively the entire global supply. The parallel to a single formula synthesised at a handful of licensed plants and dispersed into every engine on the planet has already been drawn for the additive this device replaced;¹ drawn here, it runs the other direction — not one product shipped everywhere from a few points, but every catalytic converter on the planet’s roads depending, without exception, on ore raised from two points.²

3. The interval excavates itself

A passenger car’s converter carries, typically, on the order of three to seven grams of platinum, a comparable range of palladium, and one to two grams of rhodium — concentrations, gram for gram, well above anything either producing deposit mines from rock. That arithmetic made a parked car, for a run of years late in the interval, a richer and far more accessible source of the metal than the ground: removable, with a battery-powered reciprocating saw and a practised hand, in as little as a minute or two, and traceable afterward to no registry, no depositor, and — unlike almost everything else recovered in this discipline — no institutional intent behind its removal at all.

Insurers in one large national market recorded 50,503 catalytic-converter theft claims in 2021 (+76 pT) and 64,433 the year after; independent repair-record tallies put the 2022 (+77 pT) total nearer 153,000 units actually removed, the gap between the two figures a rough measure of how many thefts were never insured, filed, or counted at all. The metal driving it was rhodium, rarest and dearest of the three: it traded, at a single-day high in 2021 (+76 pT), above US $29,000 the troy ounce — more than fifteen times gold’s price for the same weight — before the market for all three metals fell sharply enough that reported thefts in 2023 (+78 pT) dropped by roughly half alongside it.³ Replacement, uninsured, commonly ran into four figures and could leave a vehicle undriveable for as long as a part took to source; the cost fell, as it does in every recorded instance of this kind, on whoever could least absorb it.

None of this is the recovery archaeology this discipline elsewhere practises on the interval’s own refuse. It happened inside the interval, in real time, driven by nothing more coordinated than a spot price — an economy of extraction running in parallel with, and faster than, either of the two geological producers it was quietly cannibalising, for exactly as long as the price justified the saw.⁴ The platinum-group metals themselves do not corrode, tarnish, or dissolve on any timescale this discipline measures; the housing usually does, given long enough in an open yard, but the washcoat and its metal survive essentially unaltered, outside every corrosion regime this corpus’s taphonomy otherwise catalogues.⁵ Two billion years concentrated it into rock worth mining. The car gave it back in under two minutes.
