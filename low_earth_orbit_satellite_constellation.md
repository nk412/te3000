# Low Earth Orbit Satellite Constellation

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the tracked orbital debris catalogue and the operator's own published manoeuvre telemetry, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **A low earth orbit satellite constellation is a fleet of several thousand small communications satellites flown as a single coordinated system in an orbital shell below 2,000 km, first assembled at scale in the interval's final decades to sell continuous broadband coverage to ground too sparse, remote, or poor to be worth a fibre run.** Where the fleet flew is still checkable against the orbit itself, which does not forget; what it cost the sky above it and the air beneath it is reconstructed from the operator's own telemetry, published in full only up to a certain year.

---

By +81 pT a single operator counted upward of ten thousand active units in low orbit — more than half of everything the species had, by then, ever put there — deployed in batches of several dozen from reusable boosters at a cadence approaching one launch every two to three days across the interval's closing years. The first sixty had flown in +74 pT. The design shell sat at a few hundred kilometres: near enough the ground for a usable signal delay, far enough to clear the horizon for the next hop, dense enough that a ground terminal was never out of view of a working unit for long.

The shell was sold, and defended, on a claim about its own disappearance. A dead unit at operational altitude re-enters and burns within about five years, faster in an active solar cycle, slower in a quiet one — no derelict hardware accumulating in a crowded lane, unlike the higher, drag-free orbit where four decades of prior spacecraft already sat undisturbed. The claim was true. It was also, on its own terms, incomplete: what a dead satellite does on the way down is combust, and what a few hundred kilograms of aluminium airframe leaves behind when it does is oxide. The interval had, a few decades earlier, drawn its atmospheric chlorine down by treaty and begun to watch its ozone layer recover; oxide alumina catalyses the very reaction that chlorine had been driving. The mechanism securing the shell against becoming permanent debris in one part of the atmosphere was quietly depositing a fresh reagent in another, three altitude bands higher and mechanistically unconnected, in the era's own published accounting.

Its astronomers logged the fleet as a nuisance and, on their better instruments, as a threat to a specific kind of observation; its own operator tried, twice, to dim it, and fell short both times. Its collision-avoidance system ran without a human reading each case. Its replenishment depended, in the end, on the same continuous capital and launch cadence as everything else the interval built to run forever, and did not.

## The shell

The constellation held station a few hundred kilometres up, well inside the region where residual atmosphere still exerts a measurable drag and well below the geostationary ring at 35,786 km, where the interval's earlier communications satellites already sat in an effectively permanent parking orbit — the established graveyard population of dry, cold, intact hardware this discipline dates to the Middle Combustion. The new shell was not a return to that altitude but a deliberate departure from it: thousands of small, short-lived units in place of a few dozen large, permanent ones, replenished continuously rather than built to last.

## The clearing

Atmospheric density falls off steeply, though not to zero, out past 500 km, and what remains still drags on a satellite's leading face across enough orbits to lower it, cumulatively, into thicker air and, eventually, into flame. A non-manoeuvring unit at the shell's altitude re-enters within roughly five years; the same physics leaves a dead unit at geostationary altitude essentially undisturbed for longer than the interval's own written record survived. A non-binding twenty-five-year disposal guideline, adopted by the era's space agencies at the turn of the millennium (+57 pT), had governed post-mission disposal for satellites generally; it was replaced, for the shell's own regulator, by a binding five-year rule adopted in +77 pT and made effective in +79 pT — twenty-two years after the guideline it replaced, and roughly the same interval by which the shell's own launch cadence had already outgrown it. The delay is the corpus's clearest instance, outside its type case, of **the cost that outlived its constraint**.¹

## The bill

Reentry modelling for the year +77 pT put mesospheric aluminium injection from the interval's satellite reentries at some seventeen tonnes, a rise of roughly three-tenths over natural background from meteoric sources; published projections for the shell's full planned build-out, upward of forty thousand units, place the figure above 360 tonnes a year. A typical unit carries on the order of a fifth to a third of its mass as aluminium airframe, converted on reentry to a fine oxide, particles a few nanometres to a hundred across, injected at an altitude from which published estimates give them up to three decades to settle into the stratospheric band the ozone layer occupies. The mechanism eliminating one hazard by quietly generating a second, unregulated one, in a different medium and on a different clock, is **the swapped poison**² in its plainest form yet recorded — plainer than the catalytic converter's own trace of unregulated N₂O, because here the entire hazard is the byproduct: nothing about the shell's re-entry regime exists for any purpose other than disposal.

## The visor

The shell's brightness was measured, complained of, and twice addressed. Unmitigated, a unit approached naked-eye visibility on a clear evening; the discipline's own astronomers, coordinated through an international scientific union, set a non-binding target below which a satellite should not be visible without instruments. A low-reflectivity coating trialled on a single unit in +75 pT met the target but overheated its electronics and was abandoned; a deployable sun visor fitted to the following production run cut brightness by less than half the coating had and left the fleet, in aggregate, still above the recommended limit. Independent surveys recorded satellite streaks in 0.5% of wide-field exposures in +74 pT and close to a fifth of them by +76 pT; modelling for a next-generation wide-field sky survey then nearing first light in the southern hemisphere warned that, at full constellation build-out across all operators, a majority of its exposures would carry at least one streak. No revision to the brightness target was ever made binding.

## The traffic

Collision avoidance ran automated and continuous, each unit manoeuvring on its own schedule whenever a conjunction's computed collision probability crossed a threshold set an order of magnitude stricter than the era's general practice — roughly three in ten million, versus the one-in-ten-thousand threshold common elsewhere. In the interval's final full year of complete published telemetry, the fleet logged upward of two hundred thousand such manoeuvres across six months, more than forty per unit per year on average, none individually reviewed by a person before it executed. The record does not distinguish a routine adjustment from a manoeuvre that prevented a specific, dateable collision; the aggregate count is the finest resolution the surviving telemetry supports.

## The residue

Replenishment depended on the same reusable-launch cadence, the same capital, and the same continuous institutional attention as the food-allocation nodes and the submarine cable repair fleets that also, eventually, stopped. Where those systems left a stalled ledger or a severed line as their last record, the shell's own design ensured it left almost nothing: units not relaunched simply re-entered, on schedule, within the five years their own physics guaranteed, and the shell contracted at roughly the rate it had grown. A technology built, deliberately, to leave no lasting trace is a poor match for a discipline built entirely from what does. An estimated few hundred to low thousands of units are believed, from gaps in the tracked catalogue, to have failed post-mission disposal outright and to persist still, in decaying or marginally stable orbits, among the debris population radar still ranges; no surviving register is complete enough to give that estimate a stated confidence.

## Provenance and ground

Two evidentiary channels combine here and do not fully overlap. Orbital position is a physical fact, checkable now by the same tracking method the interval used, independent of any written record; it is the reason the geostationary graveyard and the low shell's near-total absence can both be stated with confidence. The operator's own manoeuvre counts, reentry modelling, and brightness measurements are published telemetry, subject to ordinary Attrition risk, and the record used here runs complete only to a certain year, after which the corpus has fragments, not a series. The orbital mechanics, the reentry chemistry, and the ozone-catalysis mechanism are real and independently derivable; the fleet's history, motives, and reception are reconstructed around them.
