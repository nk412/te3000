# Mass Coral Bleaching

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The thermally triggered expulsion of the microscopic photosynthetic algae that colour and mostly feed reef-building coral — a process this discipline can read independently in the skeleton the polyp leaves behind, in the chemistry of the water around it, and in a forecast record that predicted every one of its four global occurrences correctly, in advance, and changed nothing.** For the cold-storage doctrine an anxious later generation banked against it, see **Cryogenic Biobank**. Bleaching is not, before the interval's very last working decade, synonymous with reef death — a starved colony can regain its algae and its colour within weeks — and it was never, until nearly the end, a single event with a single date.

---

A coral polyp farms its own food. Packed into each one's gut lining is a dense population of single-celled algae that photosynthesise and hand back the great majority of the sugar they make, by some estimates as much as nine parts in ten, to the animal holding them. In return the polyp shelters them, feeds them carbon dioxide and nitrogen waste, and lays down, at its base, a skeleton of calcium carbonate in the aragonite form, one layer at a time, for as long as the arrangement holds. The algae are also the coral's colour; strip them out and the tissue above the skeleton is close to transparent, and the stark white mineral underneath is what a diver, or a satellite, actually sees. The condition takes its name from exactly that appearance.

Run across an entire ocean basin at once rather than one reef at a time, the mechanism produced four events in the interval's own published record, each one worse than the last: bleaching-level heat stress across 21 percent of the world's reef area in +53 pT (1998 CE); 37 percent in +65 pT (2010 CE); 68 percent across +69–72 pT (2014–2017 CE); and, in the interval's final working decade, 84 percent across +78–80 pT (2023–2025 CE) — a severity that forced a national satellite-monitoring programme to add three entirely new tiers to a bleaching-alert scale its own designers had never built to go that high. What makes the sequence worth an encyclopaedia entry, rather than a chronology table, is that none of it was a surprise on the day. It is legible twice over, in two records that owe nothing to each other and nothing to the fragile written archive this discipline usually has to make do with: one kept by the water itself, one kept, unwillingly, by the animal.

## What the polyp cannot outrun

The algae are heat-sensitive in a way the coral, unaided, is not. Water held only a degree or two above a reef's ordinary summer maximum for a matter of weeks disrupts the algae's own photosynthetic machinery badly enough that the symbiosis stops being worth the damage, and the polyp expels its garden rather than let the harm spread — a defence this discipline's biology can reliably observe without yet being able to fully explain. The same monitoring programme that logged the four global events formalised the trigger as an accumulating heat-stress metric, in degree-weeks of excess warmth: at four, significant bleaching in the more sensitive species is expected; at eight, bleaching and mortality across most species at a site. A colony that cools back down within a few weeks can recruit fresh algae and recover its colour whole. One that does not simply starves, over weeks to a few months, on the fraction of its own energy budget its tissue alone can supply — which is not much.

## The arithmetic in the water

Coral loses ground more slowly, and less reversibly, to plain chemistry. Something on the order of a third of the carbon dioxide a Combustion-Age industrial economy released did not stay in the air; it dissolved into the surface ocean, where it forms carbonic acid and measurably shifts the water's own carbonate balance — global average surface pH fell from near 8.2 before the interval to near 8.1 by its later decades, a shift that reads as modest only because the scale is logarithmic; in hydrogen-ion concentration it is closer to a quarter higher than the pre-industrial baseline. The consequence for anything that calcifies is fewer carbonate ions in the same water. Something on the order of nineteen reefs in twenty sat, before the interval, in water at or above the aragonite saturation state healthy calcification needs; by the interval's later decades, something closer to three in five did not. Below a saturation state of three, calcification measurably slows; below one, aragonite dissolves without any help from biology at all. A study of 328 large, slow-growing colonies of one massive reef-building genus, sampled across sixty-nine reefs of a single major reef system and published +64 pT (2009 CE), found calcification down 14.2 percent from a +45 pT (1990 CE) baseline — against a continuous record, held in the same colonies, running back four unbroken centuries with nothing in it resembling the final two decades of the curve. The finding was not suppressed. It ran in the period's own most widely read general-science literature, under its authors' own names, and was cited afterward at a rate any Combustion-Age scholar would have called a triumph.

## A ring for every year, if it is one ring

The record kept inside the animal is not incidental; it is this era's most literal marine analogue to a familiar deep-past technique. A slab cut through a massive coral's skeleton and read by X-radiograph shows alternating bands of dense and porous growth, one couplet most years, laid down exactly as a temperate tree lays down dense latewood against porous spring growth — the method was described in the period's own literature as early as +27 pT (1972 CE), and it is, structurally, dendrochronology's ocean cousin: a small number of long-lived, slow-growing individuals, cross-dated against each other and against an independent clock, yielding a continuous annual record centuries deep from an organism never built, or asked, to keep one. The same cross-dating discipline had already narrowed a Bronze Age merchantman's felling season, from one dunnage branch's outermost rings calibrated against a master chronology of trees still standing nearby, to within a year, four millennia before anyone thought to core a coral. The largest colonies of the type grow slowly enough, and live long enough, to carry a record close to seven centuries deep in a single skeleton nearly seven metres across — a reading disputed almost as soon as it was made, on a problem internal to the method itself: some colonies at low latitude appear to lay down two density couplets a year rather than one, which would halve the estimate if it applies here, and the interval's own scientists never fully settled which count was right before the record they were arguing over stopped being kept at all.

Extending the model already established in this corpus for a lake-sediment nitrogen shift and a polar lead-deposition spike, Devereux (2998) reads the coral growth-band record as a fourth independent clock — biogenic rather than atmospheric or lacustrine, cross-dateable against both of the others, and resolvable, where the two counting hypotheses disagree, by that cross-dating rather than by counting alone. It is also, she notes, a clock built and kept by the very organism the warming was killing: an archive and a casualty produced by one mechanism, in one tissue. She names this **the calcified verdict** — *the coral is the only witness in this corpus that wrote its own testimony in the substance of its own death* — and whether the testimony survives to be read at all depends on where a given colony happened to die. Aragonite is a metastable form of calcium carbonate; left long enough in the wrong pore water it recrystallises to the more stable calcite, and the trace-element and isotope record the count depends on blurs or vanishes in the conversion, though the gross banding can outlast its own chemistry. A skeleton that instead stayed submerged, undisturbed, in cold and fully marine water for the thousand years since could plausibly still hold both intact. There is no way to tell which fate a given core has met from the outside. It has to be cut.

## The bank that could not scale

From +66 pT (2011 CE), a zoological conservation body began collecting coral sperm at spawning and holding it in liquid nitrogen at −196 °C — the same medium, the same metastable-ice threshold, already established elsewhere in this corpus for the interval's other cryogenic reserves. By the interval's later decades the holding ran to the trillions of individual cells across some three dozen species, drawn overwhelmingly from one reef system; trial fertilisations using thawed sperm reported live coral produced at rates as high as nine attempts in ten. Unlike Svalbard's sealed base collection, never drawn on and never meant to be, every one of these programmes drew on its bank actively, breeding a thawed parent's heat tolerance into eggs from a population geography alone would never have let it meet.

The restoration literature read those success rates with real, published optimism. A sharper dissent survives too, turning not on whether the technique worked — it did — but on scale: a living reef gives, for free, a coastal defence a global meta-analysis measured at a 97 percent average reduction in incoming wave energy, benefiting upward of a hundred million people who live behind one, at a restoration cost running, at the global published median, to on the order of one-fifteenth what a built breakwater cost for comparable protection. Nobody disputed that the arithmetic favoured the reef. The dissent held only that the area under active restoration sat many orders of magnitude below the reef area a single bleaching year put at risk, and that no funding trajectory the period committed to closed that gap before its own records stop. Extending her established caution against reading a short trial's success rate as a claim about a longer or larger process, Kagiso's model applies here with little adjustment: a genebank's short-run germination equation and a coral nursery's genuinely excellent nine-in-ten fertilisation rate are, on her reading, the same category of number, asked to carry a claim about a scale neither was ever measured at.

What did not survive, in any case, was the paper trail that might have settled it. The forecasts, the bleaching-extent surveys, the fertilisation logs, all depended on satellite ground segments and public data services requiring continuous institutional upkeep of exactly the kind this discipline elsewhere reads as a debt falling due on a schedule; long stretches of the day-by-day monitoring record for the interval's final working decades are not recoverable at all, not because the reef went unwatched but because the institution watching it did not outlast what it watched. What reached 3000 CE directly, needing no login and owing nothing to any lapsed subscription, is the coral's own skeleton — legible, when it is legible at all, without the forecast that once explained what it was living through.

The forecast, while it lasted, was not wrong. Twenty-one percent, in +53 pT. Thirty-seven, in +65 pT. Sixty-eight, in +69–72 pT. Eighty-four, in +78–80 pT. Correctly, every time, and further out each time than the last.
