Crash-Survivable Flight Recorder

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from recovered recorder housings and a deep-ocean search authority’s operation logs, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

A crash-survivable flight recorder is a sealed instrument, carried aboard powered aircraft from the interval’s middle decades and routinely fitted in pairs — a flight-data unit and a cockpit-voice unit — built to preserve the final minutes of flight and cockpit sound through whatever destroyed everything else aboard. The certification figures and the recording history are the period’s own; the two long-term survival classes are inference from recovered units.

The instrument answered one design problem: to carry a usable record out of an event violent enough to leave nothing else usable. Two units did the work, and by the interval’s middle decades an aircraft routinely carried both — a flight-data recorder logging the aircraft’s parameters, mandated from +13 pT, and a cockpit voice recorder holding a continuous loop of the flight deck’s own sound, required from +21–22 pT. Each was sealed in an armoured housing, painted a conspicuous colour, and certified not against wear or age but against the destruction of the aircraft it flew in.

That certification was harmonised across the era’s aviation authorities at +51 pT, and it drew the survival envelope tight: impact, fire, crush, penetration, and the pressure of deep water, each figure a real way an aircraft is lost. The envelope did not loosen again. The memory inside the housing, meanwhile, changed medium three times — engraved metal foil, then magnetic tape, then solid-state flash — each move made for capacity and toughness. But the toughness of a box and the permanence of what it holds are different properties, and across the interval they came apart. A recorder could survive its aircraft entirely and lose everything it had recorded.

The split runs on one variable the certificate does not test: time without power. The earliest recorders scratched their trace into soft metal, a mark legible on recovery to anyone who holds the foil to a raking light — no current, no reader, no format. The solid-state units that replaced them are crash-proof and, left unread, mute: their own manufacturers rate the memory to hold data unpowered for about a year at a worn chip’s end of life, and perhaps a decade if it is kept cool. The instrument built to outlast the crash does not, in its latest form, outlast neglect. The discipline files the two classes together as the mute survivor, and reads a recorder that came up readable — as one pair did, from near 3,900 m, twenty months after its own beacon had died — as the fortunate case and not the certain one.

Certification

The harmonised standard of +51 pT specified the destruction the housing had to survive and the margin by which it had to survive it. Impact: 3,400 times gravity, sustained for 6.5 milliseconds — the shock of striking ground or water at full speed. Fire, in two separate exposures: 1,100 °C across the unit for sixty minutes, and a slower 260 °C for ten hours, the second for a fuel fire that goes on burning long after it stops burning hot. Static crush: 5,000 pounds-force on each of six faces, held five minutes. Penetration: a 500-pound weight dropped ten feet onto a quarter-inch steel pin aimed at the housing’s most vulnerable point. Immersion: the hydrostatic pressure of 20,000 feet of seawater, for thirty days. Each figure is a real accident reduced to a bench test, and a recorder that passes them all is an object engineered against the specific violences that end a flight. What the standard does not specify, because it is a survival specification and not an archival one, is how long the record inside stays readable once the violence is over and no one has yet come.

The recording mandate

What the units kept, and for how long, was set by rule and then left largely alone. Flight parameters were mandated from +13 pT; cockpit audio from +21–22 pT, on a continuous loop that overwrote itself so that only the most recent interval survived. That interval was fixed at thirty minutes — a length set by the cost and bulk of the magnetic tape the loop then ran on. Solid-state memory reached the recorders around +45–50 pT and removed the constraint entirely: capacity was no longer scarce, and a loop could be any length. The mandate did not move. The thirty-minute window stood for roughly four decades after the material that justified it had been retired, extended to two hours only at +63 pT, and to twenty-five hours — for newly certified large aircraft — only by a rule dated +70 pT and applied from +76 pT. The scholarship reads the gap between the constraint’s disappearance and the mandate’s revision as institutional rather than technical, and files it as the cost that outlived its constraint. For most of the era, the record a crash-proof box was required to preserve was bounded by the price of a material it no longer contained.

Two ways to outlast a reader

The change of medium that made the recording cheaper and the housing tougher also changed how the record fails when it is finally left alone, and it changed it into two entirely different fates. The engraved-foil recorders, fitted before any rule required them, dragged a mechanical stylus’s trace directly into a moving strip of soft metal — the same solution as an incised plaque cut into base metal precisely because ink and its readers cannot be promised. Such a trace is legible on recovery without power, without a working drive, and without knowledge of any encoding: it is a physical scratch, read by light. Solid-state flash stores its record as trapped charge held on floating gates, and trapped charge leaks. Unpowered, a flash device loses its contents on the schedule its makers publish — about a year once a chip has been worked to its rated end of life, nearer a decade if it is held cold and lightly used — and when it has leaked, the housing around it is undamaged and the record is gone. The same lineage therefore recovers in two conditions that share nothing: the foil, silent-medium and readable indefinitely, and the flash, armoured and blank. The reading turns entirely on whether anyone came before the charge did.

A recovery

One recovery is documented in full, from the search authority’s own operation logs, and it is the case against which the mute-survivor reading is calibrated. A long-haul airliner was lost over open ocean at +64 pT, the recorder pair coming to rest near 3,900 m on a plain no chart had mapped. Each recorder carried an underwater locator beacon rated to emit a single acoustic tone for thirty days at depth. By the time ships reached the water the thirty days were nearly spent, and the beacons fell silent on schedule; the one part of the instrument whose only task was to be heard was the first to go quiet, and it went quiet on time. The wreck was found at +66 pT, roughly twenty months later, not by the beacon but by side-scan sonar and a Bayesian search-probability model that redistributed likelihood across the seabed after each empty sweep, pooling belief into water the drift models had argued against. Both housings were sound. Both units read out entire — the flight-data parameters, and the full two-hour voice window the then-current rule allowed. The box had done precisely what it was certified to do, at the depth and for the duration written on the certificate.

The memory survived for a reason the certificate does not claim. Deep-ocean bottom water sits a few degrees above freezing, in permanent dark; the same cold and depth that hid the wreck for twenty months are why the flash still held its charge when the recovery vehicle reached it. The incident changed procedure rather than steel: a later international rule extended locator-beacon life to ninety days, three times what had run out here, and required aircraft to report their own position automatically in flight — a continuous thin stream that would not have to be gone looking for. What it did not change is the variable underneath. Somewhere on a plain no probability grid will be built to close on, a later recorder of this same crash-proof lineage is on the bottom now, its titanium sound and its seals holding, waiting to be lifted intact and identifiable and empty, its memory discharged in the dark long before anyone comes. The scratched foil from the age before it would still be legible. The armoured box will have kept everything except the recording.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The certification figures and the recording history are the period’s own, recovered as documentary record: the harmonised +51 pT survival standard, the +13 pT flight-parameter mandate and the +21–22 pT voice mandate, and the thirty-minute loop’s slow extension to two and then twenty-five hours, the delay read as institutional rather than technical (the cost that outlived its constraint, Castellanos¹). The physics is not invented. Bayesian search theory redistributes probability across unswept seabed cells after each null sweep, which is how the +64 pT open-ocean loss was located at +66 pT long after its thirty-day beacon had expired, reconstructed from the search authority’s own operation logs by Verhoeven². Solid-state flash holds its record as trapped charge that leaks when unpowered — rated retention on the order of a year at a chip’s worn end of life and perhaps a decade kept cool — while the earliest engraved metal foil is legible on recovery without power; the two long-term survival classes of a single functional lineage are inference from recovered units and the manufacturers’ own retention specifications (the mute survivor, Fujimori³). Deep-ocean bottom water a few degrees above freezing is why the recovered memory outlasted its beacon at all, and why no later recovery can be counted on to.
