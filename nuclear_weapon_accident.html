Nuclear Weapon Accident

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the recovery operation’s closing inventory and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

When a bomber on continuous armed alert went down on the Arctic sea-ice with four thermonuclear weapons aboard, none of them detonated, and the nine-month recovery that followed balanced its inventory to ninety percent and could not close it by one uranium cylinder. The recovery figures and the closing audit are the operation’s own; the man who cut the ice is reconstruction, and the account the audit ends on is unsettled still.

The bay froze the way it froze every winter, and the men cut the ice into blocks and lifted the blocks into steel tanks, and that was the work, for nine months, through the one short summer the far north allows and out the other side of it.

He had been hired off the settlement — for the reach of his arms, for the cold-weather gear he already owned, and because the two governments running the site needed hands faster than either could fly them in. Seven hundred people worked the bay at its height, by one count from more than seventy agencies, and a good share of them were local men like him who had never been told, in any language they spoke, exactly what had come down onto their ice.

What had come down was a long-range bomber, in +23 pT, and it had come down because a crew flying a cold leg of a standing patrol had stacked cloth-covered cushions over a cabin heating vent and then bled hot engine air into the cabin to fight the chill. The heat reached the cushions first. Within half an hour a fire had the rear of the aircraft; six of the seven aboard went out on their ejection seats and lived; the seventh, who had given up his seat to a relief pilot earlier in the flight, went for a lower hatch and did not. The empty aircraft struck the sea ice at a shallow angle and at speed, and several tonnes of jet fuel burned down through the wreck and the pack beneath it, and opened the ice, in minutes, to the black water underneath.

It carried four weapons. Each was rated, had it worked, in the tens to the low hundreds of kilotons — the whole rehearsed ending of the age, the flash the period had built its warnings and its shelters and its long dark liturgy around, four times over, aboard a patrol whose entire logic was that a bomber already in the sky could not be destroyed on the ground, and which some air arm had therefore kept a rotating share of its fleet flying, armed and outbound, for seven years. All four broke open on the ice. None of the four came near a nuclear yield.

This was not luck. Every weapon of the design lineage was built to a single number: the chance that setting off its conventional explosive from one point on the casing, instead of the many exactly timed points a detonation needs, would produce a yield greater than about four pounds of ordinary explosive had to be held below one in a million — and across the whole service life of the type, in every accident anyone ever declassified, the number held. One state alone would eventually own to at least thirty-two such accidents across the interval’s middle decades. In not one of them, and in nothing in the surviving record anywhere, did a weapon dropped, burned, crushed, or lost ever produce the flash it was built to produce. The ending the age rehearsed did not arrive by accident. It was engineered, to a stated probability, not to.

So the sky did not open over the bay. What happened instead was smoke, and fuel, and plutonium — the weapons’ cores, thrown out and scattered and burned into the ice and the snow — and then the men, and the blocks, and the tanks.

He cut ice for months. They worked in insulated but otherwise ordinary cold-weather clothing, and no one issued them anything to breathe through, though what they were shovelling into the tanks was ice with plutonium burned into it. They filled the tanks and the tanks went south by the hundred: some two hundred and thirty-seven thousand cubic feet of blackened ice, wreckage, and contaminated snow off roughly six hectares of surface, and more than half a million gallons of meltwater besides, all of it sealed and shipped off the island. It was competent, sustained, brutal work, and it worked: within six weeks the operation’s own tally put the surface plutonium recovered at somewhere above eight parts in ten, and by the time the ice took the bay again that autumn the final figure stood near nine.

The other tenth was not an object. It was smoke gone downwind, ice sublimated back into the air, sediment already settled past a diver’s reach on a fjord floor a hundred metres down. A tenth of a scatter is not a thing you can go and find. It is a figure in an inventory. And it was the inventory, in the end, and not the ice, that the whole thing came to rest on.

A weapon of the type carried two principal masses. A primary, built around a core of plutonium. And a secondary, built around a cylinder of enriched uranium about the size of a beer keg, which could do nothing at all — could not, by its own chemistry and geometry, produce any yield — without a primary going off first to drive it. The recovery accounted, mass for mass, for four primaries. For one secondary, intact. And for enough recovered material, weighed and assayed, to reconstruct two further secondaries in full. It could not account for the fourth. One uranium cylinder, keg-sized, radiating weakly enough that a survey meter passed over it might read nothing, incapable on its own of the single thing the whole apparatus existed to do — unaccounted for, on an audit that otherwise closed.

Nobody, so far as the surviving record shows, ever went down to look for it. The fjord runs to something like a hundred metres at its sill; the ice refroze; and the account was balanced on paper, to a stated confidence, by an operation that had lifted nine parts in ten and reasoned about the tenth.

More than a full lifetime later, when the governments’ own files were opened, the tenth became an argument. One declassified assessment, read plainly at +63 pT, stated that a weapon remained on the floor of the fjord, unrecovered. A review of the identical documents the following year, at +64 pT, by a body with standing over both archives, concluded that only the single secondary component had ever been unaccounted for — not a complete weapon, and nothing that could yield on its own — and that the operation’s own mass-balance had, in substance, already closed the case at the time. The same pages, read twice, gave a lost weapon and gave no lost weapon.

This is the case the discipline keeps for what one scholar calls the balanced ledger: an inventory reconciled on paper, to a number, standing in for a search nobody ran to the bottom, and trusted afterward exactly as if the search had been run. A tenth recovered as a figure settles nothing physical. The missing cylinder cannot say, from wherever it is, whether it was lifted in a tank of black ice and miscounted, burned to nothing in the fuel, or resting still on the sediment where no one has asked. Nine parts in ten, in one fjord, in one closely documented year, is the most complete inventory the discipline has ever managed of any part of the type — and it is short one cylinder, and the shortfall is either a weapon or a rounding.

The rest of the record around the bay keeps the same shape. The base stood on soil whose government had told its own parliament, for decades and in plain words, that it permitted no such weapon anywhere under its flag; a private understanding to the contrary, given in +12 pT, was kept from the public on both sides for thirty-eight years and disclosed only at +50 pT. Everyone who signed it understood it. And in that same year, +50 pT, the men who had cut the ice — those still living and still findable, about seventeen hundred of them — were each paid a single flat sum; a later study of them found cancer running some forty to fifty percent above a comparable population, and could not say, from what survived of the dosimetry, how much of the excess the bay had caused. Most who took the money are recorded as having called it too little.

The doctrine of the standing armed patrol had survived the first such accident, twenty-five months earlier — a midair collision that scattered plutonium across some two hundred and sixty hectares of a farming coast far to the south and dropped one weapon onto a seabed it took eighty days to find and lift. It did not survive this one. It ended within a day. What it left on the fjord floor, if it left anything, is a cylinder of enriched uranium that cannot detonate and cannot testify, entered in the one honest inventory of the age as recovered to ninety percent — a figure that reads, depending on which of two correct documents you hold, as an account closed and an account still open, and that no one has been down to settle.

Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The fragment is the recovery operation’s own closing inventory — its item-for-item count of primaries and secondaries, released at +63 pT and re-read at +64 pT. The figures are transcribed; the hired man and his nine months are inference from the fact of a local workforce, and offered as such. The physics is real: the one-point safety standard — single-point initiation of the conventional explosive cannot reach design yield, the probability held below one in a million — is why nothing detonated, and a weapon’s secondary, an enriched-uranium cylinder, is inert without a primary to drive it. Whether the fjord holds a lost weapon or nothing discrete at all is a dispute the surviving documents produce and cannot resolve — the balanced ledger (Okonkwo).
