# Broiler Gallus

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a Crawford population of the Gallus Horizon and a recovered growth trial, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The broiler Gallus is the fast-growing, short-lived domestic fowl engineered through the Combustion Age's final century for meat yield and market age alone, and the type fossil by which the age's own geological horizon is now dated.** The growth curve, the two-firm hold on the world's breeding stock, and the standing population are record; the reading of the bone is reconstruction, and was for a scholarly generation wrong.

---

At any instant in the interval's final decades a standing population near 25 × 10⁹ broiler Gallus was alive on the planet, a combined living mass roughly three times that of every wild bird of every wild species then remaining. It is the type fossil of the Gallus Horizon — the single most common vertebrate bone in the type-locality core at Crawford Lake, and one of the diagnostic markers by which the age is now assigned a geological interval of its own.

The animal is an engineered product, and the engineering is documented. It was bred from +5 pT for three commercial terms — rate of gain, mass of feed converted to meat, and the share of that meat laid on the breast — and for nothing else. It traces almost entirely to two winning crosses from a single national breeding contest, run in three rounds across +1 to +3 pT, and to the two firms descended from them, which came to hold on the order of seven to nine in every ten birds of the world's commercial parent breeding stock. Half a century of closed, measured selection followed, and it worked: on identical feed, a line unselected since 1957 reaches about 905 g at forty-two days, while the working line current as of 2005 reaches better than four kilograms at the same age — over four times the live mass, diet held constant, the genetics alone made to answer for it.

What physically survives to 3000 CE is the bone, and the bone was read wrong. The oversized, porous, frequently malformed skeletons dug in quantity out of former landfill were taken, for a scholarly generation, as a recurring die-off of the young; a maize-heavy dietary carbon signature, absent from every earlier domestic line, later showed them to be adults slaughtered at market weight while skeletally still juvenile. The same anoxic landfill that produced the misreading is the reason the bone survives at all — the routine discard of the age's most successful engineered animal, kept without anyone meaning to keep it.

## The specification

The broiler Gallus is specified, not described. Its performance is stated the way a component's is: live mass at a fixed market age, mass of feed consumed per unit mass of meat produced, and the proportion of the dressed carcass carried as breast muscle. A controlled trial recovered intact from the period's technical-text harvest isolated the contribution of the genetics from that of the feed. Three lines of one lineage — one held unselected since 1957 (+12 pT), one since 1978 (+33 pT), and a working line current as of 2005 (+60 pT) — were raised side by side on a single modern ration and killed at forty-two days. The 1957 line reached about 905 g; the 1978 line about 1,808 g; the 2005 line about 4,202 g. That is better than four times the live mass at the same age off the same grain, a compounding gain near 3.3 percent added to the growth curve every year for close to fifty years, with the share carried on the breast up by nearly 80 percent on the same run. The feed did not do it; the trial was built to prove that, and it did.

The body the specification produces is heavy and unfinished at once, a pairing absent from every earlier domestic fowl. The distal breadth of a leg bone in a six-week carcass can run close to double that of the same bone in a fully grown wild Red Junglefowl — the ancestor whose genome the lineage still very nearly wholly shares — while every marker of maturity in it reads as a juvenile's: porous, open at the growth plate, grown for size before it was ever grown for strength. A distinct twist at the base of the cervical vertebrae, absent from the wild stock and from every domestic line before this one, runs through a small, measurable fraction of the birds from the egg. The bird cannot forage its own way to slaughter weight, and it was never asked to.

## The two bloodlines

The contest was a public competition, run in three rounds across +1 to +3 pT by a national grocery chain, a bench of government breeding scientists, and volunteer judges drawn from more than forty jurisdictions, against a brief that asked, in as many words, for a farm bird with a breast like a turkey's. Entrants did not send birds. They sent hatching eggs, which were hatched together under one set of lamps, raised on one feed to one age, and killed on one morning; the dressed carcasses were judged on rate of gain, feed conversion, the width laid across the breast, and skin clean of tears and pinfeathers. The design took the coop, the grain, and the weather out of the result and left the bird. Two crosses won — one outright, one immediately behind it — and both were bought into commercial hatching inside the decade.

Two firms grew from the two lines. Within a decade one held something near a quarter of the market for parent breeding stock; by +35 to +44 pT the two together held close to six in ten; at the interval's height they held on the order of seven to nine in every ten birds of the world's commercial parent stock. This is **single-filament infrastructure** (Ibarra) carried from a supply line to a heritable trait — the old stud-farm problem at the scale of a planet, an empire's whole cavalry traced back to a handful of prize stallions at a few royal studs, a single bad season at any one of them a matter of state. The narrowing was never concealed. The trade press printed the market-share figures every year, plainly, the way a stud's steward posts a stallion's covering book, and no party with the standing to widen the pool ever found the risk worth what widening it would cost in yield.

## The misreading

For a scholarly generation the landfill deposits of this bone — oversized, porous, frequently malformed, and dug overwhelmingly out of what had been municipal landfill — were read as a recurring mortality of the young. The reading was reasonable: by every skeletal measure the bird is immature, open-plated and unfused, exactly as a juvenile would be. The correction came from the chemistry, not the shape. A carbon signature of a diet built overwhelmingly on one grain, maize, unlike the mixed feed carried in the tissue of every earlier domestic chicken, established that the assemblages were not dead young at all but adults, slaughtered at market weight while their skeletons remained, by every other measure, a juvenile's. The animal had been bred to reach the mass of an adult before its skeleton could finish becoming one; the die-off had never happened. The misreading was a straightforward inference from a body engineered to look, in the ground, like something it was not.

## The type fossil

The bone survives because the landfill that took it was anoxic, and anoxia does not so much preserve collagen as arrest its decay — **the surplus that survives** (Osei): an archive made with no curatorial intention at all, out of the routine discard of a market animal, by the same conditions that mummify the tissue rather than let it rot. No institution set out to keep it. The age's most successful engineered animal was kept because it was thrown away in sufficient quantity, into ground that happened not to let it go. Recovered assemblages from the type locality are termed **Crawford populations**, after Crawford Lake, and the broiler femur and tibiotarsus are the single most common vertebrate bones in its core. The bird is cited as corroborating typology in both the period's own 2024 CE refusal to ratify a geological interval of its own name and the Terran Stratigraphic Commission's ratification of that interval in 2412.

It is a bird descended entire from a shortlist settled across three seasons, selected on three numbers, and narrowed onto two bloodlines by people who wrote the narrowing down and published it. By the interval's last decades the number of them alive on the planet at any single instant ran to some twenty-five thousand million.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. What physically survives is the bone — a **Crawford population** of the Gallus Horizon, kept by the anoxic landfill that arrests collagen rather than lets it rot (Osei's *surplus that survives*).¹ The contest, the two winning crosses, the two-firm hold on the world's parent breeding stock, and the three-generation growth trial are record: the trial was published in the period's own poultry-science literature (+69 pT / 2014 CE) and recovered complete in the **Code Deposit**'s technical-text harvest.² The physiology is transcribed, not invented — the feed-controlled genetic gain in live mass at forty-two days, the ~3.3 percent compound annual growth-rate gain, the breast-yield share, the near-doubled tibiotarsus breadth at skeletal immaturity, the diagnostic cervical malformation, and the maize-diet carbon signature that overturned the juvenile-die-off reading.³ The concentration of one heritable trait onto two bloodlines is read after Ibarra as single-filament infrastructure carried from a supply line to a gene.⁴
