The Narrow Hand

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a single silver-halide photograph in the Longyear Deposit and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Narrow Hand is the Historical Faculty’s gloss for the lost name of a live-captioning workforce — writers who rendered broadcast speech into text in real time, by hand, on a twenty-two-key chorded keyboard, for viewers who could not hear it — and who are now known, through one shared statistical table, to have been unwittingly bound to a distant-water fishing fleet they never touched. The workforce is recovered from one photograph; the binding is recovered from the Code Deposit. Their own name for themselves does not survive, only its sense.

The single frame that anchors the entry shows a low room of perhaps thirty writers at identical machines, each machine a small stepped keyboard of unmarked keys, each writer wearing a single earpiece, the wall behind them carrying a clock and a printed card; it is a night desk of a live-captioning pool, photographed at work, and it is the only image of the workforce that survives. The people in it wrote spoken broadcast into on-screen text at the speed it was spoken, for audiences who were deaf or hard of hearing, using a keyboard that does not spell letters but strikes whole syllables and whole words as chords — a hand-operated variable-length code that, over a century of undirected practice, converged on very nearly the shortest encoding the mathematics of the language allows. The stream they produced was, incidentally, one of the largest bodies of naturalistic, timestamped spoken English ever written down. A generation later, unattributed and unknown to any of them, that stream became the training corpus for the frequency table of a text-compression library, and that library was carried aboard the satellite terminals of a remote fishing fleet to squeeze its position and status traffic into a starved radio channel. The table was tuned to the English of broadcast news. The fleet did not speak the English of broadcast news. The two populations, with no contact and no common employer, were compressed through the same codebook, and that codebook is the single fact that binds them. The pool’s region emptied in the decades after; the photograph is what is left.

The narrow writing

The machine in the photograph is a chorded stenotype: twenty-two keys, struck in simultaneous combinations, each combination — each stroke — standing not for a letter but for a syllable, a word, or a whole phrase. A trained writer certified for real-time work sustained something above two hundred and twenty-five words a minute, far past any letter-at-a-time typist, because the writing was never letter-at-a-time. Common words were assigned briefs: single strokes, struck once. Rare words were spelled out phonetically across several strokes. This is, exactly and not by analogy, a variable-length prefix code — an assignment of short codewords to frequent symbols and long codewords to infrequent ones, built so that no codeword is the beginning of another and the stream can be read back without spaces between the strokes.

The economy of it is governed by two results the period held as settled mathematics. The first is that word frequencies in English follow a steep rank law — a small set of words accounts for a large fraction of all running text — so that assigning the shortest strokes to that small set buys most of the available saving. The second is a hard budget on the shortest strokes themselves. Twenty-two keys admit at most 2²² — about 4.19 million — distinct simultaneous combinations, and far fewer than that are reachable by a human hand pressing in one motion, so the one-stroke vocabulary is a scarce, bounded resource. A working writer’s single-stroke briefs number in the low thousands, and they are spent, of necessity, on the most frequent words. That scarcity is not a convention of the trade; it is the same constraint the period’s coding theory names the Kraft inequality — that the codeword lengths of any decodable prefix code must satisfy a fixed budget, so that making one codeword shorter forces another longer. The writers met the budget with their hands, redrafting their own brief dictionaries year over year the way a compression scheme is retrained, and the endpoint of that century of private redrafting sat close to a floor that had been calculated independently and in ignorance of them.

The floor is the entropy of the language. A generation before the pool, working with a guessing method in which a reader predicted text one character at a time from what preceded it, the redundancy of printed English had been estimated at roughly one bit of genuine information per character — against the four and three-quarter bits a naïve equal-probability encoding of the alphabet and space would spend, and the near four bits that single-letter frequencies alone bring it to. The source coding theorem states the consequence flatly: no decodable code can represent a source in fewer bits, on average, than its entropy, and none needs many more. The Narrow Hand was a workforce that had, collectively and without instruction, driven a hand-written code down toward that bound for the specific statistics of broadcast speech — which is to say it had, in its fingers, a faithful model of how one dialect of one language distributes its words.

Its own name for that skill does not survive. What survives is the recovered sense of the name — close writing, narrow writing, a hand made small — glossed from the caption of the photograph and from the trade’s surviving vocabulary; the word itself is gone, and the Historical Faculty refers to the workforce only by the meaning.

The channel of two bytes

The writers wrote against a channel narrower than their hands. Broadcast captions of the era rode a data service tucked into a single unused line of the analogue television picture, and that service carried, by its own specification, two seven-bit characters per video field, sixty fields a second — a gross rate of nine hundred and sixty bits per second, of which a share was spent on control codes that positioned and coloured the text and cleared the screen. The effective width left for characters was a little over a hundred a second, a hard ceiling below the rate at which a fast speaker emits information. A writer who fell behind could not widen the channel. The only remedy was to send less: to drop filler, to compress a clause to its sense, to let a redundant phrase go unwritten so the caption did not lag the picture into unintelligibility. The trade taught this as a rule, and a printed card carrying it is legible on the wall in the photograph, in the clipped imperative of a working maxim:

WRITE THE SENSE, NOT THE WORDS, WHEN THE HANDS FALL BEHIND.

The card is an instruction in lossy source coding, issued to a human operator, and it is the one line of the workforce’s own language the photograph preserves. What it describes — spend the scarce channel on the information and discard the redundancy — is the same operation the machines downstream would perform on the writers’ own output, with none of the writers’ judgement about which words carried the sense.

Because the captions were generated live and to a schedule, they were also timestamped and, in many services, retained: logged verbatim against the clock as a by-product of the broadcast, in plain text, a format that outlived almost nothing else of the era’s audiovisual record but happened to survive where it had been folded into something durable. The broadcast tapes rotted on the ordinary schedule of magnetic media. The caption logs, as text, did not — not as an archive anyone kept for its own sake, but as a fixture buried inside a piece of software.

The table that travelled

The convergence is Okonkwo’s, read out of the Code Deposit — the single Sunday snapshot of the world’s public software, self-verifying because every file in it is named by a fingerprint of its own contents, so that a recovered file is provably the original. In it sits a small text-compression library of the early Late Combustion Age, built around a static code table: a fixed assignment of short bit-strings to frequent words and letter-sequences, long bit-strings to rare ones, computed once from a training corpus and shipped baked-in so that sender and receiver need not negotiate it. The library’s repository carried, as such repositories did, the corpus it had been trained on, bundled in as test data and sample input and never removed. That corpus is the caption logs — hours of naturalistic broadcast English, timestamped, one pool’s night desks legible in the word statistics. The table’s short codewords are the pool’s briefs in another substrate: the same frequent words, made short for the same reason, by the same mathematics. What the writers had approached with their hands, the library computed in a pass and froze.

The library was cheap, correct, and freely copied, and it was carried — because it was small and asked nothing of the channel — aboard the satellite messaging terminals of a distant-water fishing fleet working grounds a hemisphere from any broadcast the corpus was drawn from. Those terminals spoke a store-and-forward maritime data service at six hundred bits a second, on metered, time-slotted bursts shared across the whole fleet, where airtime was costly and each scheduled report was held to a short packet: identity, a position in coded degrees and minutes, a status field, and what free text would fit in the bits that remained. The library compressed the free text to make more of it fit. Against the fleet’s traffic it did the opposite of what it did against broadcast. The vocabulary of the grounds — the names of the banks and the seamounts, the species and the gear, the numeric strings of coordinates and catch weights, the small hard lexicon of vessels in trouble — is vocabulary that is vanishingly rare in broadcast news, and the table, computed from broadcast news, had given every rare word its longest codeword. A message built mostly of such words compressed to more bits than its own plaintext, and overran the burst; the compressor was executing a correct rule — encode each symbol at a length matched to its probability in the training source — under the one condition that made the rule harmful, that the source it was encoding was not the source it had been trained on. An overrun report spilled to a second slot on the next satellite pass, minutes later, or was truncated to fit. For a routine catch report a few minutes’ delay was nothing. The messages richest in the rare vocabulary, and so the most swollen by the table, were the ones sent when a vessel was in a condition it had no ordinary words for — which is to say the messages for which the minutes counted were the messages the codebook made longest.

Neither population knew of the other. The writers were never told their night logs had become anyone’s statistics; the fleet’s operators were never told their terminals carried the frozen speech-habits of a captioning pool. The physical fact that binds them is one table of codeword lengths, and behind it one quantity — the entropy of a particular dialect of English, measured once and built into a tool that then met a language it did not fit.

What the water reached

The workforce did not end in an event. Live captioning by hand was undercut across the +70s and +80s pT (2020s–2030s CE) by automatic speech recognition good enough for the cheaper grades of the work, and the pools thinned into small specialist remnants for the broadcasts that still would not tolerate a machine’s errors. The region where the Narrow Hand had concentrated — a workforce clusters, historically, around the few schools that teach a rare and slow-learned skill — was among the interiors and coasts the following decades redistributed, and the trade’s living transmission, teacher to writer, did not survive the dispersal intact. The satellite terminals outlasted their captioners; the compression library, content-addressed and immortal in the Code Deposit, outlasted both. What did not survive is the pool’s name, its numbers, the identities of the thirty at the machines, and any record of whether a single overrun burst ever cost a single vessel the minutes the arithmetic says it could have cost.

The reading

That last question is where the scholarship divides and does not close. One reading takes the codebook mismatch as an operative harm: the traffic that mattered most was the traffic the table swelled most, and in a channel measured in bursts of a few hundred bits, a message that overran was a message delayed or cut, so that the shape of the codebook is written into whatever the fleet failed to get through. The other reading holds the harm cosmetic — that a store-and-forward channel loses and delays messages for a dozen ordinary reasons of weather, geometry, and congestion, that no burst-level log of that fleet survives to attribute any single failure to the table rather than to the sky, and that a compression scheme a few percent worse than optimal on a minority of traffic is, against those losses, negligible. The two readings rest on the same secure facts — the table’s provenance in the caption logs, its measurable mismatch to the fleet’s lexicon, the physics of the burst — and part only on the one thing the record cannot supply, which is what the fleet sent and what arrived. The photograph shows the writers who made the statistics. It does not show the sea that inherited them.

Provenance and ground

The entry rests on a single silver-halide photograph in the Longyear Deposit, one frame of an occupational photo-essay that survived on the upper racks; it fixes the workforce, the machines, and the wall card, and nothing of the pool’s name or roster. The binding to the fleet rests on the Code Deposit: the compression library, its baked-in code table, and the caption corpus bundled with it as test data are all present and content-verified, and the identity of the table’s training text with the caption logs is Okonkwo’s, read in his standing method of taking a repository’s incidental contents as an unintended record. The information-theoretic facts — the rank law of word frequency, the entropy estimate for English, the source coding theorem and the Kraft budget on prefix codes, the two-character-per-field caption channel and the six-hundred-bit store-and-forward burst — are the period’s own established results and figures. What the fleet transmitted, and whether the codebook ever cost it, are not in the record and are not reconstructed here.
