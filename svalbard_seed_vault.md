# Svalbard Seed Vault

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the reserve's deposit-and-withdrawal register and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The one time the Arctic crop-seed reserve was ever emptied and refilled: a research institute, dispossessed of its home genebank by a siege, drew its own backups down out of the mountain, grew them out in exile, and carried the regenerated seed back up.** The transaction counts and dates are the register's own; the curator, the cold rooms, and the fields in exile are reconstruction.

---

The rescue was finished before the emergency began. Years before the city closed, the institute had done the ordinary, unglamorous thing its charter told it to do: it had dried a duplicate of its working collection down to a low, measured moisture, sealed each sample in laminate foil, and shipped the packets north — to a reserve cut more than a hundred metres into a sandstone ridge on an Arctic archipelago, nine hundred metres along the same slope from the film archive it is forever confused with. The reserve had opened at +63 pT (2008 CE) against asteroid strikes and general ruin, and its founding rhetoric ran to the end of the world. What it actually did, for this institute, was smaller and duller and exactly enough. It held the boxes at −18 °C, above a mountain that sat by itself at −3 to −4 °C, under forty to sixty metres of overburden and a hundred jurisdictions' worth of other people's boxes — a design meant for four and a half million samples, a little over a million already lodged — and it promised, under a rule the discipline calls the black-box principle, never to open them. Ownership stayed with the depositor. The reserve's own staff had neither the right nor the means to look inside. That was the point.

The packets in the north were a base collection: set aside expressly not to be grown, tested, or drawn on, as distinct from the active collection the institute kept at home and worked every season — lending seed to breeders, regenerating lots as they aged, answering the questions a genebank exists to answer. A base collection answers nothing. It is the copy that is meant to still exist when the working copy cannot. For most of the reserve's life that distinction was administrative, a line in a deposit agreement nobody expected to invoke. Then the working copy could not.

The city where the institute's home genebank stood — on the old dryland frontier, in the country where the wild grasses were first taken in hand — went under siege in an ordinary war. Whether the people of that city died or went is not a thing the surviving record settles, and this reconstruction will not pretend to. What the record holds is narrower: cold rooms that needed a current the grid had stopped reliably giving, a front line that moved across the districts between the genebank and any road its seed could safely take, and a small staff keeping the working collection alive on fuel and improvisation while deciding, season by season, that they could not keep it there. The active collection was going the way active collections go — not in a flash, but in the slow attrition of the upkeep a living archive needs and a besieged one cannot get.

So in +70 pT (2015 CE) the institute filed a request the reserve had never received before. In the seven years the mountain had been open, seed had only ever gone in. Now an institute asked for its own back. The first tranche was some thirty-eight thousand samples — bread and durum wheats, barleys, lentils, chickpeas, faba beans, forage legumes, and the wild grasses and vetches that were the collection's real treasure, the drought-hardy ancestors of half the world's bread, shaped over ten thousand years to exactly the dry heat the region's own climatologists had already forecast, and published, would spread. The boxes came down out of the mountain and went not home — home was unreachable — but to new stations the institute had stood up on borrowed and rented ground elsewhere, out of the war's way.

The curator who signed the request had deposited some of those boxes herself, years before, and had never once seen inside them; the black-box rule ran both ways, and a depositor's own eyes were as unwelcome as anyone's once the foil was sealed and the accession logged. What came back down the mountain were the packets exactly as sent, matched box for box against the register, carrying the labels she had printed. Whether the seed inside them was still alive was the one thing neither the register nor the mountain could tell her. A decade-old lot, dried and cold, ought to germinate — the arithmetic said so, the old rule that a seed's life roughly doubles for each degree of chill and each point of moisture drawn out of it — but that arithmetic had been fitted to trials of weeks and stretched across centuries, and it had never quite been a measurement so much as a hope with an equation attached. The deepest thing the record had to set beside it was a single date-palm seed from a burned storeroom in a desert fortress by the Dead Sea, some two thousand years in the dry dark before a research unit coaxed it to sprout at +60 pT (2005 CE) — a sample of one, of one species, under conditions nobody had controlled. The institute's seed had waited a decade. The only way to know was to wet a sample and watch.

So through those seasons the institute's work was, in the plainest sense, gardening. Seed on damp paper, and counts of how many pushed out a radicle. Plot after plot of the survivors sown into fields that were not theirs, hand-managed against cross-pollination and mislabelling and the ordinary losses of a bad year, under a sky whose rains came at the wrong times. An orthodox seed makes all of it possible: dried and cold, its metabolism runs down almost to stopped, and it waits, and then given water and warmth it does the one thing a frozen archive cannot do for itself — it becomes a plant, and the plant makes new seed, more than went in. That is the only way a base collection is ever turned back into a working one. You cannot shelve your way to a living lot. You have to grow it. Enough came up. Not all — never all — but enough, accession after accession, to rebuild the collection the siege had taken out of the copy the mountain had kept.

The mountain, meanwhile, chose those same years to fail its own advertisement. In +72 pT (2017 CE) — the year of the second withdrawal — an unusually warm, wet autumn dropped rain where the ridge expected snow, onto ground whose permafrost had been recorded thinning for decades in a warming trend the region's scientists had measured and published well before the first packet was ever shelved. Meltwater ran into the reserve's access tunnel and froze there, fifteen metres in, stopped by the tunnel's grade rather than by anything anyone had designed. It reached no seed chamber. No sample was touched. But the premise that had let the reserve's builders treat the cold as a free and permanent property of the rock had just failed a live test at the front door, in public, within nine years of opening. The reserve had a public treasury behind it. It waterproofed the tunnel, pulled the heat-throwing equipment out of it, cut drains to route the meltwater around the workings, and threaded coolant pipe through the near-surface rock to hold it frozen by machine rather than on the mountain's word — done by about +74 pT (2019 CE), at a cost reckoned afterward in the tens of millions. The film archive nine hundred metres down the same slope, a small firm selling storage by the reel, got no comparable rescue; the frozen ground beneath it is thought to have given out sometime between +180 and +260 pT. The mountain warned both institutions in the same year. Only one had a state behind it, and only one acted.

By +72 pT the institute had begun the traffic that closes a loop: sending seed back up. Not the same seed — the withdrawn packets had been planted and spent — but its children, regenerated stock grown out in exile, dried and packed and carried north again to the reserve, a fresh backup of a collection rebuilt entirely from the old one. The withdrawals ran to three tranches, +70, +72, and +74 pT, some hundred and sixteen thousand samples in all, and by the last of them the register was logging boxes going down the mountain and fresh boxes coming up it in the same seasons — the one full withdraw-and-return cycle the reserve ever ran. An institution had lost its home collection to a war, drawn its own duplicate out of a ridge on the far side of the planet, made it live again on land it did not own, and put the living result back into the ridge. The reserve had been sold to the age as insurance against the end of everything. What it actually did, once, when it was needed, was let a dispossessed genebank hand itself back its own seed, and then watch that genebank carry the next generation of it up the mountain, in boxes it still could not open. By the low standard the black-box principle actually sets — can an institution get its material back and use it — the rescue held.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The withdrawal in three tranches (+70, +72, +74 pT), its ~116,000-sample total, the ~38,000 of the first, and the redeposit of regenerated stock are logged in the reserve's own deposit-and-withdrawal register — boxes counted under the black-box principle that let the operator tally what it could not open. The curator, the cold rooms, and the fields in exile are inference from the shape those numbers make. The rest is real and pre-Contraction: orthodox seed dried and held at −18 °C above a −3 to −4 °C permafrost floor, in a chamber over a hundred metres into the rock; a base collection distinct from a working active one; the +72 pT meltwater that reached fifteen metres into the access tunnel, froze, and touched no chamber; the ground-freezing retrofit by +74 pT; and the neighbouring film archive, left to passive permafrost, whose frozen ground is thought to have failed between +180 and +260 pT.
