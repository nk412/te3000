# The False Star

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a single tracked-exposure night photograph, preserved as unlabelled test data inside the Code Deposit, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The False Star is the Historical Faculty's name for an unidentified optical way-beacon on a Late Combustion Age overland displacement route, recovered only because its flash left a beaded arc of light across one photographer's tracked exposure of the night sky.** The name is borrowed, not invented for the occasion: it is the label an automated star-matching routine gave the beacon's own light, decades before any scholar looked at the frame and saw a beacon rather than an error.

---

**A way-beacon, in the sense the discipline gives the word, is any fixed light, sound, or radio source that a moving population came to steer by without anyone having built it for that purpose — and the False Star is the cleanest surviving instance of one.** It is known from a single photograph: a wide-field night exposure, taken on a motorised sky-tracking mount to hold the stars to points, in which a ground-fixed light beyond the frame's true subject blinked on and off across the exposure and, because the mount was turning the whole camera to cancel the sky's rotation, left not a smear but a string of separated points along a shallow arc. From the geometry of that arc alone — nothing else in the frame identifies it — the beacon's flash period, approximate intensity, and probable technology are recoverable to a working tolerance; its builder, its exact site, and the route it lit are not. The image reached the record by accident, bundled as sample data into a small piece of astronomical software years after it was taken, and was carried into the Code Deposit's single snapshot of the world's public code with that software rather than with any archive of the corridor it shows. A now-standard method for reading flash timing out of accidental long exposures — Lindqvist's — was built to extract exactly this kind of evidence, and the False Star is the case it was built on.

## An arc of points, not a streak

The frame's ostensible subject is the galactic core, framed low over a ridgeline with a wide, fast lens. The camera sat on a motorised equatorial mount, driven at the sidereal rate — 15.041 arcseconds of arc per second, the rate at which the sky appears to turn against a fixed point on the ground, set by the length of the sidereal day rather than the ordinary 24-hour clock day — so that over an exposure of several minutes the stars, which share that same apparent motion, sit still on the sensor while everything genuinely fixed to the ground rotates slowly the other way beneath them. This is the standard technique for a single wide-field night frame sharp enough to hold faint starlight without trailing it into streaks; the cost is that the terrain, the horizon, and anything sitting on it smear into a shallow arc over the same exposure, in exact proportion to how long the shutter stayed open.

A point low in that arc did not smear. It blinked. Across an exposure independently timed, from the arc-length of the frame's own foreground drift, at very close to three hundred seconds, the source appears as sixty-one distinct, sharp points spaced along the arc rather than as one continuous line — evidence, on its own, that the source was flashing rather than burning continuously, and that each individual flash was too brief for even the mount's slow drift to smear it into a dash. Divided across sixty intervals, the three-hundred-second exposure gives a flash period of five seconds, evenly enough spaced across the frame that no interval departs from the mean by more than a few percent — a regularity a moving, wavering, or hand-carried light could not have held, and the first fact that rules out a lamp, a fire, or a vehicle's headlamp, and rules in a built, powered, self-timed device.

The individual points are the second fact. At the sidereal drift rate, a flash lasting even a full second would smear across roughly fifteen arcseconds of the frame — resolvable, at the lens's focal length, as a short dash rather than a point. That none of the sixty-one points shows any elongation at all bounds the true flash duration to something on the order of a millisecond or less: long enough to register on the sensor, far too brief to move measurably against the sky's drift. A flash that short and that regular, repeating on a multi-second cycle, is not a filament or a light-emitting diode driven continuously and shuttered; it is the discharge signature of a gas-filled flash tube — a xenon strobe — fired once per cycle from a capacitor charged in the interval between flashes. The technology long predates the corridor and was, by the mid-twenty-first century, the standard mechanism for exactly this kind of duty: a compact stored charge, discharged in a fraction of a millisecond, produces a peak brightness many orders of magnitude above anything the same average electrical power could sustain if driven continuously — a genuine and large gain in visibility purchased at the price of being visible for only an instant at a time.

## The measure of the light

That gain is not free, and the physics of exactly how much it buys is what lets the frame's one point of light be turned into an estimate of the device's size and purpose rather than left as a curiosity. The apparent brightness a distant point source delivers to an observer falls with the inverse square of distance and, additionally, with the transmissivity of the air the light must cross — a relation formalised for exactly this class of problem, in the same period's own lighthouse and aviation-lighting engineering, as Allard's Law. Set against the photograph's own photometric floor — how faint a genuine star the frame can be shown to have recorded, by comparison against catalogued stars appearing elsewhere in the same exposure — and against a conservative, period-typical figure for night-sky atmospheric clarity, Allard's Law brackets the source's peak intensity, for any plausible siting distance across the kind of saddle or ridge terrain the corridor is independently inferred to cross, to the low thousands of candela: bright enough, at the several kilometres of unobstructed line of sight a ridge-top or saddle site would command, to sit clearly above the threshold of night vision, and well short of the tens of thousands of candela the period's largest civil obstruction beacons carried. That a mere millisecond of light could plausibly reach so far, rather than merely nearby, is answered by a second and older result from the same engineering literature, the Blondel–Rey relation for the effective intensity of a brief flash: a pulse much shorter than the eye's own roughly fifth-of-a-second integration time is seen as dimmer than its instantaneous peak, but the loss is modest, not proportional to the pulse's full brevity, so that a flash tube's enormous peak candela survives the conversion to perceived brightness largely intact. The engineering logic these two results together describe — store slowly, release in an instant, and let the eye's own physiology do the rest of the work of visibility — is what makes a five-second, millisecond-duty cycle the efficient choice for a device that had, most plausibly, only a small solar panel and a small battery to draw on, and needed to be seen across kilometres rather than metres.

## The saddle, at night

None of this identifies the corridor. What can be said is drawn from where such a device would be useful rather than from anything the frame itself records: a beacon visible for kilometres, on a multi-second cycle distinct from a star's steadiness, sited on high ground, serves no purpose to a stationary population and every purpose to one moving through unlit terrain after dark — and the wider redistribution to which this corridor is presumed to belong ran, across the continent it crossed, on foot and by whatever vehicles still ran, over generations spanning roughly +80 to +160 pT (2025–2105 CE). Night movement on such routes is amply attested elsewhere in the record, for reasons the surviving fragments do not settle in any one case, and a fixed light on a five-second cycle, distinguishable from any star by its rhythm alone, is a wayfinding aid a walking population could use without instruction — in the same way older maritime practice used a lighthouse's distinct "character," its particular rhythm of flashes, to let a sighted vessel identify which light it was seeing rather than merely that a light was there. Nothing in the surviving record shows that this device was ever intended to serve that function. Nothing rules it out.

## Beacon or debris

That absence of intent is where the reading divides, and the discipline does not close the division. One school takes the beacon as deliberate infrastructure of the displacement itself — a way-marker sited and maintained, however informally, by or for a population that needed one, in the same improvised register as other attested corridor works of the period: cached water, marked fords, repurposed structures. The other takes it as debris that outlived its original errand: a hazard or obstruction light, of the kind period civil-engineering practice fixed to towers, masts, and other tall structures to warn aircraft and vehicles away, left solar-powered and self-sufficient on a structure whose own purpose had already lapsed, blinking correctly and on schedule to a sky that had stopped sending anything to avoid it, and adopted only afterward, opportunistically, by travellers who had no way to know which kind of light they were steering by. The photograph cannot decide between them; a device built for either purpose looks, in a single frame taken from kilometres away, identical. Nothing about a five-second xenon cycle says whether the last hand to touch it meant it for the road or had simply stopped being able to turn it off.

## A stray light in someone else's data

The frame was never kept as a record of anything. Its earliest recoverable trace is not an archive entry but a source-code commit, in a small, now-orphaned repository for an amateur astrophotography tool built to flag satellite trails and sensor artefacts in tracked exposures — a streak-versus-star classifier, the kind of narrow utility one photographer wrote for their own use and put up publicly rather than keep private. The file was added, undated in its own header but datable from the commit history to +112 pT (2057 CE), as one of a handful of bundled test images, with a one-line comment in the commit log: `test asset: terrestrial light bleed in lower frame, useful negative case for arc-vs-point classifier`. Whoever wrote that line was using the beacon's own flashes as an example of the thing their software was meant to reject. The repository, along with tens of millions of others, was swept up whole in the single Sunday snapshot of the world's public software that forms the Code Deposit — not because the frame mattered, but because everything did, indiscriminately, that particular week. That the reel carrying it survived at all owes nothing to the frame either: the consignment shipped late, held back by a shipping delay unrelated to its contents, and so was loaded onto the upper racks of the storage chamber rather than the lower ones — the same accident of shelving that spared the whole of the Code Deposit when the chamber later flooded and its lower racks dissolved in acidified water. The frame survived because of where a crate sat on a shelf, for reasons that had nothing to do with a beacon on a mountainside a world away.

The "false star" name is a later layer again. A cross-catalogue survey, run generations afterward against every recoverable astronomical-looking frame in the Code Deposit to build a working sky atlas from accidental amateur images, plate-solved this exposure against known star positions and flagged several of the arc's sixty-one points as unmatched detections — the routine, correctly executed, doing exactly what such software is built to do with a bright point that is not where any catalogued star should be. Its own diagnostic vocabulary for such a result is *false star*: a plausible-looking detection that the catalogue disowns. Lindqvist's contribution was noticing that the "false stars" in this one frame were not scattered noise, as the routine's flags elsewhere in the corpus mostly were, but lay in a smooth, evenly spaced arc consistent with a single terrestrial source under sidereal drift — and from that regularity built the general method, now standard, for reading a flashing ground light's timing and rough intensity out of any tracked exposure that happens to have caught one.

## Provenance and ground

The chronology rests entirely on the one frame and its own file and repository history; nothing about the corridor, the beacon's builder, or its site is attested independently. The photometric and timing reconstruction is Lindqvist's, run against catalogue star positions elsewhere in the same exposure for calibration. The frame's path into the record — bundled as test data, then swept into the Code Deposit's snapshot, then spared by the consignment's shelving — is read from the repository's own commit metadata, following Okonkwo's standing method of treating a software repository's incidental contents as an unintended record of whatever it happened to be carrying. Marchetti's general caution against reading a piece of code's own comments as if they were composed testimony applies here as elsewhere: the commit log's phrase is a note to a future maintainer, not a description meant to survive as anything else.
