# Intermodal Shipping Container

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the vessel-position record of the anchorages either side of a canal that closed for six days, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **Six days in +76 pT, when a single ship laid corner to corner across a one-lane canal held the better part of a planet's seaborne trade at anchor, seen from the bridge wing of one of the hundreds of hulls that waited.** The swelling and draining of the anchorage is transcribed from the fleet's own position broadcasts; the mate who counts the hulls, and everything he thinks, is reconstruction.

---

By the fourth morning the anchorage held more cargo than most nations owned, and the mate had run out of things to do with it.

His ship had swung to her anchor since Tuesday, one steel island in a roadstead that had not existed a week before — a hundred hulls, then two hundred, laid across the approaches in the loose order of their arrival, laden box ships mostly, stacked nine and ten high with the corrugated steel boxes that were the whole reason any of them were here and the whole reason none of them were moving. Somewhere to the south, in a reach of the cut too narrow to call a channel — one lane of dredged water between two banks of desert — a single ship of the largest class ever built, better than twenty-four thousand boxes at a lift, had come across the wind and put her bow in one bank and her stern very nearly in the other, and lay there now, corner to corner, a wall. He had never seen her. He never would. She was a position on a screen and a voice on a distant net and the reason his own four-to-eight watch had stopped being a watch, there being nothing left in it to keep.

So he counted hulls instead. It was a thing to do with eyes trained to find the one ship on a horizon that meant something.

The cut was the point. Two of them, really — this one and its cousin on the far side of the world, each a ditch chopped through an isthmus so a hull could pass between two oceans without spending three weeks running down the length of a continent and back up the other side. Between them they carried the trade of a planet. He had been through this one a dozen times and never once thought of it as narrow; you thought of it as the short way. Now it was one lane, and one hull was across it, and one hull across a one-lane reach closes it in both directions for exactly as long as it takes to move that hull and not an hour less. A thing that fails shut fails completely, whatever its size. No one had designed the dependency. It had accreted, the way a footpath accretes, because it was shorter — until the manufactured goods of a whole species ran through an eye you could throw a stone across.

He went below and made his rounds anyway, because a ship at anchor is still a ship. The boxes were the marvel and he knew it, even bored, even here. Two lengths only — twenty feet and forty, the long just twice the short — and at each of a box's eight corners a single cast-steel socket cut to the one geometry the whole trade had settled on across the years from +16 to +24 pT: a rotating pin, a twist-lock, that a crane, a rail wagon, a truck bed, and the cell guides of his own holds could each take hold of the identical way, so that none of them ever had to know, or ask, or open the thing to see what was inside. That was the trick entire. The machinery did not care what it carried. It cared only about the corners. Nine-tenths of everything a household owned that it had not grown or dug for itself had ridden in one of these, in a fleet past twenty million boxes making some eight hundred million lifts a year — and it *worked*; that was what he'd have told you, leaning on a lashing bridge in the dark. The first man to try it had sailed fifty-eight of them out of a northeastern port to one on the gulf coast, +11 pT, on a made-over tanker, and cut the cost of putting cargo aboard from near six dollars a ton to sixteen cents. After that there was no other way to do it, and no going back to the days of a thousand men and a thousand slings.

By the fourth morning some of the hulls around him had weighed and turned for the long way round the cape, the sum finally coming out on the side of three extra weeks of fuel over one more day of waiting on a tide. He did the other sum, the big one, the one the whole net was passing about like a rumour: the value of the goods sitting dead still on both sides of one grounded ship, reckoned by the day. It was a figure with too many zeros to feel. He stopped feeling it and went back to counting hulls, which had the mercy of being a number he could see.

What moved the ship in the end was not the figure and not the outrage. It was tugs — a dozen of them, working the clock around against a hull that outweighed all of them together by two orders of magnitude — and dredgers eating the bank out from under her bow, and, at the last, the moon: a spring tide, the fullest water of the month, arriving on the sixth day to lift what the engines had loosened. That was the part he respected. Nobody panicked and nobody prayed it away. They measured the tide and dug the sand and waited for the water, and on the sixth day the stern came free, and then the bow, and the wall was a ship again, under way, and behind her the roadstead began, hull by hull, to unstack.

It closed over. Within a season it was a story nobody told, one grounding among the tens of thousands of clean passages that bracketed it. What lasts of it is not the six days. It is the record the ships kept of themselves without meaning to: every hull of any size broadcasting its name and place on a fixed schedule, the whole anchorage swelling and then draining as a field of timed points, from which the queue can be counted still.

The boxes last too, after a fashion. The ones riding the weather deck are built of a steel that stays sound only because it is let to dry between soakings — it grows a tight, close skin of its own rust that slows the rest of the rusting to a fraction — and the several hundred that go over the side in an ordinary year, counted to the box and published by the trade's own body without any alarm at all (some fourteen hundred a year through +63 to +74 pT; a low of two hundred and twenty-one in the quiet year +78 pT; near fifteen hundred in +80 pT against two hundred and eighty million carried, one storm alone accounting for six hundred and forty of them), lose that skin the moment they settle and stay, and revert to ordinary steel corroding on the ordinary schedule, down where the traffic was always thickest.

Down there lie the discipline's two most-prized wrecks: sealed box ships, one at the northern mouth of this very cut and one in the Strait of Malacca, read the way a whole Bronze Age hull is read — every class of thing the age moved, in the proportion it moved them. They lie where they lie for no better reason than that these were the two narrowest places the most ships ever passed. A trade squeezed onto two channels drowns onto the same two channels. The best whole samples we have of the age are not a curator's achievement. They are a traffic statistic.

The authority that owned the cut published its own loss for the closure — the tolls it did not take — at some fourteen million dollars for each of the six days it stood shut. The value of the trade held dead still on either side of the single hull across it, by the same authority's reckoning, ran to nine thousand million a day.

Nearly three orders of magnitude between them.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The roadstead is real evidence: through the interval every hull of any size broadcast its identity and position on a fixed schedule, and the surviving broadcasts show the anchorages north and south of the cut swelling across six days of +76 pT (2021 CE) and draining after — Diallo's is the founding study of reading such vessel logs.¹ The queue is transcribed; the mate, his rounds, and his arithmetic are inference from the shape the tracks make. The chokepoint's binary failure (Ibarra²), the box's two lengths and single corner geometry, the handling-cost collapse of the first sailing, the fleet and the traffic, and the weathering steel that grows a protective skin only under wet-dry cycling and loses it on permanent submersion (Halvorsen³) are all real, published, and pre-Contraction.
