# Intermodal Shipping Container

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the trade's own published loss returns and the canal authority's closure accounts, and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The intermodal shipping container reduced the world's non-bulk sea cargo to one steel box of two lengths and a single corner geometry, and the trade of a planet to two canals — one of which a single grounded ship could, and once did, close for six days.** The box's dimensions, the fleet and traffic totals, the annual loss returns, and the closure's costs are transcribed from the industry's and the canal authority's own published figures; the reading of the disproportion is inference.

---

In +76 pT a container ship of the largest class then built, driven across the wind in a one-lane reach of an inter-ocean canal, grounded corner to corner and closed the channel in both directions for six days. The authority that owned the cut published its own loss — the tolls it did not take — at some fourteen million dollars for each day it stood shut. The value of the trade held at anchor on either side of the single hull, by the same authority's reckoning, ran to nine thousand million dollars a day. Nearly three orders of magnitude separated the two figures, and only the smaller was anyone's to book.

The box beneath the disproportion was the plainest object of its age and its most consequential. Standardising it standardised the trade, and the trade, seeking the short way between oceans, standardised itself onto two canals cut through two isthmuses; what could be carried in one box was carried in twenty million, and what ran through one canal could be stopped by one ship. The boxes are lost at sea by the thousand every year, counted and published without alarm, and the few that stay down have furnished the discipline its two finest samples of the **Combustion Age** — not by any curator's design, but because they sank where the traffic ran densest.

## The box

It came in two lengths only, twenty feet and forty, the long exactly twice the short, and at each of its eight corners a single cast-steel fitting cut to one geometry: a twist-lock socket that a crane, a rail wagon, a truck bed, and a ship's cell guides could each take hold of the identical way. The whole trade converged on that geometry across +16 to +24 pT. Nothing downstream of a sealed box had to know what it held; the machinery cared only about the corners, and onto that indifference the age loaded upward of nine-tenths of everything it moved by sea that was neither poured nor pumped in bulk — a fleet past twenty million boxes making on the order of eight hundred million loaded moves a year, its largest vessels carrying better than twenty-four thousand boxes at a lift.

It began small and never reversed. The first operator sailed fifty-eight boxes on a converted tanker from a northeastern port to one on the gulf coast in +11 pT, cutting the cost of putting cargo aboard from $5.86 a ton to $0.16. After that there was no other way to do it, and no return to the thousand men and thousand slings of break-bulk handling.

## The isthmus problem

A trade of that size ran, at two points, through channels a stone could be thrown across. Between two oceans a hull could round the length of a continent, three weeks each way, or pass through one of two ditches cut through an isthmus; it passed through the ditches. No one designed the dependency. It accreted, the way a footpath accretes, because it was shorter — until the manufactured goods of a species ran through two eyes of dredged water one lane wide.

A single lane that carries both ways fails completely when it fails at all, whatever its throughput: one grounded hull closes it for exactly as long as it takes to move that hull, and not an hour less. The +76 pT closure is read as the diagnostic case — a six-day, one-vessel stoppage of a planet's seaborne trade, the cost to the owner of the cut three orders of magnitude below the cost to the trade it held (**the isthmus problem**, Ibarra).¹ What freed the ship was tugs, dredgers eating the bank from beneath her bow, and a spring tide on the sixth day; behind her the anchorage unstacked hull by hull, and within a season the grounding was one clean-passage statistic among tens of thousands.

## Loss

Boxes are lost overboard in the ordinary course too, counted to the unit and published each year, without alarm, by the trade's own body: about fourteen hundred a year on average across +63 to +74 pT, a low of two hundred and twenty-one in +78 pT, near fifteen hundred in +80 pT — against some two hundred and eighty million boxes carried that year, one storm alone accounting for six hundred and forty of them. The loss fraction is about five in a million.

What goes over the side changes chemistry as it sinks. The weathering steel of a container's hull holds a tight protective skin of its own rust only while it is let to dry between soakings; permanently submerged, the same alloy sheds that skin and reverts to ordinary steel corroding at the ordinary rate (**the drowned patina**, Halvorsen).² The boxes drown where the traffic is thickest, which is where the channels are narrowest. The discipline's two best mixed-cargo samples of the age — sealed box ships read the way a Bronze Age hull is read, every class of good in the proportion it moved — lie at the northern mouth of one canal and in the Strait of Malacca, the two **Uluburun wrecks**, on the seabed for no better reason than that these were the two narrowest places the most ships ever passed. A trade squeezed onto two channels drowns onto the same two. The best whole samples the age left of itself are not a curator's achievement. They are a traffic statistic.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction only in its reading; almost every quantity in it is transcribed. The box's two lengths and single corner geometry, the convergence of +16 to +24 pT, the first sailing's fifty-eight boxes and the collapse in handling cost, the fleet and traffic totals, and the annual loss returns are real, published, and pre-Contraction. So is the weathering-steel chemistry: an alloy that forms a protective oxide patina only under alternating wet-dry cycling and loses it on permanent submersion (Halvorsen). The +76 pT closure and both its figures are the canal authority's own accounts. The network's hub-and-feeder hierarchy, and the order in which its ports lost scheduled service as regional trade contracted, are read from the ships' own call logs (Diallo).³ What is inference is the reading — that the boxes lost and the ships best preserved concentrate at the same two narrow places not by any curator's judgement of what mattered, but because a trade run through two chokepoints fails, and drowns, at the chokepoints.
