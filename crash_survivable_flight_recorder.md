# Crash-Survivable Flight Recorder

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a deep-ocean search authority's operation logs and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **The twenty-month hunt for a flight recorder lost with an airliner over open ocean, recovered intact and readable from three thousand nine hundred metres down long after its locator beacon had gone dark — reconstructed from the search authority's own operation logs.** The grid, the depths, the dates, and the recovery are transcribed from those logs; the analyst who read them, and the cold that turned out to be her margin, are inference.

---

The beacon had thirty days of battery in it, and it spent them talking to no one.

She started from that — the analyst whose search grid the logs preserve, working a rectangle of open ocean where the water fell past three thousand nine hundred metres onto a plain no one had mapped. The two recorders were down there, the flight-data unit and the voice unit, both certified to survive precisely this and to be found afterward. She could not hear them. She had never been going to hear them. By the time the first ships reached the water at +64 pT the thirty days were spent, and the locator beacons — rated for exactly that, one acoustic tone at one frequency for one month at depth — had fallen silent on schedule, the way everything about these instruments was built to keep to a schedule. The one part of the whole system whose only job was to be heard was the first part to die, and it died on time.

The first searches did what searches had always done: they trusted the last position the aircraft had reported and the way the sea moves things, drew a circle around it, and swept. They found nothing. They widened the circle and swept it again and found nothing again. Surface debris was logged and drift-tracked back to competing origins; sonar runs crossed the wrong plain and came home clean. The logs from those seasons read as a catalogue of confident, careful, empty passes, and they run to nearly two years.

What she brought to it, in the season the logs change character, was older than the aircraft: a method out of deep-sea wreck-hunting, worked out generations before for lost submarines and sunk hulls. She cut the seabed into a grid of cells and gave each cell two numbers — how likely the wreck lay in it, and how likely a single sweep would see the wreck if it did. Then she let the failures do the work. Every empty pass was fed back in; a cell swept clean did not fall out of the map so much as hand its probability to the cells not yet looked at. The drift models had been treated as the answer. She treated them as one prior among several and weighted them down each time they sent a ship somewhere and the ship came back with flat mud and nothing on it. Run by run, the belief pooled into water the earlier reasoning had argued against — close in to the last known position, in the deep, where the confident models had said the wreck should not be.

The side-scan towfish ran its lines a few hundred metres off the bottom, painting the seabed in returned sound. Over the plain the sound came back the way sound comes back off sediment: soft, patternless, geological. Then on one line it came back hard and angular and repeated — a scatter of bright returns with edges, laid across the mud in a shape sediment does not make. A debris field has a geometry. She had spent twenty months learning the difference between geometry and its absence, and the difference was on the screen.

The rest was not luck. A remotely operated vehicle went down the four kilometres of water at +66 pT, and the recorders were in the field the grid had marked, and they came up. Both housings were sound. The instrument had been certified, under a standard harmonised across the era's aviation authorities at +51 pT, to take three thousand four hundred times gravity for six and a half milliseconds, a fire at eleven hundred degrees for an hour and a slower one at two hundred sixty for ten, five thousand pounds of crush on each of six faces, a dropped spike, and the pressure of twenty thousand feet of seawater for thirty days. It had come to rest under thirteen thousand feet and stayed there, watertight, for the better part of two years — well inside every figure on the certificate. Read out on the bench, both units gave up their recordings entire: the flight-data unit's parameters, mandated since +13 pT; and the voice loop, required since +21 pT on a rolling window first fixed at thirty minutes by the cost and bulk of magnetic tape, and kept at thirty minutes for four decades after solid-state memory had made the length free, extended to two hours only at +63 pT and to twenty-five only by a rule dated +70 pT and applied from +76 pT. On this aircraft it held the two hours the newest rule then allowed, and every one of them read clean.

The boxes did exactly what they were written to do, at the depth and for the duration they were written to do it, on schedule. The one thing in the whole account that had to improvise was the search that found them. What the incident changed afterward was procedure, not steel: locator-beacon life was extended by later rule to ninety days, three times what had run out here, and aircraft were separately required to report their own position automatically as they flew — a thin continuous stream nobody would have to spend twenty months going to find. The lesson taken was not that the box had failed. It hadn't. It was that trusting a single silent object to wait on the seabed for someone clever enough to find it was a bet you could lose for two years even in the winning.

She won hers because of the cold. This is the part the logs do not mention, because no one working the search had any reason to think about it: the memory she read out was flash memory, and flash memory forgets. Its own makers rated it to hold data unpowered for about a year once a chip had been worked to the end of its rated life, and for something nearer a decade only if it were kept cool. The seabed at three thousand nine hundred metres sits a few degrees above freezing, in permanent dark. The same cold and depth that had hidden the wreck for two years were the only reason its memory still held anything at all when she reached it — the cold bought the margin the beacon's battery never had. She recovered a readable recorder inside a window she never knew was closing.

The recorders before this one did not need that window. The earliest of them, fitted before there was a rule requiring any, wrote by dragging a stylus across a moving strip of soft metal foil, scratching the flight into the metal itself — a trace legible without power to anyone who recovers the foil and thinks to hold it to a raking light, the same solution as an incised votive plaque, cut into base metal precisely because ink and its readers cannot be promised. Foil gave way to tape, and tape to flash, for capacity and for toughness; and every step made the housing harder to destroy and its memory quicker to forget once no one was reading it.

So somewhere on a plain no probability grid will ever be built to close on, a later recorder of this same crash-proof lineage is waiting to be recovered. Its titanium will be sound. Its seals will have held. It will have survived the impact and the fire and the pressure exactly as certified, and it will be lifted, one century from now or ten, intact and identifiable and empty, its memory a clean dead grid discharged in the dark long before anyone came for it. The scratched foil from the age before it will still be legible. The armoured box will have kept everything except the recording.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The search is real evidence — a probability-mapped seabed hunt run long after the recorder's beacon had expired, preserved in the search authority's operation logs and first read closely by Verhoeven.¹ The grid method, the years of empty passes, the depth near 3,900 m, the +64 pT loss, and the +66 pT recovery are transcribed; the analyst, her patience, and the cold as her unearned margin are inference from the shape they make. The physics is not invented: Bayesian search theory redistributes probability across unswept cells after each null sweep; the certification figures and the loop-length history are the period's own (**the cost that outlived its constraint**, Castellanos²); flash memory holds data unpowered from about a year at a worn chip's rated end to perhaps a decade kept cool, while the earliest engraved foil is legible without power on recovery — the two classes of **the mute survivor** (Fujimori³). Deep-ocean bottom water sits a few degrees above freezing, which is why the memory outlasted the beacon at all, and why no later find can count on it.

2. Castellanos, R. (2979). *The Thirty-Minute Loop: Regulatory Lag After a Constraint Disappeared.* — Coins **the cost that outlived its constraint**: reads the gap between the tape-to-solid-state transition and the eventual duration mandate as institutional, not technical.
3. Fujimori, T. (2983). "The Mute Survivor: Two Taphonomic Classes of Crash-Protected Flight Memory." *J. Anthropogenic Stratigraphy* 76: 140–189. — Coins **the mute survivor**: engraved foil, legible without power, against solid-state memory, crash-proof but silent within decades of its last reading. Declines to estimate how many recovered late units, read once and never copied, are now blank a second time.
