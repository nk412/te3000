# Extreme Ultraviolet Lithography

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **Extreme ultraviolet lithography is the process that patterned nearly every advanced logic device fabricated after +74 pT, produced for the whole of its commercial life by one systems-integration firm, one optics firm, and one absorbed light-source line — and its working fuel is the same scarce, filamentary-traded metal that gave the Bronze Age its name, three thousand years earlier.** For an unrelated industrial process resting on an equally narrow supplier base, see **Industrial Nitrogen Fixation**. For a consumer additive distributed by an equally few licensed plants, see **Leaded Petrol**. For what became of the design record once the firms stopped being read, see **Attrition, the**.

---

Tin ore is not common, and where it is workable at all it tends to cluster: a handful of placer streams, a handful of vein systems, a wreck's worth moving at a time between them. Bronze Age smiths needed it in roughly a one-part-in-ten alloy with copper, always sourced, always shipped in, and the trade in it left the archaeology of the whole era filamentary — a scatter of ingots on a small number of sunken hulls standing in for a network that must, on the demand, have been far wider than its surviving evidence. Three thousand years later the demand for tin had not gone away; it had changed shape entirely. By the closing decades of the Combustion Age, a single machine — one design, from one production line — was consuming molten tin at a rate no Bronze Age port ever moved, not to cast it, but to shoot it.

That machine is the subject of this entry, and the coincidence of the metal is not decoration. It is the cleanest instance in the corpus of an old scarcity recurring in a new form, at a different scale, for a reason that has nothing to do with the reason it was scarce the first time.

## The problem no lens can solve

A lithographic system works by projecting a pattern through an optical column onto a light-sensitive coating, the way an old-fashioned photographic enlarger projects a negative onto printing paper, except that the "print" is a circuit layer a few tens of nanometres wide and the whole optical path must resolve it without smearing. Every earlier generation of the technology used light in the deep ultraviolet, transmitted through shaped glass lenses, because glass is transparent to it. At 13.5 nanometres — inside the extreme ultraviolet band, at the long edge of the soft-X-ray region — nothing is transparent. Glass absorbs it. Air absorbs it over a few millimetres. There is no lens to be ground, of any material, at any cost. The entire optical path, source to wafer, had to be redesigned around two constraints that no earlier generation of the technology had ever had to satisfy simultaneously: everything happens in vacuum, and every element that shapes the beam does it by reflection, not refraction.

Reflection at 13.5 nanometres is not free either. No single surface reflects it well at any but grazing angles. The solution, arrived at independently by several national laboratories in the early 1990s and then engineered for three more decades, is a mirror built not as a surface but as a stack: forty to eighty alternating layers of molybdenum and silicon, each three to four nanometres thick, tuned so that the faint reflection from every interface in the stack arrives at the exit face in phase with every other. Constructive interference across dozens of buried boundaries does the work no single reflecting surface could. The result, at best, returns about seventy per cent of the light that struck it. The wavelength itself was not chosen for any property of the tin plasma that would eventually illuminate it; it was chosen because 13.5 nanometres is close to the best a molybdenum-silicon stack can be tuned to reflect at all. The source was built to match the mirror, not the other way round.

## The reflection tax

A production scanner's optical path carries the beam through a source-collector mirror and then a train of projection mirrors — commonly six in the systems that reached volume manufacture, more in the generation that followed. At seventy per cent per surface, six reflections compound to roughly one-eighth of the light that entered the column reaching the mask and wafer; a seventh mirror would take it under a tenth. Every photon lost this way is lost to absorption inside the multilayer stack itself, as heat the mirror substrate then has to shed without distorting by more than a fraction of a nanometre — because a mirror that swells out of tolerance under its own accumulated heat degrades the pattern it exists to hold flat. The substrates are polished, before any reflective coating goes on, to a residual roughness measured in tenths of a nanometre: surfaces smoother, at the relevant scale, than almost anything else manufactured in bulk. None of this is achieved by one firm. The mirrors were made throughout the technology's commercial life by a single optical-manufacturing house in southern Germany, under a development partnership with the systems integrator that ran in parallel for decades before either delivered a saleable machine.

## Hitting a falling drop, fifty thousand times a second

The light itself comes from tin because a tin plasma, correctly ionised, emits strongly and narrowly in exactly the band the mirrors can use — a coincidence of atomic structure, not of economics; the relevant transitions sit in ions stripped of eight to thirteen electrons, in an unresolved tangle of lines that happens to peak near 13.5 nanometres. The engineering answer, arrived at only after other target materials and other excitation schemes were tried and abandoned across the 1990s and 2000s, is to fire tin at the light rather than heat it in bulk: a generator ejects molten tin droplets some thirty micrometres across — a fraction of the width of a human hair — into a vacuum vessel at fifty thousand droplets a second, each travelling upward of two hundred kilometres an hour. A first, low-energy laser pulse strikes each droplet just ahead of the main shot, flattening it into a thin disc to present more surface to what follows; a second, far more powerful pulse, from a carbon-dioxide laser delivering on the order of thirty kilowatts of average power at the same fifty-kilohertz repetition rate as the droplets themselves, vaporises and ionises the disc into the plasma that emits the useful light. The conversion is not generous: on the order of five per cent of the drive laser's energy leaves the plasma as usable in-band radiation, collected by a curved mirror that sits directly in the debris field the plasma throws off and is kept clean, continuously, by a flow of hydrogen gas that scrubs re-deposited tin from its surface as a volatile compound before it can build up. Every one of those fifty thousand droplets a second, every working second the machine runs, is annihilated once and not recovered.

## One integrator, one optics house, one absorbed laser line

The light-source subsystem — laser, droplet generator, timing electronics — was developed largely by a laser-manufacturing firm in Southern California, brought inside the systems integrator by acquisition in +67 pT specifically to secure control of the one component nobody else had solved. The mirrors remained, for the technology's whole commercial run, the German optical house's alone. The systems integrator itself, the firm that assembled source, optics, mechanical stage and vacuum system into a single saleable machine, was spun out of a consumer-electronics conglomerate in +39 pT and built its plant in one town in the Low Countries. Three components, three singular suppliers, one machine: by the time the approach reached volume production in +74 pT, every advanced logic device manufactured anywhere on the planet owed a manufacturing step to all three at once, and to no substitute for any of them, because none existed.

That concentration was not a secret arrived at in hindsight. The approach itself had been ranked last of four candidate successor technologies by an industry task force in +52 pT, a year after a national funding body had voted to end its support for the research programme entirely; it survived only because a coalition of domestic chip manufacturers judged the alternatives worse and funded a parallel national-laboratory partnership to keep it alive, at a cost, by the time the partnership's programme concluded in +58 pT, of some hundreds of millions and over a hundred and fifty patent filings — a public record, before a single commercial machine shipped, that this specific technology, chosen over three others, would end in exactly this narrow a supply chain, because narrowing it was the only way any of the three firms could recover their development cost. Analysts published the resulting exposure as a strategic vulnerability for years afterward. Nothing about the concentration was corrected, because nothing about the physics or the economics offered a way to correct it.

## What it cost to own

A production machine ships disassembled, in on the order of two hundred and fifty crates, aboard several cargo aircraft and dozens of trucks, and reassembles on site to something on the order of a hundred and eighty tonnes drawn from more than a hundred thousand individually specified parts, sourced from close to eight hundred suppliers across the machine's full bill of materials — the three singular chokepoints sitting inside a much larger, merely ordinary supply chain of pumps, stages, and control electronics. A standard-tier unit sold, in the technology's mature years, for on the order of a hundred and fifty to two hundred million; the following generation, built around a wider-aperture optical system to push resolution further, priced above three hundred million. Production ran to some tens of units a year worldwide — fifty-three of the extreme-ultraviolet tier, out of four hundred and forty-nine lithography systems of every kind, in one representative year (+78 pT) — a rate set not by demand, which exceeded it throughout the technology's run, but by how many of the German house's mirror sets and the integrator's own final-assembly bays could be turned out in a year.

In +79 pT the government under whose jurisdiction the integrator and its acquired laser division sat began requiring licences for export of the newest machine tier to the jurisdiction then consuming the largest share of the world's advanced logic manufacturing capacity, citing the concentration risk the industry's own analysts had been describing for a decade; within a year the restriction extended to servicing machines already delivered. It is not this encyclopaedia's business to adjudicate the dispute that followed — the record survives well enough, in trade filings and licence logs, that later readers may. What the restriction demonstrates without needing adjudication is that the chokepoint was real enough, and understood well enough, to be worth legislating around.

---

Bronze smiths never had the option of substituting the metal; there was no other alloy that did what a ten-per-cent tin bronze did, and so the trade in it stayed narrow for as long as bronze mattered, until a cheaper, more evenly distributed metal replaced it entirely. The lithography industry had, in principle, the option: other target materials, other wavelengths, other approaches were tried across two decades and set aside, not because tin uniquely permitted the process but because tin, driven exactly this way, was the first approach to be made reliable enough to sell. No replacement had displaced it by the Contraction. The last fully documented year of production put the technology's global output, across every firm and every generation of the design, at under a hundred machines — each one consuming its molten tin fifty thousand droplets at a time, thirty micrometres across, every working second, recovering none of it.
