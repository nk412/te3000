The Ridge Advance

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a nickel identification plate recovered from a collapsed relay mast and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Ridge Advance is the Historical Faculty’s name for a fixed clock-rate offset, undetected in its own period, carried by the highest mast of an unnamed nine-station microwave relay chain built to shorten the light-path between two automated clearing floors. The chain’s layout and the offset are read from one surviving identification plate and the correction log riveted beside it; what the offset cost the floors it served is not recoverable beyond what the log implies.

It was not built to communicate. The chain carried no voice, broadcast, or human-addressed traffic: its whole function was to move a settlement instruction between two automated clearing floors faster than a rival route could, against a ceiling no engineering could move — the speed of light. Nine line-of-sight hops crossed a temperate mountain interior in place of a buried fibre route, whose glass core slows light to roughly two-thirds of its free-air speed. Fewer, taller masts meant fewer hops, and each hop’s own processing delay was a cost paid regardless of distance, so height, not distance, is what the builders minimised for. The tallest mast, raised last, carried the chain over the range’s highest gap in a single span. It also ran measurably fast, for a reason its builders had no cause to compute.

The lever nobody was solving for

Radio line of sight is set by Earth’s curvature and, weakly, by refraction; the standard telecom estimate puts a mast’s horizon near 4.1√h kilometres for height h in metres, so height bought hop length directly. It also, unrelatedly, bought clock rate: general relativity runs a clock at height h fast by gh/c² ≈ 1.09 × 10⁻¹⁶ per metre, per second — the same lever, altitude, working through two unconnected laws on the same hardware, and nobody sizing a mast for line of sight was solving for the second law at all.

The chain’s correction subroutine zeroed this nightly against a leased satellite time reference, without incident from commissioning at +134 pT (2079 CE). Its last recorded invocation is dated +151 pT (2096 CE); every log line after that is unbroken, but the correction column reads zero throughout — the chain relayed and timestamped on, against a reference it no longer had, until Mast Four’s footing gave way at +179 pT (2124 CE). By the plate’s own last-logged rate, unzeroed for that whole interval, Mast Four’s clock had advanced against Floor A’s by an estimated 168 microseconds.

Provenance and ground

The entry rests on one nickel-alloy identification plate, riveted to Mast Four’s clock housing and recovered with a fused fragment of its own correction log from the mast’s collapsed footing. Nickel resisted the acidic runoff that reduced the mast’s mild-steel lattice to a stain in the till within roughly two centuries, a preferential survival Halvorsen’s taphonomy catalogues elsewhere. The plate stamps the mast’s design elevation and oscillator model; the log’s zeroed entries are read against it. Ibarra cites the chain as a compact instance of her single-filament doctrine — nine masts and one satellite reference standing in for an entire settlement path. Neither floor’s own trading record survives.
