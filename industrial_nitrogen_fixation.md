# Industrial Nitrogen Fixation

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the light-nitrogen line in still-water sediment and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **One reaction pulls fertiliser out of open air; in a single season, and without a valve moved, it was turned to making explosives instead — the tale of the vessel that fed and killed by the same warmth, reconstructed from the isotope line the reaction wrote into the mud of still lakes worldwide.** The sediment curve and its dates are the record; the works chemist, the closed sea, and the redirect are inference from where the curve bends.

---

On the morning the sea was closed to him, the chemist walked the length of the converter hall as he did every morning, and found nothing wrong. The reaction was running. It was always running now; that was the marvel of it, and he had long since stopped being able to see the marvel, the way a man stops hearing a river he has built his house beside.

What ran in the vessels was the tamest sentence in all of chemistry and the hardest that had ever been made true. Nitrogen and hydrogen go to ammonia — one part of the air's own gas and three of hydrogen, closing to two of ammonia — and the going of it gives back about ninety-two kilojoules for every mole formed, a warmth you could feel through the wall of the vessel if you laid a hand to it, which no one did, because the steel stood at four hundred and fifty degrees and held two hundred atmospheres against the sky, and a hand laid to it would not come back.

The trouble had never been the wanting. The air is four parts in five nitrogen; a man breathes a whole life of fertiliser and starves in a field of it, because the two nitrogen atoms in the air hold to one another by a triple bond of nine hundred and forty-one kilojoules to the mole — one of the strongest handshakes any two atoms make — and for the entire length of life before this hall the only things on the planet that could break it at any scale were lightning and a narrow guild of bacteria living in the roots of certain plants. That was the whole of it. Lightning, and a bacterium, and now this room.

What broke it here was a floor of iron. Iron grown from crushed magnetite and reduced in place, its fine crystallites propped open against their own slow slumping by a wash of alumina and handed a small generosity of electrons by a trace of potash — not an elegant chemistry, and no one pretended it was, but a compromise forced through by men who could pour a steel that held the pressure without going brittle at it. It was ugly and it was a triumph and both at once. It worked the first year and it worked every year after; a full century on, with the age itself long over, nothing better had ever been found to put in its place. That was the thing the chemist had stopped seeing. He had taught the strongest bond in the ordinary world to come apart on a bed of rust, at his convenience, by the tonne.

Before the hall there had been the desert. All the reactive nitrogen a whole planet's agriculture could buy had come, for the better part of a century, through two doors and mostly through one: a rainless coastal basin whose caliche was scraped and shipped the long way round a distant cape, one country holding perhaps four parts in five of the world's traded nitrate by the time the chemist was a young man. The same salt went to the field and to the shell-filler both, out of the same pits, in the same hulls — bread and powder drawn from one ground. Men had already fought over that ground, a war of some four years, a full generation before this morning, and before the shortage it would one day cause had so much as arrived. It was the oldest shape in trade: one thing, essential, from one place, on one thin line of ships.

And this morning the line was cut. Word had come up from the coast that the lane to the desert was closed — a blockade, hulls turned back at the mouth of it — and every man in the hall who could count knew what that meant, and it was not, first of all, about bread. The nitrate fed the fields, yes; it also made the nitric acid, and the nitric acid made the propellant and the burst, and there was no other industrial source of either. On any sober reckoning the war that had just begun should end inside a year for want of the one salt, and end for everyone the same way and at the same time: not enough nitrogen left in the country to kill with.

The hall did not let the war end. The reaction in the vessels had been running at true works scale for barely a year and a season, and it made nitrogen out of the open air, which no blockade anywhere could close. It had been built to feed. It was turned, that season, to the other trade, and the turning cost nothing — not a valve moved, not a grain of the catalyst changed, not one word of the tame sentence rewritten. Ammonia to nitric acid is a further step and a well-known one; the hall simply took it. The nitrogen that would have gone under a field went into a shell instead, and the shell did not know it, and the field did not yet miss it, and the reaction — which knows nothing at all — ran on exactly as warm. The war ran four more years.

No one in the hall wrote down that this was strange. Why would they. It was the same reaction; the difference was downstream and out of sight, a matter of which pipe the ammonia left the hall by, and a plant is not built to feel that difference any more than the air is. The chemist walked the length of the hall and found nothing wrong, because by the only measure the hall had ever been given — was it running, was it clean, was it warm — there was nothing wrong to find.

When the sea opened again the hall turned back to what it had been made for, and only then could the thing show what it truly was. It scaled as no desert ever could. By the interval's later decades the world ran on the order of a hundred and fifty to a hundred and eighty million tonnes of ammonia a year, four parts in five of it bound for the field, and the people who ate off that field did the one thing no other animal's numbers had ever done. They stopped drawing the curve that every living thing draws against its own supply, and began instead to draw the curve of a chemical yield — climbing with the tonnage, held up by it, because the tonnage was, at the bottom of everything, the food.

It was not free, and the unfreedom was of a kind the hall could not see and was never once asked to price. Making the hydrogen took gas out of the ground and put a share of the age's carbon into the air, from a reaction whose own product carried none — a percent or two of everything the age burned, spent on the one salt. Half the nitrogen laid on a field is taken up by the crop; the other half leaves, down the water table and up as gas and out to the river mouths, and gathers, at a large enough scale, into water with the oxygen gone out of it: a dead patch off any well-fed delta, or seventy thousand square kilometres of one enclosed northern sea. The gas that leaves upward is stable and long-lived and quietly eats the high ozone, and by the age's own late reckoning it was the largest such agent still rising when the record thins, and no one ever wrote the treaty that would have covered it. All of it was published, in the age's own journals, in its own hand. None of it moved a valve.

What reads all this at a thousand years' remove is neither a ledger nor a hall. Both are gone, the way nearly all of the age's writing is gone. It is the mud. The nitrogen the hall made is a shade lighter than the nitrogen the bacteria make — a hair's difference in the weight of the atom, the fifteen against the fourteen — and that lightness rained out of the shared sky into the bottom of every still lake that would hold a layer, and it is lying there yet: a line that begins to shift within a decade of the desert losing its monopoly and then steepens hard across the very years the halls multiplied, in lake beds on every continent, none of them joined to any other except through the air they all breathed. The mud kept the count that the paper did not. And the count is this. By the age's second half, something close to half of the nitrogen atoms in a living human body — in the muscle of the hand that would not touch the steel, in the child that hand was feeding — had passed, at least once, through a hall like his; and between two-fifths and one-half of all the people then alive were eating, whether they were ever told so or not, out of the open air.

## Provenance and ground

This is a reconstruction. The transcribed record is the isotopic one: a lightening of the ¹⁵N:¹⁴N ratio in lake-bottom sediment across the Northern Hemisphere, onset c. −50 pT (1895 CE) and steepening through −35 to −15 pT (1910–1930 CE) — a durable clock that survives, in the mud, independent of the written record the Attrition took (Petrosyan). The chemist, the closed sea, and the redirect are inference from where that curve bends, read against the interval's one great war. The chemistry is real and unaltered: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, ΔH ≈ −92 kJ/mol, over a magnetite-derived iron catalyst promoted with K₂O and Al₂O₃ at 400–500 °C and 150–300 atm, breaking an N≡N bond of ≈941 kJ/mol. First run at industrial scale −32 pT (1913 CE); redirected without modification from fertiliser to explosives feedstock on a blockade in −31 pT (1914 CE), extending a war otherwise headed for nitrate-shortage collapse by some four years; postwar output ≈150–180 Mt ammonia/yr; two-fifths to one-half of the population dependent for its food's nitrogen. The dead are not counted here, and no collapse is claimed.
