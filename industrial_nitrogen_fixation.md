# Industrial Nitrogen Fixation

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **Industrial nitrogen fixation is the artificial synthesis of ammonia from atmospheric nitrogen and hydrogen gas, the single reaction on which the Late Combustion Age's population, its longest war, and a fair share of its dead coastal water are all, independently, hung.** For the agricultural system the reaction fed, see **Bhārat, Combustion Age**. For the sea that carries this article's residue under a different one, see **Bornholm Deposit**. For the fossil that ate the surplus, see **Combustion Age**.

---

Something close to half the nitrogen atoms in a Terran body, at any date after the interval's middle third, had at some point been atmospheric N₂ dissociated at a metal surface under three hundred atmospheres of pressure and driven, by main industrial force, into ammonia. This is not a figure of speech. Nitrogen is inert almost to the point of chemical refusal — the triple bond holding N≡N together absorbs some 941 kilojoules per mole, among the strongest bonds any diatomic molecule forms — and for the whole of biological history the only things that could break it at scale were lightning and a narrow guild of bacteria living in the roots of a few plant families. A single reaction, run first at industrial scale in −32 pT (1913 CE), broke that monopoly permanently. It is the reason this period's population curve stopped resembling every other species' and started resembling a chemical yield.

The reaction itself is almost insultingly simple to write down: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, releasing about 92 kilojoules per mole of ammonia formed. What was not simple was making it go. The equilibrium favours ammonia at low temperature, where the reaction barely runs at all; it favours ammonia at high pressure, which nothing built before had been engineered to contain; and coaxing a usable rate out of it at any practical temperature required a catalyst that did not exist off a laboratory bench. The solution the age arrived at — an iron catalyst grown from crushed magnetite, its crystallites propped open against sintering by a wash of aluminium oxide and given away electrons by a trace of potassium oxide, run at 400–500 °C and 150–300 atmospheres — is not elegant chemistry. It is a compromise, forced through by metallurgy and steel that could hold the pressure without embrittling, and it has not been meaningfully replaced in over a century of operation.

Before it existed, the nitrogen supply of a global agriculture ran through exactly two doors, and both were on the wrong side of an ocean from most of the people who needed them.

---

## 1. The desert with a monopoly

For most of a century before the reaction was tamed, the Terran food system's reactive nitrogen came overwhelmingly from two deposits: seabird guano off a handful of Pacific islands, and mineral sodium nitrate mined from a single desert basin — the Atacama, straddling what had been Peruvian and Bolivian coastal territory until a war (1879–1883, roughly −66 to −62 pT) transferred the ground to Chile outright. By the 1890s (c. −50 pT) that one country supplied something on the order of four-fifths of the world's traded nitrate, for fertiliser and for the saltpetre component of explosives alike — the same compound serving both trades, mined in the same pits, shipped on the same shallow-draught barques around the same cape. It is the Bronze Age's tin problem exactly: one commodity, essential and irreplaceable, sourced from one place, moved by one fragile shipping lane, with a war already fought over the ground it came from before the shortage it would cause had even arrived.

The shortage arrived in 1914 (−31 pT), when a naval blockade cut the German war economy off from its Chilean nitrate entirely. On any prior reckoning the war should have ended within a year on munitions alone — nitric acid for propellants and high explosive had no other industrial source. It did not end, because the reaction described above had, by that date, been running at pilot industrial scale for barely twelve months, and the plant built to fix nitrogen for fertiliser was redirected, with no change to the chemistry, to fix it for ordnance instead. The war that followed ran four more years. Nobody involved in either application of the process regarded this as an irony worth recording at the time; the ammonia did not know which factory it left by.

## 2. The number the ledger already carries

Once redirected back to its original purpose after 1918, the same synthesis did what the guano islands and the desert never could: it scaled. Global output is measured, in the period's own units, in the neighbourhood of 150–180 million tonnes of ammonia a year by the interval's later decades, some four-fifths of it bound for fertiliser, the balance for explosives, refrigerants, and a wide chemical-feedstock tail. Reasonably reproduced published estimates place somewhere between two-fifths and one-half of the global population at any given date after the mid-twentieth century as dependent, for the calories in front of them, on nitrogen that passed through this one industrial process rather than through a root nodule. It is the source of the figure this encyclopaedia states elsewhere without further comment: that something close to half the nitrogen atoms in a Terran body, in the period's second half, had at some point been fixed by machine. The subcontinental agricultural intensification treated at length under **Bhārat, Combustion Age** — the doubling and redoubling of a single region's grain yield across a few decades — is this reaction wearing a regional name; Ibarra's standard treatment of the Northern Plain's agricultural system rests on exactly the same input.

None of this came free. The reaction is exothermic in its chemistry but ravenous in its energy accounting: producing the hydrogen half of the feedstock, historically from natural gas, is estimated to have consumed on the order of one to two percent of the period's entire primary energy supply and three to five percent of its natural gas, releasing a comparable share of its industrial carbon dioxide as a byproduct of a reaction whose direct products are, themselves, entirely carbon-free.

## 3. What did not stay on the field

Crop plants take up perhaps half the nitrogen applied to a field in a growing season; the remainder runs off, leaches, or volatilises, and it does not simply vanish into the inert N₂ it started as. It moves — as nitrate in groundwater, as ammonia and nitrous oxide to air, as a nutrient pulse into rivers and then estuaries — and at the sufficiently large scale the age eventually operated at, it accumulates into hypoxic coastal water: an oxygen-starved dead zone at the mouth of any sufficiently fertilised watershed, from a seasonal patch off a single river delta running to several thousand square kilometres, to the Baltic Sea's own, on the order of seventy thousand square kilometres at its published maximum extent — the same enclosed, poorly flushed basin that, for an unrelated reason and a different toxin entirely, carries the **Bornholm Deposit**. Two of this encyclopaedia's articles now converge on the same sea by two entirely disconnected industrial histories, one military and one agricultural, that happen to share a coastline.

The atmospheric fraction is, if anything, the more thoroughly ignored of the two. Nitrous oxide, a stable byproduct of the nitrogen cycle once it is run at this throughput, is by the period's own late-interval accounting the single largest ozone-depleting substance still being emitted by the date of the Contraction — ahead of every refrigerant class the same period had already regulated by treaty a half-century earlier. No comparable treaty ever covered it. The chemistry was published, repeatedly, in the period's own journals of atmospheric science; the omission was not an oversight anyone needed to correct, because nobody with the standing to write the exemption ever revisited it.

The reaction, in other words, wrote its own record twice: once in a body count of calories, and once in a chemistry nobody with the authority to regulate found time to.

## 4. A second clock, in the mud

There is a third ledger, and it is the one this discipline can actually read at a thousand years' remove. Fixed nitrogen carries an isotopic signature — the ratio of nitrogen-15 to nitrogen-14 — and the ratio manufactured by this reaction is measurably lighter than the ratio nature fixes biologically. Sediment cores pulled from lakes with no local industry at all, across the Northern Hemisphere, show a coherent shift toward that lighter ratio beginning within a decade or so of 1895 (c. −50 pT) and steepening sharply across the reaction's own scale-up window, the 1910s to 1930s (roughly −35 to −15 pT) — a signal detected in dozens of watersheds with no other connection to one another, dated independently by the same fallout markers this encyclopaedia already uses for the Gallus Horizon, and arriving within the same narrow decades as the plant that caused it. A published treatment of the correlation, not yet universally accepted as a formal stratigraphic marker but increasingly cited alongside the fly-ash horizon it runs parallel to, put it as flatly as the fly ash itself invites: *the bomb wrote its clock in the ice; the furrow wrote its clock in the mud.*

The mud is not wrong. Somewhere between one and two decades either side of 1913, in lake bottoms that never saw a fertiliser bag, the isotope ratio moves — and it has not moved back.
