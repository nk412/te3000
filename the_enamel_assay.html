The Enamel Assay

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a curated dental assemblage — several thousand sampled human molars retained as a verification archive — and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Enamel Assay is the Historical Faculty’s name for a Late Combustion technique that read the strontium- and oxygen-isotope signature locked into a person’s tooth enamel to adjudicate where that person had spent early childhood, deployed at inland relief and residency stations to test the origin claims of people on the move. The recovered teeth and their measured ratios are physical record; the use to which the ratios were put is reconstruction, read from the stations’ retained assemblage and its field apparatus, and the ambiguity in that use has not been resolved.

In +124 pT (2069 CE), an inland residency station on a dry interior escarpment, poleward and upland of coasts already two generations abandoned, logged the extraction of its ten-thousandth enamel sample. The Enamel Assay was a provenance measurement — a determination, from the isotope chemistry of a single tooth, of the geology of the water a person had drunk as a small child — repurposed from a decades-old archaeological and forensic method into a gate. The gate stood on the road: across roughly +80 to +160 pT (2025–2105 CE) on the order of a billion people relocated inland and upland under a hundred local names, and the stations that received them at the dry interiors used the assay to sort a claimed origin from a measured one. The method was real, exact, and older than the crisis it was turned on; what it measured was not where a person had come from but where that person had been a child, and the two are the same only for someone who never moved. The measurement was rigorous. What the stations did with it is the contested part, and the teeth do not settle it.

The mineral clock

Enamel is the most thoroughly mineralised tissue the body makes — about 96% hydroxyapatite by weight, a calcium-phosphate lattice — and, unlike bone, it is not remodelled: once a crown has finished forming in childhood it exchanges no further material with the body for the rest of a life. Whatever chemistry is locked into it at formation stays locked. Two isotope systems ride in on that chemistry. Strontium substitutes for calcium in the lattice, the Sr²⁺ ion sitting in the Ca²⁺ site it slightly overfills, and it arrives already carrying a ratio — ⁸⁷Sr to ⁸⁶Sr — set not by the body but by the rock under the childhood catchment. That ratio is a geological clock: ⁸⁷Sr is the daughter of rubidium-87, which decays with a half-life near 48.8 billion years, so old continental crust rich in rubidium has spent aeons accumulating radiogenic ⁸⁷Sr and reads high — granites and ancient gneiss above 0.716, sometimes past 0.730 — while young volcanic rock reads near 0.703–0.704 and marine carbonate near 0.707–0.709. The strontium passes from rock to soil to groundwater to plant to person with no mass fractionation the measurement cannot correct for, so the ratio in the enamel is, to within the fifth or sixth decimal place, the ratio of the ground that raised the child. The second system, the ratio of oxygen-18 to oxygen-16, tracks the drinking water itself — its value falling with latitude, altitude, and cold — and gives a second, coarser coordinate. Read together against a map of the two quantities across the land surface, the pair place a childhood.

The reading was done by mass spectrometer — thermal-ionisation, or a multi-collector plasma source for teeth sampled in place — resolving ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr to a precision near ±0.00001, one part in a hundred thousand, after normalising out instrument drift against the fixed ⁸⁸Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratio of 8.375209. It is among the most precise routine measurements the age could make on a human being, and it was being made, at the interior stations, on the living.

The sorting

A residency or relief claim in the interior turned on origin: whether a person belonged to a catchment, was owed its thinning water and grid, could be enrolled or must be turned back onto the road. Documentary origin had largely stopped being checkable — the civil registers were among the first institutions the Contraction dissolved — so the stations reached for an origin the body carried and could not revise. The claimant presented; a tooth was taken; the ratio was measured and matched against an isotope map; a claimed birthplace either agreed with the enamel or did not. The apparatus was terse. One laminated operator card, recovered with the assemblage, carries the whole procedure:

PRESENT LOWER LEFT FIRST MOLAR. IF ABSENT OR RESTORED, USE CONTRALATERAL. ONE TOOTH PER CLAIMANT. DO NOT SAMPLE ROOT.

The first molar finishes forming its enamel by about age three and a half. A person who moved once as a child — and the people at these gates were, definitionally, people who moved — carries more than one homeland in a single jaw: the first molar holds the water of the first place, the third molar, whose crown does not complete until about age twelve or thirteen, holds the water of wherever the family had reached by then. The card specified one tooth, and it specified the earliest one. The assay that was built to catch people misrepresenting where they were from recorded, by its own written procedure, only where they had been before they were four, and read a childhood move as a lie about origin.

What the enamel kept

The stations retained the sampled teeth, indexed against each claim, as a verification archive — the physical basis for a decision that could be challenged. Enamel is the hardest tissue in the body and the most durable in the ground; where the paper claims and the electronic registers decayed on the ordinary schedule of the Attrition, the teeth did not. The retained assemblage came through three thousand years intact, and with it the very quantity the stations had measured, because enamel does not remodel: the ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr a mass spectrometer reads from a recovered molar in the 3000 CE laboratory is the same ratio the station read from the living mouth. The instrument that sorted the moving and the survey that now maps them are one measurement on one unchanging mineral. From the assemblage, the Faculty reconstructs an isoscape of the road — thousands of childhood catchments, plotted where the teeth were sampled, tracing displacement from the dry interiors back toward coasts and plains the stations had turned people away from. Okonkwo, D., reading the archive as an unintended census under the standing method for such records, notes that its coordinate is systematically the wrong one: it maps where each claimant had been an infant, not the journey, and where a jaw preserved both molars the two disagree far more often than a settled population’s would — the move written into the mouth the station declined to read.

Whether the disagreement was error or instrument is the open question. Adeyemi-Sørensen, T. (2903), holding that the single-tooth rule was a throughput economy on overwhelmed stations, reads the assay as a measurement misapplied by people with no time to sample twice; a second school reads the same rule as the point — a gate that admitted a childhood but not a life, calibrated to a settled world by operators who knew, or did not, that the claimants before them were the counter-instance. The teeth carry both readings and adjudicate neither; the field cards are identical whichever is true.

Provenance and ground

The entry rests on the retained dental assemblage of a single interior station, recovered intact from dry, high sediment and dated by its enclosing layer — radiocarbon being unavailable for the age — to the +120s pT (2060s CE), together with the station’s field apparatus, of which the quoted operator card is the sole legible text. Halvorsen, E. supplies the taphonomy: enamel’s survival where the associated bone and all documentary record did not. The isotope chemistry is read directly from the teeth; the station’s designation, its authority, and the disposition of any individual claim are not attested in the assemblage and are not reconstructed here.
