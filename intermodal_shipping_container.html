Intermodal Shipping Container

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition. Rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

A corrugated-steel box, built to one of two standard lengths and closed by a single repeated mechanical fitting at each corner, that let cargo cross unopened from ship to railcar to truck chassis and became, within two decades of its introduction, the load-bearing unit of the Combustion Age’s seaborne trade. For the general model of a single, continuously funded interface standing in for an entire supply chain, see Leaded Petrol. For what became of the paper and electronic record of what any given box carried, see Attrition, the.

The standard box measured, in the period’s own units, either twenty or forty feet in length, eight feet in width, and a little over eight feet in height; a set of national and international standards bodies converged on these dimensions and on a single corner-fitting geometry across +16 to +24 pT (1961–1969 CE), late enough in the box’s working life that the first several years of service ran on a half-dozen incompatible regional designs before the industry settled on one. The fitting is the whole invention: a cast-steel socket at each of the box’s eight corners, engineered to accept a rotating pin — a twist-lock — that a crane, a ship’s cell guide, a railcar, or a truck chassis could each engage identically, so that the box’s contents never had to be known, inspected, or touched by whatever moved it next. The first sailing under the concept, +11 pT (1956 CE), was a converted wartime tanker carrying fifty-eight such boxes from Newark to Houston; the operator, a trucking-firm owner from the American South unhappy with how much of a shipment’s cost and time went to manual handling at the dock, published his own figures for the crossing — cargo that had cost $5.86 a ton to load by hand cost sixteen cents a ton loaded as sealed boxes lifted whole. The name for a single twenty-foot unit, the twenty-foot equivalent, was coined a decade later, +24 pT (1969 CE), once the trade had grown large enough to need a unit of account.

It is now the vessel for most of what a Combustion-Age household owned that it did not grow or make locally: something upward of nine-tenths of the world’s non-bulk cargo — everything that is not grain, ore, oil, or another commodity moved loose in a ship’s own hold — travelled boxed, in a fleet exceeding twenty million individual units making on the order of eight hundred million loaded moves a year by the interval’s later decades, on vessels whose largest class had grown, across barely half a century, to carry over twenty-four thousand boxes at once. The interface is the whole story of the scale: a system that can move anything at all, precisely because none of its handling machinery is built to know what it is moving, concentrates an entire planet’s manufactured goods onto a single physical standard and, downstream of that standard, onto a very small number of places where the standard’s own geometry creates a bottleneck no ship can route around.

The isthmus problem

Two of those bottlenecks are artificial: canals cut across land bridges to let a ship move between ocean basins without circumnavigating a continent. The busier of the two, at the northern mouth of a canal joining a landlocked sea to an ocean gulf, is a single navigable channel for most of its length; a vessel of the class introduced from the +60s pT (2000s CE) onward, if it goes aground across that channel, closes it entirely, in both directions, for as long as it takes to refloat. This happened, extensively documented in the period’s own commercial press, over six days in late +76 pT (March 2021 CE): a single grounded, foreign-flagged megaship blocked the canal end to end, and the channel authority’s own published estimate placed the value of trade held up on either side of the vessel at some nine billion dollars a day, against a toll-revenue loss to the authority itself of roughly fourteen million dollars for each day the canal stood closed — a difference of nearly three orders of magnitude between what the chokepoint was worth to the system passing through it and what it was worth to the institution that owned it. Ibarra’s model of single-filament infrastructure, built from a very different case — an agricultural system dependent on one class of engineered nitrogen and, elsewhere in her work, on a handful of licensed antiknock-compound plants — applies to the isthmus without modification: a facility whose failure mode is binary (open or closed, whatever its capacity) is a single point of failure regardless of how much volume it normally carries, and the interval’s shipping industry, having built a planetary logistics network around exactly two such crossings, treated the arrangement as background rather than as risk.

The same stretch of water carries this encyclopaedia’s clearest structural correspondence to the deep past. This discipline already reads two sealed, general-cargo wrecks — one in the Strait of Malacca, one at that same northern canal mouth — as its nearest equivalent to a Bronze Age wreck excavated whole off a Mediterranean headland eight centuries before the interval this article covers: a single vessel, mixed cargo, every artefact class the age moved, in roughly the proportion it moved them. The two locations were not chosen for excavation. They chose themselves, the way the original wreck chose its own headland — by being the place where an unusually high density of ships passed an unusually narrow strait, so that whatever went down there went down in volume. A trade system concentrated onto two channels concentrates its shipwrecks onto the same two channels; the discipline’s best single-vessel samples of the age are not a curatorial achievement so much as a traffic statistic.

The patina that needed weather

The steel is where the article’s real chemistry sits. Most boxes are built from a low-alloy weathering steel, formulated so that its first exposure to open-air moisture forms a tight, adherent oxide layer — a patina — that then slows further corrosion to a fraction of the rate of plain structural steel, roughly an eighth by the manufacturers’ own figures, provided the surface is allowed to dry out between wettings. The mechanism is specific and well characterised: the protective layer only stabilises under alternating wet and dry cycling: rain, then evaporation, then rain again. Steel of the identical alloy that is instead kept continuously wet — buried, waterlogged, or permanently submerged — never forms the stable layer at all, and corrodes at close to the ordinary, unprotected rate. A box built to survive twenty-five to forty years of open-deck salt spray precisely because it dries between wettings is, the moment it goes over the side and stays down, built out of a steel with no advantage left in it whatsoever. The alloy does not fail differently underwater. It simply stops being the alloy it was engineered to be, and reverts to being any other steel of the same composition, which is a much shorter-lived thing.

Boxes go over the side in numbers the industry’s own trade body counted and published every year without apparent alarm: an average of roughly 1,400 lost annually across +63–74 pT (2008–2019 CE), a record low of 221 in +78 pT (2023), a rebound to 576 the following year, and roughly 1,478 in +80 pT (2025) — against a global total of some 280 million boxes carried that year, a loss fraction on the order of five thousandths of one per cent. The published tallies are precise to the individual box and treated, consistently, as an acceptable operating variance rather than an emergency: in the same +80 pT report, a single incident accounted for 640 of the year’s losses, some forty-three per cent of the annual total, concentrated in one storm, off one vessel, and the trade body’s own commentary characterised the year as merely “above the recent average,” not exceptional. Nothing about the corrosion chemistry above was withheld from that industry; the wet-dry dependence of a weathering alloy is textbook metallurgy, published decades before the box that relies on it was first welded. What is missing from the record is not the knowledge. It is any published reckoning of what several hundred boxes a year, each one reverting the moment it submerges from a slow eighth-rate corrosion to an ordinary one, actually deposit on the world’s continental shelves across seven decades of routine loss — a number nobody in the period seems to have found it necessary to add up, on the reasonable-sounding grounds that no single year’s total was ever large.

What did not travel as well

None of this — the corner-fitting geometry, the vessel schedules, the customs paperwork that let a sealed box cross a dozen jurisdictions without inspection — survives the way the box itself might. Cargo manifests, bills of lading, and the automated scheduling records that told a crane which box to lift next were, by the interval’s final decades, electronic almost without exception, subject to the same migration debt as every other digital record of the age, and gone with the rest of it in the general Attrition. What is left, for the discipline that studies this system, is the standard’s shadow in the objects that used it: a corrugated steel panel of a specific gauge, a corner casting of a specific cast geometry, found wherever the sea or the ground kept them, telling us that a box of exactly this interface passed through, and telling us next to nothing about what was inside it, who packed it, or where it was going.

One further asymmetry is worth stating without resolving it. As regional service contracted through the Contraction, the deep-water hub terminals — the ports that had absorbed the greatest capital investment in cranes, dredged channels, and rail connections — kept receiving diverted calls for markedly longer than comparably sized feeder ports lost to the same contraction years earlier, a pattern read from surviving vessel-call logs rather than from any surviving institutional record of why. The hierarchy that persisted tracked the depth of capital already sunk into a given harbour, not the size of the population it served or the volume of cargo that population still needed moved. Whether that reflects rational institutional triage, market inertia, or something less examined is a question this discipline is not presently in a position to answer, and the surviving logs, being logs of ship movements and not of anyone’s reasoning, will not settle it.

A container lost from a deck manifest in a storm off any coast begins, from the moment of submersion, reverting: the patina that was protecting it stops forming, and an eighth-rate corrosion becomes a full one. Of the several hundred boxes the record shows going down in an ordinary year, one incident alone, in the most recently completed count available to this encyclopaedia, accounted for forty-three per cent.
