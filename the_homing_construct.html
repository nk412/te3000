The Homing Construct

Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from a single versioned source repository in the Code Deposit and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.

The Homing Construct is the Historical Faculty’s designation for an engineered genetic element, recovered only as its own design source code, built to copy itself onto the matching chromosome and so drive a chosen trait through a wild population faster than ordinary inheritance permits. Its sequence, its target, and the log of its revisions survive bit-exact; whether any physical copy of it was ever synthesised, released, or is now anywhere alive is not recoverable.

The fragment is a versioned source repository: a directory tree of a few hundred files — construct maps, a single twenty-letter target sequence, a population-genetics simulation, and a log of some four hundred revisions, each named by a fingerprint of its own contents and so provably unaltered since it was written. It describes a homing gene drive: a cassette encoding a sequence-guided cutting enzyme, inserted into a genome at the very site its guide directs the enzyme to cut. From the code alone the mechanism, the target, and the intended reach are exact. Who wrote it, whether a molecule was ever built from it, and what became of the release it was designed for, are not.

The twenty letters

An organism carrying the construct on one chromosome and a wild sequence on the other cuts the wild copy at the guide’s target and repairs the break by copying the whole cassette across, using the intact chromosome as template. A cell that began heterozygous ends homozygous, so nearly every gamete carries the drive. Reported transmission in the period’s own experiments ran near 95% and as high as 99%, against the 50% ordinary inheritance allows — an element that biases its own descent. The entire specificity rides on the guide’s twenty-nucleotide spacer, one string out of the roughly 1.1 × 10¹² that four bases can spell at that length, set beside a short recognition motif the enzyme requires; the repository fixes exactly one such string. A comment marking the design goal states that a single release should pay for itself within one breeding season. The propagation it sets in motion is, by construction, permanent: the drive keeps copying itself for as long as the target species persists, and the cost of arresting it, should it need arresting, falls on generations not yet hatched.

What the log records

A drive fails when the cut is repaired not by copying but by rejoining the loose ends, which leaves a small insertion or deletion: a target the guide can no longer recognise and, if the gene still works, a resistant sequence immune to the drive that spreads in its place. Every drive the period tested threw such alleles. The way around it, legible across the repository’s revisions, was to aim the guide at a sequence so functionally constrained that any edit to it is itself lethal or sterilising, so that resistance cannot arise without paying the same price. The model bundled with the source projected:

Revision (by target) Target sequence Transmission to offspring Outcome in the bundled model
early coding sequence, unconstrained ~95% at first functional resistance arises; drive stalls; population recovers within tens of generations
intermediate multiplexed cut sites ~97% resistance delayed, not prevented
late intron–exon splice boundary, functionally constrained ~99% drive nears fixation in 7–11 generations; sex-determination disrupted, one sex sterilised; no functional resistance recovered

What the repository records past that is contested three ways, none adjudicated. On one reading, associated with Ibarra, the late revisions’ containment notes and a cost model priced per hectare are the marks of an operational programme — an entire species’ inheritance run through one twenty-letter filament, her single-filament doctrine in miniature. On another, Marchetti’s, nothing here is more than a simulation study that never left the machine, and to read a repository’s field parameters as a field is the standing error of taking code for the event it models. On a third, Okonkwo’s, the question has no author to answer it: by the Late Combustion Age, sequence-design tools proposed candidate guides and wrote their own maintainer-styled commit messages, and the surviving log cannot be sorted into human-chosen and machine-proposed lines. Whether a programme decided anything, or a design process was simply left running over the space of possible targets, is not in the record to recover.

The last revision fixes the guide at the constrained target and closes without a field note. Whether that twenty-letter sequence is, at any moment now, present in anything alive is not a question the code can answer.

Provenance and ground

The entry rests entirely on one repository in the Code Deposit — the single Sunday snapshot of the world’s public software, self-verifying because every file is named by a fingerprint of its own contents, so that a recovered file is provably the original. The consignment shipped late and was shelved high, and so survived the flooding that dissolved the chamber’s lower racks. What survives is design, not deposition: source code proves that a construct was specified to the base, not that one was ever synthesised, released, or propagated. No biological material is attested, and the age’s radiocarbon record could not have dated it in any case. The path into the record, and the datable commit log, are read after Okonkwo’s standing method of taking a repository’s incidental contents as an unintended record; Marchetti’s caution against reading a commit message as composed testimony applies here with unusual force, the authorship of these messages being precisely what cannot be established.
