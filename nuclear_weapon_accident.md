# Nuclear Weapon Accident

*Terran Encyclopaedia, 3000 CE edition — Recovered Narratives. Reconstructed from the recovery operation's closing inventory and rendered into Reconstructed Late Combustion English by convention of the Historical Faculty.*

> **No sealed nuclear weapon is recorded as having detonated by accident anywhere in the surviving literature; the one Combustion-Age recovery that came closest to a full accounting of a dispersed weapon balanced its inventory to ninety percent and could not close it by a single uranium cylinder.** The recovery figures and the two later readings of the closing audit are the operation's and the archives' own; whether a fjord floor a hundred metres down holds a lost weapon, or nothing discrete at all, is the one question the record raises and cannot answer.

---

**A nuclear weapon accident is a mishap involving a sealed thermonuclear weapon — dropped, burned, crushed, or lost — that produces no nuclear yield.** The term is a category of the interval's middle decades, when one nuclear-armed state alone would eventually declassify at least thirty-two such incidents across +5 to +35 pT, and it is defined by the thing that did not occur. Every weapon of the period's design lineage was built to a single stated number: the probability that firing its conventional explosive from one point on the casing, rather than the many exactly timed points a fission detonation requires, would yield more than about four pounds of ordinary explosive had to be held below one in a million. The standard was called one-point safety. Across the whole service life of the type, in every accident anyone ever declassified, it held. No weapon dropped, burned, crushed, or lost is recorded anywhere as having produced the flash it was built to produce. The ending the age rehearsed did not arrive by accident; it was engineered, to a stated probability, not to.

This is the interval's largest engineered non-event. The age had rehearsed its own ending around exactly that flash — built the warnings, the shelters, the long civil liturgy of the airborne alert — and kept, for seven years, a rotating share of a bomber fleet flying armed and outbound on the reasoning that an aircraft already aloft could not be destroyed on the ground. Two of those bombers came down with their weapons aboard, twenty-five months apart. On the first, in +21 pT, a midair collision scattered the plutonium of dispersed weapons across some two hundred and sixty hectares of a farming coast to the south and dropped one weapon onto a seabed that took eighty days to find and lift. On the second, in +23 pT, an aircraft on the far-northern leg of the standing patrol went down onto the sea ice of an Arctic fjord with four weapons aboard. All four broke open. The conventional explosive of each fired. None of the four approached a nuclear yield.

What the fjord received instead was fire, jet fuel, and plutonium burned into the ice, and a recovery that ran nine months and closed its books to ninety percent. The tenth it could not recover was not an object anyone could go and find; it was a figure in an inventory. And it was the inventory, not the ice, that the case came to rest on: a mass-balance accounting, component for component, for every part of four weapons but one — a single secondary cylinder of enriched uranium, keg-sized, inert on its own, unaccounted for on an audit that otherwise closed. A full lifetime later the same pages were read twice and yielded, once, a weapon lost on the fjord floor, and once, no weapon at all.

---

## 1. The two dispersals

Both accidents fell inside the same doctrine and produced the same class of result: a weapon whose conventional explosive detonated — on impact, or in fire — scattering the plutonium of its primary with no fission yield. Neither was a detonation in the sense the age feared. Both were dispersals.

The coastal case, +21 pT, followed a collision during aerial refuelling. Weapons fell to farmland and to the sea; the high explosive of those that struck land fired and spread plutonium oxide across roughly two hundred and sixty hectares of cultivated coast, and the contaminated soil and crop were drummed and carried off. One weapon entered the water and was located and raised only after eighty days — the interval's standard instance of recovering a deliberately hardened object after the fact, at depth, by reasoning rather than by any signal from the weapon itself.

The Arctic case, twenty-five months later, began with a cabin fire. The crew of a bomber flying a cold leg of the continuous airborne patrol had stacked cloth-covered cushions over a heating vent and bled hot engine air into the cabin against the chill; the cushions ignited first. Within about half an hour the fire had the rear of the aircraft. Six of the seven aboard left on ejection seats and lived; the seventh, who had earlier given his seat to a relief pilot, made for a lower hatch and did not. The aircraft struck the fjord ice at a shallow angle and at speed, and several tonnes of burning fuel opened the pack to the black water beneath. Its four weapons — each rated, had it functioned, in the tens to the low hundreds of kilotons — broke apart on the ice, their conventional explosive firing, their cores thrown out and burned into the snow. The doctrine of the standing armed patrol had survived the coastal accident. It did not survive this one; it ended within a day.

## 2. The Arctic accounting

The recovery was competent, sustained, and physically brutal. Blackened ice, wreckage, and contaminated snow were cut from the fjord surface, sealed into steel tanks, and shipped off the island: some two hundred and thirty-seven thousand cubic feet of it off roughly six hectares of surface, with more than half a million gallons of meltwater besides. Within about six weeks the operation's own tally put recovered surface plutonium above eight parts in ten; by the time the ice closed the fjord that autumn the figure stood near nine. The missing tenth was smoke gone downwind, ice sublimated back into the air, and sediment settled past a diver's reach on a fjord floor near a hundred metres down. A tenth of a scatter is not a recoverable thing. It is a number.

The audit that mattered was not of the plutonium but of the weapons. A weapon of the type carried two principal masses: a primary, built around a plutonium core, and a secondary, built around a cylinder of enriched uranium roughly the size of a keg, which could produce no yield of any kind without a primary detonating first to drive it. The recovery accounted, mass for mass, for four primaries, for one secondary intact, and for enough recovered material, weighed and assayed, to reconstruct two further secondaries in full. It could not account for the fourth. One uranium cylinder — keg-sized, radiating weakly enough that a survey meter passed over it might read nothing, incapable on its own of the single thing the whole apparatus existed to do — was unaccounted for on an audit that otherwise closed. So far as the surviving record shows, nobody ever went down to look for it. The ice refroze, and the account was balanced on paper, to a stated confidence, by an operation that had lifted nine parts in ten and reasoned about the tenth.

## 3. Two readings

More than a full lifetime later, when the two governments' files were opened, the missing tenth became an argument the same documents could not settle. One declassified technical assessment, released at +63 pT, stated that a weapon remained on the floor of the fjord, unrecovered. A review of the identical documents the following year, at +64 pT, by a body with standing over both archives, concluded that only the single secondary component had ever been unaccounted for — not a complete weapon, and nothing that could yield on its own — and that the operation's own mass-balance had in substance closed the case at the time. The same pages, read twice, gave a lost weapon and gave no lost weapon.

This is the case the discipline keeps for what one scholar calls **the balanced ledger**: an inventory reconciled on paper, to a number, standing in for a search nobody ran to the bottom, and trusted afterward exactly as if the search had been run. A tenth recovered as a figure settles nothing physical. The missing cylinder cannot say, from wherever it is, whether it was lifted in a tank of black ice and miscounted, burned to nothing in the fuel, or resting still on the sediment where no one has asked. Nine parts in ten, in one fjord, in one closely documented year, is the most complete inventory the discipline has of any dispersed weapon of the type — and it is short one cylinder, and the shortfall reads, depending on which of two correct documents one holds, as an account closed or an account open.

## 4. Consent, workforce, and settlement

The record around the fjord keeps the same shape wherever it is complete. The base from which the patrol flew stood on soil whose government had told its own parliament, for decades and in plain words, that it permitted no nuclear weapon anywhere under its flag. A private understanding to the contrary, given in +12 pT, was kept from the public on both sides for thirty-eight years and disclosed only at +50 pT. Everyone who signed it had understood it.

In that same year, +50 pT, the workers who had cut and loaded the contaminated ice — those still living and still findable, about seventeen hundred of them — were each paid a single flat sum. They had worked in insulated but otherwise ordinary cold-weather clothing, issued nothing to breathe through, handling ice with plutonium burned into it. A later study of the group found cancer incidence running some forty to fifty percent above a comparable population, and could not say, from what survived of the dosimetry, how much of the excess the fjord had caused; causation is recorded as undetermined. Most of those who took the payment are recorded as having called it too little.

What the doctrine left on the fjord floor, if it left anything, is a cylinder of enriched uranium that cannot detonate and cannot testify, entered in the one honest inventory of the age as recovered to ninety percent — an account that no one has been down to settle.

## Provenance and ground

The fragment is the recovery operation's own closing inventory — its item-for-item count of primaries and secondaries — released at +63 pT and re-read at +64 pT; the figures here are transcribed from it and from the accident record, not composed. The physics is real. One-point safety — that single-point initiation of a weapon's conventional explosive cannot reach design yield, the probability held below one in a million — is why nothing detonated across the declassified record, and a weapon's secondary, a cylinder of enriched uranium, is inert without a primary to drive it. Whether the fjord holds a lost weapon or nothing discrete at all is a dispute the surviving documents produce and cannot resolve — **the balanced ledger** (Okonkwo).
